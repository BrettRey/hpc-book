**Strengths**
- The chapter has a strong architectural through-line: from “The HPC claim” to “Empirical signatures” to “The HPC audit,” it keeps returning to one core proposal (form-value coupling plus maintenance). That coherence is publishable.
- The distinction in “The stabilizer: a feeling” between detector output and target property is methodologically promising. Treating intuitions as measurements rather than ground truth is exactly the right anti-positivist move for judgment data.
- “The phenomenology of coupling: What it’s like” is commendably self-limiting at the outset (“mnemonics, not explanatory load-bearing beams”). That disclaimer is disciplined and protects against over-reading first-person report.
- The chapter does some genuine risk-taking: it names potential dissociations (garden paths, comparative illusions), and in the “Definiteness thread” it includes an explicit falsifier. That is better than most theory chapters in this area.

**Major Concerns**
- The mechanism is still under-specified where it matters most. “The detector’s Bayesian” gestures at computation, but no state variables, update rules, or observables are given. As written, the “immune system” and “zipper” do more explanatory work than an explicit mechanism.
- Construct validity is unstable. “Structural meaning” starts as a focused morphosyntactic construct, then broadens to include information structure, clause type, and eventually “appropriateness channels” (register, accent, identity). That expansion risks category drift: the explanandum shifts mid-chapter.
- The acceptability/grammaticality distinction is not yet secure. In “Entrenchment in action,” you say the detector and what counts as grammatical are “not separable.” If so, the later claim that acceptability is merely a noisy readout becomes difficult to defend without circularity. Right now, you alternate between constitutive and evidential roles for entrenchment.
- The phenomenology section, despite good caveats, still looks partly post hoc. The triadic mapping (“jolt, block, correction” to sign/object/interpretant) reads interpretive rather than predictive. A skeptic will ask what outcome would falsify that mapping.
- Empirical framing mixes robust findings with speculative placeholders. The LBE passage introduces “~3-sigma absence,” “schema drift,” and a specific competitor-activation story with a precision level not matched by described methods. This looks like rhetorical over-precision unless you provide operational definitions and design details.
- What a skeptical specialist will reject first: the claim that grammaticality is itself an HPC kind, rather than a useful modeling stance. Why? Because the chapter has not yet shown independent diagnostics that distinguish “HPC grammaticality” from a relabeled bundle of distribution, processing load, and sociolinguistic norm enforcement.

**Priority Fixes**
- Add one explicit mechanistic schema (inputs, latent state, detector output, repair behavior, timescale). Keep metaphors as glosses, not engines.
- Freeze the construct boundary for this chapter: either morphosyntactic grammaticality only, or a clearly separated second claim about generalized appropriateness.
- Resolve the constitutive/evidential tension: state whether entrenchment defines grammaticality, estimates it, or both at different levels.
- Split empirical claims by status: established evidence, plausible extrapolation, and conjecture. Remove faux precision where methods are not yet provided.
- Tighten the phenomenology section so each “register” corresponds to a measurable signature (e.g., RT cost, repair probability, confidence report).

**Decisive Tests**
- Pre-registered longitudinal exposure study crossing construction type (marginal vs categorical) and exposure regime; model whether change is detector recalibration, grammar change, or task adaptation.
- Cross-community calibration experiment (e.g., age constructions, dialectal article patterns) measuring rapidity and reversibility of acceptability shifts by interlocutor identity.
- Orthogonal manipulation study: morphosyntactic violation vs semantic anomaly vs register mismatch, testing whether they produce distinct behavioral and metacognitive profiles.
- Triadic-register falsification test: derive distinct predicted signatures for “jolt,” “block,” and “correction,” then test separability; if they collapse onto one factor, revise the triadic mapping.
- Direct model comparison against relevance-based accounts (“Relevance and the immune system”): specify discriminating predictions, not just conceptual compatibility.
