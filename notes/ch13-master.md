# Chapter 13: Sign Language Anaphor — Master Plan

**Working title:** "Where the Hands Point"
**Target:** ~6,000 words (8 sections, matching Ch 12 scale)
**Epigraph candidate:** Something from Sandler or Padden on space as grammar, not curiosity. TBD after drafting.

## A. What the chapter does in the book

**Forward load:** Tests modality independence — the claim that HPC architecture is substrate-neutral. If the coupling/maintenance/projection apparatus works only for spoken language, the framework is weaker than advertised. This is the last case study before the synthesis chapter (Ch 14, formerly Ch 13).

**Backward load:** Draws on Part II apparatus (clusters, mechanisms, failure modes, projectibility) and parallels the specific moves of Ch 9 (countability: individuation ≠ count syntax), Ch 10 (definiteness ≠ deitality), Ch 12 (gender: transparent vs entrenched maintenance).

**The discovery:** Anaphoric tracking and spatial agreement have been conflated because both run through spatial loci. The village sign language evidence shows they're separable clusters — same spatial vehicle, different mechanisms, different projective profiles. This parallels definiteness/deitality exactly.

## B. The core theoretical move

### Two clusters, one spatial channel

| | Referent tracking (semantic) | Spatial agreement (formal) |
|---|---|---|
| Function | Introduce, maintain, reactivate discourse referents | Mark person/number on verbs via directionality; trigger locus features |
| Maintained by | Spatial working memory, communicative pressure, interactive alignment | Grammatical entrenchment, acquisition regularisation, verb-class constraints |
| Kata Kolok | Robust pointing (1/6 of all signs); absolute spatial frame | No productive agreement verbs (3/839 modified tokens; De Vos p. 152) |
| ABSL | Word order + reference tracking emerged early | No agreement morphology after multiple generations (Sandler et al. 2005 p. 28) |
| NSL | Shared-reference spatial modulation: cohort 2 = 0.88 vs cohort 1 = 0.63 | Productive agreement still developing across cohorts (Senghas & Coppola 2001 p. 5) |
| Homesign | Deictic inventories 41–62% of lexicon (Goldin-Meadow & Feldman 1977 p. 2) | No agreement system |
| Mature SLs (ASL) | Loci, pointing, binding constraints, role shift | Obligatory object agreement, verb-class system, directional morphology |

### The budding parallel

The agreement system budded from the pointing/tracking system through grammaticalization, just as the article system budded from demonstratives (Ch 10). In both cases:
- The semantic function existed first
- A formal mechanism grammaticalised to serve it
- Once established, the formal mechanism developed its own internal logic
- The two can decouple

### The Schlenker/Kuhn resolution

Schlenker (loci as variables) describes the discourse-tracking cluster. Kuhn (loci as features) describes the morphosyntactic cluster. Both are right — about different clusters. The locus is the shared spatial vehicle, which is why they look like one thing. HPC dissolves the debate: two overlapping clusters, same substrate, different mechanisms.

## C. Chapter structure

### §13.1 — The modality challenge (~500 words)

Open with a concrete image: a signer establishing a locus for an absent referent, then pointing back to that location three sentences later. The hand returns to empty space and the referent is there. Spoken language hides this architecture inside pronouns and agreement morphology; sign language makes it visible.

The challenge: if HPC categories are maintained by mechanisms, those mechanisms shouldn't care whether the signal is acoustic or manual-visual. Same functional work, different physical channel. This chapter tests whether the architecture survives the switch.

Key facts to deploy:
- Comparable proposition rate across modalities (~1/1–2s; Sandler & L-M Ch 1 p. 3)
- "Modality independent upper limit" (Sandler & L-M Ch 1 p. 3)
- Sign languages are natural languages with full structural apparatus (not gestural approximations)

No exoticising. Treat sign languages as what they are: independent test cases for claims about language.

### §13.2 — How spatial reference works (~800 words)

Descriptive section. Three components:

1. **Loci:** Locations in signing space associated with discourse referents. Can be created "on the fly" with no clear upper bound beyond memory (Schlenker 2017 p. 4). First/second person anchored to signer/addressee; third-person loci established by pointing, naming, or agreement verbs.

2. **Pointing:** Pronouns that point to established loci. Near-unambiguous for discourse referents (Sandler & L-M Ch 3 p. 26). But: "pronominal signs cannot be characterised exclusively either as pronouns, or as pointing gestures" (Cormier et al. 2013 p. 244). Hybrid from the start.

3. **Agreement verbs:** Path/facing changes on verb forms that link to argument loci. Object agreement obligatory, subject agreement optional in ASL (Sandler & L-M Ch 3 p. 30). Verb classes: agreeing/plain/spatial, cross-linguistically robust (Sandler & L-M Ch 3 pp. 31–34).

Also introduce role shift briefly here (body position, eye gaze mark perspective change — a different mechanism for the same referent-tracking function). Garcia & Sallandre 2020: transfer structures reach 70% of narrative reference, 80–95% in maintenance contexts.

Close with Steinbach & Onea's observation: "The more prominent a discourse referent is, the less explicit the pointing to its R-locus can be" (2016 p. 10). This connects accessibility hierarchies across modalities.

### §13.3 — Two clusters, one channel (~900 words)

**The chapter's analytical contribution.** Present the two-cluster separation.

Start from what looks like a unified system in ASL: referents get loci, pronouns point to loci, verbs agree with loci. Everything runs through space. This is the definiteness story before you separate deitality: one system, apparently.

Then the seams. Introduce the Schlenker/Kuhn debate:
- Schlenker: "A sign language locus appears to play very much the role of a formal index" (2017 p. 10). Loci track discourse referents, support binding, obey crossover constraints. This is the tracking cluster.
- Kuhn: "the strong loci-as-variables hypothesis has been falsified" (2016 p. 18). Same-locus pronouns can receive different interpretations. Loci pattern with morphosyntactic features — agreement, underspecification, feature-checking. This is the formal cluster.
- Kuhn 2022: "loci must be mediated by a featural layer" (p. 1). Disjunctive referents require spatial sums, midpoint pointing — mereological composition that goes beyond variable assignment.

The resolution: both are right, about different clusters. The locus is the shared spatial vehicle. In mature sign languages, the two clusters are tightly coupled. But they needn't be.

### §13.4 — Mechanisms (~700 words)

Four mechanisms that maintain the referent-tracking cluster:

1. **Spatial working memory.** Constrains active loci to ~3–4 (Steinbach & Onea 2016 p. 2: "roughly 2–3 recursive splits in real discourse"). Perniss & Özyürek 2015: DGS shows systematic spatial modification for reference tracking that co-speech gesture does not replicate at the same level (predicates: DGS RI=.70, M=.49; gesture no significant difference). The modality carries the full referential load (Perniss & Özyürek 2015 p. 19).

2. **Acquisition.** Morgan et al. 2002: complex verb constructions not adult-like until after age 9. Youngest children default to partial structures (B-part only: 90%) — they can track the referent semantically but can't map the full formal expression. "The developmental problem lies not in understanding the event but in getting the complete conceptual-linguistic mapping" (p. 18). This is the formal cluster lagging behind the semantic cluster.

3. **Interactive alignment.** Interlocutors converge on shared spatial models. Senghas & Coppola 2001: second NSL cohort showed higher shared-reference spatial modulation (0.88 vs 0.63) — not just more spatial marking, but more *consistent* marking for the same referents (p. 5–6).

4. **Role shift as backup.** When loci decay (too many referents, too much discourse distance), signers shift to constructed action: body lean, eye gaze, facial expression. Garcia & Sallandre: these transfer structures dominate in maintenance contexts (80–95%). Two maintenance regimes for one cluster — Peirce's cable, not chain.

Three mechanisms that maintain the agreement cluster specifically:

5. **Verb-class entrenchment.** Agreeing/plain/spatial verb classes are cross-linguistically robust. Morphophonological constraints on realisation (four-language comparison: Sandler & L-M Ch 3 p. 38).

6. **Acquisition regularisation.** NSL second-generation signers created consistent directional marking that first-generation signers lacked (Senghas et al. 1997 pp. 9–10). Children regularise the system.

7. **Body-as-subject anchor.** Padden et al. 2010 p. 19: "the body is a central reference point for all types of verbs." This iconic anchoring provides a stable base for agreement to grammaticalise from.

### §13.5 — Failure modes and perturbation (~600 words)

1. **Locus decay.** Over discourse distance, loci lose specificity. Neutral pointing becomes ambiguous; prominence and pragmatics bias resolution (Steinbach & Onea 2016 pp. 29–31). Empty-locus agreement yields presupposition failure (Steinbach & Onea 2016 p. 6). This is the tracking cluster fraying.

2. **Competition.** More than 3–4 active referents strain the system. Schlenker 2017: ambiguity pressure, degraded same-locus parses, disambiguation via finer spatial structure (pp. 10–12). Same-locus possessives allow all four readings under ONLY-ONE — the system doesn't crash but becomes indeterminate (Kuhn 2016 pp. 14–15).

3. **Refresh strategies.** Re-establishing (explicit re-pointing), role shift (body position takes over from loci), classifier predicates (persistent handshape tracks referent through predicate chains; Sandler & L-M Ch 5 pp. 91–92). Kata Kolok: context-poor settings trigger increased list-buoy and anaphoric strategies over absolute pointing (De Vos 2012 p. 259).

4. **Referential ambiguity in the wild.** Cormier et al. 2013: 789/25,000 tokens ambiguous in reference and/or function (p. 235). The system tolerates noise — as HPC predicts for maintained clusters with multiple redundant mechanisms.

### §13.6 — Cross-linguistic variation (~800 words)

The coupling-tightness gradient across sign languages. This parallels Ch 11's treatment of adjective categories across spoken languages.

**ASL:** Tight coupling. Productive agreement verb system, rich locus architecture, binding constraints parallel spoken-language systems (Schlenker 2018 pp. 10–11). Both clusters well-developed and tightly linked.

**BSL:** Slightly looser. Less spatial agreement, more body shift and constructed action. Cormier et al. 2013 BSL corpus: singular pronominals favour palm-sideways, plurals pattern with adverbials (p. 239). The tracking cluster is maintained more by role shift, less by agreement morphology.

**ISL:** Intermediate. Youngest cohort shows divergent axis distribution (Z 25%, X 42%) and high agreement marking (~75% of transfer verbs), while older cohorts are mostly uninflected (Padden et al. 2010 pp. 14–18). The agreement cluster is actively forming.

**Village sign languages:**

- *Kata Kolok:* Tracking cluster present (pointing dominant), agreement cluster largely absent. Absolute spatial frame rather than body-relative. "Exophoric reference predominates" (De Vos 2012 p. 259). But list buoys and anaphoric strategies available as context demands — the tracking cluster has backup mechanisms even without agreement.

- *ABSL:* Word order and prosodic marking emerged early; agreement did not. Older signers avoided multi-argument clauses: 47% one-argument clauses (Sandler et al. 2005 p. 6). "The transition into language is gradual and not abrupt" (p. 26). Distinct subsystems stabilise at different rates — syntax before morphology, prosody before agreement.

- *NSL:* Emergence tracked across cohorts. First generation: no two-animate-arguments-with-one-verb structures (Senghas et al. 1997 pp. 5–6). Second generation: consistent directional layout, new verb orders, but still developing. "Productive consistent verb agreement takes at least 2–3 generations" (Padden et al. 2010 p. 19).

**Prediction:** Mechanism density should predict clustering tightness. Languages with more maintenance strands (agreement + pointing + role shift + classifier handshape) should show tighter coupling. Languages with fewer strands should show looser coupling and more reliance on context. This is the same prediction the book makes for spoken languages.

### §13.7 — The HPC verdict (~500 words)

Three questions:

1. **Is there a property cluster?** Yes. Referent introduction, maintenance, reactivation, binding, accessibility decay, agreement marking, person distinction, role shift — these co-occur reliably but not invariably. Village SLs show subsets.

2. **Are there mechanisms?** Yes. Spatial working memory, acquisition regularisation, interactive alignment, verb-class entrenchment, iconic anchoring. These are specific, testable, and modality-sensitive but functionally parallel to spoken-language mechanisms.

3. **Does it project?** Yes. Knowing that a sign language has productive agreement verbs licenses predictions about locus specificity, person-marking patterns, and null-argument licensing. Knowing it lacks agreement narrows predictions to pointing-based tracking with contextual support. The category earns its inferential keep.

**The modality-independence claim holds** — with a structured caveat. The *architecture* is substrate-neutral (clusters maintained by mechanisms, projecting for purposes). The *mechanisms themselves* are modality-sensitive (spatial working memory rather than phonological entrenchment). The coupling tightness varies across sign languages for the same mechanistic reasons it varies across spoken languages: more maintenance strands = tighter coupling.

**The discovery restated:** What the sign language literature treats as one system ("spatial reference") is two overlapping clusters — referent tracking and spatial agreement — coupled through the same spatial channel. The Schlenker/Kuhn debate is dissolved: both describe real clusters. The village evidence shows they can decouple. The agreement system budded from the tracking system, just as the article system budded from demonstratives in Ch 10.

### §13.8 — Forward to Ch 14 (~300 words)

Where sign language anaphor sits in the coupling spectrum: moderate coupling (transparent mechanisms, predictable failures, narrow domain — like gender in Ch 12, but with visible machinery). The stack chapter can now include a sign language row.

Transition: the modality test passed. The next chapter asks whether the architecture is scale-invariant — whether coupling works the same way at every level of the stack, from phonemes to constructions. Sign language anaphor provides a new data point for that question.

## E. Rhetorical notes

- **No exoticising.** Sign languages are natural languages. Don't frame them as surprising or remarkable. Frame modality independence as *expected if the theory is right*, and the chapter as testing that expectation.
- **Correctio sparingly.** The book has been flagged for overusing epanorthosis. Use it at most once (§13.3 when the unified-system appearance gives way to two clusters). Don't use "no, it's actually..." patterns elsewhere.
- **Aphorism sparingly.** One good closing line for §13.7, not scattered throughout. The chapter's strength is the empirical decomposition, not verbal cleverness.
- **Concrete before abstract.** §13.2 describes the system before §13.3 analyses it. Numbers and corpus data throughout (De Vos's 3/839, Cormier's 789/25000, Senghas's 0.88/0.63).
- **Rapoport's rules for Schlenker/Kuhn.** Present both positions charitably before dissolving the debate. Neither is wrong.
- **No "wh-" terminology.** Use "interrogative," "relative," or specify the word.
- **Contractions.** Use them. "Doesn't," "can't," "won't."
- **Paragraphs ~60 words.** Max 100.
