**Strengths**
- The chapter has a clear, non-trivial target: in **“The HPC claim”**, grammaticality is defined as a coupling between morphosyntactic form and “structural meaning,” not truth-conditional content. That is a serious theoretical move, not just terminological relabeling.
- The distinction in **“The stabilizer: a feeling”** between grammaticality (the coupling) and acceptability (detector output) is valuable and aligns with illusion data. The garden-path/comparative-illusion contrast in **“Grammaticality illusions”** is an effective demonstration of selective fallibility.
- You explicitly include variation and education effects in **“Individual differences”** (with Dąbrowska), and you frame community-indexed entrenchment in a way that can, in principle, explain sociolinguistic structure rather than treat it as nuisance variance.
- The chapter occasionally becomes properly falsifiable, especially the **“Scoped prediction / Falsifier”** in the definiteness thread. That is the right direction for a book often read as conceptual.

**Major Concerns**
- Mechanism is still under-specified. The text cycles through “zipper,” “immune system,” “standing wave,” and “spinning top,” but never commits to an explicit detector architecture with state variables and update rules. A skeptical reader will say this is still metaphor-rich, mechanism-light.
- In **“Entrenchment in action,”** the line that the “reality is constituted by what gets entrenched” risks collapsing target and instrument: if entrenchment defines grammaticality and the detector is entrenchment-shaped, what independent check remains? You need a non-circular identification strategy.
- **“The phenomenology of coupling”** is disciplined in rhetoric (“narrow claim”), but the Peircean mapping (jolt/block/correction to sign/object/interpretant) reads post hoc unless tied to independent measures. At present it is elegant, not decisive.
- Variation is acknowledged but not architecturally central. You still mostly write as if there is one basin and individuals differ in threshold. For usage-based work, heterogeneity should be constitutive: multiple partially overlapping basins indexed by education, register, and network position.
- Some empirical claims are too absolute or too precise for current support: “categorically rejected regardless of exposure,” “across tasks, speakers, and presentation modes,” and “~3-sigma absence.” These look like placeholders for quantification not yet delivered.
- What a skeptical specialist will reject first: the swing from strong grammaticality realism to “there’s no ‘grammar’ there to resist” in **“Looking forward.”** That sounds rhetorically powerful but theoretically unstable.

**Priority Fixes**
- Replace metaphor stacks with one explicit model: detector output as a function of constructional probability, parsing cost, and speaker-specific exposure history; define failure criteria.
- Rework acceptability-vs-grammaticality with a triangulation protocol (ratings, online processing, production/repair behavior, corpus distribution) so detector error can be demonstrated independently.
- Move heterogeneity earlier and formalize it: not “different thresholds,” but population-structured grammars with education and discourse-community effects as core parameters.
- Soften absolute claims and add boundary conditions and anticipated effect sizes.
- Keep phenomenology, but mark it as hypothesis-generating unless linked to operational diagnostics.

**Decisive Tests**
1. Longitudinal exposure experiments on marginal constructions with strong controls (including LBE), stratified by education/literacy profile; test whether change appears in production/repair as well as ratings.
2. Factorial design crossing frequency and processing complexity; show detector dissociations (easy-parse/low-frequency vs hard-parse/high-frequency) predicted by your architecture.
3. Audience-switch experiments in bidialectal/bilingual speakers; if community indexing is constitutive, detector calibration should shift rapidly with interlocutor/register cues.
4. Direct model comparison with relevance-theoretic accounts using items that dissociate inferential difficulty from morphosyntactic mismatch. If your account is right, mismatch should predict “wrongness” beyond relevance cost.
