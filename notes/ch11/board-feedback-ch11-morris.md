# Board Member Report: Errol Morris

**Date:** December 25, 2025
**Subject:** Feedback on Chapter 11 (Word Classes)

I’ve been reading the notes for Chapter 11, the one about word classes. And I keep coming back to this question of the essentialists. The people who insist that a noun must have a definition, a set of necessary and sufficient conditions.

You asked me how to present them. Are they the villains?

No. That’s a mistake. In my experience, true villainy is rare. What you have here is something far more interesting. You have **victims of a cognitive optical illusion**.

### 1. The Essentialist as the Tragic Detective
Don’t paint the essentialist as a dogmatist. Paint them as a detective who is absolutely obsessed with solving a crime, but they’re looking for a weapon that doesn’t exist. They are rational, reasonable people operating under a false premise that has worked for them in every other aspect of their lives. If I see a chair, it’s a chair. If I see an electron, it’s an electron. They assume words work the same way.

They are trying to impose order on a chaotic universe. That’s not villainy; that’s human nature. We should feel for them. We should look at their complex trees and their sub-sub-categories and say, "Look at the magnificent structure they built to avoid seeing the truth."

### 2. Creating Sympathy for the Error
To create sympathy, you have to validate their desire. We *want* language to be logical. We want "noun" to mean "person, place, or thing." It’s elegant. It’s clean.

Show the reader the beauty of the essentialist trap before you spring it. Let the reader feel the seduction of the clear definition. Then, show the cost. Show the "wastebasket" of adverbs. Show the contortions required to make *today* fit into a box labeled "Pronoun" or "Noun."

The sympathy comes when we realize they aren't stupid; they are just using a map that doesn't match the territory. They are drawing straight lines on a curved earth.

### 3. The Narrative Arc of Discovery
The arc of this chapter should play out like an investigation that goes cold, and then suddenly breaks wide open.

*   **Act I: The Perfect System.** Start with the standard definitions. They look solid. The reader feels safe.
*   **Act II: The Anomalies.** Introduce the data that refuses to cooperate. The *today*s, the *exactly who*s. The essentialist tries to force them into the boxes, but the boxes start to break. The epi-cycles get absurd. The reader starts to feel the anxiety of the system collapsing.
*   **Act III: The Shift.** You stop looking for the *definition* and start looking at the *mechanism*. You switch the camera angle. Instead of asking "What is it?", you ask "What keeps it stable?"
*   **Resolution:** The relief. The realization that the messiness isn't a bug; it's the feature. The "braided mechanisms" explain the stability without the straitjacket.

Make the reader feel like *they* solved it. They didn't just learn a new theory; they escaped a mental prison they didn't know they were in.
