## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/devos2012-sign-spatiality-kata-kolok.pdf
**Facts**
- Kata Kolok is reported to lack a productive agreement-verb system for transitive verbs (p. 150).
- In a 6-hour spontaneous corpus sample of selected transitive predicates, 3 modified tokens were found out of 839 total (pp. 152–153).
- The special form `TALK-TO-ME` is not obligatory and is not a productive inflectional paradigm (p. 154).
- Corpus analysis reports roughly one in six manual signs is a pointing sign (p. 44).
- In 1,183 transcribed pointing signs, only two tokens used neutral-space diagrammatic mapping for topography (pp. 202–203).
- Role-shifted neutral-space pointing to absent addressees is rare: 7 tokens in a >1,000-token pointing dataset (p. 234).
- Absolute pointing for person reference is dominant; localization-style loci are absent as the default strategy (pp. 223, 235).
- In context-poor/mobile settings (e.g., van travel), signers increased list-buoy/anaphoric strategies over absolute pointing (p. 259).
- Table 16.1 shows complementary distribution with Adamorobe: Kata Kolok (+entity classifiers, +list buoys, -agreement verbs, -body-anchored timeline, +absolute celestial timeline) (pp. 449–450).
- NSL reportedly developed spatial verb inflection quickly, while Kata Kolok and ABSL did not, undermining a pure “time-depth only” account (pp. 160, 162, 446).

**Quotations**
- “Kata Kolok has been reported not to exhibit any evidence of agreement verbs” (p. 150).
- “the only instances in 1,183 transcribed pointing signs” (p. 203).
- “exophoric reference predominates in Kata Kolok discourse” (p. 259).

**Key arguments (HPC-relevant)**
- Anaphora/deixis in Kata Kolok is maintained by a cluster of cues (geography, discourse slot, form, non-manuals), not one spatial-locus mechanism (pp. 44, 175, 425–427).
- Spatial reference and person reference show strong exophoric dependence; endophoric/locus-like mechanisms are available but low-frequency or context-bound (pp. 203, 234–235, 259).
- Proposed causal link: robust anaphoric pointing may be a prerequisite for agreement-verb systems (p. 235).
- Cross-linguistic variation across village sign languages is substantial and non-teleological (pp. 446–452).

**Cross-references**
- Senghas et al. (NSL emergence) used to contrast rapid development of spatial inflection (pp. 160, 162, 446).
- Sandler et al. and Aronoff et al. cited on ABSL development and agreement absence (pp. 160–162, 445–446).
- Nyst (Adamorobe) and Schuit et al. (Inuit) cited as contrasting village-sign patterns (pp. 174, 449–451).
- Pyers et al. on cohort differences and spatial cognition in NSL (p. 455).

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/sandler-etal2005-emergence-grammar.pdf
**Facts**
- Older signers in ABSL/ISL frequently avoid transitive argument marking by splitting events into one-argument clauses: 47% in ABSL, 33% in ISL (p. 6).
- For ABSL second-generation signers (n=9), 99 of 150 clauses were one-argument; in 51 multi-argument clauses, SOV was 61% (SVO 16%, OSV 10%) (p. 7).
- NSL first cohort reportedly had no two-human-nouns-plus-one-verb responses; second cohort introduced new orders but still no such response type (p. 6).
- Younger ABSL signers showed more prosodically complex dependency marking than older signers (roughly half to three-quarters of utterances complex) (p. 10).
- Younger signers aligned about twice as many timing/facial/head-body prosodic cues at constituent boundaries (p. 10).
- The chapter reports no agreement morphology in ABSL despite early syntactic structuring (pp. 13, 28).

**Quotations**
- “users of a new language initially avoid marking argument structure grammatically” (p. 6).
- “the transition into language is gradual and not abrupt” (p. 26).
- “Word order emerged early and robustly” (p. 28).

**Key arguments (HPC-relevant)**
- Early systems rely on workaround clusters (event-splitting, one-argument clauses, word order), then gradually conventionalize morphology/prosody (pp. 6–7, 26–28).
- Distinct subsystems (syntax, prosody, morphology, lexicon) stabilize at different rates (pp. 26–28).
- Communicative adequacy can precede mature grammatical marking, suggesting staged cluster consolidation rather than binary presence/absence (pp. 26–27).

**Cross-references**
- Senghas et al. on NSL cohort change and argument structure (pp. 6, 30, 33).
- Marsaja/Kata Kolok and Washabaugh/Providence Island used as comparative village-sign evidence (pp. 7–8).
- Aronoff et al. 2005 on sign morphology trajectory (p. 28).

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/padden-etal2010-grammar-of-space.pdf
**Facts**
- ABSL dataset: 169 verb tokens; ISL dataset: 412 responses across three age groups (p. 9).
- ABSL overall path direction: Z-axis 65%, X-axis 26%, diagonal 9% (p. 10).
- ABSL transfer sentences: no referential loci in 71% (46/65); when loci were set (29%), many forms looked like emergent single/double agreement (p. 12).
- ISL overall axis distribution: Z 54%, X 28%, diagonal 14%, none 4% (p. 13).
- Youngest ISL group pattern diverges: Z 25%, X 42%, diagonal 32%; transfer verbs especially favor X-axis (50%) (pp. 14–15).
- ISL youngest group marked agreement in almost 75% of transfer-verb forms; older groups mostly uninflected and had only two double-agreement tokens each (p. 17).
- After removing a special “I/YOU anchoring” strategy in three older signers, older-group forms were 80% non-agreeing (p. 18).
- Authors conclude productive consistent verb agreement takes at least 2–3 generations and develops non-linearly (p. 19).

**Quotations**
- “not even a highly motivated grammatical system like agreement … emerges overnight” (p. 2).
- “the body is a central reference point for all types of verbs” (p. 19).

**Key arguments (HPC-relevant)**
- Early organizing principle is “body as subject,” with later spread of third-person spatial agreement conventions (pp. 19–22).
- Spatial grammar emerges gradually and competitively (body-iconicity vs spatial-locus iconicity), not as an immediate modality default (pp. 2, 20–22).
- Agreement remains variably optional in ISL even when advanced, implying cluster-based stabilization rather than strict all-or-none obligatoriness (p. 19).

**Cross-references**
- Meir et al. on “body as subject” framework (pp. 10, 19–20).
- Sandler et al. (ABSL emergence), Aronoff et al. (historical morphology), and ASL literature used as comparative anchors (pp. 1–5, 20, 23).

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/senghas1997-argument-structure-nsl.pdf
**Facts**
- Sample: 8 early-exposed signers (<6 years at exposure), 4 first-generation vs 4 second-generation (p. 3).
- First generation did not produce two-animate-arguments-with-one-verb structures; events with two animate participants used two verbs (pp. 5–6).
- First-generation argument structure strongly used N1V1N2V2-like interleaving in relevant classes (pp. 5–6).
- Directional consistency in first generation was mixed (2 consistent, 2 inconsistent) within and across sentences (pp. 7–8).
- In second generation, base N1V1N2V2 became rare; new orders with adjacent verbs increased (new orders = 28% in relevant classes) (p. 9).
- All second-generation signers showed consistent directional layout usage (pp. 9–10).

**Quotations**
- “Single verbs … are not able to take a full range of arguments” (p. 11).
- “directional movements on the two verbs have become highly consistent” (p. 11).

**Key arguments (HPC-relevant)**
- Early NSL uses structural workaround clusters (two verbs for two animates), then restructures syntax and directional marking together (pp. 5–11).
- Emergent agreement-like directionality appears as system-level consistency across signers, not isolated lexical behavior (pp. 9–11).
- Child-driven grammaticalization operates through staged reorganization, not single-feature emergence (pp. 10–11).

**Cross-references**
- Goldin-Meadow & Mylander on homesign precursors (p. 1).
- Kegl & Iwata on creolization framing (p. 2).
- Supalla on sign-language agreement typology (pp. 2, 12).

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/senghas2001-nsl-spatial-grammar.pdf
**Facts**
- 24 signers analyzed by cohort (pre/post 1983 entry) and age-at-exposure groups (early/middle/late) (p. 4).
- Early/middle exposure groups produced more spatial modulations than late-exposed signers (p. 4).
- Second cohort showed higher shared-reference spatial modulation than first cohort (0.88 vs 0.63), with significant early/middle advantages (p. 5).
- Non-shared-reference spatial modulation did not differ meaningfully across cohorts (0.48 vs 0.40) (p. 5).
- Second cohort signed faster overall (234 vs 194 morphemes/minute), especially early-exposed signers (p. 6).
- Authors argue differences are not reduced competence in younger cohort; rather, grammatical system enrichment by younger learners (pp. 6–7).

**Quotations**
- “such changes originate in children aged 10 and younger” (p. 2).
- “the second cohort has reanalyzed the location … as indicating something akin to co-indexing” (p. 6).

**Key arguments (HPC-relevant)**
- Children contribute targeted regularization (shared-reference linking), not just increased spatial marking volume (pp. 5–6).
- System becomes more specific and constrained over cohorts, showing cluster refinement via acquisition dynamics (pp. 6–7).
- Supports mechanism-based emergence: sequential cohorts of young learners extend sensitive-period effects socially (p. 7).

**Cross-references**
- Goldin-Meadow & Mylander homesign findings used as prior evidence for child-led structure creation (p. 2).
- Ross & Newport / Singleton & Newport on learners surpassing inconsistent models (p. 2).

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/goldin-meadow1977-language-without-model.pdf
**Facts**
- Study followed six deaf children of hearing parents (17–49 months at first visit) across repeated home sessions (p. 2).
- Children built lexicons including large deictic inventories; deictic signs comprised roughly 41–62% of each child’s signs (p. 2).
- Children produced systematic semantic-ordering tendencies in two-sign phrases (patient/act/recipient relations) (pp. 3–4).
- Mothers and children overlapped on only about 25% of characterizing sign types (p. 4).
- Children produced multisign semantic phrases earlier and in higher counts than mothers (e.g., David 127 vs mother 41 across first four interviews) (p. 4).
- Authors conclude system structure was child-invented rather than copied from caretaker gesture input (p. 4).

**Quotations**
- “can spontaneously develop a structured sign system” (p. 2).
- “no evidence that the children learned … by imitating their mothers’ gestures” (p. 4).

**Key arguments (HPC-relevant)**
- Strong evidence for acquisition-driven emergence and entrenchment under degraded input conditions (pp. 2–4).
- Structural regularities arise without a full language model, supporting mechanism-first accounts of cluster formation (pp. 3–4).
- Caregiver input contributes but does not determine the child system’s grammatical organization (p. 4).

**Cross-references**
- Brown/Slobin language-development frameworks used for comparison (p. 4).
- Gardner/Premack ape-language studies used as contrast case for spontaneous structure emergence (p. 4).
