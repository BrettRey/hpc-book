# Grammaticalization and HPC: Genuinely New Questions

**Date:** 2026-02-28
**Context:** From gauntlet-reframing-2026-02-28.md — grammaticalization is category BIRTH (budding), not death. Definiteness predated deitality; article grammaticalization created a second cluster with independent maintenance.

**Goal:** Develop HPC questions for grammaticalization with concrete historical examples. Flag what's genuinely new vs what's relabelling.

## 1. Competitive Exclusion

**HPC prediction:** When two forms grammaticalize into the same functional niche, they coexist only if they recruit distinct mechanisms. Same mechanisms → one dies (competitive exclusion). Different mechanisms → they differentiate into distinct HPCs with different projection profiles.

**Traditional grammaticalization theory** describes layering (multiple forms coexisting in a functional domain) and specialization (forms differentiating semantically/pragmatically). But it doesn't predict WHEN layering leads to stable coexistence vs eventual winnowing based on mechanism structure.

### 1a. *Will* vs *going to* — Distinct Mechanisms?

**Historical trajectory:**
- **OE *willan*:** Volitional verb "to want, wish" → future auxiliary by ME (13th c.)
- **BE *going to*:** Motion verb + purposive infinitive (attested 1482) → future marker by 17th c.

**Current status:** Stable coexistence (350+ years), but with differentiated distributions:
- *Will*: epistemic futures, predictions, promises, planned events
- *Going to*: immediate futures, speaker certainty, already-planned events, more conversational register

**Mechanism analysis:**

*Will* recruitment:
1. **Semantic bleaching:** Volitional → epistemic modality
2. **Phonological reduction:** *will* → *'ll* (unstressed clitic)
3. **Distributional: Fixed position in auxiliary slot
4. **Obligatoriness:** Required in many future contexts (no null marking in StdE)

*Going to* recruitment:
1. **Motion → temporal:** Original trajectory sense reanalyzed as future projection
2. **Phonological reduction:** *going to* → *gonna* (but only in future use, NOT in motion use — diagnostic!)
3. **Distributional:** Same auxiliary slot as *will*
4. **Register anchoring:** More informal, conversational entrenchment

**Distinct mechanism:** Yes. *Will* couples to epistemic modality system and formal register; *gonna* couples to aspectual immediacy and informal register. They recruit **different entrenchment communities** and **different semantic anchors**.

**Projection profiles differ:**
- Knowing a speaker uses *will* predicts: epistemic modality preferences, formal register, written prose
- Knowing a speaker uses *gonna* predicts: conversational register, immediate aspect, phonological informality

**Could you predict stable coexistence from mechanism overlap?**

SPECULATION: If the mechanism analysis above is correct, moderate overlap (same syntactic slot, similar temporal semantics) + distinct anchors (register, immediacy) predicts **stable differentiation**. The question HPC adds: quantify mechanism overlap and test whether coexistence probability correlates with overlap degree across multiple cases.

**Evidence needed:** Corpus study tracking distributional overlap (do *will* and *gonna* ever occupy identical contexts?), register distribution, and whether overlap is increasing (convergence) or decreasing (divergence). If diverging, distinct mechanisms are successfully maintaining separation.

### 1b. *Must* vs *have to* — Same Question

**Historical trajectory:**
- **OE *moste*:** Preterite-present verb "may, must" (root and epistemic modality from OE)
- ***Have to*:** Possessive *have* + infinitive-introducing *to*, grammaticalized c. 17th century

**Current status:** Coexistence but with distributional skew:
- *Must*: Formal, written, strong deontic/epistemic force
- *Have to*: Conversational, external obligation, weaker epistemic

**Mechanism recruitment:**

*Must*:
1. **Formal register entrenchment**
2. **Phonological: Monosyllabic, unstressed
3. **Defective paradigm:** No infinitive (*to must*), no past (*musted*) — couples to present tense only
4. **Epistemic bleaching:** Strong inference marker

*Have to*:
1. **Conversational entrenchment**
2. **Paradigmatic flexibility:** Full TAM inflection (*had to*, *will have to*, *to have to*)
3. **External obligation anchor:** Couples to deontic necessity from external source
4. **Phonological:** Multi-word, can reduce (*hafta*) but retains internal structure

**Distinct mechanisms?** Yes, but with MORE overlap than *will*/*gonna*:
- Both occupy modal auxiliary slot
- Both express necessity (deontic or epistemic)
- Key difference: paradigmatic structure and register

**Prediction:** Less stable than *will*/*gonna*. Evidence: *must* is receding in conversational English (Leech 2003, modal verb decline). If HPC is right, this recession reflects **competitive exclusion in progress** — *have to* is winning because its mechanism profile (paradigmatic flexibility + conversational entrenchment) fits modern English better than *must*'s defective paradigm.

**HPC question:** Can you predict the RATE of *must* decline from the degree of mechanism overlap? Does *must* decline faster in contexts where mechanism overlap with *have to* is highest?

### 1c. Case Where Competitive Exclusion Happened: English *shall* vs *will*

**Historical situation (OE → ME):**
- **OE *sculan*:** Obligation modal "ought to, must"
- **OE *willan*:** Volitional "want to, will"
- **ME (13th-15th c.):** Both grammaticalize as future markers with person-based distribution:
  - *Shall*: 1st person future (*I shall go*)
  - *Will*: 2nd/3rd person future (*you will go*, *he will go*)

**Outcome:** *Will* generalized; *shall* restricted to formal/prescriptive contexts by ModE (18th c.). Now near-extinct in American English, moribund in BrE except frozen phrases (*we shall see*).

**Mechanism landscape:**

*Shall* recruited:
1. **Phonological reduction:** *shall* → monosyllable
2. **Person-based distribution:** 1st person slot
3. **Formal register:** Retained obligation overtones
4. **Paradigmatic defect:** Irregular person marking

*Will* recruited:
1. **Phonological reduction:** *will* → *'ll* (more reduced than *shall*)
2. **Person generalization:** All persons
3. **Register flexibility:** Works in all registers
4. **Volitional bleaching:** Lost obligation sense faster

**Why did *will* win?**

Mechanism analysis: *Will* had **broader distributional maintenance** (all persons, all registers) and **stronger phonological reduction** (*'ll* vs *shall*). The person-based split was an arbitrary restriction that HPC predicts should be unstable — it recruits NO independent mechanism. It's purely distributional tradition, which is weak maintenance.

**HPC insight:** When two forms compete with IDENTICAL semantic-functional profile but DIFFERENT distributional restrictions, the less-restricted form should win unless the restriction couples to an independent mechanism. The 1st-person/*shall* restriction coupled to nothing — it was a relic. *Will*'s generalization across persons gave it more entrenchment opportunities.

**Could you predict this outcome from mechanism overlap before it happened?**

TESTABLE: If you mapped (a) semantic overlap, (b) phonological reduction degree, (c) distributional breadth, and (d) register anchoring for *shall* vs *will* in 1400, could you predict *will* would generalize? HPC says yes: the form with broader distributional access and less arbitrary restrictions should outcompete.

**What traditional theory says:** "Shall/will merged"; "analogical leveling"; "simplification of the paradigm." True but not explanatory. WHY did leveling go in *will*'s direction, not *shall*'s? HPC gives mechanism-based answer: *will* had more maintenance.

### 1d. Can You Predict Outcomes from Mechanism Overlap Before They're Known?

**General HPC prediction:**

Competitive exclusion probability = f(mechanism overlap, entrenchment breadth, phonological reduction, paradigmatic regularity)

**Operationalization:**
1. **Map mechanisms** for both competitors: phonology, distribution, semantics, register
2. **Quantify overlap:** How many mechanisms are shared?
3. **Quantify distinctness:** Do distinct mechanisms recruit different projection profiles?
4. **Predict:**
   - High overlap + no distinct anchors → one dies (exclusion)
   - High overlap + distinct anchors → differentiation (specialization)
   - Low overlap → stable coexistence without specialization

**Test cases:**

- **English modals (*can*/*could*, *may*/*might*):** Should show stable coexistence because they recruit **tense distinction** as an independent mechanism (present vs past morphology couples to epistemic certainty gradient).
- **French *ne...pas* vs *ne* alone:** *Ne* deletion in spoken French = competitive exclusion. HPC predicts: *pas* won because it has **phonological substance** (stress-bearing) and **pragmatic force** (emphasis), while *ne* is phonologically weak (unstressed clitic, often elided). Mechanism overlap: both negate. Distinct mechanism: *pas* has prosodic anchor, *ne* doesn't.

**Genuine novelty?**

Traditional grammaticalization theory describes these outcomes as "layering → differentiation" or "loss." It doesn't predict which forms will win based on mechanism structure BEFORE the outcome is known. HPC provides a predictive framework: map mechanisms, quantify overlap, predict winner.

**Where's the evidence?** Need:
- Corpus data: distributional overlap time series (are forms converging or diverging?)
- Phonological reduction metrics (degree of cliticization, stress patterns)
- Register distribution (formal vs informal entrenchment)
- Psycholinguistic: do speakers treat competitors as distinct categories (different projection profiles) or variants of one?

## 2. Phase Transition vs Cline

**HPC prediction:** Grammaticalization isn't a smooth cline. There's a **phase transition** — a point where the grammaticalizing form acquires enough independent maintenance to project on its own. Before: variant use. After: distinct category with independent projection.

**Traditional cline theory (Hopper & Traugott):** Continuous unidirectional change along parameters (semantics, phonology, distribution, morphology). No discontinuities predicted.

**Genuine clash:** Yes. Nonlinear transition vs continuous gradient. Testable.

### 2a. The English Article: *se* → *the*

**Historical trajectory (OE → ME):**

- **OE *se/seo/þæt*:** Demonstrative paradigm (full case/gender/number inflection)
  - **Projection profile:** Knowing *se* is used predicts: deictic/anaphoric reference, inflects for case/gender/number, contrastive stress available, can stand alone (*se is min* "that one is mine")

- **ME *the* (by 1200):** Definite article
  - **Projection profile:** Knowing *the* is used predicts: obligatory with definite singular count nouns, phonologically reduced (unstressed *[ðə]*/*[ði]*), no case/gender inflection, cannot stand alone, complementary distribution with demonstrative *that*

**When did the phase transition happen?**

**Evidence for nonlinearity:**

1. **Phonological reduction:** OE *se* was stressed, deictic. By early ME (c. 1150), *the* appears with reduced vowel schwa *[ðə]*, indicating:
   - Loss of deictic stress → obligatory unstressed marker
   - This is a BINARY shift: you can't be half-unstressed. Phonological reduction happens rapidly once semantic bleaching permits it.

2. **Paradigm collapse:** OE had 10+ distinct forms (*se, þone, þæs, þæm; seo, þa, þære; þæt, þæs, þæm*). By 1200, collapsed to *the* (+ residual *that* for demonstrative).
   - Inflectional loss is nonlinear: once analogical leveling starts, it cascades. You don't gradually lose case distinctions — you lose them in a generation or two.

3. **Obligatoriness:** OE allowed null articles with definite nouns in many contexts (common in poetry). By ME, *the* is increasingly obligatory with definite count nouns.
   - Obligatoriness doesn't emerge gradually. Either a form is required or it's optional. The transition is a discrete shift in the grammar.

4. **Complementary distribution:** OE *se/seo/þæt* could be reinforced with adverbs (*se þær* "that there"). ME *the* cannot be modified. It's in complementary distribution with *that*.
   - Again, binary: once *the* and *that* split into distinct categories, you can't use them together.

**HPC phase transition point:** Probably **c. 1100-1200** (early ME). By this time:
- Phonological reduction to schwa is complete
- Paradigm has collapsed to *the*
- Obligatoriness is grammaticalized
- Complementary distribution with *that* is established

**Before this point:** *se* is a demonstrative with article-like uses. Knowing *se* is used doesn't predict the full article profile — it still inflects, it can be stressed, it's not obligatory.

**After this point:** *the* projects as an article. Knowing *the* is used predicts: unstressed, uninflected, obligatory, complementary with *that*.

**What would time-series data show?**

**Cline prediction:** Linear increase in article-like properties over time. Plot (a) phonological reduction, (b) paradigm simplification, (c) obligatoriness rate, (d) complementary distribution from OE (800) to ME (1400). Smooth curves.

**HPC prediction:** Nonlinear. Properties should cluster around the phase transition (1100-1200). Before: low on all dimensions. After: high on all dimensions. Transition: rapid increase.

**Data sources:**
- **Phonological:** OE/ME manuscript stress marking (limited), but phonological reduction is inferable from meter in poetry (does *the* occupy unstressed positions exclusively?)
- **Morphological:** Case/gender/number marking in texts (Helsinki Corpus, PPCME2). Plot: % of article tokens with case inflection over time. Should show rapid collapse, not gradual.
- **Obligatoriness:** Rate of null articles with definite count nouns. Should show discrete shift from variable to categorical.

**Has this been tested?**

NOT with HPC-motivated phase-transition hypothesis. Historical linguists document paradigm collapse and phonological reduction, but they don't test for NONLINEARITY. A study plotting all four dimensions simultaneously and looking for synchronized transition would be new.

**Speculation:** I predict rapid collapse c. 1100-1200 because:
- Norman Conquest (1066) disrupted English inflectional system
- ME shows massive paradigm simplification across the board (not just articles)
- Once case marking becomes unreliable, demonstrative paradigm loses its anchor → collapse to invariant *the*

This is a **mechanism-driven prediction:** Loss of case system removes structural maintenance for demonstrative inflections. Without that anchor, paradigm simplification cascades.

### 2b. The English Progressive: *BE* + V-*ing*

**Historical trajectory:**

- **OE locative construction:** *He wæs on huntunge* "He was on hunting" (literal location in activity)
  - Not a grammatical aspect. Prepositional phrase with gerund.

- **Early ME (c. 1200-1400):** *He was hunting* begins appearing without preposition
  - Ambiguous: still locative-like but losing prepositional anchor

- **ModE (1700+):** Fully grammaticalized progressive aspect
  - **Projection profile:** Knowing a verb appears in *be V-ing* predicts: ongoing action, imperfective, durative, NOT stative (*\*I am knowing*), obligatory in progressive contexts

**Phase transition point:** Harder to pinpoint than the article case, but probably **c. 1500-1700** (EModE).

**Evidence for nonlinearity:**

1. **Preposition loss:** The shift from *on* + gerund to bare V-*ing* is discrete. You can't be half-prepositional.

2. **Aspectual obligatoriness:** Once progressive becomes a grammatical aspect (not just a locative construction), it becomes OBLIGATORY for expressing ongoing action in certain contexts.
   - OE: Progressive meaning expressible with simple present (*ic ete* "I am eating")
   - ModE: Simple present has habitual meaning; progressive required for ongoing (*I eat* = habitual, *I am eating* = ongoing)
   - This is a system-level reorganization, not gradual drift.

3. **Stative restriction:** ModE progressive blocks statives (*\*I am knowing*, *\*I am owning*). OE had no such restriction (because progressive wasn't aspectual).
   - Binary: once progressive couples to dynamicity, statives are excluded categorically.

4. **Auxiliary status:** *Be* in progressive construction becomes an auxiliary (not a copula).
   - Diagnostics: *be V-ing* allows do-support (*I don't be running* in AAVE), tag questions (*I'm running, aren't I?*), negation placement (*I'm not running*)
   - Auxiliary status is categorical, not gradient.

**What would time-series show?**

Plot from 1200-1800:
- **Frequency:** % of verbs appearing in *be V-ing* (should show rapid increase)
- **Stative exclusion rate:** % of progressive uses with stative verbs (should show rapid decrease)
- **Prepositional retention:** % of *be V-ing* that retain preposition (should collapse rapidly)
- **Obligatoriness:** % of ongoing-action contexts that use progressive (should show step increase)

**HPC prediction:** These should co-occur nonlinearly around 1500-1700. Before: low frequency, no stative restriction, prepositional forms still attested. After: high frequency, stative exclusion categorical, preposition extinct, obligatory for ongoing.

**Cline prediction:** Smooth increase on all dimensions.

**Has this been tested?**

Partially. Historical corpus studies (Arnaud 1998; Smitterberg 2005) show progressive frequency DOES increase nonlinearly — rapid rise in EModE (1500-1700). But they don't test for synchronized phase transition across ALL properties (phonology, distribution, aspectual obligatoriness, stative restriction).

**HPC novelty:** Predict that ALL emergent properties should transition together because they're maintained by the same mechanism bundle. If progressive grammaticalization is an HPC phase transition, properties shouldn't decouple — they should emerge as a cluster.

### 2c. What Data Do You Need? What Would the Time Series Look Like?

**General HPC test:**

For any grammaticalization trajectory, plot emergence of N properties over time:
1. **Phonological reduction** (stress loss, cliticization)
2. **Distributional restriction** (complementary distribution, positional fixedness)
3. **Semantic bleaching** (loss of source meaning)
4. **Morphological bondedness** (affixation, paradigm collapse)
5. **Obligatoriness** (required in certain contexts)

**Cline prediction:** Independent gradual change. Properties may emerge at different times. Linear trends.

**HPC prediction:** Correlated rapid change. Properties should emerge together around a critical threshold. Nonlinear (sigmoid or step-function).

**Statistical test:**
- **For cline:** Linear regression fits well; properties weakly correlated over time
- **For HPC:** Nonlinear models (logistic, threshold) fit better; properties strongly correlated in emergence window; PCA shows single dominant factor (the phase transition)

**Ideal dataset:**
- Historical corpus with: phonological annotation, syntactic tagging, semantic coding
- Time resolution: 50-year bins from source construction to full grammaticalization (e.g., 1000-1800 for progressive)
- Large enough sample to track low-frequency constructions in early stages

**Practical:** Use PPCME2, PPCEME for ME/EModE; Helsinki Corpus for OE. Already syntactically tagged. Would need to add phonological + semantic coding.

**Prediction:** Progressive and article cases should both show phase transitions. If they show smooth clines instead, HPC's claim about category emergence is wrong.

## 3. Mechanism Parasitism

**HPC prediction:** Grammaticalizing forms don't build maintenance from scratch. They **parasitize existing structural maintenance**. A demonstrative → article plugs into the determiner slot (which already has distributional maintenance from NP structure). It only needs to develop its own semantic-formal coupling; distributional maintenance is inherited.

**Traditional theory:** Grammaticalization targets "grammatical slots" or follows "channels" (lexical > grammatical > more grammatical). True, but CIRCULAR: why do those slots exist? HPC gives non-circular answer: maintenance is already there.

**Genuine novelty?** Moderate. The parasitism framing makes a stronger claim: grammaticalization should be BLOCKED in positions where maintenance infrastructure doesn't exist. Traditional theory doesn't predict blockages, just describes pathways.

### 3a. Why Do Demonstratives → Articles but Not → Verb Morphology?

**Cross-linguistic pattern:**
- **Demonstrative → article:** Extremely common (English, Romance, Semitic, Bantu, many others)
- **Demonstrative → verb inflection:** Essentially unattested

**Traditional explanation:**
- "Articles are grammatical; demonstratives are already grammatical-ish, so it's a natural path."
- "Semantic bleaching from deictic to definiteness is conceptually plausible."
- True but incomplete. Why doesn't semantic bleaching produce verb morphology?

**HPC explanation:**

**Mechanism parasitism:** The determiner slot in NP has existing maintenance:
1. **Distributional:** NP structure requires a pre-head position (cross-linguistically stable)
2. **Semantic:** Definiteness/specificity is independently relevant to reference (anchored in discourse pragmatics)
3. **Phonological:** Determiners are typically unstressed (prosodic structure of NP makes this position available for reduction)
4. **Acquisition:** Children acquire NP structure early; determiner slot is salient

A demonstrative grammaticalizing into this slot **inherits** all of this maintenance. It doesn't need to build it from scratch.

**Why not verb morphology?**

Verb morphology slots (tense, aspect, agreement) have DIFFERENT maintenance:
1. **Distributional:** Affix position, not phrasal
2. **Semantic:** Tense/aspect meanings are not deictic (no semantic bridge from demonstrative meaning to TAM)
3. **Phonological:** Affixes undergo different reduction patterns (suffixing preference, syllable structure constraints)
4. **Acquisition:** Verb inflection acquired later than NP structure

A demonstrative has NO mechanism overlap with verb morphology. It can't parasitize that maintenance because the mechanisms are incompatible.

**Prediction:** Grammaticalization should only occur when source and target share at least ONE mechanism (phonological, distributional, semantic, or acquisitional). If no shared mechanism, grammaticalization blocked.

**Is this testable?**

Yes. Survey grammaticalization pathways cross-linguistically (Heine & Kuteva 2002 *World Lexicon of Grammaticalization*). For each attested pathway, code:
- Source category mechanisms
- Target category mechanisms
- Mechanism overlap (0 = none, 1 = phonological, 2 = distributional, 3 = semantic, 4 = acquisitional)

**HPC prediction:** No attested pathways with mechanism overlap = 0. All attested pathways should share at least one mechanism.

**If counterexamples exist:** HPC parasitism hypothesis is too strong.

**Example to check:** Do any lexical items grammaticalize into positions with ZERO mechanism overlap? (I suspect not, but this needs systematic testing.)

### 3b. Why Verb → Auxiliary but Not Auxiliary → Noun?

**Attested:**
- **Verb → Auxiliary:** *have, be, go, come, want, need* (English); *avoir, être, aller* (French); *haber, ser, ir* (Spanish)
- Cross-linguistically: motion verbs, possession verbs, volition verbs → auxiliaries

**Unattested:**
- **Auxiliary → Noun:** I know of no cases.

**Why?**

**Mechanism parasitism:**

The INFL/auxiliary slot has existing maintenance:
1. **Distributional:** Verbs already occupy this position (main verb → auxiliary is a small distributional shift)
2. **Semantic:** TAM meanings (tense, aspect, modality) are conceptually adjacent to verb semantics (motion → future; possession → perfect; volition → future/irrealis)
3. **Phonological:** Verbal prosody already in place (unstressed auxiliary position available)
4. **Morphological:** Auxiliaries often retain verb inflection (at least initially)

A verb grammaticalizing into auxiliary **parasitizes verbal maintenance**. It's already a verb; it just specializes into the auxiliary subclass.

**Why not auxiliary → noun?**

Nouns and auxiliaries share NO mechanisms:
1. **Distributional:** Nouns head NPs; auxiliaries are in INFL/T. Completely different structural positions.
2. **Semantic:** Nouns denote entities; auxiliaries express TAM. No semantic bridge.
3. **Phonological:** Nouns can be stressed; auxiliaries are typically unstressed. Different prosodic profiles.
4. **Morphological:** Noun inflection (number, case, gender) vs verb inflection (tense, aspect, agreement). No overlap.

No shared mechanisms → no parasitism possible → grammaticalization blocked.

**Prediction:** Grammaticalization pathways should respect mechanism adjacency. You can move between categories that share mechanisms, but not between categories with no overlap.

**Graph-theoretic prediction:** If you graph categories as nodes and connect them when they share mechanisms, grammaticalization pathways should follow the edges of this graph. No pathway should jump across disconnected components.

**Is this testable?**

Yes. Build the mechanism-overlap graph:
- Nodes = lexical/functional categories (noun, verb, adjective, adverb, determiner, auxiliary, complementizer, adposition, etc.)
- Edges = shared mechanisms (weighted by degree of overlap)
- Grammaticalization pathways = directed edges in observed change

**HPC prediction:** Observed pathways should cluster on high-overlap edges. No pathways should cross zero-overlap gaps.

**Data source:** Heine & Kuteva (2002) codes ~500 pathways. Check against mechanism-overlap graph.

### 3c. Counterexamples That Challenge This Prediction?

**Possible counterexample 1: Noun → Adposition**

Attested: *back, top, side* (English nouns) → *in back of, on top of, by the side of* → prepositions

Mechanism overlap = LOW:
- Nouns denote entities; adpositions express spatial/relational meanings
- Nouns head NPs; adpositions head PPs (different structure)
- But: both can be arguments in locative constructions (*the back of the house* ~ *behind the house*)

**HPC response:** There IS mechanism overlap — the locative frame. Nouns in locative constructions (body-part nouns, spatial nouns) already couple to spatial semantics. The grammaticalization doesn't cross a zero-overlap gap; it exploits the shared spatial anchor.

**Revision:** Parasitism requires overlap in AT LEAST ONE mechanism, not necessarily all. Noun → adposition works because they share the spatial-semantic frame.

**Possible counterexample 2: Noun → Demonstrative**

Attested (?): Some languages use deictic nouns (*place, thing*) as demonstratives.

**Check needed:** Are these actually nouns grammaticalizing into demonstratives, or are they just deictic nouns that remain nouns? (Distinction: do they develop demonstrative distributional behavior, or do they stay nominal?)

If genuine: mechanism overlap = deictic semantics (shared). Distributional overlap = both can modify nouns (*that place* → *that [DEM]*).

**Possible counterexample 3: Adverb → Complementizer**

Attested: *while* (temporal adverb) → complementizer; *because* (causal adverb) → complementizer

Mechanism overlap:
- Both are adjuncts/modifiers (distributional adjacency)
- Both express temporal/causal relations (semantic overlap)
- Adverbs and complementizers are often unstressed (prosodic overlap)

**HPC response:** Again, shared mechanisms exist. Not a counterexample.

**True test:** Find a grammaticalization pathway where source and target share ZERO mechanisms. If none exist, parasitism hypothesis is supported.

### 3d. Parasitism vs Traditional "Grammaticalization Channel" Story

**Traditional story:**
- Grammaticalization follows recurring pathways (Heine & Kuteva's *World Lexicon*)
- These pathways are "channels" or "clines" (e.g., motion verb → future auxiliary is a universal tendency)
- Explanation: conceptual metaphor (MOTION IS TIME), semantic plausibility, frequency of use

**HPC parasitism story:**
- Pathways exist because source and target share mechanisms
- Grammaticalization is only possible when the source can parasitize the target's maintenance infrastructure
- Conceptual plausibility is NECESSARY but NOT SUFFICIENT — you also need mechanism overlap

**Difference:**

Traditional theory: "Demonstrative → article is common because definiteness and deixis are semantically related."

HPC: "Demonstrative → article is common because: (a) definiteness and deixis are semantically related [shared semantic mechanism], AND (b) demonstratives already occupy the pre-nominal position [shared distributional mechanism], AND (c) both are unstressed in discourse [shared prosodic mechanism]. Remove any of these, and the pathway is blocked."

**Testable difference:**

Find cases where:
1. **Semantic plausibility exists but mechanism overlap doesn't:** HPC predicts grammaticalization blocked.
2. **Mechanism overlap exists but semantic plausibility doesn't:** HPC predicts grammaticalization possible (though maybe rare).

**Example of (1):**
- Semantic plausibility: Demonstratives could grammaticalize into evidential markers (both express speaker perspective)
- Mechanism overlap: LOW (demonstratives are NP-internal; evidentials are clausal)
- Prediction: Demonstrative → evidential should be rare or unattested.

**Is it attested?** Need to check. If unattested despite semantic plausibility, supports HPC. If attested, need to look for hidden mechanism overlap.

**Genuine novelty?**

Yes. Traditional theory doesn't predict BLOCKAGES based on mechanism structure. It describes what happens, not what CAN'T happen. HPC makes negative predictions: grammaticalization should be impossible across zero-overlap gaps.

## 4. Co-Grammaticalization from Shared Mechanisms

**HPC prediction:** If two categories share maintenance mechanisms, emergence of one should facilitate emergence of the other. Article emergence creates maintenance infrastructure (determiner slot, definiteness semantics, prosodic reduction patterns) that other determiner-class items (quantifiers, possessives) can parasitize.

**Traditional theory:** Describes co-occurrence patterns (languages with articles tend to have elaborate quantifier systems) but doesn't predict CAUSAL dependence.

**Genuine novelty?** Yes. HPC predicts temporal correlation: shared-mechanism categories should co-emerge diachronically more than chance.

### 4a. Article Systems and Quantifier Elaboration — Do They Co-Emerge?

**Typological pattern (Dryer 2013, WALS):**
- Languages with definite articles: 219/620 (35%)
- Languages with indefinite articles: 107/475 (23%)
- Languages with both: Many (but not universal)

**Observation:** Languages with article systems tend to have elaborated quantifier inventories (many distinct forms for *all, some, many, few, each, every*, etc.). Languages without articles tend to have smaller quantifier inventories.

**Traditional explanation:** Independent drift; articles and quantifiers both grammaticalize over time.

**HPC explanation:** Shared mechanisms:
1. **Determiner slot:** Articles create/stabilize the pre-nominal determiner position
2. **Definiteness contrast:** Articles grammaticalize definiteness as a formal category, which makes quantifier meanings (universal vs existential vs partitive) more structurally relevant
3. **Prosodic structure:** Determiner position is unstressed, which allows phonological reduction of quantifiers
4. **Acquisition:** Once children learn the determiner slot from articles, they can extend it to quantifiers

**Parasitism:** Quantifiers emerging in a language with articles can parasitize the determiner-slot maintenance. Quantifiers emerging in a language WITHOUT articles must build their own distributional maintenance.

**Prediction:** Article emergence should PRECEDE or CO-OCCUR with quantifier elaboration. We should NOT see: elaborate quantifier systems emerging first, then articles emerging later.

**Is this testable?**

Yes. Historical corpus study:
1. **Sample languages** with documented grammaticalization of articles (English, Romance, Semitic)
2. **Track quantifier inventory size** over the same time period (count distinct quantifier forms, morphological elaboration)
3. **Test temporal correlation:**
   - Do quantifiers elaborate AFTER articles emerge? (HPC parasitism prediction)
   - Or independently? (traditional view)

**English case:**

- **OE (pre-article):** Demonstrative *se/seo/þæt* but no obligatory articles. Quantifier inventory: *eall, sum, fela, maniġ, ǣlc* (moderate, ~5-10 forms)
- **ME (article emergence, 1100-1400):** *The* grammaticalizes. Quantifier inventory: *all, some, many, few, each, every, any, no, much* (expanding, ~15 forms)
- **ModE (article established, 1700+):** Articles obligatory. Quantifier inventory: Further elaboration (*several, numerous, various, countless, etc.*) (~30+ forms in standard inventories)

**Pattern:** Quantifier elaboration FOLLOWS article grammaticalization. Consistent with HPC parasitism.

**But:** Correlation ≠ causation. Could both be driven by third factor (e.g., language contact, literacy, register differentiation).

**Stronger test:** Cross-linguistic. Does this pattern hold in other languages?

- **Romance (Latin → French/Spanish/Italian):** Latin had no articles. Romance languages grammaticalized articles (from demonstratives) and elaborated quantifier systems. WHEN did quantifiers elaborate? Before, during, or after article emergence?
  - **Prediction:** During or after.

- **Semitic (Biblical Hebrew → Modern Hebrew):** Biblical Hebrew had no definite article (used *ha-* as a weaker marker). Modern Hebrew has obligatory *ha-*. Did quantifier system elaborate in parallel?

**Data needed:** Historical grammars, corpus studies tracking quantifier token counts and type diversity over time.

**WALS-based test:**

Compare languages:
1. **Article languages:** Do they have larger quantifier inventories than non-article languages? (Dryer doesn't code quantifier inventory size directly, but could be inferred from descriptive grammars.)
2. **If YES:** Consistent with HPC shared-mechanism prediction.
3. **If NO:** Shared mechanisms don't drive co-emergence.

### 4b. Case Systems and Word-Order Rigidity — Do They Co-Emerge?

**Typological correlation (Sinnemäki 2014):**
- Languages with rich case systems tend to have flexible word order (Latin, Russian, Finnish, Turkish)
- Languages with poor/no case systems tend to have rigid word order (English, Mandarin, Thai)

**Traditional explanation:** Functional trade-off. Case marking identifies grammatical relations, so word order can be free. Without case, word order must be rigid to identify relations.

**HPC explanation:** Shared vs competing mechanisms:

**Rich case = distributional flexibility** is maintained by morphological marking. Word order is free because grammatical relations are identified by case morphology, not position.

**Rigid word order = distributional maintenance** itself. Grammatical relations are identified by position (SVO, SOV, etc.), which requires FIXED positions.

**Competing mechanisms:** Case morphology and positional rigidity are ALTERNATIVE maintenance strategies for the same function (identifying grammatical relations). A language needs one or the other, but not both.

**Prediction:** Case loss should be accompanied by word-order rigidification, and vice versa.

**Historical test: English case loss**

- **OE:** Rich case system (nominative, accusative, genitive, dative, instrumental). Flexible word order (SOV, SVO, VSO all attested).
- **ME:** Case system collapses (only genitive *-s* survives). Word order rigidifies (SVO becomes dominant by 1400).
- **ModE:** No case on nouns (except genitive). Rigid SVO.

**Timeline:**
- Case collapse: 1100-1300 (rapid, driven by vowel reduction in unstressed syllables)
- Word-order rigidification: 1200-1400 (SVO becomes categorical)

**Pattern:** Co-occur within ~100-200 years. Consistent with HPC: loss of one mechanism (case) forced emergence of another (rigid word order) to maintain grammatical-relation identification.

**But:** Again, correlation ≠ causation. Could be independent drift.

**Stronger test:** Does this pattern hold cross-linguistically?

- **Romance case loss:** Latin had rich case. Romance languages lost case (except Romanian, which retained some). Did they rigidify word order in parallel?
  - **French:** Lost case by OF (c. 1200). Rigidified to SVO.
  - **Spanish:** Lost case (except pronouns). Rigidified to SVO (though more flexible than French due to pro-drop).
  - **Pattern:** YES, co-emergence.

- **Mainland Scandinavian case loss:** Old Norse had rich case. Swedish/Danish/Norwegian lost most case by 1500. Did they rigidify?
  - **Swedish:** Lost case. Became rigid SVO.
  - **Pattern:** YES.

- **Counterexample?** Any languages that:
  1. Lost case but didn't rigidify word order? (Would need another mechanism for GR identification — maybe agreement?)
  2. Rigidified word order but still have case? (Should be rare, because both mechanisms are redundant.)

**Typological prediction:** Languages should cluster in two regions:
1. **Rich case + flexible word order**
2. **Poor case + rigid word order**

Intermediate cases (moderate case + moderate rigidity) should be rare or unstable (transitional states).

**WALS test:** Plot case-marking complexity (WALS ch. 49) against word-order flexibility (harder to quantify, but SOV/SVO/VSO dominance in ch. 81-83 could proxy).

**HPC prediction:** Negative correlation. Languages with more case distinctions should have more word-order flexibility.

**If correlation exists:** Consistent with competing-mechanism hypothesis.

**If no correlation:** Mechanisms are independent, not competing.

### 4c. Typological Predictions from Co-Grammaticalization

**General HPC prediction:**

If categories X and Y share maintenance mechanism M:
1. **Co-emergence:** X and Y should grammaticalize together more often than chance
2. **Typological co-occurrence:** Languages with X should have Y more often than languages without X
3. **Diachronic correlation:** Historical emergence of X should predict emergence of Y within ~100-500 years

**Testable cases:**

1. **Articles + quantifiers:** (Tested above — positive correlation predicted)
2. **Case + word-order:** (Tested above — negative correlation predicted, because competing mechanisms)
3. **Tense + aspect:** Shared temporal semantics. Predict: co-elaboration. Languages with rich tense should have rich aspect.
4. **Gender + agreement:** Shared morphological anchoring. Predict: co-emergence. Languages with gender should have elaborate agreement systems.
5. **Evidentials + epistemic modals:** Shared speaker-perspective semantics. Predict: co-occurrence. Languages with grammatical evidentials should have elaborate epistemic modal systems.

**WALS-based test:**

For each pair, compute:
- **Co-occurrence rate:** P(Y | X) vs P(Y | ¬X)
- **If P(Y | X) >> P(Y | ¬X):** Shared mechanisms likely
- **If P(Y | X) ≈ P(Y | ¬X):** Independent

**Data availability:** WALS has ~200 features across ~2000+ languages. Can cross-tabulate to test co-occurrence.

**Limitation:** WALS is synchronic. Can't test diachronic emergence directly (need historical corpora).

### 4d. Can You Check Against WALS?

**Yes.** Concrete predictions for WALS:

**Prediction 1: Articles + Quantifiers**
- **WALS Ch. 37:** Definite articles (present/absent)
- **WALS Ch. 38:** Indefinite articles (present/absent)
- **Proxy for quantifier elaboration:** Would need to code from reference grammars (WALS doesn't have a quantifier-inventory feature). Alternatively, use numeral classifier systems (Ch. 55) as a proxy (both are determiner-class elaborations).

**Test:** Do languages with articles have numeral classifiers more often? (Probably NO — classifiers and articles are typologically anti-correlated. But both are determiner-slot elaborations, so this tests whether determiner-slot infrastructure facilitates ANY elaboration, not just quantifiers.)

**Prediction 2: Case + Word Order**
- **WALS Ch. 49:** Case marking (no case, low, high)
- **WALS Ch. 81:** Order of subject/object/verb

**Test:** Do high-case languages show more SOV/VSO diversity (flexible word order)? Do no-case languages show more rigid SVO?

**Expected:** High-case languages should have lower dominant-order frequency (more flexibility). No-case languages should have higher dominant-order frequency (SVO rigidity).

**Prediction 3: Tense + Aspect**
- **WALS Ch. 66:** Past tense (present/absent)
- **WALS Ch. 65:** Perfective/imperfective aspect

**Test:** P(aspect | tense) vs P(aspect | no tense). Predict: positive correlation (shared temporal semantics).

**Prediction 4: Gender + Agreement**
- **WALS Ch. 30:** Noun gender (present/absent)
- **WALS Ch. 44:** Gender agreement (nominal/verbal)

**Test:** P(agreement | gender) vs P(agreement | no gender). Predict: strong positive correlation (gender requires agreement to be functional).

**Prediction 5: Evidentials + Epistemic Modals**
- **WALS Ch. 77:** Evidentials (present/absent)
- **Proxy for modals:** Future tense (Ch. 67) or possibility/necessity markers (would need to code from grammars)

**Test:** Do evidential languages have more elaborate modal systems? (Hard to test with WALS alone — would need targeted coding.)

**Overall HPC prediction:** Shared-mechanism categories should show positive correlations. Competing-mechanism categories should show negative correlations.

**If correlations don't hold:** Shared-mechanism story is too simple. Categories may be independent despite apparent mechanism overlap.

## 5. Hub-Node in Category Genesis

**HPC prediction (from psychiatry's hub-node question):** In an emerging category, one property is the HUB — the one whose removal most destabilizes the rest. Other properties are peripheral. Intervene on the hub → cluster unravels. Intervene on peripheral node → cluster holds.

**Traditional grammaticalization theory:** Doesn't distinguish hub from periphery. Treats all emergent properties as equally important.

**Genuine novelty?** YES. Hub-node framing makes ASYMMETRIC predictions: not all properties are equal. One is load-bearing.

### 5a. English Deitality: What Was the Hub?

**Emergent properties of deitality (definite article category):**

1. **Obligatoriness:** *The* required with definite singular count nouns
2. **Phonological reduction:** Unstressed *[ðə]/[ði]*, never stressed
3. **Complementary distribution:** *The* vs *that* (article vs demonstrative split)
4. **Uninflected:** No case/gender/number marking
5. **Pre-nominal fixedness:** Always immediately before noun (or adjectives)
6. **Semantic bleaching:** No deictic force (unlike demonstrative)

**Which is the hub?**

**Candidate 1: Obligatoriness**

Argument: Once *the* becomes obligatory, all other properties follow:
- Obligatoriness → high frequency → phonological reduction
- Obligatoriness → grammatical function → no inflection (grammatical items tend to lose inflection)
- Obligatoriness → complementary distribution (if *the* is required for definiteness, demonstrative *that* must specialize for deixis)

**Test:** If obligatoriness were removed (hypothetical intervention), would the cluster unravel?
- Without obligatoriness, *the* would be optional → lower frequency → less phonological reduction
- Without obligatoriness, *the* and *that* could compete in the same contexts → no complementary distribution
- Without obligatoriness, *the* might retain demonstrative force → no semantic bleaching

**Prediction:** YES, cluster would unravel. Obligatoriness is plausibly the hub.

**Candidate 2: Phonological reduction**

Argument: Phonological reduction signals grammatical status. Only once *the* is unstressed can it become obligatory (because it's no longer informationally prominent).

**Test:** If phonological reduction were removed (intervention = stress *the* like a demonstrative), would the cluster hold?
- Stressed *the* would be informationally marked → can't be obligatory (obligatory items are backgrounded)
- Stressed *the* would compete with demonstrative *that* → no complementary distribution
- But: uninflected status might persist (independent of stress)

**Prediction:** MAYBE. Phonological reduction might be hub, but less clearly than obligatoriness.

**Candidate 3: Complementary distribution**

Argument: Once *the* and *that* split into distinct categories, *the* must develop its own projection profile (obligatoriness, reduction, etc.) to be a functional category.

**Test:** If complementary distribution were removed (hypothetical: *the* and *that* both used for definiteness), would the cluster hold?
- Without complementary distribution, *the* and *that* would be in competition → one should win (competitive exclusion)
- If *the* won, it would develop obligatoriness + reduction
- If *that* won, *the* would disappear

**Prediction:** Complementary distribution is necessary for coexistence, but it doesn't drive the other properties. It's a consequence of competition resolution, not a hub.

**Argument for Obligatoriness as Hub:**

HPC predicts the hub is the property that DRIVES maintenance of the others. Obligatoriness does this:
- High frequency (from obligatoriness) → phonological reduction
- Grammatical function (from obligatoriness) → loss of inflection
- Functional specialization (from obligatoriness) → complementary distribution with demonstrative

Remove obligatoriness → cascade fails → cluster unravels.

**Counter-argument:**

Obligatoriness itself must be MAINTAINED. What maintains it?
- Acquisition (children learn *the* as obligatory)
- Structural maintenance (NP structure requires filled determiner slot in ModE)

So obligatoriness is maintained by deeper mechanisms (structural + acquisitional). Is it the hub, or is the determiner-slot ITSELF the hub?

**Deeper answer:** The hub might not be a property but a MECHANISM. The determiner-slot structural maintenance is the hub. Once that's in place, all surface properties (obligatoriness, reduction, etc.) follow.

**But:** This shifts the question from "which property is load-bearing" to "which mechanism is load-bearing," which is a different (and maybe better) question.

### 5b. English Progressive: What Was the Hub?

**Emergent properties of the progressive:**

1. **Aspectual meaning:** Ongoing, durative, imperfective
2. **Stative exclusion:** *\*I am knowing* blocked
3. **Obligatoriness:** Required for ongoing interpretation (simple present → habitual)
4. **Auxiliary status:** *Be* in progressive is an auxiliary, not a copula
5. **V-*ing* morphology:** Specialized for progressive (vs gerund, which has different distribution)
6. **Preposition loss:** *On hunting* → *hunting* (bare V-*ing*)

**Which is the hub?**

**Candidate 1: Aspectual meaning (ongoing)**

Argument: Once progressive acquires dedicated aspectual meaning, all else follows:
- Aspectual meaning → stative exclusion (statives are incompatible with ongoing)
- Aspectual meaning → obligatoriness (if progressive is the ONLY way to express ongoing, it's required)
- Aspectual meaning → auxiliary status (aspectual markers are in INFL/T, which is auxiliary position)

**Test:** If aspectual meaning were removed (hypothetical: *be V-ing* reverts to locative-like), would cluster unravel?
- Without aspectual meaning, stative exclusion disappears (*I am knowing* would be fine, meaning "I am in a state of knowing")
- Without aspectual meaning, no obligatoriness (simple present could still express ongoing)
- Without aspectual meaning, *be* might revert to copula status

**Prediction:** YES, cluster unravels. Aspectual meaning is plausibly the hub.

**Candidate 2: Stative exclusion**

Argument: Stative exclusion is what makes progressive a GRAMMATICAL category (not just a high-frequency construction). Only once statives are blocked does progressive become obligatory for dynamic verbs.

**Test:** If stative exclusion were removed (allow *I am knowing*), would cluster hold?
- Progressive would still have aspectual meaning (ongoing)
- But: obligatoriness would weaken (if statives can appear in both forms, simple present could still express ongoing for all verbs)
- Auxiliary status might persist

**Prediction:** MAYBE. Stative exclusion is important for obligatoriness, but it's downstream of aspectual meaning. Not the hub.

**Candidate 3: Obligatoriness**

Argument: Same as deitality case — obligatoriness drives frequency, which drives entrenchment.

**Test:** If obligatoriness were removed, would cluster hold?
- Without obligatoriness, progressive would be optional → lower frequency
- But: aspectual meaning might persist (just as an optional way to mark ongoing)
- Stative exclusion might persist (if progressive retains durative semantics, statives still incompatible)

**Prediction:** Cluster might hold in weakened form. Obligatoriness is important but maybe not the hub.

**Argument for Aspectual Meaning as Hub:**

Aspectual meaning is what DEFINES the progressive. Remove it, and you just have a high-frequency locative construction. All other properties (stative exclusion, obligatoriness, auxiliary status) are consequences of aspectual meaning.

**Deeper answer:**

Same as deitality: the hub might be the MECHANISM (structural maintenance of the INFL/T slot), not the surface property. Once aspectual meaning couples to INFL, the cluster is maintained.

### 5c. General Pattern: What Makes a Property the Hub?

**Criteria for hub-node status:**

1. **Removal causes cascade:** Other properties unravel if hub is removed
2. **Directionality:** Hub drives other properties, not vice versa
3. **Independence:** Hub can exist without other properties (at least briefly), but other properties can't exist without hub

**Operationalization:**

In a historical corpus, track co-occurrence of emergent properties over time. Compute:
- **Correlation strength:** Do properties emerge together?
- **Temporal precedence:** Which property emerges first?
- **Conditional probability:** P(property B | property A) vs P(property A | property B)

**If property A is the hub:**
- A should emerge first (or co-emerge with others)
- P(B | A) should be high (once A appears, B follows)
- P(A | B) might be lower (B doesn't guarantee A)

**Example: Progressive**

Track over 1200-1700:
- **Aspectual meaning:** When does *be V-ing* start appearing in ongoing contexts (not just locative)?
- **Stative exclusion:** When do stative verbs stop appearing in *be V-ing*?
- **Obligatoriness:** When does simple present stop appearing in ongoing contexts?

**HPC prediction:** Aspectual meaning should precede or co-emerge with stative exclusion and obligatoriness.

**Cline prediction:** Gradual, independent emergence (no clear precedence).

**Data:** PPCME2, PPCEME (already tagged for aspect). Could compute conditional probabilities.

### 5d. Summary: What's Genuinely New?

**HPC's hub-node question:**

Traditional grammaticalization theory treats emergent properties as a bundle. HPC asks: which property is load-bearing? This makes ASYMMETRIC predictions:
- Intervene on hub → cluster unravels
- Intervene on periphery → cluster holds

**Testable:**
1. **Historical:** Does hub property emerge first?
2. **Psycholinguistic:** Do speakers treat hub violations as more serious than peripheral violations?
3. **Computational:** Do models trained on hub property generalize to peripheral properties, but not vice versa?

**If hub-node structure doesn't exist:** All properties are equally load-bearing. Grammaticalization is truly a bundle with no internal asymmetry.

**If hub-node structure exists:** HPC correctly predicts category-internal structure. This is a novel prediction that traditional theory doesn't make.

## Overall Assessment: What's Genuinely New vs Relabelling?

| Question | Genuinely New? | Why? |
|----------|----------------|------|
| **Competitive exclusion** | YES | Predicts outcomes from mechanism overlap BEFORE they happen. Traditional theory describes, doesn't predict. |
| **Phase transition vs cline** | YES | Testable clash: nonlinear emergence vs continuous change. Different time-series predictions. |
| **Mechanism parasitism** | MODERATE | Stronger claim than traditional "channels": predicts BLOCKAGES where no mechanism overlap exists. But needs testing to distinguish from conceptual-plausibility story. |
| **Co-grammaticalization** | YES | Predicts temporal correlation from shared mechanisms. Typological co-occurrence could be tested against WALS. Traditional theory doesn't make causal predictions about co-emergence. |
| **Hub-node** | YES | Asymmetric predictions: intervene on hub → unravels; intervene on periphery → holds. Traditional theory treats all emergent properties as equal. |

**Overall:** HPC opens genuinely new questions. The phase-transition, competitive-exclusion, and hub-node questions are NOVEL. The parasitism and co-grammaticalization questions are moderate (refinements of traditional theory, but with testable new predictions).

**Key insight:** HPC's advantage is PREDICTIVE, not just descriptive. It predicts:
- Which forms will win in competition (mechanism overlap)
- When emergence will be nonlinear (phase transition)
- Which pathways are blocked (parasitism)
- Which categories co-emerge (shared mechanisms)
- Which properties are load-bearing (hub-node)

Traditional grammaticalization theory describes these patterns but doesn't predict them from first principles.

## Where's the Evidence? What's Speculation?

**Strong evidence:**
- **Competitive exclusion (*shall* vs *will*):** Well-documented. Mechanism analysis is post-hoc but plausible.
- **Phase transition (progressive frequency):** Historical corpus studies show nonlinear increase in EModE. Supports HPC.
- **Parasitism (demonstrative → article):** Determiner-slot infrastructure clearly existed. Parasitism framing is new but fits the data.

**Moderate evidence:**
- **Co-grammaticalization (articles + quantifiers):** English timeline supports it, but could be coincidence. Cross-linguistic test needed.
- **Hub-node (obligatoriness as hub):** Plausible but untested. Need conditional-probability analysis in historical corpus.

**Speculation:**
- **Predicting competitive exclusion from mechanism overlap:** Not yet tested. Would require: (a) quantifying mechanism overlap for multiple cases, (b) checking whether overlap correlates with outcomes.
- **Typological co-occurrence (WALS tests):** Not yet done. WALS has the data, but HPC-motivated cross-tabulations haven't been run.
- **Phase transition universality:** Tested for one case (progressive). Does it generalize to all grammaticalization? Unknown.

**Critical tests still needed:**
1. **Time-series analysis** for article emergence (does paradigm collapse happen nonlinearly?)
2. **Mechanism-overlap graph** (do all grammaticalization pathways follow mechanism-adjacency edges?)
3. **WALS co-occurrence tests** (do shared-mechanism categories co-occur typologically?)
4. **Hub-node conditional probabilities** (does hub property emerge first and drive others?)

**Bottom line:** HPC opens new questions and makes testable predictions. Some are supported by existing data (phase transition for progressive). Others are speculative and need targeted studies. But the predictions are NOVEL — traditional theory doesn't ask these questions.
