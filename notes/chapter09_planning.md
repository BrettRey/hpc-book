### Proposed Section Breakdown

**§9.1 The Cluster**  
*What properties co-occur in count nouns?*

- Introduce the count property cluster: singular/plural contrast, *a(n)*, low cardinals, *many/few*, distributives, plural agreement
- Key point: these properties *cluster* — knowing one predicts others
- Contrast with expectation of random scatter or tidy uniformity
- Opening hook: the *furniture* student ("three furnitures") — why does this feel wrong?

**§9.2 Three Levels**  
*Ontology, construal, grammar*

- Distinguish: ontological discreteness (world) ≠ semantic individuation (construal) ≠ morphosyntactic countability (grammar)
- *Cattle* as paradigm case: ontologically discrete bovines, but grammatically intermediate
- This prevents the reductionist error: "count nouns are nouns for countable things"

**§9.3 The Mechanism**  
*Bidirectional inference*

- Count morphosyntax → individuated construal (comprehension)
- Individuated construal → count morphosyntax (production)
- Multi-timescale operation: processing (ms) → acquisition (years) → institutional (decades)
- Why the cluster is *self-reinforcing*: inference couples the properties

**§9.4 The Hierarchy**  
*Tight before loose*

- Properties differ in precision thresholds
- When individuation weakens, tight properties fail first: singular, *a(n)*, low cardinals → then moderate: *several*, distributives → then loose: *many*, high numerals, agreement
- The hierarchy is *implicational*: no noun accepts tight while rejecting loose
- Falsifiability: finding such a noun would challenge the account

**§9.5 Quasi-Count Nouns**  
*The stable intermediates*

- *Cattle*, *police*, *clergy*, *poultry*, *vermin* — \textit{CGEL}'s quasi-count class
- They accept loose properties, reject tight ones — exactly the predicted intermediate position
- Why stable? Functional anchoring: *officer* handles singulative for *police*; *cow* for *cattle*
- Contrast with *folks*: no functional anchor, therefore unstable (variability in *three folks*)

**§9.6 Diachronic Case: *Pea***  
*How the cluster self-completes*

- *Pease* was mass; final /z/ reanalyzed as plural; speakers back-formed *pea*; count cluster filled in
- This is the mechanism *in action*: cluster pressure creates gaps; gaps get filled
- Contrast: *cattle* stable for centuries because *cow* bleeds pressure; *pease* had nothing

**§9.7 Passing the Test**  
*Countability as genuine HPC*

- Apply Two-Diagnostic Test from Chapter 8:
  - **Projectibility:** Knowing a noun is count lets you predict its behaviour with quantifiers, agreement, etc.
  - **Homeostasis:** The bidirectional inference mechanism maintains clustering; functional anchoring stabilizes intermediates
- Countability is in the upper-right quadrant: genuine HPC kind

**§9.8 Predictions** *(from structural feedback action plan)*  
*Minimum 3–5 bullets; at least one discriminating prediction*

- **HPC-kind diagnosis predicts** (scoped to contemporary English, Standard register): count morphosyntax-with-modifiers patterns should cluster; quasi-count nouns should occupy stable intermediate positions; diachronic shifts should follow hierarchy order.
- **Convenience/nominalist diagnosis predicts:** operationalization changes should reshuffle membership freely; *cattle* and *cattle*-like nouns could pattern either way depending on annotation.
- **Essentialist diagnosis predicts:** sharp boundary between count and mass; quasi-count should be unstable or resolvable.
- **Stabilizer tie-ins:** Acquisition: quasi-counts harder to learn than full count/mass. Entrenchment: high-frequency quasi-counts more stable. Transmission: Zipfian profile facilitates learning.
- **Discriminating prediction:** If *cattle*-like nouns were being actively maintained by a mechanism (vs merely persisting via frozen accident), we'd expect (i) acquisition difficulty proportional to distance from prototype, and (ii) diachronic replenishment when functional anchors disappear (cf. *pea*).
- **Reclassification trigger:** If quasi-count nouns show no correlation between functional-anchor presence and diachronic stability (i.e., anchored and unanchored items drift at the same rate), reclassify as frozen accident (thin) rather than actively maintained HPC.

**Coda / Transition**  
- Brief glance forward: Chapter 10 (Definiteness) will show a case where form and meaning may constitute *separate* overlapping HPCs — a more complex configuration

## Advisory Board Feedback (Incorporated)

### From Ruth Millikan (Teleosemantics)
> Distinguish *actively maintained* (function) from *passively persisting* (no pressure to change). *Cattle* is the latter.

**Action:** In §9.5, clarify that quasi-count stability through functional anchoring is *passive* (displacement bleeds pressure) rather than *active* (the pattern serves a function). This matters for predicting what might destabilize.

### From Adele Goldberg (Construction Grammar)
> Locate precision demands in *constructions*, not noun features. The noun provides construal; construction selects.

**Action:** In §9.4 (The Hierarchy), explicitly frame the thresholds as properties of *constructions*, not nouns. [*a* N] selects for high-precision individuation; [*many* N] tolerates low. The noun contributes a signal; the construction sets the bar.

### From Joan Bybee (Frequency/Entrenchment)
> Add chunking as cognitive mechanism. Make *pea* vivid.

**Action:** In §9.3 (The Mechanism), add a sentence on chunking: high-frequency sequences like *many cattle* are stored as units; *three cattle* isn't entrenched and fails online assembly. In §9.6 (*Pea*), make the diachronic case vivid: speakers hearing *pease* as plural *expected* a singular; the gap created by that expectation got filled.

### From Eleanor Rosch (Prototype Theory)
> Frame HPC as *completing* prototype work, not replacing it.

**Action:** In §9.4 or §9.7, acknowledge that prototype theory identified the gradience; HPC explains *why* the gradient has the shape it does. The relationship is complementary.

### From Michael Tomasello (Acquisition)
> Flag quasi-count acquisition difficulty as testable. Separate learning from transmission.

**Action:** In §9.3 or §9.7, add a paragraph: Quasi-count patterns should be *harder* to acquire than full count or full mass, because children must learn the cluster is incomplete. This is a testable prediction (developmental data sparse). Explicitly distinguish individual-level learning from population-level transmission (Kirby).

### From Carl Zimmer (Science Communication)
> Find a single controlling metaphor for precision thresholds.

**Action:** Use the *lock-and-key* metaphor: some locks (constructions) are precision-machined (*a*, *three*); others are loose (*many*). The noun's key (individuation profile) either fits or doesn't. OR integrate with spinning-top from earlier chapters: the top is in a basin (the count cluster), but at different distances from the center (precision). Choose one and commit.

### From Edward Tufte (Visual Presentation)
> Keep the matrix. Audit threshold diagram and bar charts.

**Action:** 
- **Matrix (noun × property):** Keep. It's the empirical core — shows the triangular pattern at a glance.
- **Threshold diagram:** Consider cutting or simplifying. The prose conveys the hierarchy. If kept, it needs to do unique work.
- **COCA bar charts:** Consider replacing with a table. The prose already gives the numbers.

### From George Lakoff (Conceptual Metaphor)
> Unify dynamic stability (spinning top) with precision thresholds (depth in basin).

**Action:** The *hierarchy* is the shape of the basin. Tight properties require the top to be centered; loose properties are satisfied anywhere in the basin. A quasi-count noun spins stably but off-center — satisfies basin membership but not central precision. Draft with this integrated model.
