**Strengths**  
Chapter 15 has a strong constructional spine at its best moments. The opening frames social correction around a specific form (`The thing is is...`) and immediately gives distributional cues (lexical attractors, discourse context), which is exactly the right usage-based entry point (`chapters/chapter15.tex:6`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:35`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:37`). The “mixed bin” section is also effective when it treats existential `there’s` as its own stabilized pattern rather than failed agreement (`chapters/chapter15.tex:64`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:67`). I also like the acquisition move: com-sits as learned conditioning environments aligns well with exemplar-driven learning (`chapters/chapter15.tex:158`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:164`). This continues the Chapter 14 claim that grammar is community-calibrated and construction-specific (`chapters/chapter14.tex:263`, `chapters/chapter14.tex:315`). So the chapter already contains the right ingredients for a construction grammar account of social stabilization.

**Major Concerns**  
The chapter repeatedly drifts from constructional behavior into abstract “category realism” talk. The violinist analogy and later nominalist section are philosophically rich, but they displace form-meaning evidence (`chapters/chapter15.tex:86`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:190`). From a usage-based perspective, social stabilization should be demonstrated as shifting distributions over constructions, not primarily defended as ontology.  
Second, the conditioning formalization is under-specified for construction grammar. You model `P(Form | D,R,C)` and `P(Form | Social)` (`chapters/chapter15.tex:73`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:173`), but Chapter 14 defines grammatical organization as form-value coupling (`chapters/chapter14.tex:261`). Without an explicit meaning side, conditioning looks like style stratification rather than constructional reweighting.  
Third, the dialect/register/community subsections often state mechanisms without enough concrete constructional consequences. You mention nominalizations/get-passive suppression, but not distributions over particular schemas or partial productivity patterns (`chapters/chapter15.tex:112`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:127`). This leaves unresolved a core concern already flagged in planning: “show how a construction’s distribution changes across com-sits” (`notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:68`, `notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:73`; also `notes/chapter15_planning.md:9`).

**Priority Fixes**  
1. Recast each of the three mechanism sections as one explicit constructional distribution: a schema, conditioning variables, and predicted token frequencies (dialect, register, community).  
2. Upgrade the formalism to form-meaning pairings, not form alone (e.g., `P(Construction=form+function | D,R,C)`), to align with Chapter 14’s zipper claim (`chapters/chapter14.tex:261`).  
3. Add one table of constructional alternations by context (double copula, existential `there’s`, passive/nominalization choices, one discourse-community genre marker) with concrete diagnostics.  
4. Restore planned worked examples that are currently missing (`Do you lift?`, code-switching, singular `they`) and present them as distributional families (`notes/chapter15_planning.md:21`, `notes/chapter15_planning.md:44`).  
5. Close with Chapter 16-style falsifiable constructional predictions to bridge directly into the gauntlet framework (`chapters/chapter16.tex:63`, `chapters/chapter16.tex:101`).
