**Strengths**
- The chapter has a clear argumentative spine: grammaticality as a maintained form-structural-meaning coupling, with judgments as a fallible detector (`§The HPC claim`, `§The stabilizer: a feeling`). That is publishable in principle because it offers an explanatory target, not just a taxonomy.
- You do real rhetorical discipline in `§The phenomenology of coupling`: “readout of the mechanism, not evidence independent of it.” That sentence is exactly what skeptical philosophers want to hear before phenomenology appears.
- The acceptability/grammaticality distinction is stated early and sharply (“Acceptability’s what the detector reports. Grammaticality’s the category”). Good.
- `§Relevance and the immune system` is strategically smart: it names a live rival and marks overlap before difference.
- The “Scoped prediction / Falsifier” block near `§The HPC audit` is your strongest move toward risk.

**Major Concerns**
1. The account is still partly metaphor-led where skeptics will demand mechanism-led argument. “Zipper,” “immune system,” “standing wave,” and “spinning top” are vivid, but you do not yet specify state variables, update rules, and causal pathways tightly enough. A formal syntactician will say this is explanatory redescription.
2. The chapter slides between detector calibration and category constitution (`§Entrenchment in action`: “the reality is constituted by what gets entrenched”). That is a philosophical high-wire claim; without argument, it looks like verificationism in linguistics clothing.
3. Acceptability vs grammaticality is stated rigorously, then softened by usage that re-fuses them. Example: “detector reports ungrammaticality” in illusion discussion can sound like phenomenal report is still primary evidence, just with caveats.
4. Empirical precision is uneven. “\~3-sigma absence,” “across tasks, speakers, and presentation modes,” and “no amount of exposure” are strong quantificational claims with no immediate methodological scaffolding. To skeptics, this reads as over-precise placeholder rhetoric.
5. The first line a specialist will reject is likely in `§Looking forward`: “there’s no ‘grammar’ there to resist.” That sounds sectarian, not argumentative. Close second: “That’s why syntax owns it” (`§Value at every grain`). Both risk alienating exactly the interdisciplinary audience you need.
6. Heavy reliance on `reynolds2026lbe` (unpublished) for a central empirical hinge weakens uptake. Philosophers and linguists will treat this as internal evidence unless triangulated.

**Priority Fixes**
1. Add a minimal explicit mechanism box: entities (speaker/hearer/community), latent grammaticality state, detector output, processing load, entrenchment prior, and update dynamics. One diagram would do more than five metaphors.
2. Separate timescales explicitly: online parsing misfire, medium-run satiation, long-run community change. Right now “category moves” risks conflating adaptation with grammar change.
3. Reframe absolute claims as conditional tests. Replace “no amount of exposure” with bounded claims tied to task class and population.
4. Repair sectarian phrasing:
- Replace “syntax owns it” with “syntax has the best-developed measurement tradition for this detector.”
- Replace “no grammar there to resist” with “no independent formal barrier has yet been established under changing social priors.”
5. In `§Empirical signatures`, add an adversarial table: MMMG vs relevance-theoretic vs constraint-based predictions. That signals scientific seriousness, not school loyalty.

**Decisive Tests**
1. Pre-registered dissociation study for the “three registers” (`§Three registers`): independent measures of jolt, blocking, and correction across garden paths, comparative illusions, and clear structural violations. Falsifier: one undifferentiated factor explains all variance.
2. Mixed-effects latent-variable model: acceptability ratings predicted by structural-violation diagnostics plus surprisal and exposure history. Falsifier: structural term adds no variance beyond processing/exposure.
3. Satiation boundary test (`§Satiation`): repeated exposure with production follow-up over weeks. Falsifier for “schema drift”: ratings rise but no productive generalization appears.
4. Rival adjudication (`§Relevance and the immune system`): items with high inferential recoverability but morphosyntactic mismatch (e.g., age-construction errors) versus inferentially hard but structurally licensed items. Falsifier: relevance-only model fits as well or better than form-value mismatch model.
