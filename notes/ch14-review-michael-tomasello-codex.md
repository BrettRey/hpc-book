**Strengths**
- The chapter has a strong, publishable core claim: grammaticality as a maintained form–value coupling rather than an axiom. The opening move in “The HPC claim” and the line “the feeling of ungrammaticality… is a noisy detector, not an oracle” are conceptually sharp and potentially field-shifting.
- You do real theoretical work separating structural meaning from truth-conditional content (the “wiring diagram” passage). That is exactly the right move if you want a usage-based yet non-reductionist account.
- “Grammaticality illusions” and parts of “Empirical signatures” are the chapter’s best evidence-facing material. Garden-path/comparative illusion asymmetry is a good fit to a detector architecture.
- The community-indexed entrenchment discussion (“language-specific… register-specific,” “entrenchment within a discourse community”) is close to what acquisition and usage-based models need: distributions, social embedding, and adaptation.

**Major Concerns**
- The mechanistic story is not yet mechanistic enough. “Zipper,” “immune system,” and “spinning top” are useful heuristics, but skeptical readers will ask: what are the explicit causal variables and update rules? Right now, “all the way down” (in “Entrenchment in action”) risks circularity: detector tracks coupling, coupling is what gets entrenched, feeling stabilizes coupling.
- “The phenomenology of coupling” is disciplined in tone, but the triadic mapping still reads post hoc. The “jolt/block/correction” mapping to sign/object/interpretant is plausible, yet not independently operationalized. Philosophers will say you are redescribing introspection, not testing architecture.
- Acceptability vs grammaticality remains under-specified. You say “acceptability’s what the detector reports; grammaticality’s the category,” but if grammaticality is itself constituted by entrenched community couplings, the line can collapse into “stable acceptability under a social prior.” You need stricter criteria for divergence cases.
- Acquisition predictions are the biggest gap. A usage-based account must predict developmental trajectories: when children show mismatch detection, how repair behavior precedes explicit judgments, how input sparsity creates persistent false negatives/false positives. Those predictions are mostly absent.
- In “Empirical signatures,” some claims look over-precise or placeholder-like (“~3-sigma absence,” schema-drift extrapolation, strong LBE universality claims). Skeptics will attack that first as rhetorical overreach before they engage your framework.

**Priority Fixes**
- Add a compact developmental mechanism subsection: item-based schemas -> analogical extension -> competition/entrenchment -> community calibration. State age-graded predictions explicitly.
- Separate three levels with causal arrows: online parser dynamics, long-run entrenchment, and normative correction ecology. Do not let “feeling” do all explanatory work.
- Reframe absolute claims (“syntax owns it,” “no amount of exposure”) into conditional, test-linked claims.
- In “The phenomenology…” keep the triadic mapping, but state what would disconfirm each register as a distinct process.

**Decisive Tests**
1. Developmental trajectory test: longitudinal children (3–9) on production, repair, and judgment for the same constructions. Prediction: repair sensitivity emerges before stable explicit ungrammaticality judgments.
2. Micro-community entrenchment experiment: train novel morphosyntactic pairing with controlled prestige/register manipulation. Prediction: cohort-structured shift first in production/comprehension, then in explicit judgments.
3. Triple-measure dissociation test for the “three registers”: online disruption (RT/eye-tracking/EEG), repair behavior, explicit norm judgment across garden paths, true violations, and register violations. Prediction: partial dissociation, not one global acceptability factor.
4. Strong falsifier: if high-frequency exposure shifts ratings but never shifts generalization to unseen items in the same construction, your “coupling maintenance” account is too weak; it is mere familiarity, not grammatical learning.
