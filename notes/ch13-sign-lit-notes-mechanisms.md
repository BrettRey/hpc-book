## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/cormier2013-pronouns-pointing-sign-languages.pdf

### Facts
- A BSL lexical-frequency study found **789 pointing signs** ambiguous in reference and/or function out of **25,000 tokens** (PDF p006 / article p235).
- ASL/BSL studies reported that plural pronominal signs are less indexical than singulars; indexical precision decreases as referent number increases (PDF p009 / article p238).
- In a BSL corpus sample (**646** non-first-person pronominal/locative signs from **211** signers), singular pronominals/determiners favored palm-sideways, while plural pronominals patterned with adverbials (palm-down) (PDF p010 / article p239).
- In Kata Kolok and IPSL, first-person plural can be expressed without a lexicalized WE form (e.g., points to included referents / transnumeral forms) (PDF p008–p009 / article p237–238).
- In Cooperrider’s corpus data, only **3%** of “I” and **6%** of “me” co-occurred with self-pointing; **48%** of self-points co-occurred with contrastive stress (PDF p013 / article p242).

### Quotations
- “pronominal signs cannot be characterised exclusively either as pronouns, or as pointing gestures” (PDF p014 / article p244).
- “language and gesture can co-exist in the same structure” (PDF p014 / article p243).

### Key Arguments (HPC-Relevant)
- The system is **hybrid-clustered**, not single-feature-defined: participant-role marking patterns with gesture, while syntactic distribution patterns with pronouns (PDF p013–p014 / article p242–244).
- A key failure pressure is **referential ambiguity** in actual corpus data, against claims of categorical unambiguity (PDF p006 / article p235).
- Cross-linguistic variation in plural/person marking (ASL/BSL vs IPSL/Kata Kolok) supports a **family-resemblance architecture** rather than strict invariant grammar (PDF p008–p009, p013 / article p237–238, 242).

- Senghas & Coppola 2010 on emergence from locative pointing in Nicaraguan Sign Language (PDF p013 / article p242).

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/lillomartin-meier2011-agreement-sign-languages.pdf

### Facts
- In a 24-minute mini-corpus, gaze did **not** reliably distinguish addressee vs non-addressee pointing for person-marking purposes (e.g., no significant difference for addressee vs non-addressed points, Fisher p=.59) (PDF p009).
- Preliminary Auslan corpus findings (116 pointing forms) also failed to support a clean second/third person gaze split (PDF p009–p010).
- Directional verbs are described as encoding person (first/non-first) and number, with multiple verb classes (PDF p012, p024).
- Cysouw’s typological survey is cited as showing first/non-first syncretism is not unique (31 languages singular; 42 non-singular; second/third blurring attested) (PDF p025).
- Referential loci are treated as discourse-stable: once a referent is associated with an R-locus, the same locus is typically reused unless motivated change occurs (PDF p026).
- Diachronic/typological variation: ABSL reported with spatial verbs but no person-agreeing verbs; some village sign systems reported without directionality; LSN cohorts show increasing spatial modulation/systematization over time (PDF p033–p034).

### Quotations
- “a sign language pronoun is equivalent to a spoken language pronoun plus a pointing gesture” (PDF p027).
- “once a referent is associated with an R-locus, the same R-locus will be used” (PDF p026).

### Key Arguments (HPC-Relevant)
- Core claim is a **language+gesture coupling** model: abstract grammatical indices + gesturally grounded loci (PDF p027).
- They defend agreement/person analyses but explicitly acknowledge non-canonical profile (e.g., object prominence, optional subject marking), which supports **clustered property diagnostics** rather than a single criterion (PDF p032, p040).
- Emergence and historical change in directional systems suggest **mechanism-driven stabilization** over time, not all-at-once categorical grammar (PDF p034, p041).

### Cross-References to Track

- Aronoff et al. 2004/2005 on modality-linked agreement analyses (PDF p018, p033).
- Senghas & Coppola 2001 on early LSN cohort changes (PDF p034).

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/perniss-ozyurek2015-visible-cohesion.pdf

### Facts
- Across DGS, German speech, and German co-speech gesture, referents were marked with more overt material in re-introduction than maintenance contexts (main context effect reported) (PDF p013).
- Overt form distributions:  
  DGS RI 66% nominal / 34% pronominal vs M 40% / 60%;  
  German RI 75% / 25% vs M 7% / 93%;  
  co-speech gesture with overt speech forms RI 89% / 11% vs M 8% / 92% (PDF p014).
- For referring expressions, co-speech gesture showed more spatial modification in RI than M, but DGS referring expressions did not show that RI>M contrast (PDF p014–p015).
- Referent-location consistency was high and iconically aligned with scene layout:  
  REs: gesture 87%, DGS 94%;  
  Predicates: gesture 94%, DGS 96% (PDF p015–p016).
- For predicates, DGS showed RI>M spatial localization (M=.49, RI=.70), while co-speech gesture predicates did not show a significant RI/M difference (PDF p015–p016).
- DGS enactment predicates were often localized (42% M, 54% RI), unlike gesture enactments (4% M, 0% RI) (PDF p016, Table 2).

### Quotations
- “visual modality must carry the full load both of identifying referents and marking referential context” (PDF p019).
- “Contributing to discourse cohesion by making referents more ‘visible’ … through spatial modification” (PDF p020).

### Key Arguments (HPC-Relevant)
- Strong evidence for a **two-cluster architecture**: quantity-based referential marking shared cross-modally, but spatial modification deployed differently in unimodal sign vs bimodal speech+gesture (PDF p018–p020).
- Mechanism-like claim: DGS can shift referential burden into predicate localization, supporting null/overt tradeoffs (PDF p018–p019).
- High spatial consistency plus iconic layout mapping supports **alignment/entrenchment mechanisms** in discourse tracking (PDF p015–p016, p019).

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/morgan2002-complex-verb-bsl.pdf

### Facts
- Comprehension of AB constructions rises with age: youngest group (3;2–5;11) 40/40%; middle (6;0–8;11) 60/80%; oldest (9;0–12;0) 90/90% on two target sentences (PDF p016, Table 2).
- Production shows strong early fragmentation: youngest group produced sole B-part in 90% and full AB in 0%; oldest group full AB in 70% (PDF p016, Table 3).
- Developmental shift in B/AB production pattern is significant (χ²=13.3, df=2, p<.001) (PDF p016–p017).
- Simple transitive production reached 100% in oldest group, but AB production remained lower (70%), with persistent non-manual marking deficits (PDF p017, p019).
- Authors report prolonged non-manual/perspective-shift development and continued non-adult-like profiles even in older children (PDF p019).

### Quotations
- “the developmental problem lies not in understanding the event but in getting the complete conceptual-linguistic mapping” (PDF p018).
- “full structure not seen in its adult form until after 9;0” (PDF p002, abstract).

### Key Arguments (HPC-Relevant)
- Clear failure mode evidence: children default to partial constructions (B-part-only) under perspective-shift load, consistent with **cluster fragility under integration pressure** (PDF p017–p020).
- Proposed mechanism: difficulty integrating manual argument structure + non-manual perspective markers in a single mapping (PDF p020).
- Supports modality-sensitive but not modality-isolated development: abstract mapping principles plus modality-specific expression demands (PDF p021).

### Cross-References to Track

- Anderson & Reilly 1998 on non-manual morphology timelines (PDF p008, p020).

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/meier2002-modality-structure-sample.pdf

### Facts
- The chapter explicitly frames signed languages as test cases for modality effects/non-effects on linguistic structure (PDF p011).
- Shared architecture claims (tabled): conventional vocabularies, duality of patterning, productivity, syntactic structure, agreement/word-order tradeoffs, acquisition timing, lateralization parallels (PDF p012).
- Modality-linked processing/typology fact: ASL sign-rate is slower than English word-rate, but proposition-rate can be comparable; in one comparison ASL averaged 1.5s/proposition vs SEE 2.8s/proposition (PDF p018).
- Signed languages are argued to favor nonconcatenative morphology; sequential affixes are comparatively rare (PDF p026).
- SEE-exposed deaf children reportedly innovated directional verb modulations despite nonspatial instructional input (PDF p027).
- Candidate typological asymmetries: first/non-first person patterning and object-agreement prominence in sign (PDF p028–p029).
- Signed languages are argued to show relative typological uniformity compared with spoken-language diversity (PDF p029–p030).

### Quotations
- “what are the effects and non-effects of modality upon linguistic structure?” (PDF p011).
- “signed and spoken languages share many properties” (PDF p012).

### Key Arguments (HPC-Relevant)
- Provides explicit **modality-neutral vs modality-sensitive decomposition** useful for HPC framing: some properties robust across substrate, others channel-conditioned (PDF p011–p016).
- Offers causal candidate set (articulators, perception, iconic/indexic affordances, language age/history), useful as **mechanism cluster** inputs (PDF p016, Table 1.2).
- Emphasizes possible historical pathways from gesture to linguistic structure in sign emergence (PDF p022–p023, p027).

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/goldin-meadow2018-sign-gesture-coming-of-age.pdf

### Facts
- The review’s core claim: signers produce imagistic gestures alongside categorical signs, and sign should be compared to speech+gesture (PDF p001, p014).
- Agreement/indicating verbs in sign are highlighted as problematic for strictly spoken-language-style agreement analyses because loci are not finite/listable and forms vary with referents (PDF p007).
- Cross-linguistic/silent-gesture comparison: handshape patterns differed across ASL/Auslan/TSL and silent gesturers, but motion/location were more similar across groups (PDF p008; Schembri et al. evidence).
- Categorical perception/neural evidence: ASL signers showed categorical perception for hand configuration but not place-of-articulation; neuroimaging linked handshape variation more directly to left language regions than location/motion variation (PDF p008).
- Gesture-sign mismatch predicts learning in deaf signers similarly to gesture-speech mismatch in hearing speakers (e.g., 65% vs 22% post-instruction success by mismatch level) (PDF p013).

### Quotations
- “signers gesture just as speakers do” (PDF p001).
- “sign should not be compared with speech – it should be compared with speech-plus-gesture” (PDF p014).

### Key Arguments (HPC-Relevant)
- Strong support for **mixed categorical+imagistic clustering** within sign systems, not a single formal criterion (PDF p007–p008, p014–p015).
- Mechanism emphasis: representational mismatch effects in learning suggest complementary channels/features jointly sustain performance (PDF p013–p014).
- Developmental trajectory argument: silent gesture and homesign show graded emergence of linguistic properties, consistent with **continuum/entrenchment** rather than sharp boundaries (PDF p014–p015).

### Cross-References to Track
- Liddell 2003 and Lillo-Martin & Meier 2011 on agreement/indicating debates (PDF p007, p013).
- Schembri et al. 2005; Emmorey et al. 2003/2013 on handshape vs motion/location evidence (PDF p008).
- Duncan 2005 and Goldin-Meadow et al. 2012 for co-sign gesture integration and learning prediction (PDF p013–p014).
