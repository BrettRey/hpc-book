**Strengths**
- The chapter has a clear target and a strong organizing claim: in `The HPC claim`, grammaticality is the coupling between morphosyntactic form and “structural meaning,” and in `The stabilizer: a feeling` the judgment signal is explicitly demoted from oracle to detector. That is the right move for a methodology-facing chapter.
- The dissociation logic is well chosen. `Grammaticality illusions` uses both directions of error (“feels ungrammatical, is grammatical” and “feels grammatical, is ungrammatical”), which is exactly the kind of pattern a detection architecture should explain.
- The chapter repeatedly tries to constrain itself (for example, `The phenomenology of coupling` says phenomenology is “readout… not evidence independent of it”). That is methodologically mature, even when execution slips.
- You do attempt risky language in places (the `Definiteness thread` has a scoped prediction + falsifier). That is publishability-positive.

**Major Concerns**
- The account is not yet mechanistic enough for skeptics. “Zipper,” “immune system,” and “spinning top” are useful heuristics, but the chapter often substitutes metaphor for algorithm. In `The stabilizer: a feeling` you say the detector is “Bayesian,” but there is no explicit data-generating model: priors over what units, likelihood from what parser state, and what decision rule maps to acceptability ratings?
- The acceptability-to-grammaticality inference is still too fast. In `Satiation`, claims like “the category itself moves” and “schema drift in real time” overreach relative to standard AJ data, which are notoriously task-sensitive. Rating shifts alone do not identify movement in grammatical representation versus decision criterion shifts, adaptation to task pragmatics, or memory familiarity.
- `Three registers` (jolt/block/correction) is interesting but currently under-operationalized. Right now it reads as post-hoc phenomenological labeling, not a testable mapping to independent measures. A skeptical philosopher of language will call this introspective taxonomy without sufficient bridge principles.
- Some empirical phrasing is over-precise placeholder language (“~3-sigma absence,” “categorically rejected regardless of exposure”) without showing the statistical model, sampling frame, or boundary conditions. That invites immediate methodological attack.
- What a skeptical specialist rejects first: the claim in `Value at every grain` that “syntax owns” grammaticality plus the extension to “grammaticality as a species of appropriateness.” Many will see this as category creep unless you sharply separate morphosyntactic ill-formedness from social/register sanctions in measurement terms.

**Priority Fixes**
1. Add an explicit measurement model: latent grammaticality state, detector noise, task decision layer. State which observable (rating, RT, repair, confidence) identifies which component.
2. Recast strong representational claims as hypotheses unless supported by converging evidence beyond ratings.
3. In `Phenomenology`, tie each register to at least one operational metric (e.g., jolt = early disruption cost; block = increased repair/disfluency; correction = stable repair preference).
4. Tighten claims about satiation/LBE with clear scope conditions and known heterogeneity across labs/tasks.
5. Separate `appropriateness` from `grammaticality` with explicit diagnostic criteria, not just prose distinctions.

**Decisive Tests**
1. **Process-dissociation AJ design**: cross grammatical status (licensed/violating) with processing load (easy/garden-path) under speeded vs unspeeded judgments. Detector-noise view predicts load-sensitive false alarms; latent grammatical contrasts should persist in unspeeded conditions.
2. **Satiation adjudication test**: repeated exposure plus transfer to novel items and production choices. If grammar/schema shifts, transfer should occur; if criterion shift only, effects should stay task-local.
3. **Triadic-register validation**: preregistered multimethod study linking “jolt/block/correction” to distinct signatures (online processing, disfluency/repair behavior, offline correction convergence).
4. **Relevance vs zipper discrimination**: construct items where inferential relevance is high but morphosyntax mismatches, versus low relevance but grammatical structure. Competing theories make different predictions on acceptability and correction behavior.
