# Chapter 12: Editorial Report

*Pro-form Gender*

---

## 1. Argument

The chapter's central claim -- that English gender is a system-wide personhood partition operating across all pro-forms, not just a he/she/it pronoun distinction -- is well-motivated and successfully defended. The move from a narrow pronominal description to a full inventory (Table 12.x) is the chapter's strongest contribution: it reframes gender as an HPC coupling between a semantic cluster (personhood) and a lexico-grammatical cluster (the pro-form inventory), and the three arguments against item-by-item presuppositions (bidirectional inference, chain coherence, boundary cases tracking a shared dimension) in section 12.4.4 are tight and persuasive. One logical gap: the chapter claims early on that "violations aren't just semantic infelicities; they're grammatical errors" (end of section 12.1), but the evidence for this is largely introspective judgment on constructed examples. The chapter would benefit from a sentence acknowledging the methodological status of this claim, since Chapter 14 later treats the grammaticality/acceptability distinction as itself requiring mechanistic analysis.

## 2. Structure

The chapter's organization is clean and mirrors the Ch 9/Ch 10 template effectively: puzzle, hierarchy, designatum-driven mechanism, inventory, coupling/maintenance, evidence/limits, transition. The "What the HPC framing buys" subsection (section 12.4.4) is excellently placed -- it arrives just after the coupling architecture is laid out but before the evidence section, so the sceptic's objection is addressed at the moment of greatest vulnerability. One structural question: the "Evidence and limits" section (section 12.5) is doing double duty as both cross-linguistic evidence and slippage diagnostics. The slippage subsections (boundary designata, number interference, competition, anchoring, constructional interference) amount to nearly half the chapter's length and could be their own section rather than subsections of "Evidence and limits." The current nesting buries some of the chapter's most original material (especially the constructional-interference analysis of demonstratives) under a heading that suggests secondary status.

## 3. Prose

The writing is at its best in the opening puzzle -- the dog examples land immediately and the "same dog; same tail" parenthetical is a model of the book's self-aware voice. The designatum section (section 12.3) is crisp and well-paced; the three-way designatum/referent/antecedent distinction is handled with admirable economy. The maintenance-spectrum section (section 12.5.1) comparing English, French, and German is effective, though the rhythm flags slightly in the slippage subsections: the pattern of "consider X / the coupling predicts Y / this is Z" repeats across five subsections in quick succession. The constructional-interference subsection on demonstratives (section 12.5.5) recovers momentum because the analysis is genuinely surprising, but the preceding subsections on number interference (section 12.5.2) and competition from other pressures (section 12.5.3) feel like they could be compressed into a single subsection. The much/count-mass paragraph in section 12.5.2 is flagged as possibly epiphenomenal; if so, it could be shortened or folded into a footnote rather than occupying a full paragraph that ends without resolution.

## 4. Evidence

The inventory table (Table 12.x) is the chapter's strongest piece of evidence and a genuine contribution to the descriptive literature. The cross-linguistic data from Haspelmath (1997) on the who/what universal and Siemund (2008) on dialectal variation are well-chosen and do real work. The Spanish/French interlinear glosses effectively demonstrate the designatum-driven vs. antecedent-driven contrast. But the chapter's ratio of predicted-to-confirmed evidence is the highest in Part III. The three "empirical footprints" (children assigning pro-forms to robots based on ToM; novel entities rapidly assigned pro-forms; gender repairs faster than number repairs) are stated as predictions but none is evidenced. The chain-coherence effects, which carry substantial theoretical weight (they are the main argument against item-by-item presuppositions), rest entirely on constructed examples and introspective judgments. The chapter hedges honestly ("if these judgments hold up"), but this is a lot of weight on a thin empirical base for a chapter in the fourth case-study position, where the book's evidential gradient is supposed to be deliberately weakening.

## 5. Cross-chapter coherence

The chapter connects well to the coupling continuum from Ch 8, the Peircean apparatus from Ch 7, and the parallel architectures of Ch 9 (countability) and Ch 10 (definiteness). The transition to Ch 13 is clean: the four-case-study summary in section 12.6 sets up the zipper synthesis effectively. Two cross-chapter issues: (1) Ch 11 promises that Ch 12 will address "gender diffusing beyond the pronoun system" as a prediction from the pronoun-braid analysis, but Ch 12 doesn't explicitly pick up this thread -- the diffusion claim is implicit in the extended inventory but never named as such. A sentence cross-referencing Ch 11's prediction would close the loop. (2) The book-level editorial report notes that Ch 12's promissory predictions include "Theory-of-Mind robot experiments and chain-coherence corpus tests," and these do appear in the empirical-footprint passages, but they are never collected into a falsification or defeat-conditions section comparable to those in Chs 9 and 10. Given that Ch 16 will need to build a synthesis ledger, an explicit predictions subsection would help.

## 6. House style issues

**Judgment markers on numbered examples.** Every numbered example in the chapter uses `\ea[*]` -- the asterisk marker denoting ungrammaticality -- regardless of the example's actual grammatical status. Examples (3)--(6), (7)--(10), (11)--(12), (14)--(16), (17)--(18), (20)--(21), (22)--(24), (26)--(27), (28)--(30), (31)--(33), (34)--(35), (37), (38)--(40), and (41)--(43) are all marked `[*]`. Most of these are grammatical sentences being used as positive illustrations. The `[*]` should be `[]` (empty brackets, i.e., grammatical) for the majority; only examples presented as ungrammatical under their intended construal should carry `\ungram{}` or `[*]`. This is a systematic error affecting approximately 35 examples. **Fix all instances: use `[]` for grammatical examples, `[\ungram{}]` for ungrammatical ones, `[\marg{}]` for marginal.**

**En-dash vs. tilde-dash.** The style guide specifies `~-- ` (non-breaking space before en-dash, regular space after) for parenthetical asides. The chapter uses this convention correctly in most places but inconsistently: some instances use `~--` without a trailing space (e.g., line 23: `three-way distinction~-- masculine, feminine, neuter~-- realized`), which appears correct, but others break the pattern. One instance at line 253: `one's} (generic personal) followed by` -- this passage uses parentheses rather than dashes for a parenthetical that might better match surrounding style. Minor; no action needed unless consistency pass is done.

**`\term{}` vs. `\mention{}` usage.** Generally correct throughout. The chapter appropriately uses `\term{}` for technical concepts being introduced (designatum, personhood cluster, semantic transparency, mechanistic drift, chain-internal coherence, constructionally conditioned, etc.) and `\mention{}` for cited linguistic forms. One edge case: "pro-form inventory" appears both as `\term{pro-form inventory}` (line 268) and without markup in running text. If this is a technical term of art in the chapter's framework, it should be consistently marked on first use and then left unmarked.

**Quotation marks.** `\enquote{}` is used correctly throughout for direct quotations from sources. No bare double-quote violations found.

**Example cross-references.** Correctly formatted with `(\ref{ex:...})` throughout.

**Footnotes.** Two footnotes; both are well-placed and substantive. No style issues.

## 7. Specific recommendations

1. **Fix judgment markers on all numbered examples.** Replace `[*]` with `[]` on every grammatical example (approximately 35 instances). Reserve `[*]` or `[\ungram{}]` for examples presented as ungrammatical. This is the most urgent fix; the current markup is misleading and would produce asterisks in the compiled PDF on examples that are perfectly grammatical.

2. **Add a predictions/defeat-conditions subsection.** After section 12.5.3 ("Passing the tests") and before the slippage subsections, or at the end of the chapter before "Looking forward," add a short subsection collecting the three empirical footprints into explicit predictions with named defeat conditions. This parallels the falsification sections in Chs 9 and 10 and gives Ch 16's synthesis ledger something to point to.

3. **Promote the slippage material to a top-level section.** Currently sections 12.5.4 through 12.5.5.5 (boundary designata through constructional interference) are subsections of "Evidence and limits." Elevate them to a section titled something like "Where the clusters slip" (which is already the subsection title at line 371). This gives the constructional-interference analysis of demonstratives -- one of the chapter's most original moves -- appropriate visibility.

4. **Compress the number-interference and competition-from-other-pressures subsections.** Sections 12.5.2 and 12.5.3 both illustrate how other grammatical systems can loosen the personhood-pro-form coupling, and neither is long. Merging them into a single subsection ("Interference from other systems") would tighten the pacing in the middle of the chapter where repetition sets in.

5. **Close the loop with Ch 11's prediction.** Add a sentence, likely in section 12.1 or 12.4.2 ("The coupling"), explicitly acknowledging Ch 11's prediction that gender would diffuse beyond the pronoun system. Something like: "Chapter 11 predicted that the pronoun-braid analysis would extend to a wider inventory; this chapter confirms that prediction."

6. **Address the `much` question or demote it.** The paragraph on `much` (lines 400--401) raises the question of whether `much`'s non-personal restriction is gender or count/mass semantics, then leaves it unresolved. Either commit to a position (the epiphenomenal reading seems more likely and the chapter seems to lean that way) or move the observation to a footnote. As it stands, it introduces a complication and then walks away from it.

7. **Strengthen the chain-coherence evidence.** The chain-coherence argument is central to the HPC case (it distinguishes coupled clusters from independent presuppositions) but rests entirely on constructed examples. Even a brief note acknowledging that corpus or experimental confirmation would be needed -- and what it would look like -- would protect the argument from a reviewer's objection that the key evidence is introspective. The "if these judgments hold up" hedge in section 12.5.4 is honest but could be sharper about what "holding up" would require.

8. **Consider adding a brief note on singular `they` and system change.** The chapter mentions singular `they` several times (lines 131, 292, 411) but never draws out the implication that the system is actively changing. Since the book's framework is designed to handle diachronic dynamics, a paragraph noting that the spread of singular `they` as a personal epicene pronoun is a live perturbation -- and what the HPC framework predicts about its trajectory -- would be a natural addition and would connect to Ch 16's emphasis on observable change.
