# Book-Level Editorial Report

*Based on precis of all 17 chapters. This is a structural and argumentative assessment, not a line-level edit.*

*Updated after the Ch 16 split (Feb/Mar 2026): old Ch 16 ("What Changes" / gauntlet) split into Ch 16 ("How categories travel") + Ch 17 ("The gauntlet").*

---

## 1. The arc works

The book's argument builds cleanly across four parts. Part I (Chs 1--3) earns the reader's attention by showing that the category problem is real, that essentialism caused it, and that nominalism doesn't fix it. Part II (Chs 4--8) delivers the theoretical apparatus with genuine philosophical depth. Part III (Chs 9--13) deploys the apparatus on increasingly challenging cases. Part IV (Chs 14--17) turns the framework on itself (grammaticality as HPC), adds the missing social layer, demonstrates that cross-domain extension falls out of the framework, and lays down falsification conditions. The promises made in early chapters are, overwhelmingly, kept in later ones.

The Ch 16 split has strengthened the ending. The old single-chapter "What Changes" was flagged as thin on practical methodology and heavy on promissory content. Now Ch 16 ("How categories travel") develops a constructive argument about cross-domain extension and cognitive linguistics engagement, while Ch 17 ("The gauntlet") handles predictions and defeat conditions with two worked cases instead of one. The old report's central recommendation -- give the closing more room -- has been implemented.

This is a well-architected book.

## 2. Chapter-order discrepancy

The chapter outline lists Ch 6 as "Projectibility and the good bet" and Ch 7 as "The Stabilisers." The actual book reverses this: Ch 6 is the mechanisms chapter, Ch 7 is the projectibility chapter. The actual order is better (you need the mechanisms before you can talk about their epistemic payoff), but the outline should be updated. **[Status: CHAPTER_OUTLINE.md now lists these correctly as of the Feb 2026 update.]**

## 3. Part IV's four chapters

The outline originally specified two Part IV chapters (14: grammaticality, 15: what changes). The book now has four: Ch 14 (grammaticality), Ch 15 (social stabilisation), Ch 16 (how categories travel), Ch 17 (the gauntlet). The additions are well-motivated:

- **Ch 15 (social stabilisation)** fills a genuine gap -- the inter-speaker question that Parts II-III's individual-level mechanisms left open. The question of whether it belongs in Part II or Part IV remains valid, but the pacing argument is sound: Part II was already five chapters.

- **Ch 16 (how categories travel)** is the strongest addition. It engages cognitive linguistics (the nearest tradition) with Rapoport's Rules, develops Boyd's theory-constitutive metaphor, and predicts that cross-domain extension should track mechanism density. The Boyd circle (the framework predicts the capacity it uses) adds explanatory coherence without circularity. This chapter turns what was implicit throughout the book into an explicit theoretical contribution.

- **Ch 17 (the gauntlet)** now has room to develop two worked cases (*data* + pro-form gender) rather than one, which addresses the old report's central criticism. The research agenda section collects twelve open fronts honestly.

## 4. The evidential gradient in Part III is a feature, not a bug -- but needs flagging

Part III is deliberately designed so that the strongest case (countability, Ch 9) comes first and each subsequent case is weaker: definiteness (Ch 10) has looser coupling, lexical categories (Ch 11) have variable mechanism density, gender (Ch 12) has promissory predictions. This is smart architecture -- it shows the framework handling diminishing returns, not cherry-picking. But the book never *tells* the reader this is the design. Without a sentence or two in the Part III introduction (or the Ch 9 opening) saying "we start with the tightest case and deliberately loosen," a reader might wonder why the evidence gets thinner as the book progresses.

## 5. Recurring structural issues

### 5a. The orphaned Parallel Architecture paragraph

Flagged in both Ch 1 and Ch 3. Culicover and Jackendoff's Parallel Architecture appears in both chapters without being integrated into the argument. This needs either a clean home (probably Ch 3, where it fits the "existing approaches that have the right instincts" survey) or removal. **[Status: TODO comment added to Ch 1 L67; decision pending.]**

### 5b. The Peircean apparatus: earned or imposed?

Peirce enters in Ch 3 (cable metaphor, interpretant), develops in Ch 7 (would-be, correlation vs mediation, habit = projection), and is used substantively in Chs 9--14. The Ch 14 precis flags that the triadic mapping to phenomenological registers "may feel imposed rather than earned." The Ch 13 precis flags that the Peircean mediation-gradient section "restates the coupling continuum in sign-theoretic vocabulary but does not obviously add explanatory content." These are two independent flags pointing at the same worry: the Peirce might sometimes be relabelling rather than explaining. Worth a careful pass asking, at each deployment: does the Peircean vocabulary do work that the HPC/coupling vocabulary alone cannot?

Ch 16's identification of Peirce's cable metaphor as a theory-constitutive metaphor (SS16.4) is one of the better Peirce deployments: it adds genuine content (the cable is an instance of the capacity being theorised).

### 5c. The coupling coda in Ch 8

Multiple precis note that the coupling continuum (hard/loose/composite), introduced as a "coda" to the failure-modes chapter, is substantial enough to feel like a distinct theoretical contribution. Ch 13 builds its entire argument on it. This material might deserve its own section heading or a more explicit framing as "the framework's last theoretical commitment before the case studies."

## 6. Promissory content accumulates -- but is now collected

Almost every chapter makes predictions it does not test. The old report flagged this as a growing problem. Ch 17's research agenda section (SS17.5) now collects twelve open fronts in a single honest ledger, which directly addresses the concern. The IOUs are no longer scattered; they're catalogued with enough specificity that a reader can assess whether the debts are genuine research questions or evasions. This is a significant improvement.

The remaining concern is the asymmetry between degradation and genesis predictions. The gauntlet's three predictions include one constructive prediction (phase transition in grammaticalization), but it receives less development than the two degradation predictions (asymmetric fraying, sticky-lock drift). The book has said more about category death than category birth. The budding account (Ch 17, SS17.2) is a genuine contribution but remains more schematic than the degradation cases.

## 8. The discourse level is still missing

Ch 13's summary table includes discourse as a tier in the zipper but gives it no section and no evidence. Ch 17's research agenda acknowledges this (SS17.5, "The category zipper" paragraph: "If the framework claims scale-invariance, skipping discourse is conspicuous"). The acknowledgment is honest, but the gap remains. Either add a section (even a brief negative case) or explicitly scope the book's claims to exclude discourse in a more prominent location than the research agenda.

## 9. Cross-chapter terminology is mostly consistent

The precis show stable use of: stabiliser, coupling (hard/loose/composite), projectibility, field-relative, failure modes (thin/fat/negative), the two-diagnostic test, the HPC slogan, theory-constitutive metaphor, cross-domain extension, Boyd circle. One potential inconsistency: Ch 6 and Ch 7 both use "stabiliser" and "mechanism" somewhat interchangeably. The book made a terminological choice early (Ch 6 introduces "stabiliser" explicitly), but if the terms are meant to be distinguished, the distinction should be enforced.

New Ch 16 vocabulary (theory-constitutive metaphor, cross-domain extension, functional domain) is introduced with `\term{}` and used consistently.

## 10. What's working well

- **The running examples are excellent.** *otherwise*, *fun*, *cattle*, *data*, the verbless clause, *be like*, *whose*, the trail -- these recur across chapters and give the reader anchors.
- **The confessional voice.** Reynolds's willingness to admit his own essentialist habits (Ch 1), evidential asymmetries (Ch 6), and ugly terminology (Ch 10's "deitality") builds trust.
- **The deliberate weakening across Part III.** Starting with the strongest case and loosening is the right move -- it shows integrity.
- **The failure modes.** Ch 8 is the chapter that makes the framework falsifiable, and it does its job. The negative cases in Ch 13 extend this well.
- **The social stabilisation chapter.** Ch 15 fills a real gap and the mixed-bin diagnostic is genuinely useful.
- **The cognitive linguistics engagement.** Ch 16's Rapoport's Rules treatment of cognitive linguistics is one of the book's most generous and productive engagements with an existing tradition. The trail example is memorable and well-argued.
- **The two-case gauntlet.** Ch 17 now develops both *data* (degradation) and pro-form gender (tight maintenance) at comparable resolution, addressing the old report's central criticism.
- **The Boyd circle.** Ch 16's reflexivity argument (the framework predicts the capacity it uses) adds explanatory coherence without circularity. This is a genuine philosophical contribution.

## 11. Structural recommendation

The old report recommended giving the closing more room. The Ch 16/17 split has done this effectively. The remaining structural recommendations are:

1. **Close the Huddleston and Bell threads in Ch 17.** Two sentences total. See SS7 above.
2. **Develop the phase-transition prediction.** Ch 17's third prediction (constructive: genesis) needs a worked sketch comparable to the *data* and gender cases.
3. **Flag the Part III evidential gradient.** One sentence at the start of Part III explaining the deliberate tightest-to-loosest ordering.
4. **Resolve the Jackendoff paragraph** in Ch 1 (move, footnote, or cut).
5. **Address the discourse gap** in Ch 13 more prominently than the Ch 17 research agenda.
