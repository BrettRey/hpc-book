# Book-Level Editorial Report

*Based on précis of all 16 chapters. This is a structural and argumentative assessment, not a line-level edit.*

---

## 1. The arc works

The book's argument builds cleanly across four parts. Part I (Chs 1--3) earns the reader's attention by showing that the category problem is real, that essentialism caused it, and that nominalism doesn't fix it. Part II (Chs 4--8) delivers the theoretical apparatus with genuine philosophical depth. Part III (Chs 9--13) deploys the apparatus on increasingly challenging cases. Part IV (Chs 14--16) turns the framework on itself (grammaticality as HPC), adds the missing social layer, and lays down falsification conditions. The promises made in early chapters are, overwhelmingly, kept in later ones.

This is a well-architected book.

## 2. Chapter-order discrepancy

The chapter outline lists Ch 6 as "Projectibility and the good bet" and Ch 7 as "The Stabilisers." The actual book reverses this: Ch 6 is the mechanisms chapter, Ch 7 is the projectibility chapter. The actual order is better (you need the mechanisms before you can talk about their epistemic payoff), but the outline should be updated.

## 3. Part IV's extra chapter

The outline specifies two Part IV chapters (14: grammaticality, 15: what changes). The book has three: Ch 14 (grammaticality), Ch 15 (social stabilisation), Ch 16 (what changes). The addition is well-motivated -- the inter-speaker question was a genuine gap -- but it has two consequences:

- **Ch 15 may belong in Part II, not Part IV.** Social stabilisation is part of the theoretical apparatus, not an "implication." If mechanisms are in Ch 6 and projectibility is in Ch 7, why is social maintenance in Ch 15? The answer is presumably pacing (Part II was already five chapters), but a reader might wonder why the social layer wasn't part of the fix.

- **Ch 16 gets compressed.** The "what changes" chapter -- arguably the one most readers will remember -- is flagged as thin on practical methodological advice. Three paragraphs of fieldwork and study-design gestures after 15 chapters of theory risks a soft landing. (More on this below.)

## 4. The evidential gradient in Part III is a feature, not a bug -- but needs flagging

Part III is deliberately designed so that the strongest case (countability, Ch 9) comes first and each subsequent case is weaker: definiteness (Ch 10) has looser coupling, lexical categories (Ch 11) have variable mechanism density, gender (Ch 12) has promissory predictions. This is smart architecture -- it shows the framework handling diminishing returns, not cherry-picking. But the book never *tells* the reader this is the design. Without a sentence or two in the Part III introduction (or the Ch 9 opening) saying "we start with the tightest case and deliberately loosen," a reader might wonder why the evidence gets thinner as the book progresses.

## 5. Recurring structural issues

### 5a. The orphaned Parallel Architecture paragraph

Flagged in both Ch 1 and Ch 3. Culicover and Jackendoff's Parallel Architecture appears in both chapters without being integrated into the argument. This needs either a clean home (probably Ch 3, where it fits the "existing approaches that have the right instincts" survey) or removal.

### 5b. The Peircean apparatus: earned or imposed?

Peirce enters in Ch 3 (cable metaphor, interpretant), develops in Ch 7 (would-be, correlation vs mediation, habit = projection), and is used substantively in Chs 9--14. The Ch 14 précis flags that the triadic mapping to phenomenological registers "may feel imposed rather than earned." The Ch 13 précis flags that the Peircean mediation-gradient section "restates the coupling continuum in sign-theoretic vocabulary but does not obviously add explanatory content." These are two independent flags pointing at the same worry: the Peirce might sometimes be relabelling rather than explaining. Worth a careful pass asking, at each deployment: does the Peircean vocabulary do work that the HPC/coupling vocabulary alone cannot?

### 5c. The coupling coda in Ch 8

Multiple précis note that the coupling continuum (hard/loose/composite), introduced as a "coda" to the failure-modes chapter, is substantial enough to feel like a distinct theoretical contribution. Ch 13 builds its entire argument on it. This material might deserve its own section heading or a more explicit framing as "the framework's last theoretical commitment before the case studies."

## 6. Promissory content accumulates

Almost every chapter makes predictions it does not test. This is appropriate for a theory book, and individually each promissory note is fine. But by Ch 16, the reader has been promised:

- Classifier-language predictions for countability (Ch 9)
- Prosodic rescue and acquisition-order tests for definiteness (Ch 10)
- A falsifier for the adjective-plumage diagnosis (Ch 11)
- Theory-of-Mind robot experiments and chain-coherence corpus tests for gender (Ch 12)
- Discourse-level evidence for the zipper (Ch 13)
- Satiation and schema-drift experiments for grammaticality (Ch 14)
- Conditioned-vs-pooled modelling for social stabilisation (Ch 15)
- The entire gauntlet (Ch 16)

That is a lot of IOUs. The book would benefit from a moment of honest accounting -- perhaps in Ch 16 -- that collects these into a single research agenda rather than leaving them scattered. (The synthesis ledger in Table 16.1 may already do this; if so, it should be cross-referenced more explicitly.)

## 7. The "what changes" problem

Ch 16 is the weakest landing in the book. The précis identifies three issues:

1. **Heavy reliance on *data* as the sole worked example.** A second case at comparable resolution would demonstrate that the gauntlet generalises.
2. **The Ladyman and Ross ontological claim gets gestural treatment.** If scale-dependent ontology is the book's philosophical punchline, it needs more than an epigraph and an echo.
3. **Practical methodology is thin.** The title promises "what changes"; the chapter delivers predictions and defeat conditions (which is valuable) but not a worked illustration of what a linguist would do differently on Monday. A single "before/after" research vignette -- "here's how you'd study X under essentialism, and here's how HPC changes the design" -- would do enormous work.

## 8. The discourse level is missing

Ch 13's summary table includes discourse as a tier in the zipper but gives it no section and no evidence. This is a genuine omission. If the framework claims scale-invariance from phonemes to constructions, skipping discourse (speech acts, conversational structure, genre) is conspicuous. Either add a section (even a brief one with a negative case showing why discourse categories are hard to maintain as HPCs) or explicitly scope the book's claims to exclude it.

## 9. Cross-chapter terminology is mostly consistent

The précis show stable use of: stabiliser, coupling (hard/loose/composite), projectibility, field-relative, failure modes (thin/fat/negative), the two-diagnostic test, the HPC slogan. One potential inconsistency: Ch 6 and Ch 7 both use "stabiliser" and "mechanism" somewhat interchangeably. The book made a terminological choice early (Ch 6 introduces "stabiliser" explicitly), but if the terms are meant to be distinguished, the distinction should be enforced.

## 10. What's working well

- **The running examples are excellent.** *otherwise*, *fun*, *cattle*, *data*, the verbless clause, *be like*, *whose* -- these recur across chapters and give the reader anchors.
- **The confessional voice.** Reynolds's willingness to admit his own essentialist habits (Ch 1), evidential asymmetries (Ch 6), and ugly terminology (Ch 10's "deitality") builds trust.
- **The deliberate weakening across Part III.** Starting with the strongest case and loosening is the right move -- it shows integrity.
- **The failure modes.** Ch 8 is the chapter that makes the framework falsifiable, and it does its job. The negative cases in Ch 13 extend this well.
- **The social stabilisation chapter.** Ch 15 fills a real gap and the mixed-bin diagnostic is genuinely useful.

## 11. Structural recommendation

If I could change one thing about the book's structure, it would be to give Ch 16 more room. The gauntlet is the book's promise to the reader that this isn't just-so storytelling. Right now it reads as a prospectus. One fully worked gauntlet run -- not *data* as a sketch, but a complete audit of a category the reader hasn't seen before, using the seven-step template from Ch 8 -- would transform the ending from promissory to demonstrated. This doesn't need to be long (2,000--3,000 words), but it would make the book's close as strong as its opening.
