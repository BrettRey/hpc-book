# Chapter 14: Grammaticality itself

Chapter 14 is the centrepiece of Part IV. It makes the book's deepest and most recursive claim: grammaticality is not a primitive of linguistic theory but is itself an HPC kind -- a maintained coupling between morphosyntactic form and structural meaning. The chapter calls this coupling the "zipper": morphosyntax provides one set of teeth, structural meaning the other, and grammaticality is what it feels like when the zipper closes. An ungrammatical sentence is a zipper that won't mesh.

The argument begins by distinguishing "structural meaning" -- the subcompositional wiring diagram that grammar contributes before lexical content fills the slots -- from compositional semantics, contextual felicity, and pragmatic inference. Dependency relations, argument structure, temporal anchoring, clause type, and information structure are all structural meaning; Chomsky's "Colorless green ideas sleep furiously" has perfect wiring and absurd components, which is why it feels strange but not ungrammatical. With this established, the HPC claim follows: grammaticality is the coupling between form and this structural semantics, and the coupling is maintained by a feeling of ungrammaticality that acts as the "immune system" of the grammar -- a noisy detector, not an oracle.

The chapter then develops a five-part mechanism sketch (coupling state, processing channel, detector output, decision threshold, entrenchment update) that separates token-time parsing from individual entrenchment from community norm dynamics. This separation yields identification strategies: if processing load rises while coupling is stable, we should see transient detector spikes without durable shifts in repair norms; if coupling weakens, repair preferences should shift durably.

A phenomenology section maps the experience of ungrammaticality onto three registers -- a pre-reflective qualitative jolt, reactive resistance (hesitation and repair), and normative correction (the stabilised habit reasserting itself) -- connected to the Peircean triadic architecture of Chapter 7 (sign, object, interpretant). Table 14.1 operationalises each register with timescales, observable markers, confounds, and falsifiers. The chapter is careful to mark these as readout of mechanism, not evidence independent of it.

Grammaticality illusions provide the key evidence. Garden-path sentences ("The horse raced past the barn fell") feel ungrammatical but are grammatical; comparative illusions ("More people have been to Russia than I have") feel grammatical but are not. Phillips, Wagers, and Lau (2011) are cited for the concept of "selective fallibility" -- the comprehension system's systematic vulnerability to some constraints and not others. Satiation evidence (Snyder 2000, 2022) shows that repeated exposure improves acceptability for some constraints but not others; left-branch extraction remains near-categorically rejected, which Reynolds (2026) attributes to the density of entrenched workarounds (pied-piping, fused heads, the "big mess" construction). Vagnino and Walker (2026) supply "schema drift" as a mechanism for how satiation might work: the category itself deforms, not just the input representation. Dabrowska (2012) provides evidence of systematic individual differences tracking educational and exposure ecologies.

A crucial move locates grammaticality as a species of appropriateness narrowed to the morphosyntactic channel. Register fit, accent, lexical precision, and pragmatic felicity are all form-value couplings with their own detectors and failure modes; the asterisk probes only one of them. Two operational tests distinguish grammaticality from other appropriateness channels: the minimal repair must require morphosyntactic change, and speakers in the relevant discourse community must show convergent repair preferences.

The chapter's most architecturally important claim is the dual-role thesis: grammaticality is simultaneously a category (the HPC coupling) and a mechanism (the stabiliser for all other grammatical categories). Nouns are maintained partly because nounhood is grammatical -- using a word in noun slots triggers no mismatch signal. The analogy is to a spinning top: both the object and the spin that keeps it upright.

Tensions are few but real. The three-register phenomenology is carefully hedged ("mnemonic rather than mechanistic" if the measures collapse to one factor), but the mapping to sign/object/interpretant may still feel imposed rather than earned -- the chapter announces the connection to Chapter 7's triadic architecture without demonstrating independent motivation for the tripartite structure beyond Peircean taxonomy. The engagement with Scott-Phillips (2024) on relevance theory, while collegial, is somewhat brisk; the concession that the accounts "may ultimately converge" could undercut the motivation for having a distinct MMMG framework if the reader takes it too seriously. Finally, the Homer Simpson thought experiment in the closing section -- imagining social uptake shifting left-branch extraction from impossible to entrenched -- is vivid but may strike some readers as insufficiently constrained, since the chapter has not established what would prevent any constraint from being overridden given enough memetic cachet.
