# Chapter 9: Countability

Chapter 9 opens Part III ("Categories Reconsidered") and serves as the flagship application of the HPC framework built across Part II. Its central claim is that countability in English is not a static binary feature ([+count] vs [-count]) but a homeostatic property cluster -- specifically, a coupling between two distinct sub-clusters: an individuation cluster (semantic: boundedness, atomicity, enumerability, homogeneity resistance) and a count cluster (morphosyntactic: plural marking, cardinal selection, quantifier patterns, agreement). The chapter argues that this two-cluster architecture, held together by bidirectional inference between form and construal, is what makes countability a genuine HPC kind rather than a mere taxonomic label.

The chapter connects backward to Part II at every turn. The spinning-top/basin metaphor from Chapter 4 structures the hierarchy of count properties; the discreteness framework from Chapter 5 explains why judgments about cattle and folks feel sharp despite gradient individuation; Chapter 6's projectibility criterion is tested explicitly; Chapter 7's stabilizers (acquisition, entrenchment, transmission) are identified as the maintenance machinery; and Chapter 8's failure-mode diagnostics provide the audit template applied in the penultimate section. The chapter also reaches forward, promising that definiteness (Chapter 10) will stress the framework with a looser coupling, and that lexical categories (Chapter 11) will test it under cross-linguistically variable maintenance regimes.

The evidence is layered. The LEGO epigraph and the pea back-formation illustrate how bidirectional inference drives cluster self-completion. Object-mass nouns (furniture, footwear) demonstrate that individuation and count morphosyntax can dissociate, proving the two-cluster architecture. The implicational hierarchy -- tight properties (a(n), low cardinals) fail before loose ones (many, plural agreement) as individuation weakens -- is illustrated with a matrix running from book (full count) through cattle and police (quasi-count) to furniture and water (mass). Diachronic cases are well chosen: pea shows the cluster extending via reanalysis; data shows it eroding as datum dies. The stability of cattle (anchored by cow) is contrasted with the instability of folks (no singulative anchor), supported by COCA frequency data showing an 8.6-fold suppression of three folks relative to three people. An agent-based model simulates the data drift, with 20-seed sensitivity runs and a freeze-learning control. Cross-linguistic parallels draw on Welsh singulatives (Grimm 2018), Mandarin classifiers, Halkomelem optional number (Wiltschko 2008), and Yudja numeral-mass combinations (Lima 2014), framed as "ablation by perturbation" with explicit disconfirmation conditions.

The chapter is the strongest case study in the book, and deliberately so -- it is meant to set a benchmark that later chapters can fall short of in instructive ways. Two places feel slightly thin. First, the cross-linguistic section on classifier languages is explicitly programmatic: the chapter acknowledges the detailed work has not been done, and the prediction about tight/loose asymmetries in classifier systems remains promissory. Second, the individuation cluster's maintenance story leans on a broad evolutionary-convergence argument (edge detection recurs from macrophages to echolocation to infant object files) that, while suggestive, moves quickly across levels of biological organisation without fully securing the analogy. The macrophage parallel in particular risks overreach -- the chapter flags that the claim is computational, not homological, but the passage still asks the reader to carry a lot of trust across a large inferential gap. Neither weakness undermines the chapter's core argument, but they are the points where a sceptical reviewer would push hardest.
