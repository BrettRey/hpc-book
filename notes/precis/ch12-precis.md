# Chapter 12: Pro-form Gender

The chapter argues that English gender is not a three-way pronominal distinction (he/she/it) but a system-wide personhood partition operating across the entire class of pro-forms, including interrogatives (who/what), relatives (who/which), compound determinatives (somebody/something, anywhere/anyone), and locative/temporal items (where/when, here/there/then). The central claim is that this extended system exhibits HPC architecture: two clusters (a semantic personhood cluster and a lexico-grammatical pro-form inventory) are coupled by designatum-driven inference and maintained by overlapping mechanisms. The chapter's distinctive contribution to the Part III sequence is a case where coupling is tight but scope is narrow: only a few dozen lexical items participate, yet within that domain the personhood constraint bites hard.

The chapter connects backward to the coupling continuum introduced in Chapter 8 and to the Peircean apparatus from Chapter 7. The dog examples in the opening (the same animal taking *it* in one sentence and *who* in another) are presented as a direct illustration of Peirce's conditional habits: same referent, different construals, different interpretants, different forms. The transition forward promises Chapter 13's synthesis, where countability, definiteness, lexical categories, and gender are mapped onto a single gradient from hard-coupled phonemes to loosely coupled constructions. Gender is positioned as the case that shows coupling tightness and domain breadth can come apart.

The evidence is layered. The chapter opens with a hierarchy (personal/non-personal, with epicene and sexual subtypes under personal, locative and temporal under non-personal) and a full inventory table showing the system's breadth. A designatum/referent/antecedent three-way distinction, drawn from Huddleston and Pullum, does crucial work: the "French fries" metonymy example (Wu 2025) shows a speaker using *she* for a customer designated by a non-personal NP, contrasted with Spanish and French where antecedent-driven agreement blocks this shift. Three maintenance mechanisms are identified: cognitive grounding (personhood is pre-linguistic, supported by Csibra 2003 and Carey 2009), semantic transparency (the mapping is largely predictable, unlike French arbitrary assignment), and alignment and repair (gender corrections are socially charged, per McConnell-Ginet 2014). A sceptic's challenge is explicitly addressed: why not just item-by-item presuppositions? The answer invokes bidirectional inference, chain coherence, and the fact that boundary cases (pets, robots, collectives) track a shared dimension rather than scattering idiosyncratically. Cross-linguistically, Haspelmath (1997) on the who/what universal and Siemund (2008) on dialectal variation anchor the claim that the architecture is robust but its semantic grounding is a tunable parameter.

The maintenance spectrum (English designatum-driven, French entrenchment-driven, German hybrid) is effective and well-illustrated, but the chapter's empirical coverage of the claimed predictions is thin. The "empirical footprints" offered for each mechanism (children should assign pro-forms to robots based on Theory-of-Mind tests; novel entities should be rapidly assigned pro-forms; gender repairs should be faster than number repairs) remain promissory. The chain-coherence effects, which do substantial theoretical work, rest on constructed examples and introspective judgments rather than corpus or experimental evidence, and the chapter acknowledges this ("if these judgments hold up"). The treatment of demonstratives as constructionally conditioned is a genuine complication that enriches the analysis, but the discussion of *much* as possibly epiphenomenal of the count/mass system rather than a direct gender participant feels underdeveloped, raising a question it does not fully resolve. On the whole, the chapter is architecturally sound, the inventory table is a genuine contribution, but the ratio of predicted-to-confirmed evidence is higher here than in the countability or definiteness chapters.
