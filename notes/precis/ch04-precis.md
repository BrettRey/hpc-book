# Chapter 4: Categories without essences

Chapter 4 is the payoff for Part I's demolition work. It introduces the maintenance view: categories are real because they are maintained, not because they satisfy definitions. The central claim is the HPC commitment stated early in the chapter: a category is real when it is associated with a robust property cluster and there exist processes that tend to produce and stabilize that cluster, yielding projectible generalisations. The commitment is designed to be vulnerable -- it can fail in specific, diagnosable ways (thin clustering, measurement artefact, multiple distinct processes masquerading under one label), and those failure modes are flagged here for development in Chapter 8.

The chapter builds from biology to linguistics through Boyd's original HPC framework and Millikan's copied kinds and unicepts. The species parallel is presented as load-bearing, not decorative: species and grammatical categories face the same philosophical problem (they support induction without having essences), and the same style of explanation (mechanism-based, historical, non-essentialist) resolves both. A table of parallel mechanisms -- acquisition/reproduction, communicative success/natural selection, interactive alignment/gene flow, processing limitations/development, functional pressure/ecological niche -- makes the structural correspondence explicit while a footnote cautions that these are functional analogues, not homologues.

The four named stabilizers (acquisition, entrenchment, interactive alignment, iterated transmission) plus functional pressure are sketched concretely enough to show the framework is not hand-waving, but briefly enough to defer the detailed treatment to Chapter 7. The engagement with Ambridge's radical exemplar model is sharp: Ambridge identifies the right mechanisms (analogical generalisation across stored tokens) but draws the wrong conclusion, treating "emergent" as "less than real." The honeycomb analogy is turned back on itself -- hexagons are real, and so are nouns.

The chapter handles three significant philosophical interlocutors. Khalidi's simple causal theory (one-way causation without feedback) is taken seriously rather than dismissed: it may describe thin clusters or low-frequency items, but robust categories show corrective feedback (alignment resisting innovation, entrenchment anchoring prototypes), which is genuine homeostasis. The spinning top versus ball-in-valley figure does effective work here. Slater's stable property cluster framework is presented as a liberalizing amendment -- multiple routes to stability (homeostatic mechanisms, frozen accidents, metastability, causal isolation), plus the useful distinction between instance stability and cliquish stability. Magnus's objection that HPC is neither necessary nor sufficient for natural-kind status is accepted rather than resisted: the book doesn't need the honorific "natural kind," only mechanism-maintained clusters that support scoped induction.

The practical payoffs section is the chapter's bid for buy-in from working linguists: boundary cases become data (not anomalies), variation becomes tractable (predicted by the framework rather than embarrassing to it), diachrony becomes visible (basin migration rather than category replacement), cross-linguistic comparison becomes empirical (same mechanisms, same clustering), and the "right analysis" question transforms from definitional to mechanistic. The refactoring metaphor -- reorganising without changing what the code does -- positions the maintenance view as complementary to existing research programmes rather than competitive with them.

The chapter closes by connecting forward in two directions. The comparative-concepts subsection engages Haspelmath's nominalism: the yardstick is constructed, yes, but the question is whether it is responsive to structure maintained by mechanisms. And the handoff to Chapter 5 frames the discreteness problem as the next necessary step: if categories are maintained, their boundaries must be maintained too, and the sharpness of those boundaries is a dynamical achievement rather than an intrinsic essence. A falsification section gives the framework teeth by specifying what would count as evidence against it: category boundaries unresponsive to targeted mechanism perturbations, or absence of the predicted variance signature near boundaries.

One tension worth noting: the chapter's treatment of phonological categories via sign languages (Nicaraguan Sign Language, Al-Sayyid Bedouin Sign Language, Battison's symmetry and dominance conditions) is rich and well-evidenced but sits somewhat awkwardly in a chapter whose primary job is introducing the HPC framework for grammatical categories. The sign-language material makes a strong case for convergent maintenance across modalities, but it risks diluting the chapter's focus at the point where the reader most needs to consolidate the core argument. The infant animacy data (O'Doherty et al., the rubber duck) similarly extends the framework's reach impressively but pulls attention away from the grammatical core. Whether these sections earn their length depends on how much cross-domain reinforcement the reader needs at this stage versus how much they need a tighter, faster path from HPC commitment to the payoffs.
