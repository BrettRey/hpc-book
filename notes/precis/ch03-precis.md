# Chapter 3: What we haven't been asking

Chapter 3 is the hinge of Part I. Having shown in Chapter 1 that linguistic categories resist stable boundaries and in Chapter 2 that essentialism cannot supply the definitions it promises, the chapter now argues that nominalism -- the most prominent alternative -- also fails, though for the opposite reason. Where essentialism posits essences that can't be found, nominalism infers that there is nothing to find. The chapter's central claim is that both positions share a blind spot: neither asks what *maintains* categories. By the chapter's end, the reader has been given the core slogan of the book -- "categories are real because they are maintained" -- and a roadmap for Part II.

The chapter opens with Haspelmath's comparative-concept programme and Croft's Radical Construction Grammar, presented charitably as serious responses to the definitional failures of Chapter 2. Their position is coherent: if definitions keep failing, stop looking for definitions. But the chapter identifies a cost. If cross-linguistic categories are purely methodological yardsticks, nominalism cannot explain why certain clusters recur across unrelated languages, why the same functional solutions keep showing up in similar configurations. The move from "no essence" to "no reality" is too quick; it assumes essence is the only ground for reality.

The phase-transition analogy does the pivotal conceptual work. Water, ice, and steam share no intrinsic essence of phase; their discreteness is maintained by statistical-mechanical dynamics. The analogy is explicitly flagged as a model, not a metaphor, and it opens the space the chapter needs: discrete, stable, projectible structure without essences. The island-constraint case (Cuneo and Goldberg 2023) then demonstrates the payoff in linguistics -- replacing the search for a structural definition of islands with a mechanism (foreground-background clash) that explains variable extraction resistance.

A survey of usage-based linguistics, construction grammar, sociolinguistics, and functionalism follows, acknowledging that each has "the right instincts" -- looking for dynamics rather than definitions -- but arguing that none has made mechanism fully central or provided explicit diagnostic criteria for when a category is real versus merely convenient. This is the gap the book aims to fill.

The chapter then introduces its two theoretical pillars. Peirce's cable metaphor (1868) and his concept of the interpretant -- the habit of inference a sign produces -- supply an account of what categories *do*. Boyd's homeostatic property cluster kinds (1991) supply an account of what *sustains* them. The synthesis is stated explicitly: Peirce without Boyd is semiotics without causal grounding; Boyd without Peirce is mechanism without an account of purpose.

The longest section, on the "lexeme obsession," is the chapter's most original contribution. It argues that the essentialism-nominalism debate has fixated on categories of items (nouns, verbs, adjectives) while neglecting categories of categories -- feature systems like gender, number, tense, definiteness. Extended comparisons of English, Shilluk, and Japanese number systems show that the cross-linguistic unity of NUMBER cannot rest on shared exponence, predictability, or productivity. The chapter raises "existential disputes" (is tense reducible to aspect? is gender reducible to number?) and argues that only a mechanistic framework can settle them empirically.

A brief section on grammaticality itself previews the book's deepest question: whether grammaticality is a primitive or a maintained cluster. The chapter closes with a compressed roadmap of Part II (Chapters 4-8).

The argument is generally strong, but a few tensions are visible. The architectural-realism paragraph (Culicover and Jackendoff's Parallel Architecture) sits orphaned between the nominalism critique and the phase-transition analogy, flagged with \bigskip markers on both sides -- it reads like a note that hasn't been integrated. The survey of linguistic traditions (usage-based, construction grammar, sociolinguistics, functionalism) is efficient but risks feeling like a checklist; the claim that none has made mechanism "fully central" could use sharper substantiation, since Schmid (2020) and Spike (2020) are cited as partial syntheses without much discussion of how far they actually go. Finally, the Peirce-Boyd synthesis, while compellingly stated, arrives somewhat late and compressed; the reader gets the formula but not yet the worked demonstration -- which is by design (that is Part II's job), but the chapter might benefit from a sentence or two acknowledging that the synthesis is being promissory at this stage.
