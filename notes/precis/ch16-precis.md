# Chapter 16: What Changes

Chapter 16 is the book's closing move, and it is deliberately austere: no new case study, no new theoretical apparatus, no slogan. Its central argument is that the HPC framework earns credibility only by specifying the outcomes that would kill it, and that the book owes the reader an explicit ledger of those commitments. The chapter frames this as a "gauntlet" -- a sequence of gates (show clustering, identify stabilisers, demonstrate projection) that any proposed category must clear, followed by predictions and stress tests that can force confirmation, scope narrowing, or outright downgrading.

The chapter connects backward to essentially every preceding chapter. The synthesis ledger (Table 16.1) rows map onto the Part III case studies -- countability (Ch 9), definiteness/deitality (Ch 10), lexical categories (Ch 11), pro-form gender (Ch 12) -- and onto Ch 14 (grammaticality) and Ch 15 (social stabilisation). Each row names a mechanism commitment, an observable signature, and a signal that would force downgrading. The shared statistical test across rows is the conditioned-model-versus-pooled-baseline comparison established in Ch 15. The chapter also retrieves the zipper image from Ch 13, now treating it as an instrument rather than a metaphor: put the zipper under load, ask where the teeth slip.

Two empirical predictions carry the weight. First, fraying is asymmetric: when a stabiliser weakens, diagnostics should erode in a structured, non-synchronous order, not as uniform noise. The running example is English *data*, where recession of *datum* should erode tight singulative frames (*a datum*, *three data*) before looser ones (*many data*), with agreement lagging behind both. Second, drift has an order -- the "sticky-lock effect": usage and semantic pressure move first, morphosyntactic locks lag, and lagging locks fail in bursts rather than smoothing out indefinitely. Both predictions are operationalised with named variables and testable contrasts (age-graded corpora, perturbation designs, held-out log-loss on out-of-construction splits).

Two stress tests define the chapter's falsification conditions. Stress test A (the essentialist's best case) asks for a category that projects robustly with no identifiable maintenance structure. Stress test B (the nominalist's best case) asks for evidence that variation is a random walk once sampling noise is controlled. Both are framed with preregistration language and proxy-family declarations -- a genuine attempt to limit post hoc rescue.

The *data* example is well-chosen as a running test bench: it is measurable, mid-shift, and familiar enough to be instantly legible. But the chapter leans on it heavily, and no second case is developed at comparable resolution. The gauntlet diagram (Figure 16.1) and the two tables do useful bookkeeping, yet the chapter could feel thin to a reader who wants to see the gauntlet applied in real time rather than prospectively. The final section makes a strong ontological claim -- that the sustaining *is* the reality -- but the argument from Ladyman and Ross about scale-dependent ontology, while evocative, receives only an epigraph and a brief echo rather than a developed philosophical engagement. The closing line ("Words that won't hold still can still hold together -- until they don't") lands well as rhetoric but does not quite discharge the burden of showing what concretely changes in day-to-day linguistic practice; the "what changes" of the title gets three paragraphs of gestural fieldwork and study-design advice rather than worked methodological illustration.

Overall, the chapter does what it promises -- lay down the gauntlet -- with admirable concision. Its principal risk is that the concision reads as promissory: the predictions are stated but not yet tested, and readers hungry for at least one worked gauntlet run may feel the chapter ends one section too soon.
