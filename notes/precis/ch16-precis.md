# Chapter 16: How Categories Travel

Chapter 16 makes the constructive case that cross-domain extension -- the capacity to apply one category's inductive structure to another domain -- falls out of the HPC framework as a predicted consequence rather than an additional stipulation. If categories are maintained clusters, and two maintained clusters share mechanism-sustained property overlap, treating one as the other generates predictions. The boundary between productive and unproductive extension tracks the boundary between shared and unshared mechanisms.

The chapter opens with an extended, Rapoport's-Rules-compliant engagement with cognitive linguistics (SS16.1, "The nearest tradition"). Rosch's prototype effects, Lakoff's radial categories, Lakoff and Johnson's conceptual metaphor, Bybee's entrenchment, and Langacker's Cognitive Grammar are credited with genuine contributions: documenting clustering that essentialism couldn't see, and taking seriously the mechanisms that produce it. The gap identified is not empirical but diagnostic: cognitive linguistics described gradience and cross-domain mapping but lacked the apparatus for predicting when a cluster projects and when a mapping is productive versus dead. Prototype theory can see internal structure but can't tell you whether that structure is one category with sub-parts or two categories whose extensions overlap. The definiteness/deitality case (Ch 10) and the proper noun/proper name distinction (SS7.x) are adduced as cases HPC resolves and prototype theory cannot.

SS16.2 introduces Boyd's (1979) theory-constitutive metaphor (TCM): some metaphors in science aren't pedagogical scaffolding but constitute causal claims about natural kinds that no available literal vocabulary captures. Cross-domain extension is defined: when two HPC kinds share partial mechanism-sustained property overlap, inferences licensed by one can extend to the other. This is what metaphor *is*. Noyes and Keil (2019) provide experimental teeth: generic statements transmit kind assumptions (rich inductive structure) without necessarily transmitting essence assumptions. Gentner's (1983) structure-mapping theory is connected: HPC specifies what makes relational structure available for mapping (mechanism-sustained maintenance). A brief contrast with literary metaphor (Dickinson's "Hope is the thing with feathers") distinguishes conventionalizing metaphor (mechanism-supported overlap) from poetic metaphor (precise selection at the mechanism boundary).

SS16.3 develops a worked example: canine TRAIL as an HPC category. Thesen (1993) and Hepper and Wells (2005) on trail directionality, Fagan et al. (2025) on canid vs felid route reuse. The category degrades when mechanisms are removed (removal test). A tracking dog on concrete demonstrates partial cross-domain extension (same source identity, different substrate mechanics). A dog following painted soccer lines demonstrates near-complete mechanism divergence with residual property overlap. The phylogenetic point: the capacity for category extension predates language; what humans add is semiotic amplification. LLMs sharpen the contrast: they detect structural correspondences but lack endogenous selectivity.

SS16.4 ("The Boyd circle") makes the framework reflexive. The application of HPC from biological species to grammatical categories is itself a cross-domain extension on partial property overlap. The theory predicts a capacity it exemplifies. This isn't vicious circularity: the theory doesn't presuppose its conclusion but predicts a capacity that its own history instantiates. Peirce's cable metaphor (CP 5.265) is identified as a TCM in Boyd's sense. Hofstadter and Sander's (2013) analogy-as-categorization thesis is connected and constrained: what makes one analogy productive is shared mechanism-sustained properties.

SS16.5 ("What this means") draws five consequences for cognitive linguistics: (1) prototype effects are mechanistically grounded, not primitive; (2) cross-domain mappings work because of shared mechanism-sustained structure, not image schemas alone; (3) the framework is self-applying and adds a falsification condition cognitive linguistics lacks; (4) failure modes (thin/fat/negative) diagnose when metaphors will work; (5) the framework can count categories where prototype theory cannot. A methodological caution notes that the HPC triad's three legs strongly covary in practice; the defeat conditions and failure modes are where the distinction does sharpest work. The section closes by connecting to Ch 17's gauntlet: the TCM from biology to linguistics is what licences the gauntlet's predictions.
