# Chapter 7: Projectibility and the Good Bet

Chapter 7 is the pivot of Part II. Where Chapter 6 (the stabilisers) catalogued the mechanisms that maintain linguistic categories, this chapter argues that projectibility is what those mechanisms are *for*. The central claim: a category is real not because it has an essence or a definition, but because it licences reliable inference across novel instances. Mechanisms produce projectibility; the observable property cluster is the trace they leave. The slogan -- "Categories are real because they're projectible. They're projectible because they're maintained" -- is unpacked into three components (profile, stabilised by mechanisms, projectible relative to purposes), with the first two subordinated to the third.

The chapter's principal evidence comes from Divjak, Wisniewski, and Milin's work on Polish aspect. Three computational models are compared: one that ignores aspect entirely (lemma-concrete), one that learns aspect from distributional cues (aspect-concrete), and one that learns from the textbook semantic definitions of boundedness and totality (aspect-abstract). The definitional model predicts imperfective well (98%) but perfective poorly (77%), and against native-speaker gap-filling judgments it performs worst of all -- the model with no aspect category best predicts human preferences. This is framed as a failure of definitional projectibility: the category is real as a stable pattern, but the definitions don't track the mechanisms that maintain usage. An extended volcanic-island metaphor drives the point home: the semantic-temporal distinctions that originally carved the opposition have gone dormant; what maintains the pattern now is lexeme-specific cue structure. A didactic agent-based model demonstrates the qualitative signature of this mechanistic drift.

A Peircean section anchors the theoretical apparatus. Projection is identified with Peirce's interpretant -- the habit of inference a sign generates -- and projectibility is characterised as a would-be: a conditional disposition that outruns any finite track record. This move gives the chapter its answer to Goodman's grue problem: green is projectible not because it has been projected (Goodman's circular bootstrap) but because mechanisms sustain the would-be. The distinction between brute correlation and genuine mediation (via the interpretant) explains why NPIs, despite sharing distributional sensitivity to negation, fail to constitute an HPC kind.

The concept of field-relative projectibility occupies the chapter's middle third. The tomato (fruit for the botanist, vegetable for the chef), proper nouns versus proper names, constituency versus dependency grammar, and the non-grammaticalisation of colour categories all illustrate the same claim: overlapping extensions can be carved by distinct HPCs, each maintained by different mechanisms and projectible for different analytical purposes. Prasertsom, Smith, and Culbertson's experimental finding that animacy-based noun classes are learned better than colour-based ones -- and that the bias is domain-general, not grammar-specific -- provides the sharpest evidence that projectibility is constrained by predictive structure, not perceptual salience.

The chapter closes by addressing whether interest-relativity makes the framework circular (Craver, Onishi and Serpico, Lemeire) and arguing it does not: interest selects the question, but whether the mechanism answers it is empirical. A section on degrees of projectibility connects the framework to experimental data on judgment variability (Favier and Huettig on Dutch; Sassenhagen et al. on verb frequency; Graziano-King and Cairns on adjective comparatives), predicting that variance tracks entrenchment strength.

The main tension is one the chapter acknowledges but does not fully resolve: the relationship between the category label and the finer-grained mechanisms. If the lemma-concrete model wins, and speakers don't need an "aspect" representation as a mediating variable, then in what sense does the category exist? The coral-reef answer (the category is an emergent alignment) is gestured at but could bear more pressure -- particularly regarding when an emergent alignment earns ontological status and when it is merely a statistical summary. The chapter promises that Part III will demonstrate field-relative projectibility across countability, definiteness, word classes, and gender; those chapters now carry the burden of making this operational.
