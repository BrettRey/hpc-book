# Chapter 3: Editorial Report

*"What we haven't been asking"*

---

## 1. Argument

The chapter's core argument is sound and well-sequenced: nominalism is a coherent response to essentialism's failures but purchases descriptive freedom at the cost of explanatory reach. The phase-transition analogy does genuine conceptual work by opening the possibility of discrete, stable, projectible structure without essences, and the Peirce-Boyd synthesis gives the reader a formula to carry forward. The "lexeme obsession" section is the chapter's most original contribution, reframing the ontological question from "what box does this word go in?" to "what kind of thing is this box?" -- a move that earns the book's right to treat gender, number, tense, and definiteness as first-class HPC candidates. One logical gap: the chapter claims that nominalism "cannot explain why certain clusters recur across unrelated languages" (line 32), but this requires a stronger showing than it gets. Haspelmath could reply that convergent functional pressures plus independent grammaticalisation pathways produce recurrence without shared kinds; the chapter needs to distinguish its position from that reply more sharply, or concede that the argument turns on what counts as "explaining" recurrence.

## 2. Structure

The chapter has three distinct movements: (1) the nominalism critique and phase-transition alternative (opening through the island-constraint case), (2) the survey of linguistic traditions and Peirce-Boyd synthesis, and (3) the "lexeme obsession" section on categories-of-categories. The first two movements flow well. The transition from the survey of traditions into the Peirce-Boyd synthesis (line 66) is clean and well-earned. The "lexeme obsession" section, however, is long enough to be a chapter within a chapter -- it runs from line 88 to line 199 and covers gender, number (English/Shilluk/Japanese), definiteness, tense-aspect reduction disputes, and existential disputes. This material is excellent, but its sheer breadth risks losing the reader's sense of forward momentum. The grammaticality section (lines 201--222) then arrives late and compressed, which is a structural problem because grammaticality is arguably the book's deepest question and this is its first sustained appearance. Finally, the "What Part II provides" roadmap (lines 224--241) does its job but the closing paragraph (lines 239--241) restates the slogan without adding anything the reader doesn't already have.

## 3. Prose

The prose is generally strong -- the opening three paragraphs on Haspelmath and Croft are a model of charitable but pointed summary. The voice is confident without being aggressive, and the Rapoport's Rules spirit is well maintained. The best writing is in the phase-transition passage (lines 36--47), which is vivid and precise. The Shilluk number material (lines 118--148) is absorbing and earns its detail. Two pacing problems: first, the `\bigskip` separators (lines 20, 52, 78, 99, 113, 153, 167, 193, 239) create a fragmented rhythm -- the chapter reads in places like a sequence of standalone vignettes rather than a continuous argument. Several of these could be replaced with transitional sentences that maintain the argumentative thread. Second, the definiteness preview (lines 158--165) and the grammaticality section (lines 201--222) are both compressed relative to the space given to gender and number, creating an imbalance: the reader gets 80 lines on number but only 22 on grammaticality.

## 4. Evidence

The evidence is well-chosen and layered. The Haspelmath/Croft presentation is fair and well-sourced. The phase-transition analogy is appropriately flagged as "a model, not a metaphor" (line 47). The island-constraint case (Cuneo and Goldberg 2023) is a strong example of the mechanism-seeking payoff, and the Shilluk number data -- with the specific counts from Remijsen and Ayoker (906 singular, 668 plural, 238 unspecified) -- is a concrete anchor. The Japanese material on `hito-bito` and classifiers is effective for triangulation. One evidential weakness: the survey of linguistic traditions (lines 54--64) is efficient but thin. The claim that none of these traditions has made mechanism "fully central" is load-bearing for the chapter's raison d'etre, but the citations (Schmid 2020, Spike 2020, Dahl 2016) are dropped without enough discussion to show where they fall short. A reader who knows Schmid's entrenchment-conventionalisation model might object that it *does* make mechanism central, and the chapter should say more precisely what it lacks.

## 5. Cross-chapter coherence

The chapter successfully receives from Ch 2 (definitional failures, standing-wave metaphor, the biology parallel) and delivers to Ch 4 (the spinning-top image is planted here at line 74, cashed there). The Peirce-Boyd synthesis introduced here (lines 67--76) is developed properly in Ch 7 and deployed throughout Part III -- the promissory structure works. The definiteness preview (lines 158--165) sets up the two-cluster architecture of Ch 10 correctly. However, the roadmap at lines 231--237 is slightly out of sync with the actual book: it mentions Chs 4, 7, and 8 but skips Chs 5 and 6, which is disorienting since discreteness (Ch 5) and the stabilisers (Ch 6) are substantial theoretical chapters. The roadmap should account for all five Part II chapters. The book-level editorial report flags that the Parallel Architecture paragraph sits orphaned in both Ch 1 and Ch 3 -- it remains orphaned here (lines 21--23), bracketed by `\bigskip` markers, and needs either integration or removal. The terminology "natural kind" appears throughout this chapter (e.g. lines 8, 28, 30, 34, 93, 103, etc.) but the book's own terminological decision (per CLAUDE.md) is to use "category" throughout and avoid "kind" -- this chapter has not been updated to reflect that decision.

## 6. House style issues

### Orphaned paragraph with `\bigskip` markers
Lines 20--24: The Parallel Architecture paragraph is bracketed by `\bigskip` on both sides and uses `\emph{types}` and `\emph{mechanisms}` rather than semantic macros. This is clearly a note awaiting integration, not finished prose.

### `\emph{}` instead of semantic macros
- Line 22: `\emph{types}` and `\emph{mechanisms}` -- should be `\term{types}` and `\term{mechanisms}` if these are technical concepts, or plain text if they are ordinary emphasis. The house style reserves italics for `\mention{}` (linguistic forms) and `\term{}` (technical terms).
- Line 70: `\emph{has}` and `\emph{does}` -- these are contrastive emphasis, which is acceptable, but the chapter should be consistent in how it handles emphasis-for-contrast versus emphasis-for-mention.
- Line 76: `\emph{for}` -- contrastive emphasis, acceptable.

### Missing `\mention{}` on linguistic forms
- Line 149: `that's the empirical question` -- lowercase `that's` following a period suggests this sentence was meant to start with a capital T. Also, this is the only instance of an uncapitalised sentence-initial word in the chapter.

### Bare `\textit{}` instead of `\mention{}`
No instances found -- the chapter consistently uses `\mention{}` for linguistic forms. Good.

### `\enquote{}` usage
Consistently applied for quotations. Good.

### Dashes
The chapter uses en-dashes with spaces (`~--`) throughout, consistent with house style. No em-dashes found Correctly applied.

### `\bigskip` as structural separator
Lines 20, 52, 78, 99, 113, 153, 167, 193, 239: Nine `\bigskip` separators in a single chapter is excessive. The style guide does not prescribe `\bigskip` as a structural device; these should be replaced with transitional prose, section breaks, or removed.

### Uncapitalised sentence
Line 149: `that's the empirical question.` -- should be `That's the empirical question.`

### Potential source-grounding flag
Line 119: The Shilluk number data (906 singular, 668 plural, 238 lacking plurals, 9 plurals lacking singulars) is attributed to Remijsen and Ayoker (2019, ch. 2). Given the source-grounding LAW and that Shilluk data was previously flagged for fabrication risk, these numbers should be verified against the source PDF before final publication.

## 7. Specific recommendations

1. **Integrate or remove the Parallel Architecture paragraph (lines 21--23).** This paragraph has been flagged as orphaned in both the Ch 1 and Ch 3 precis and in the book-level editorial report. Decision needed: if it stays, it belongs in the survey-of-traditions section (after line 64) with a sentence connecting it to the mechanism gap. If it goes, delete it and the flanking `\bigskip` markers.

2. **Replace `\bigskip` separators with transitional sentences or section breaks.** Nine `\bigskip` markers in one chapter fragments the argument. The separators at lines 99, 113, 153, 167, and 193 are within the "lexeme obsession" section and could be replaced with transitional sentences that maintain the argumentative thread. The separator at line 78 ("Here is where the enterprise stalls") could become a new `\subsection{}` or simply follow as a new paragraph after the Peirce-Boyd synthesis. The separator at line 239 before the closing paragraph is unnecessary.

3. **Fix the uncapitalised sentence at line 149.** `that's the empirical question.` should be `That's the empirical question.`

4. **Expand the roadmap (lines 231--237) to account for all five Part II chapters.** Currently mentions Chs 4, 7, and 8 but skips Chs 5 (discreteness) and 6 (stabilisers). Even a clause each would prevent the reader from feeling disoriented when they encounter two unannounced chapters.

5. **Sharpen the response to the Haspelmath convergence reply (around line 32).** The claim that nominalism "cannot explain why certain clusters recur" is vulnerable to the counter that convergent grammaticalisation under similar functional pressures explains recurrence without positing shared kinds. Add a sentence distinguishing "convergent pressure" (which the book accepts) from "maintained cluster" (which nominalism lacks).

6. **Give the survey of linguistic traditions (lines 54--64) more teeth.** The claim that no tradition has made mechanism "fully central" is the chapter's warrant for existing. Engage briefly with Schmid (2020) or Spike (2020) to show specifically where they fall short -- e.g., that Schmid provides a mechanism inventory but not diagnostic criteria for when a category is real versus merely convenient.

7. **Audit the chapter for "natural kind" / "kind" terminology.** The book's terminological decision is to use "category" throughout and avoid "kind." This chapter uses "natural kind" extensively (lines 8, 28, 30, 34, 93, 103, 175, 179, 185, 191, 197, 214, 235). Some of these are in discussions of other people's terminology (Boyd, Haspelmath) where "natural kind" is appropriate; others are the author's own voice and should switch to "category." Decide which instances to keep (attributions) and which to change.

8. **Rebalance the space given to grammaticality (lines 201--222) relative to number (lines 115--198).** The number section has 83 lines of detailed cross-linguistic comparison; the grammaticality section has 21 lines. Given that grammaticality is arguably the book's deepest question (and gets its own chapter, Ch 14), its first sustained appearance here deserves more room. One approach: condense the number section slightly (the Japanese classifier material could be tightened) and expand the grammaticality preview to include a concrete example of properties coming apart (e.g., garden-path sentences that are grammatical but judged unacceptable).

9. **Remove or integrate `\emph{}` on lines 22.** Replace `\emph{types}` and `\emph{mechanisms}` with semantic macros or plain contrastive emphasis, depending on whether this paragraph survives recommendation 1.

10. **Verify the Shilluk data (line 119) against Remijsen and Ayoker (2019, ch. 2).** The specific counts (906, 668, 238, 9) should be checked against the source PDF, per the source-grounding protocol. This data was flagged as a category requiring verification in the project's source-grounding LAW.
