# Chapter 6: The Stabilizers

Chapter 6 is the causal engine room of the book. Where Chapter 5 established that discreteness is achieved rather than given, Chapter 6 asks the load-bearing question: what does the achieving? Its central claim is that grammatical categories are maintained by braids of cognitive, social, and transmission-level mechanisms -- "stabilizers" -- whose interactions produce the clustering that makes categories projectible. The chapter introduces the term "stabilizer" (foregrounding maintenance, drawing on Illari and Williamson 2012) and distinguishes it from mere correlates: a stabilizer is something whose removal would change the clustering.

The chapter opens with a biological parallel -- macrophages, not as ontological analogy but as an explanatory style -- and organizes the mechanism inventory along two dimensions (timescale and locus) in a four-quadrant figure: processing economy and predictive facilitation (fast/individual), accommodation and alignment (fast/community), acquisition and entrenchment (slow/individual), transmission dynamics and social indexing (slow/community). Fedorenko, Piantadosi, and Gibson's (2024) neural dissociation between language and general reasoning is recruited to argue that the forcing function maintaining categories is specifically communicative, not cognitive in the general sense.

Two extended case studies do the heavy lifting. First, quotatives: the emergence of English *be like* (documented by Butters 1982, Ferrara and Bell 1995, Tagliamonte and D'Arcy 2004, 2007) alongside German *so* (Golato 2000, Androutsopoulos 1998), Japanese *tte*, and Turkish *diye*. The cross-linguistic convergence (synthesized by Buchstaller 2014, Guldemann 2008) is presented as convergent maintenance under similar functional pressures, not media-driven borrowing. Five stabilizers are traced: processing economy, expressive fit, acquisition and cohort effects, social indexing (via Eckert 2012), and transmission dynamics. A stress test asks what would happen if each were removed singly -- no single strand suffices, and the chapter provides a perturbation-prediction table. A deeper section decomposes mechanisms causally (why young women lead change traces through network density to developmental psychology) and mereologically (transmission is a composite of production, signal, perception, and social processes).

The second case, independent relative *whose* (Reynolds 2024), is deliberately different: a vanishingly rare construction maintained by cognitive mechanisms (filler-gap processing, as in Boguraev, Potts, and Mahowald 2025) under stringent information-structural licensing (contrastive parallelism, deictic anchoring, structural integration). Where quotatives are maintained from above -- by social and functional pressures -- *whose*-stranding is maintained from below, by incremental parsing and recoverability constraints. Cross-linguistic parallels in German and Japanese are offered as predictions. The chapter is honest that the evidential base for *whose* is attestation data, not variationist quantification, and flags this asymmetry explicitly.

A pivotal theoretical move comes in the "mechanisms as categories, categories as mechanisms" section. If stabilizers are themselves projectible clusters maintained by further mechanisms, then the framework is self-applying: processing economy is an HPC kind; social indexing is an HPC kind. The stabilization is reciprocal -- quotatives maintain the mechanisms that maintain them, like a trail kept open by being walked. This raises a regress worry (what grounds the chain?), answered with an interventionist pragmatism (stop where causal leverage bites) and an anti-foundationalist epistemology (explanatory networks don't need bedrock).

The chapter closes with explicit theoretical commitments: three core (process ontology, interventionist realism, reciprocal maintenance) and four derived (population-distributed competence, variation as signal, cross-level coherence, operational testability). It positions itself as refactoring rather than replacing existing work -- Bybee's entrenchment, Labov's variationism, prototype theory's gradience, grammaticalization's pathways -- by providing the missing question: not what clusters, but why the cluster holds.

The main tension is the evidential asymmetry between the two case studies. The quotative case rests on decades of quantitative sociolinguistic research; the *whose* case rests on attestation patterns from a preprint, with disputed grammaticality judgments and no experimental confirmation. The chapter flags this honestly, but the architectural weight placed on *whose* -- as the case that proves the framework isn't "just sociolinguistics" -- is heavier than the evidence currently supports. The cross-linguistic predictions for German and Japanese possessive stranding are suggestive but unconfirmed. A second, subtler tension: the self-application of the framework (mechanisms are categories) is theoretically elegant but risks unfalsifiability -- if everything is an HPC kind, the framework may lose discriminatory power. The "particle" contrast case does useful work here, but the line between a genuine stabilizer and a post-hoc narrative could use sharper criteria than the chapter currently provides.

The chapter promises Chapter 7 on projectibility proper -- arguing that mechanisms don't just preserve categories but constitute them epistemically -- and threads forward to Chapter 8 on failure modes (what happens when stabilizers fail) and Chapter 10 on definiteness (where the mechanism inventory predicts two coupled HPCs rather than one).
