# Chapter 10: Definiteness and Deitality

The chapter opens with a Borges epigraph (Funes the Memorious) and a deceptively simple pair: *She wears the veil* is natural, but *She wears the hat* demands context. The puzzle is that the definite article in the first sentence isn't doing what standard accounts (Russell's uniqueness, Heim's familiarity) say it should -- there's no unique veil, no prior mention. Weak definites like *play the piano*, *go to the hospital*, and *the tiger is endangered* are not marginal exceptions; they are high-frequency, productive patterns. Reynolds's central claim is that the literature has been conflating two distinct categories: a semantic cluster (definiteness proper -- identifiability, uniqueness, familiarity) and a morphosyntactic cluster (a distributional profile inherited from demonstratives). These are two HPCs maintained by different mechanisms, and their imperfect alignment produces exactly the puzzles that have troubled semanticists for decades.

The chapter parallels Chapter 9's architecture explicitly. Where countability distinguished the individuation cluster (semantic) from the count cluster (morphosyntactic), definiteness distinguishes the definiteness cluster from what Reynolds calls the "form cluster." Three distributional diagnostics define the form cluster in English: resistance to existential *there* under neutral prosody (Milsark 1977), eligibility as complement of partitive *of*, and suitability as host for nonrestrictive modification. A diagnostic table shows that *the*, demonstratives, and genitives pass all three; *each/every* pass only one; *a/an* and *some* pass none. The key observation is that no single diagnostic is necessary or sufficient -- convergence is what matters, which is the HPC signature.

The coupling between the two clusters is explained through Millikan's proper-function framework and Himmelmann's "context expansion." The article's proper function -- signalling identifiability -- was inherited from the demonstrative source through grammaticalization. But grammaticalization dragged along distributional side effects that weren't what the article was *for*. Once those side effects stabilised, they became available for recruitment by derived functions. Weak definites exploit the form-cluster profile without performing the identifiability function; generic definites shift the domain from individuals to kinds; narrative-presentational *this* (Prince 1981) uses a form-cluster item to introduce an indefinite referent. The derived functions are parasitic: they persist only because the Normal function remains robust enough to sustain the convention. The chapter connects this to the Peircean apparatus from Chapter 7: the interpretant (the habit coupling form and construal) generates different would-bes under different conditions, which is field-relative projectibility in action.

Five maintenance mechanisms are identified for the form cluster: grammaticalization (inertia), acquisition (bootstrap -- children learn the distributional frame before mastering semantics), alignment (real-time error-correction via repair), prestige selection (dialectal protection of arbitrary variants like British *in hospital*), and transmission (generational filtering for learnability). The definiteness cluster is maintained by discourse tracking, domain restriction, and Theory of Mind, with developmental evidence (Rozendaal and Baker 2008, Brockmann et al. 2018, De Cat 2013) showing that the semantic properties are acquired sequentially -- familiarity, then uniqueness, then identifiability -- reinforcing the claim that they are cognitively dissociable components, not a unitary concept.

The terminological intervention comes late: Reynolds proposes "deitality" for the form cluster, deriving it from the deictic origins of the category. He acknowledges it is ugly, and argues that its unfamiliarity is partly the point -- you can't accidentally mean *definite* when you say *deital*. A cross-linguistic section argues that while the specific diagnostics are English-particular, the architecture (two clusters, imperfect coupling, grammaticalization as the source of the form cluster) generalises wherever demonstratives grammaticalise into articles, citing Greek (articles required with proper names) and Japanese (demonstrative system without articles) as contrasting realisations.

The chapter sits on the coupling continuum introduced in Chapter 8: definiteness is looser than countability but still systematic. It promises that Chapter 11 will test a still-looser configuration -- lexical categories like noun and verb, where the mechanisms are diffuse and the boundaries are contested.

Two tensions. First, the proper-names discussion invokes Gasparri (2025) on generic readings of names (*Italian Andrea is generally male*) and Dupre's "social-practice-unified collections," but the connection to the two-cluster architecture feels rushed -- it argues that names have definiteness-cluster properties without form-cluster marking, but the existential-pivot example (*There's a Kim here to see you*) actually shows an indefinite use, which muddies the claim that names are straightforwardly definite-cluster items. The discussion would benefit from distinguishing referential from attributive uses of names more carefully. Second, the falsifiability section lists testable predictions (prosodic rescue should selectively affect *there*-resistance but not partitive licensing; children should master distributional restrictions before semantic ones), but these are stated rather than evidenced. The acquisition prediction in particular seems to be doing double duty as both evidence for the two-cluster architecture and a prediction generated by it, which risks circularity.
