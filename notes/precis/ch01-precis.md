# Chapter 1: Words That Won't Hold Still

The chapter opens with a 2008 email from Rodney Huddleston confessing that he doesn't know how to handle *otherwise* -- a word that behaves like an adverb in one construction, an adjective in another, and something unlabelled in a third. The anecdote is not ornamental: the lead author of the most comprehensive grammar of English couldn't classify a common word, not because he was careless but because the word resists every set of necessary-and-sufficient criteria. Reynolds uses this as the chapter's through-line, returning to Huddleston's email three times, each return deepening the point.

The central claim is that neither essentialism nor prototype theory can explain why linguistic categories have the structure they have. Essentialism (illustrated through Chomsky's *Aspects*, Baker's substantive universals, and Katz's semantic feature decompositions) handles core cases well and has built the infrastructure of modern linguistics -- grammars, parsers, textbooks -- but responds to counterexamples only by multiplying stipulations. The chapter marshals a series of English examples: *fun* drifting from noun toward adjective; *near* straddling preposition and adjective; *cattle* sitting stably outside the singular-plural paradigm for centuries; *pease* regularising where *cattle* did not because no functional anchor (a *cow* equivalent) blocked analogy; and the gradual spread of *do*-support in Early Modern English, where both inverted and periphrastic patterns coexisted for generations. These are not random exceptions but systematic: the trouble clusters at category intersections (preposition-adverb, complement-modifier) and among high-frequency, broadly distributed items.

Prototype theory (Rosch's typicality experiments, Lakoff's *Women, Fire, and Dangerous Things*, Taylor's *Linguistic Categorization*) is presented as capturing something essentialism missed -- gradient judgments are real data -- but purchasing descriptive adequacy at the cost of explanation. The chapter argues that prototype descriptions record the shape of gradience without explaining its persistence: if categories are merely similarity structures around exemplars, small perturbations across generations should cause them to drift, yet the periphery of English noun-hood looks broadly the same as it did a century ago. An ecological analogy (species distribution across a landscape) sharpens the point: mapping the distribution is not the same as understanding the mechanisms that produce it.

A notable move is the confession that Reynolds himself worked within the feature-bundle tradition for most of his career, treating property clustering as a fact to record rather than a phenomenon to explain (he cites his own 2021 clustering study of determinatives and pronouns). The chapter also insists that essentialism is not confined to formalist schools: Langacker's notional noun-verb distinction, Dik's universal subject properties, and Role and Reference Grammar's Actor-Undergoer macroroles all replicate the essentialist shape. Pullum is quoted acknowledging the pragmatic fiction explicitly.

The chapter closes by naming the "maintenance view" -- the hypothesis that categories are real because they are maintained -- and promising that Part I's remaining chapters will trace the impasse's consequences: Chapter 2 on why essentialism's fixes keep failing, Chapter 3 on what falls out of view when the field retreats to nominalism or stops short of asking what holds clusters together.

The argument is well-constructed, but two places feel thin. First, the Jackendoff/Parallel Architecture paragraph sits oddly between the essentialism-prototype impasse and the introduction of the maintenance view; its connection to the chapter's argument is gestured at but not developed. Second, the quantum-physics analogy (Bell's inequality, no-cloning theorem) near the end is suggestive but does no argumentative work -- it amounts to "foundational questions sometimes pay off," which risks sounding like special pleading for the book's own project rather than advancing the case.
