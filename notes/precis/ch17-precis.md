# Chapter 17: The Gauntlet

Chapter 17 is the book's falsification chapter: it converts the theoretical apparatus into predictions with named defeat conditions, stress tests, and worked cases. The chapter opens by establishing the three debts every proposed category owes (show clustering, identify stabilisers, demonstrate projection) and presenting these as a visual evaluation pathway (Figure 17.1). The Ladyman and Ross epigraph and scale-relative ontology frame the opening: "at the scale of the isolated token there are no categories."

SS17.1 ("What the book commits to") presents the synthesis ledger (Table 17.1): five rows mapping the Part III case studies and Part IV's grammaticality/social chapters onto mechanism commitments, observable signatures, and downgrade signals. Countability should show structured boundary behaviour; definiteness/deitality should show stable decouplings; lexical categories should show differential projection by mechanism density; pro-form gender should show personhood-conditioned selection; grammaticality and social stabilisation should show patterned detector misfires and conditioning gains. The shared inferential logic across all rows is the conditioned-model-vs-pooled-baseline comparison from Ch 15.

SS17.2 ("What HPC opens") is a diff-in-diff section examining what the framework genuinely adds vs reframes vs imports from other fields. Biology: Boyd explained why vague overlapping definitions could still support induction. Psychiatry: Kendler's MPC framework and Borsboom's network theory, with three disanalogies noted. The section identifies questions the framework reframes (typological co-occurrence as shared-mechanism prediction), questions it opens (category genesis as budding, not fission -- definiteness/deitality as template), and a methodological import (Meehl's taxometric methods applied to linguistic categories).

SS17.3 ("The gauntlet") develops three predictions. Prediction 1: asymmetric fraying -- when stabilisers weaken, diagnostics erode in structured order, not uniformly; English *data* is the running case. Prediction 2: sticky-lock drift -- usage/semantic pressure shifts first, morphosyntactic locks lag and fail in bursts. Prediction 3 (constructive): grammaticalization should show a phase transition rather than a continuous cline, with a point where an emergent cluster acquires enough independent maintenance to project on its own. Two defeat conditions follow: (D1) projection without maintenance -- a robust category with no identifiable causal structure; (D2) structure without lift -- variation that's a random walk once noise is controlled. The section includes methodological discussion of model criticism vs hypothesis testing, hierarchical variance structure, and the failure-map approach to evaluating the gauntlet.

SS17.4 ("Two cases under load") operationalises with *data* (degradation case) and pro-form gender (tight-system case). Table 17.2 gives predeclared stress-test criteria. The gender case develops three stabiliser-specific predictions (cognitive grounding, semantic transparency, alignment/repair) and three falsifying findings. Chain-coherence generates a system-level processing-cost prediction.

SS17.5 ("The research agenda") collects twelve open fronts across all chapters: chapter-specific debts (countability classifier-language tests, definiteness prosodic rescue and acquisition order, adjective-plumage falsifier, pro-form gender ToM tests, discourse-level evidence, grammaticality three-register factoring, social stabilisation conditioning demonstrations) and five cross-chapter questions (hub-node empirics, category comorbidity, taxometrics, competitive exclusion, developmental cluster tightening). The *otherwise* example returns as a concrete illustration of how the gauntlet changes research practice.

SS17.6 ("Conclusion") invokes Ladyman and Ross's scale-relative ontology, the meta-Occam principle, and comparative concepts. The chapter closes with the framework's core ontological claim: "the sustaining is the reality."
