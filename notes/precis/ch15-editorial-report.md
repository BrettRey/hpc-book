# Chapter 15 Editorial Report: The Social Stabilization of Kinds

---

## 1. Argument

The chapter's argument is sound and fills a genuine gap the book has been accumulating since Part II: why categories converge *across* speakers, not just within one mind. The logical spine is clean -- mixed bin diagnosis, two conditioning dimensions (community, activity), convergence zone, acquisition as source inference, indexicality as the inverse function, then the nominalist challenge answered. One argumentative tension: the chapter claims discourse community "isn't a third kind of conditioning" but rather the overlap of dialect and register (line 98, line 143), yet the mechanisms specific to discourse communities (peer review, degree conferral, citation practices) look qualitatively different from either convergence/divergence dynamics or self-monitored register shifting. The two-dimensional framing does real work to keep the chapter tidy, but it may suppress a genuinely distinct stabiliser type. The chapter would benefit from a sentence acknowledging this suppression explicitly rather than asserting the two-axis model covers everything.

## 2. Structure

The organisation serves the argument well. The opening vignette hooks the reader with the double copula noticing, the mixed-bin section delivers the conceptual tool, the unmixing runs through dialect/register/discourse community, acquisition provides developmental grounding, and indexicality closes the loop. Two structural observations. First, the transition from the mixed-bin section (S15.2) into the unmixing section (S15.3) is slightly abrupt -- the end of S15.2 sprawls into variationist sociolinguistics, Milroy's Belfast work, and "internet English" as a fat class, all in one dense paragraph (lines 67--68). That paragraph is doing three jobs (vindicating variationists, connecting to HPC, giving a negative case) and could be split. Second, the definiteness thread at the end (lines 205--206) is doing its job, but it feels slightly mechanical -- it applies the chapter's framework to definiteness without the specificity of earlier thread entries. A concrete prediction (e.g., dialectal variation in weak-definite licensing) would sharpen it.

## 3. Prose

The writing is strong throughout. The double copula vignette (lines 7--11) is excellent -- personal, specific, and immediately functional. "Call it noise often enough and every mechanism starts to look like an exception" (line 24) is one of the best single lines in the book. The waiter's "And yourself?" example (line 102) is vivid and does real work. Where the prose drags: the long paragraph at lines 67--68 is too dense and tries to do too much in one breath. The Sperber *Little Red Riding Hood* passage (lines 48--50) is well-written but slightly tangential -- the cultural-attractor concept is introduced and then not visibly load-bearing again. The closing section (S15.5, lines 181--203) is clean but could land harder; the final line "No conditioning gain, no maintenance claim" is a good slogan, but the three sentences before it (lines 200--203) read as mechanics rather than culmination.

## 4. Evidence

The evidence is well-chosen but asymmetric. The double copula (Coppock 2018, Yale GDP) and existential *there's/there are* (Krejci and Hilton 2022) are the two sustained cases, and both work well as mixed-bin illustrations. The developmental evidence (Mehler et al. 1988, Kinzler et al. 2007, Wiese 2023) is compelling and well-deployed. The Bausch et al. (2026) single-neuron evidence (line 162) is intriguing but sits as a parenthetical aside with a hedged connection ("doesn't identify com-sits directly, but it makes context-conditioned retrieval mechanistically plausible") -- it could be cut without loss, or integrated more tightly. The habitual *be* example (line 121) is vivid and personal, but it rests entirely on the author's observation of his own children; a citation to published work on habitual *be* diffusion (e.g., Rickford 1999 or Wolfram) would strengthen it. The Pickering and Garrod (2004) and Giles et al. (1991) citations are foundational but not developed beyond name-checking -- the chapter summarises their claims without showing the data or the mechanism in detail.

## 5. Cross-chapter coherence

The chapter fulfils its promises to earlier chapters cleanly: Ch 7's stabilisers are explicitly extended to the inter-speaker level (line 13), Ch 14's grammaticality-as-detector is reframed through indexicality (lines 175--179), and the Ch 8 failure-mode vocabulary is applied to "internet English" as a fat class (line 67). The forward connection to Ch 16 is explicit and functional (lines 200--203). Two potential gaps. First, the chapter invokes "field-relative projectibility" (line 71) and the "would-be" (line 177) from Ch 7 without developing them -- these feel like back-references for insiders rather than load-bearing deployments. If the Peircean apparatus is meant to do work in this chapter, the connection between conditioned probability and the interpretant-as-habit deserves a sentence or two rather than a parenthetical. Second, the book-level editorial report notes that Ch 15 "may belong in Part II, not Part IV" because social stabilisation is part of the theoretical apparatus. The chapter's opening (lines 5--17) implicitly addresses this by framing itself as answering a question "deferred since Part II," but it might be worth making the architectural choice explicit: social stabilisation comes last because it requires the case studies of Part III to motivate the conditioning structure.

## 6. House style issues

**En-dash vs. em-dash:** No em-dash violations detected; en-dashes with spaces are used consistently throughout (`~--`). Well done.

**`\term{}` usage:** Generally correct. "conditioned" is marked `\term{conditioned\ixsq{conditioned}}` on first use (line 44), as is "unconditioned probability" (line 63). However, "orderly heterogeneity" at line 111 is marked `\term{orderly heterogeneity\ixsq{orderly heterogeneity}}` -- this is Labov's term, not the book's own, so `\enquote{}` might be more appropriate on first mention with `\term{}` reserved for subsequent use as an adopted concept.

**`\mention{}` vs. `\term{}`:** The chapter correctly uses `\mention{}` for linguistic forms throughout (*there's*, *thing*, *problem*, etc.). One possible inconsistency: "Factory B" at line 149 is marked `\mention{Factory B}` -- this is a reference to the metaphorical factory, not a linguistic form being mentioned. Plain text or scare quotes would be more appropriate.

**`\enquote{}`:** Correctly used for actual quotations throughout (lines 115, 134, 136, 147, 179, 194). The "army and a navy" line at 115 is not attributed -- this is traditionally attributed to Max Weinreich, and the chapter might want a citation or at least "as the quip goes."

**`\emph{}` for emphasis vs. `\term{}`/`\mention{}`:** Lines 13, 17, 48, 131 use `\emph{}` for contrastive emphasis (*you*, *across*, *maintained*, *maintains*). These are correct uses of `\emph{}` -- emphasis, not mention. No violations here.

**Paragraph length:** Most paragraphs fall within the ~60-word target. The paragraph at lines 67--68 is a single sentence block that runs extremely long (approximately 200+ words). Similarly, the paragraph at lines 119 (Convergence/divergence dynamics) runs long. Both should be split.

**`\begin{exe}` / `\end{exe}` usage:** Lines 28--34 use `\begin{exe}` and `\begin{xlist}` rather than the house-style `\ea ... \z` pattern. The style guide specifies `langsci-gb4e` with `\ea ... \z` and no `exe` environment. This should be converted.

**Citations:** `\citet{}` and `\citep{}` are used correctly throughout. One note: line 40 uses `\textcite{coppock2018double}` -- the house style specifies `\textcite{}` for narrative citations, which is correct, but check that this is consistent with the rest of the book (the style guide lists `\citep{}` and `\textcite{}`).

## 7. Specific recommendations

1. **Convert the numbered example at lines 28--34 from `\begin{exe}/\begin{xlist}` to the house-style `\ea/\z` pattern.** The style guide is explicit: no `exe` environment.

2. **Split the long paragraph at lines 67--68.** It currently does three things: (a) positions HPC relative to variationist sociolinguistics, (b) identifies what HPC adds (principled insistence on mechanisms, explicit prediction about degradation), and (c) gives a negative case (Milroy's Belfast, "internet English" as fat class). Each deserves its own paragraph or at least its own sentence-level break.

3. **Split the convergence/divergence paragraph at line 119.** It runs from Pickering and Garrod through accommodation, divergence, Gallois et al., basin dynamics, and overt correction. Two or three shorter paragraphs would improve pacing and let each mechanism breathe.

4. **Add a citation to the habitual *be* observation at line 121.** The claim that the author's children use habitual *be* is anecdotal; a reference to published work on habitual *be* diffusion beyond AAE (e.g., Rickford 1999 or Green 2002) would ground it.

5. **Address the two-axis suppression problem.** At line 98 or line 143, add a sentence acknowledging that discourse-community mechanisms (gatekeeping, credentialing) may constitute a genuinely distinct stabiliser axis rather than merely the overlap of dialect and register. The current framing ("not a third kind of conditioning but a zone of high mechanistic density") is clean but arguably too tidy.

6. **Trim or integrate Bausch et al. (2026) at line 162.** The single-neuron evidence is interesting but sits as a hedged parenthetical with no clear payoff. Either connect it more tightly to the com-sits argument (explaining what context reinstatement would look like for dialect/register conditioning) or cut it.

7. **Sharpen the definiteness thread (lines 205--206).** Add a concrete, testable prediction involving social conditioning -- e.g., dialectal variation in weak-definite licensing should show the mixed-bin pattern (tight within dialects, loose when pooled).

8. **Consider attributing the "army and a navy" quip at line 115.** It is traditionally attributed to Max Weinreich (YIVO, 1945). A brief attribution or footnote would be more scholarly than the unattributed form.

9. **Reconsider `\mention{Factory B}` at line 149.** This is a reference to a metaphorical entity, not a linguistic form. Plain text or scare quotes would be stylistically more appropriate.

10. **Reconsider `\term{orderly heterogeneity}` at line 111.** Since this is Labov's term being introduced via attribution, `\enquote{orderly heterogeneity}` on first mention may be more appropriate, with `\term{}` reserved for subsequent uses where the book adopts the concept.

11. **Make the Part IV placement explicit.** Somewhere in the opening (between lines 13 and 17), a sentence explaining *why* social stabilisation comes in Part IV rather than Part II would pre-empt the reader's architectural question. Something like: "The social layer requires the case studies of Part III to motivate what conditioning is conditioning *of*."

12. **Tighten the Sperber passage (lines 48--50).** The *Little Red Riding Hood* analogy is well-written but the "cultural attractor" concept isn't visibly picked up again. Either use the term later (e.g., when discussing register attractors in S15.3.2) or trim the passage to a single sentence.
