# Chapter 13: The Category Zipper

The chapter argues that the HPC framework is scale-invariant: the same diagnostic logic -- property clusters maintained by identifiable stabilizers, tested by projectibility and homeostasis -- applies from phonemes through words and grammatical categories to constructions. What changes across levels is not the framework but the coupling regime: how tightly form binds to meaning, where the interpretant bears the load, and which stabilizer families dominate. The organizing metaphor is a zipper whose teeth engage wherever mechanisms make engagement cheap and failure costly.

The chapter connects backward to Part III's four case studies (countability, definiteness, lexical categories, gender) and to the coupling continuum introduced in Section 8.x, which distinguished hard, loose, and composite coupling. It connects forward to Chapter 14 by identifying morphosyntax as the "zone of maximum systematicity" -- the region where form-object-interpretant coupling is both tight and obligatory -- and posing the question of whether grammaticality itself is an HPC kind. The promise to the reader is that the four preceding chapters are not independent case studies but points on a single mechanism-defined gradient, and that this gradient extends beyond grammar in both directions.

The chapter proceeds by controlled ascent through four tiers, each analysed via the same triadic structure (form, object, interpretant). At the phoneme level (hard coupling), form determines object with minimal mediation; the stabilizers are Stevens's quantal regions, Lindblom's dispersion pressure, Kuhl et al.'s perceptual magnets, and community transmission norms. Evidence comes from PHOIBLE 2.0 data (inventory-size ridgelines, a logistic model predicting /y/-presence from vowel inventory size with ROC-AUC around 0.70) and a stress test via the pin/pen merger, where coupling fails predictably in the environment where acoustic cues are weakest. At the word level (opaque coupling), form-object pairing is arbitrary but stable through frequency and norms; the key reference is Miller (2021) on lexemes as HPC kinds, with HistWords COHA embeddings showing that high-drift adjectives like *nice*, *sick*, *gay* retain cohesive neighbourhood trajectories (macro-F1 of 0.84 on a semantic-class recovery task). The go/went pattern provides a stress test: Bybee's frequency-analogy interaction predicts which irregulars resist regularization and which succumb. At the grammatical-category level (loose coupling), a comparison matrix synthesizes Part III's findings, mapping each case study's coupling tightness, signature diagnostic fact, and failure mode. At the construction level (composite coupling), a battery of ten constructions tested across UD English corpora (GUM, EWT, GUMReddit) demonstrates that cue bundles stabilize constructional identity, with the *or even* construction showing strong cross-corpus transfer (PR-AUC 0.886) and parallelism identified as a dominant stabilizer through ablation. Crucially, two designed-to-fail cases -- pooled resultatives and *X much?* -- confirm that the framework says no where it should: resultatives fragment under transfer, and *X much?* is register-local.

A dedicated negative-cases section provides further discriminatory power. Academic register fails because it is a conditioning effect, not a stable kind; Indo-European fails because it is a historical lineage without homeostatic maintenance; polysynthesis fails because it is a multiply realizable surface property without shared cross-family stabilizers. A null case -- the absence of grammatical definiteness in Russian despite the presence of the semantic concepts -- extends the coupling continuum to zero. The stabilizer-weighting map (Table 13.x) and mediation-gradient section then recast the whole ascent in Peircean terms: as we move up the stack, the interpretant shifts from automatic (phonemes) through stored (words) and mediating (grammar) to constitutive (constructions).

Two places feel thin. First, the discourse level appears in the stabilizer-weighting table but receives no section of its own and no evidence; it reads like a placeholder extending the gradient rather than a tested tier. Second, the Peircean mediation-gradient section (Section 13.6) restates the coupling continuum in sign-theoretic vocabulary but does not obviously add explanatory content beyond what the coupling analysis already provides -- the reader may wonder whether it is doing work or just relabelling. The construction-tier analysis is impressively operationalized, but the asymmetry with the less empirically developed grammar tier (which is handled by back-reference to Part III rather than new analysis) means the chapter's evidential centre of gravity sits at the endpoints rather than the middle of its own gradient.
