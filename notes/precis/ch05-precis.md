# Chapter 5: Discrete from continuous

Chapter 5 argues that discrete linguistic categories can emerge from continuous substrates without essences, and that the apparent tension between gradient intuitions and categorical structure dissolves once you distinguish the geometry of categories from the mechanisms that maintain them. The central claim is what the chapter calls "dynamic discreteness": boundaries are sharp, but they are achieved by active maintenance, not given by definition.

The chapter opens with a personal origin story (Ryan Nefdt's suggestion of phase transitions, a dormant paper on Sorites tolerance suddenly finding its purpose) and then states the puzzle: linguistic properties (frequency, acoustic cues, semantic features) vary continuously, yet categories (noun, grammatical, /i/ vs /e/) behave discretely. Both essentialism and exemplarism fail here. The phase-transition analogy from physics provides the guiding intuition: water has no essence of liquidity, but the phase boundaries are real and sharp, maintained by dynamics at a particular scale of observation.

The formal apparatus is a hyperreal model of vague predicates, drawing on nonstandard analysis and developed fully in Reynolds (2025). The key move is "Relative Tolerance": changes that are negligibly small relative to the current scale preserve category membership, while appreciable changes may not. Formalised with infinitesimals, this yields a sharp boundary at a hypernatural index that is structurally determinate but epistemically inaccessible. The chapter distinguishes this from Williamson's epistemicism (which posits brute inaccessibility) and from Khalidi's fuzzy realism (which dissolves the boundary altogether). The hyperreal view predicts structured boundary drift and variance signatures that epistemicism does not explain and fuzzy realism does not expect.

The formal geometry is then married to HPC mechanisms. The chapter extends the one-dimensional Sorites chain to a multi-dimensional feature space, constructing a basin-of-attraction landscape where category cores are potential minima and boundaries are ridges and saddle points. The metric question (how to measure distance in a heterogeneous feature space) is addressed via a Gower-style embedding with weighted norms, linked to Gardenfors's conceptual spaces. The weights encode homeostatic importance: properties central to the cluster get large weights. The chapter is careful to note that the feature space is hybrid, mixing conceptual dimensions (animacy, eventivity) with grammatical diagnostics (plural morphology, comparative constructions), and that HPC mechanisms keep the two aligned.

Three worked examples anchor the argument. The diachronic drift of "fun" from noun to adjective is tracked through COHA corpus data, showing convergent adjectivalisation across multiple degree-modifier environments. English reciprocals ("each other," "one another") are presented as stable boundary items, drawing on Reynolds (2025), with correspondence analysis, Jaccard distances, and specification curves all confirming their intermediate position between pronoun and determinative anchors. The Shilluk number system illustrates cross-linguistic basin comparison: shared functional mechanisms (individuation) produce overlapping basins even when exponence differs radically.

The chapter's strongest empirical contribution is the "two-layer model" of grammaticality versus acceptability. Grammaticality is discrete (binary, maintained by mechanisms); acceptability is its noisy measurement. This predicts that judgment variance should peak near category boundaries and fall off into basins, a signature partially confirmed by Sprouse et al. (2013) and Dabrowska (2010). The chapter explicitly promises a full treatment in Chapter 14.

One tension is the weight placed on the hyperreal formalism. The chapter offers a skip path, acknowledging that the mathematics may lose readers, but the entire empirical-prediction story (variance signatures, structured drift) depends on taking the formalism seriously rather than treating it as merely heuristic. Whether the hyperreal apparatus does genuine explanatory work beyond what simpler threshold-plus-noise models could deliver is a question the chapter raises but does not fully resolve. The connection between the formal model and the mechanistic story is asserted as complementary, but the chapter could say more about what each component contributes that the other cannot. The discussion of metrics, while honest about the lack of a uniquely privileged distance measure, risks leaving the empirical programme somewhat underdetermined.

The chapter closes by threading definiteness forward: borderline definites like "go to the hospital" should show the predicted variance asymmetry if definiteness has the basin structure described here. This sets up Part III's case studies.
