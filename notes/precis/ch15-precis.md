# Chapter 15: The Social Stabilization of Kinds

Chapter 15 answers a question the book has been deferring since Part II: why do categories stay aligned *across* speakers, not just within a single mind? The mechanisms introduced in Chapter 7 (acquisition, entrenchment, interactive alignment, iterated transmission) are cognitive and individual. They explain why *you* maintain a cluster, but not why your cluster and mine converge. This chapter supplies the missing social layer, arguing that linguistic categories are coordination equilibria maintained by social mechanisms operating between minds.

The chapter opens with an autobiographical vignette: the author notices the double copula ("The claim is, is that...") on a podcast and recognises the noticing itself as a mechanism at work. The double copula, documented by Janda (1980) and analysed by Coppock (2018) as a focus-marking device, serves as a running example throughout. It looks like noise when measured against a single idealised system but resolves into a stable, conditioned pattern when register and discourse context are modelled. This leads to the chapter's central diagnostic tool: the "mixed bin" problem. Apparent categorical looseness often reflects the pooling of internally tight subsystems, like dumping bolts from two precise factories into one pile and mistaking the bimodal distribution for manufacturing noise. Krejci and Hilton's (2022) analysis of existential "there's" vs. "there are" as a three-way mixture (agreeing, invariant presentative, levelled) provides the second sustained example.

The unmixing runs along two dimensions: community (dialect, after Halliday's "variety according to user") and activity (register, "variety according to use"). Dialect maintenance is illustrated through convergence and divergence dynamics (Pickering and Garrod on interactive priming, Giles on accommodation, Gallois et al. on divergence as identity work), with Milroy's Belfast study serving as a confirmed prediction: when network density collapses, dialect features level. Register maintenance is illustrated through the academic register, where explicit institutional gatekeeping (red ink, peer review) is genuinely a primary mechanism, unlike most of the grammar where invisible social inference does the heavy lifting. Where community and activity mechanisms converge, the chapter identifies Swales's "discourse community" as a zone of high mechanistic density rather than a third conditioning type.

The developmental evidence is handled through Wiese's (2023) "communicative situations" framework and Kinzler et al.'s (2007) finding that infants perform source inference from accent before they have metalinguistic concepts or named-language abstractions. Children don't start with one mixed bin and later discover variation; they build conditioned structure from the beginning.

The chapter's most theoretically consequential move is Section 15.4, on indexicality as the inverse function. If grammar predicts form from social condition, indexicality runs the inference backwards: hearing a form and attributing a source. This closes the stabilisation loop and, critically, reframes grammaticality judgments from Chapter 14: the "feeling" of ungrammaticality is partly an indexical signal, a register mismatch mislabelled as a structural violation.

Section 15.5 returns to Haspelmath and Croft's nominalist challenge from Chapter 2, now equipped to reply: categories are conditioned profiles stabilised by mechanisms, neither essences nor mere stipulations. O'Connor's (2019) work on coordination games sharpens this into a dual-payoff model (communicative clarity rewards convergence; identity management rewards divergence), predicting not just stable categories but specific stress points where stability should fray.

The chapter closes with a methodological commitment rather than a victory claim: if social stabilisation is real, models should improve when conditioning is made explicit. "No conditioning gain, no maintenance claim." Chapter 16 is promised as the place where this and other claims are turned into a gauntlet with observables and defeat conditions.

One tension: the chapter relies heavily on the mixed-bin metaphor, which works beautifully for the cases chosen (double copula, existential "there's") but could seem to explain away genuine categorical looseness rather than engaging with it. The chapter flags this risk in its defeat-check table but doesn't fully stress-test it against cases where conditioning fails to recover tight clusters. A second mild strain is the treatment of discourse community as "not a third kind of conditioning" but rather an overlap zone; the institutional gatekeeping mechanisms specific to discourse communities (peer review, citation practices, degree conferral) feel qualitatively different enough from dialect convergence or register self-monitoring that the two-dimensional framing may be doing some work to suppress a genuinely distinct axis.
