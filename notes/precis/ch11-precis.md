# Chapter 11: Lexical categories and their maintenance

Chapter 11 opens the dictionary's inventory of word classes and asks which are genuine HPCs -- categories maintained by converging mechanisms and projectible to novel instances -- and which are something less: convenient labels, historical residues, or bookkeeping categories that survive because they are cheap. The central claim is that word classes are not uniformly "real" in the same way: they fall along a gradient of mechanism density, and that gradient predicts their cross-linguistic stability, acquisitional trajectory, and diachronic resilience.

The chapter connects backward to almost every theoretical move in Part II. The noun/name distinction that opens the chapter reprises the field-relative projectibility argument from Chapter 6: noun and name are distinct HPCs with overlapping extensions, each projectible for different purposes, just as definiteness and deitality came apart in Chapter 10. The mechanism inventory (discourse frequency, morphological agreement, acquisition, structural analogy, semantic recruitment) draws on Chapter 7's stabilisers. The fat/thin/robust diagnostic labels come from Chapter 8's failure modes. The coupling continuum from Section 8.6 is applied within a single level of description for the first time: nouns and verbs are tight; adjectives are genuinely loose; adverbs are not on the continuum at all.

Four configurations organise the evidence. Nouns and verbs are the "skeleton" -- robust HPCs where five mechanisms converge, analogised to the hydrodynamic convergence that gives sharks and dolphins similar body plans (Zimmer 2015). Adjectives are "plumage" -- a thin HPC present as a large open class in English but shrinking to a dozen core items or disappearing entirely across Dixon's (2004) typological gradient, because the supporting mechanisms (discourse task, acquisition timing, morphological glue) are shallower than for nouns and verbs. The adverb label is a "wastebasket" -- a fat class with no category-level coupling, concealing at least one genuine sub-category (manner adverbs, with their own mechanism convergence) alongside degree modifiers, focusing adverbs, and connective adverbs that project only within their own narrow domains. The chapter also shows the wastebasket actively misclassifying items, arguing (via Reynolds 2024) that CGEL's reclassification of *more* as an adverb in analytic comparatives rests on a single diagnostic thread -- a thread that frays under scrutiny.

The pronoun section introduces a "braid" -- the most architecturally novel move. Three types of category operate in the same lexical territory: a lexical category (pronoun, maintained by paradigmatic mechanisms like case morphology), an operational category (the IRE family -- interrogative, relative, exclamative -- maintained by fronting, gap-binding, and scope constraints), and a semantic concept (pro-form, maintained by discourse pressure). The three are independently maintained and independently acquired. The diachronic evidence is striking: personal pronouns descend from the demonstrative stock (the /th/-initial cohort); interrogative *who* traces to PIE *\*kwis*; relative *who* grammaticalised later still through Middle English. Acquisition recapitulates this order. The lyrebird's tail serves as the biological analogue: one structure, three maintenance regimes.

The chapter's main tension is in the adjective section. The plumage diagnosis depends on showing that mechanism density tracks class thickness cross-linguistically, and the chapter acknowledges a falsifier: a small closed adjective class with its own (non-borrowed) morphological paradigm would be skeletal, not plumage. Whether such a case exists is left open. A second tension is subtler: the chapter argues that the fat adverb label survives because it is cheap and sociologically entrenched, but does not fully develop the claim that "lexical category" is itself an HPC with a small-count property -- the idea is sketched in a paragraph but not tested. Finally, the chapter's treatment of languages claimed to lack a noun-verb distinction is careful but brief; the counterfactual (no frequency asymmetries, no processing cost, no acquisitional priority) that would falsify the convergence story is stated but not explored with specific language data.

The chapter promises Chapter 12 on pro-form gender (the third prediction about gender diffusing beyond the pronoun system) and Chapter 13 as a synthesis that asks how the system of categories holds together.
