# Chapter 10: Editorial Report

*Definiteness and Deitality*

---

## 1. Argument

The chapter's central claim -- that the literature has been conflating two distinct HPCs (a semantic definiteness cluster and a morphosyntactic form cluster) -- is sound and well-motivated. The architecture explicitly parallels Chapter 9's individuation/count split, which gives the reader a familiar scaffold. The Millikan proper-function analysis does genuine explanatory work: it explains *why* the coupling exists (shared demonstrative origin), *why* it tolerates slippage (derived functions parasitize stable side effects), and *why* the slippage is systematic rather than random. The one logical gap worth flagging is a circularity risk in the acquisition prediction (SS10.7): the claim that children master distributional restrictions before semantic ones is used both as evidence *for* the two-cluster architecture and as a prediction *generated by* it. The chapter needs to decide which role this finding plays, or at least acknowledge the dual use.

## 2. Structure

The chapter's organisation is clean: hook, theoretical diagnosis, semantic cluster, form cluster, coupling, mechanisms, slippage cases, audit, forward transition. The sequencing of SS10.3 (definiteness cluster) before SS10.4 (form cluster) before SS10.5 (coupling) is the right order -- you need both parts before you can discuss how they interact. One structural question: the "Passing the tests" section (SS10.7) feels slightly perfunctory after the rich slippage cases in SS10.6. The falsifiable predictions are stated but not given the breathing room they deserve. Consider whether the prosodic-rescue prediction and the acquisition-asymmetry prediction warrant their own worked paragraphs rather than being formatted as bold-labelled blocks, which read more like notes than prose. The "Audit output" section (SS10.8) is formulaic by design (it follows the Ch 8 template), and it works, but it repeats material from SS10.7 almost verbatim -- the stress tests in SS10.8 could cross-reference SS10.7 rather than restating.

## 3. Prose

The opening hook is excellent -- the *veil*/*hat* pair is immediately gripping and earns the reader's attention without fuss. The Borges epigraph works beautifully with the generic-definites discussion in SS10.6.2, and the chapter is one of the few that explicitly returns to its epigraph, which gives it a satisfying internal arc. The voice is confident throughout, with flashes of genuine wit: "If it sounds like a minor villain in a low-budget sci-fi, that's partly the point" (SS10.7); "The pattern will happily adopt any new ritual, especially if it comes with a subscription" (SS10.6.1). The middle sections (SS10.3 and SS10.4) are more expository and slightly drier than the surrounding material -- the definiteness-cluster section in particular reads as a textbook summary of Heim, Russell, and Hawkins before the HPC payoff arrives. The pacing recovers well in SS10.5 (coupling) and SS10.6 (slippage), which are the chapter's strongest writing.

## 4. Evidence

The evidence base is appropriate but somewhat asymmetric. The form-cluster diagnostics are well-chosen and clearly presented in Table 10.2. The developmental evidence (Rozendaal and Baker, Brockmann et al., De Cat) does real work for the two-cluster claim. The weak-definite and generic-definite cases are well-handled. But the cross-linguistic section (SS10.8) is thin -- Greek gets one sentence, Japanese gets one sentence, and the prediction about grammaticalization producing form clusters wherever demonstratives become articles is stated but not tested against any specific language in detail. This is a weakness the precis already flags, and the book-level report notes Part III's deliberate evidential gradient, but at least one cross-linguistic case developed at two or three paragraphs' length (German would work, given the Ch 6 precedent) would strengthen the chapter substantially. The proper-names discussion (SS10.6.3) is the section where evidence is weakest relative to the theoretical weight placed on it: the Gasparri material on generic readings of names is interesting but somewhat tangential to the two-cluster architecture.

## 5. Cross-chapter coherence

The chapter fulfils the promises made in Chapters 5 (definiteness as a test case for variance signatures at basin boundaries) and 8 (the coupling continuum, with definiteness sitting loose of countability). The Peircean apparatus from Ch 7 is deployed substantively in SS10.2 (interpretant as habit coupling form and construal, field-relative projectibility), which is one of the stronger Peirce deployments in Part III -- it does explanatory work rather than merely relabelling. The transition to Ch 11 is well-handled: the reader understands that the next case will be still looser. One missed connection: the chapter could link its Millikan proper-function analysis more explicitly to Ch 4's treatment of Millikan's copied kinds and unicepts. The term "proper function" arrives in SS10.5 without reminding the reader that Millikan's framework was introduced in Ch 4 as part of the HPC apparatus -- a single cross-reference would close this loop. The "deitality" term is used in Chs 11 and 13 as if established; this chapter earns it, and the earning is adequate.

## 6. House style issues

**En-dash/em-dash.** The epigraph at line 8 uses an em-dash (`---`) in the attribution: `--- Borges`. Should be an en-dash with non-breaking space: `~-- Borges`. (The Borges attribution is the only em-dash I found; the rest of the chapter uses en-dashes correctly.)

**`\term{}` vs `\mention{}` usage.** The chapter is generally good here. One inconsistency: at line 419, `\mention{deital}` and `\mention{definite}` are used where the words are being introduced/discussed as *terms* (category labels), not cited as linguistic forms. These should arguably be `\term{deital}` and `\term{definite}` to match the `\term{deitality}` usage two sentences earlier. Similarly at line 421, `\mention{definiteness}` in "continued equivocation on \mention{definiteness}" -- this is a term, not a form.

**Bold labels in prose.** The mechanisms section (SS10.5, lines 314--328) uses inline bold (**inertia**, **bootstrap**, **error-correction**, **protection**, **filter**) as summary labels. The falsifiable-predictions section (SS10.7, lines 400--406) uses `\textbf{Prosodic rescue}`, `\textbf{Dialectal preservation}`, `\textbf{Acquisition asymmetry}` as bold-labelled blocks. The style guide (SS "Bold, Bullets, Enumeration") explicitly says "Avoid bold labels in prose." The mechanisms section could use these labels as parenthetical asides or inline emphasis; the falsifiable-predictions section should be restructured as prose with transitions.

**Bulleted list.** Lines 104--108 use a `\begin{itemize}` list for three mechanisms binding the definiteness cluster. The style guide says to avoid lists for "Argumentative sequences" and prefer prose with transitions. This is a borderline case (it's more of a catalogue than an argument), but converting to "First... Second... Third..." prose would be more consistent with the rest of the chapter.

**Footnote style.** The footnote at line 56 (on specificity) is quite long (three sentences, with examples). This is fine by itself, but the parenthetical example sentences within it (`\mention{I'm looking for a book~-- it's on my shelf}` and `\mention{I'm looking for a book~-- any book}`) aren't wrapped in `\ea...\z` numbered-example format. They may not need to be (they're illustrative asides), but the chapter's other inline examples use proper `\ea...\z` markup.

**Table footnote.** The footnote marker in Table 10.2 (line 168, `\textsuperscript{*}`) and its corresponding note (line 182) use a manual asterisk. This is functional but not the standard `langsci-gb4e` approach; consider using `\tablefootnote{}` or a `\tablenote{}` macro if available.

**Orphaned sentence.** Line 326: "The list isn't exhaustive." is a single-sentence paragraph. It reads as a self-conscious aside; either fold it into the preceding paragraph or drop it (the reader will understand that five mechanisms aren't all of them).

## 7. Specific recommendations

1. **Resolve the acquisition-prediction circularity (SS10.7, SS10.3).** The developmental sequence (familiarity before uniqueness before identifiability) is presented as evidence for two clusters in SS10.3 and as a prediction in SS10.7. Pick one role, or add a sentence acknowledging the dual use: "The acquisition data were part of the motivation for the two-cluster architecture; the prediction is that *the specific ordering* (distributional before semantic) will hold for the form cluster as well."

2. **Expand the cross-linguistic section (SS10.8).** Add one developed example -- German would work, given its demonstrative-derived article and well-documented weak definites (*ins Krankenhaus gehen* vs *in das Krankenhaus gehen*). Two to three paragraphs showing the architecture at work in a second language would answer the section's own question ("English-specific or universal potential?") more convincingly.

3. **Remove bold labels in the mechanisms section (SS10.5) and falsifiable-predictions section (SS10.7).** Replace `\textbf{inertia}`, `\textbf{bootstrap}`, etc. with inline parenthetical glosses or light emphasis. Restructure the falsifiable-predictions block as flowing prose with ordinal transitions ("First, prosody should selectively rescue...").

4. **Convert the itemized list at lines 104--108 to prose.** "Three distinct mechanisms bind them. Discourse tracking (seconds to minutes) binds familiarity... Domain restriction (seconds) binds uniqueness... Theory of Mind (years) binds identifiability..."

5. **Fix the em-dash in the Borges attribution (line 8).** Change `---` to `~--`.

6. **Add a Millikan cross-reference in SS10.5.** Where "proper function" is first introduced (line 196), add a brief back-reference: "Millikan's proper-function framework (introduced in Chapter 4) clarifies the logic."

7. **Tighten the proper-names discussion (SS10.6.3).** The Gasparri/Dupre material on generic readings of names (lines 369--371) is interesting but takes the discussion away from the two-cluster architecture and toward the philosophy of naming. Consider cutting the Gasparri discussion to one sentence and using the space for a sharper summary of how names demonstrate the "definiteness without deitality" cell in Table 10.1.

8. **Reduce redundancy between SS10.7 (Passing the tests) and SS10.8 (Audit output).** The stress tests in SS10.8 largely repeat the falsifiable predictions from SS10.7. Have SS10.8 cross-reference SS10.7 for the predictions and use its space for the stress-test framing (what would force *downgrading* vs mere *scope narrowing*), which is the audit template's distinctive contribution.

9. **Fix `\term{}`/`\mention{}` inconsistency at lines 419--421.** Use `\term{deital}` and `\term{definite}` when the words name categories; reserve `\mention{}` for when they appear as cited forms.

10. **Fold or drop the orphaned sentence at line 326.** "The list isn't exhaustive" either joins the preceding paragraph or is cut. The mechanisms section already implicitly conveys non-exhaustiveness by saying "at least five distinct pressures" (line 312).
