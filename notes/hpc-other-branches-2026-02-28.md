# HPC Across Linguistic Branches: Genuinely New Questions

**Date:** 2026-02-28
**Context:** Gauntlet reframing exercise. The book's case studies are morphosyntactic. Does HPC open genuinely new questions at other grains of linguistic organization, or is it just relabelling?

**Method:** For each branch, identify (a) what HPC lets you *ask* that you couldn't before, (b) concrete predictions, (c) falsification conditions, (d) where the framework breaks down.

---

## 1. Phonology

### 1.1 Are Phonemes HPCs?

**What HPC lets you ask:**

Traditional phonology: "Is /p/ defined by distinctive features?" vs "Is /p/ an exemplar cloud?" HPC dissolves this: phonemes are maintained property clusters. The question becomes: **what mechanisms maintain the clustering?**

**The clustering properties:**
- Acoustic properties (VOT, place of articulation, etc.)
- Articulatory properties (lip closure, aspiration)
- Distributional properties (can appear word-initially, contrasts with /b/)
- Perceptual properties (triggers specific categorical perception)
- Phonotactic properties (participates in voicing assimilation)

**The maintenance mechanisms:**
- **Perceptual magnet effect** (Kuhl 1991): prototypical tokens warp perceptual space, pulling nearby tokens toward the prototype. Mechanism that keeps acoustic variation clustered.
- **Hyperarticulation in clear speech:** speakers boost phonetic contrasts when clarity is needed. Keeps clusters distinct.
- **Interactive alignment:** speakers converge on shared phonetic norms in conversation (Babel 2012). Inter-speaker mechanism.
- **Phonotactic patterns:** /p/ participates in voicing assimilation, which keeps it aligned with other obstruents. Distributional mechanism.
- **Orthographic feedback:** literacy can stabilize phonemic distinctions (especially for educated speakers). Cultural mechanism.

**What projection means for phonemes:**

Knowing a sound is /p/ predicts:
- Its distribution (can appear syllable-initially)
- Its participation in phonological processes (devoices in word-final position in German)
- Its perceptual category (VOT > 30ms triggers /p/ percept in English)
- Its phonotactic behavior (can cluster with /l/ but not /n/ in onset)

This is *not* trivial. Prototype theory in phonology (Johnson 2007, Pierrehumbert 2001) describes clouds of variants but doesn't predict their *coherence across contexts*. HPC adds: the cloud is maintained by mechanisms, and its projectibility comes from mechanism-driven clustering.

**Concrete predictions:**

1. **Hub-node asymmetry:** If you experimentally perturb the perceptual magnet (e.g., shift the prototype via training), phonotactic participation should destabilize faster than articulatory properties. The hub-node question: which property is load-bearing? Testable with perceptual retraining paradigms.

2. **Mechanism ablation:** In second-language acquisition, learners lack native perceptual magnets. If phoneme categories are mechanism-maintained, L2 categories should show *looser* coupling between acoustic and distributional properties than L1 categories. Prediction: acoustic variability in L2 /p/ should not predict phonotactic behavior as strongly as in L1.

3. **Merger = HPC death:** In phoneme mergers (e.g., /ʍ/ → /w/ in most English dialects), the maintenance mechanisms should decouple *before* the acoustic distinction disappears. Test: measure perceptual magnet strength, hyperarticulation rates, and phonotactic participation diachronically. If all decline together, it's gradual erosion. If perceptual magnet weakens first, it's hub-node collapse.

**Falsification:**

- If acoustic and distributional properties vary independently across speakers (no clustering), phonemes aren't HPCs.
- If perturbing one property (e.g., acoustic) doesn't affect others (e.g., phonotactic), there's no maintenance network.
- If phoneme mergers happen without mechanism weakening, HPC adds nothing to frequency-based accounts.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Allophones:** Are [pʰ] and [p] separate HPCs or variants within /p/? HPC doesn't clearly distinguish categorical and gradient structure. If allophonic variation is rule-governed (complementary distribution), the "cluster" is actually *two* clusters maintained by the same higher-level mechanism. But then we've reintroduced rules.
- **Underspecification:** Phonological theory often assumes features are underspecified (e.g., /t/ is unspecified for voicing, gets filled in by rule). HPC assumes rich property clusters. These are incompatible. If underspecification is right, HPC is wrong about phonemes.
- **What maintains the mechanism?** Perceptual magnets are neural. Phonotactics are distributional. These are ontologically heterogeneous. HPC doesn't have a story about how neural and distributional mechanisms interact to maintain a single cluster. Boyd's mechanisms are typically same-level (genetic, ecological). Phonology crosses levels (neural, cognitive, social, distributional).

### 1.2 Phonological Processes: Mechanisms or Properties?

**What HPC lets you ask:**

Traditional phonology treats processes (assimilation, deletion, epenthesis) as *rules* or *constraints*. HPC reframes: **are these processes mechanisms that maintain clusters, or are they properties of the clusters themselves?**

Example: voicing assimilation in English plurals (*cats* /s/, *dogs* /z/). Is this:
- A mechanism that maintains the /s/-/z/ distinction (by aligning voicing across morpheme boundaries)?
- A property of the plural morpheme cluster (its realization is context-sensitive)?

**Genuine new question:** Can you distinguish maintenance mechanisms from emergent properties of clustering? In biology, gene flow is clearly a mechanism (causal input). In phonology, assimilation might be both: it's caused by articulatory ease (mechanism) but also a diagnostic property of the phoneme cluster (outcome).

**Concrete prediction:**

If assimilation is a mechanism, removing it should destabilize the phoneme cluster. Test: in dialects where voicing assimilation is lost (some L2 English speakers produce *cats* with [z]), does the /s/-/z/ distinction show more acoustic variability? If yes, assimilation is a maintenance mechanism. If no, it's just a realization rule.

**Where HPC struggles:**

The mechanism/property distinction collapses in phonology. Assimilation is both cause (articulatory alignment mechanism) and effect (diagnostic property of the cluster). HPC from biology assumes clean causal arrows. Phonology has feedback loops everywhere.

### 1.3 Sound Change: HPC Death and Budding

**What HPC lets you ask:**

Traditional historical phonology describes sound changes (Great Vowel Shift, chain shifts) but doesn't ask: **when does one phoneme become two? When do two merge into one?**

HPC offers:
- **Merger = HPC death.** Two clusters lose distinctive mechanisms (e.g., /ʍ/-/w/ merge when aspiration distinction is no longer maintained).
- **Split = budding.** One cluster develops *two* independent maintenance regimes (e.g., Old English /k/ splits into /k/ and /tʃ/ when palatalization becomes phonemicized).

**Genuine new question (from biology's speciation):** Is split a gradual process or a phase transition? Does the new phoneme acquire independent maintenance incrementally, or is there a critical point where it "budgets off"?

**Concrete prediction:**

Chain shifts (e.g., Northern Cities Vowel Shift) involve multiple phonemes moving in coordinated fashion. HPC predicts: the coordination is maintained by a *shared mechanism* (e.g., perceptual contrast maintenance — /æ/ raises to stay distinct from /ɑ/, which raises to stay distinct from /ɔ/). If you perturb the shared mechanism (e.g., introduce a new phoneme that disrupts the contrast space), the chain shift should destabilize *as a unit*.

Test: in dialects undergoing chain shift, introduce lexical borrowings that occupy intermediate positions in vowel space. If the chain shift stalls or reverses, shared mechanism is confirmed. If it proceeds unaffected, coordination is coincidental.

**Falsification:**

If chain shifts proceed without shared mechanisms (just independent random walks that happen to coordinate), HPC adds nothing.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Directionality:** Sound changes have directionality (lenition, fortition, raising, lowering). HPC doesn't predict *which* direction change goes, only that change happens when mechanisms weaken. Optimality Theory and usage-based phonology have better stories about directionality (articulatory ease, frequency effects).
- **Actuation problem:** Why does a sound change start when it does? HPC says "mechanisms weaken," but doesn't say *why* they weaken. Boyd's HPC is synchronic. Diachrony needs an external trigger (contact, social change, population structure). HPC doesn't provide it.

### 1.4 Suprasegmental Categories: Tone, Stress, Intonation

**What HPC lets you ask:**

Are tone categories (Mandarin Tone 1 vs Tone 2) HPCs? What about stress patterns (trochaic vs iambic)? Intonation contours (L+H* vs H*)?

**The clustering question:** Tones cluster:
- Pitch contour (rising vs falling)
- Duration (tone-bearing units are longer)
- Phonotactic distribution (some tones blocked in certain positions)
- Morphophonological behavior (tone sandhi)

**The maintenance question:** What keeps Tone 2 distinct from Tone 3 in Mandarin? Mechanisms:
- Perceptual contrast (pitch height and contour)
- Tone sandhi rules (Tone 3 changes before another Tone 3)
- Orthographic representation (different characters)

**Concrete prediction:**

Tone mergers (e.g., Cantonese high-level and high-rising tones merging in some dialects) should show mechanism weakening *before* acoustic merger. Test: measure tone sandhi participation and perceptual distinctness diachronically. If sandhi participation drops before pitch contours converge, tone categories are mechanism-maintained HPCs.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Privative vs equipollent:** Phonological theory debates whether features are privative (presence/absence) or equipollent (two values). HPC assumes rich clustering, which favors equipollent. But if phonology is privative (absence is just lack of mechanism), HPC is wrong.
- **Prosodic domains:** Stress and intonation operate over prosodic phrases, not segments. Are prosodic phrases HPCs? What are their properties? Their mechanisms? HPC was built for individuable entities (species, disorders). Prosodic structure is hierarchical and relational. HPC might not scale.

## 2. Semantics

### 2.1 Are Semantic Categories HPCs?

**What HPC lets you ask:**

Traditional semantics: "What's the definition of *agent*?" (semantic primitive) vs "Agent is a prototype with central and peripheral members" (cognitive semantics). HPC dissolves: **agents are maintained property clusters.** The question becomes: what maintains the agent category across contexts?

**The clustering properties:**
- Volitional control
- Causal initiation
- Sentience/animacy
- Syntactic subject position
- Thematic role (external argument)

**The maintenance mechanisms:**
- **Syntactic alignment:** agents overwhelmingly appear as subjects. Distributional mechanism.
- **Semantic entailments:** if X is an agent, X causes the event. Inferential mechanism.
- **Conceptual structure:** agents are individuated entities that act on patients. Cognitive mechanism.
- **Cross-linguistic convergence:** agent-like properties cluster similarly across languages (Dowty 1991). Universal mechanism?

**What projection means:**

Knowing a participant is an agent predicts:
- Syntactic position (subject)
- Case marking (nominative, ergative)
- Control behavior (agent controls PRO in infinitival complements)
- Passivizability (agents can be demoted)

**Concrete predictions:**

1. **Hub-node:** Which property is load-bearing for agenthood? Is it volition? Causation? Animacy? Test: construct scenarios where properties dissociate (e.g., non-volitional but causal: "The rock broke the window"). Measure syntactic behavior. If causation alone predicts subject position but volition doesn't, causation is the hub.

2. **Comorbidity:** If agent and patient share a mechanism (thematic role assignment), they should co-vary cross-linguistically. Languages with rich agent distinctions (e.g., ergative splits) should also have rich patient distinctions (e.g., differential object marking). Test against typological databases.

3. **Thematic hierarchy = mechanism strength gradient:** The thematic hierarchy (agent > experiencer > theme > patient) might reflect *mechanism strength*, not stipulated ranking. Agents have the most maintenance (syntactic, semantic, conceptual). Patients have the least. Predict: typological frequency of case marking should track position on hierarchy. Already known (Silverstein hierarchy), but HPC *explains* it.

**Falsification:**

- If thematic roles vary independently across languages (agent properties don't cluster), they're not HPCs.
- If perturbing one property (e.g., removing volition) doesn't affect others (e.g., syntactic position), no maintenance network.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Decomposition:** Generative semantics decomposes agent into primitives (CAUSE, ACT, etc.). If agent is decomposable, it's not a cluster—it's a complex of simpler elements. HPC assumes non-decomposable clustering. These are incompatible.
- **Context-dependence:** Agenthood is context-sensitive. "The key opened the door" assigns agent properties to an instrument. Is the key *in an agent cluster* or is it just triggering agent-like inferences? HPC doesn't distinguish cluster membership from analogical extension.

### 2.2 Lexical Semantic Fields: Colour, Kinship, Motion

**What HPC lets you ask:**

Are colour terms maintained clusters? What about kinship terms (*mother*, *aunt*, *cousin*)? Motion verbs (*run*, *walk*, *skip*)?

**Traditional approaches:**
- Colour: Basic Colour Terms (Berlin & Kay 1969) — universal constraints on colour term systems.
- Kinship: Componential analysis — *aunt* = [+female, +generation, ±lineal].

**HPC reframes:** These fields are maintained by mechanisms. The question: **what keeps colour terms distinct? What keeps kinship terms coherent?**

**Example: Colour terms**

**Clustering properties:**
- Perceptual salience (focal colours)
- Lexicalization (single morpheme)
- Frequency (high usage)
- Morphosyntactic behavior (can be predicates, modifiers)
- Cultural significance (used in metaphors, idioms)

**Maintenance mechanisms:**
- **Perceptual structure:** focal colours are perceptually salient (Rosch 1973). Biological mechanism.
- **Communicative need:** colour terms are high-utility for reference. Functional mechanism.
- **Cultural entrenchment:** colour terms appear in idioms, metaphors, conventional expressions. Social mechanism.

**Projection:**

Knowing *red* is a basic colour term predicts:
- Morphological simplicity (not *reddish*, not compound)
- High frequency
- Cross-linguistic translatability (most languages have a *red*-equivalent in early stages)

**Concrete prediction:**

If colour terms are mechanism-maintained, **the order of emergence** (Berlin & Kay's hierarchy: white/black > red > green/yellow > blue > brown) should reflect *mechanism strength*, not arbitrary convention.

Test: In languages undergoing colour term expansion, measure:
- Perceptual salience (focal colour judgments)
- Communicative frequency (corpus counts)
- Cultural entrenchment (idiomatic usage)

Predict: Terms entering earlier in the hierarchy should show higher scores on all three. If a language violates the hierarchy (e.g., lexicalizes *blue* before *red*), it should show reversed mechanism strength.

**Falsification:**

If Berlin & Kay's hierarchy is violated frequently and mechanism strength doesn't predict order, HPC adds nothing to the typological account.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Vagueness:** Colour terms are vague (where does *red* end and *orange* begin?). HPC assumes discrete clustering, but vagueness is gradient. How do you reconcile mechanism-maintained clusters with continuous variation in extension?
- **Relativity:** Colour term boundaries vary across languages. If mechanisms are universal (perceptual structure), why don't boundaries converge? HPC needs a story about how language-specific social mechanisms override universal perceptual ones.

### 2.3 Polysemy: One HPC or Many?

**What HPC lets you ask:**

Is a polysemous word like *bank* (river bank, financial bank) a single HPC with fuzzy projection, or two HPCs sharing a form?

**Traditional approaches:**
- Monosemy: one abstract sense, context fills in details.
- Polysemy: multiple related senses.
- Homonymy: unrelated senses (accidental collision).

**HPC reframes:** **Do the senses share maintenance mechanisms?**

**Concrete prediction:**

If *bank₁* (river) and *bank₂* (financial) are maintained by distinct mechanisms, they should show:
- Independent syntactic behavior (different argument structures)
- Independent diachronic change (one sense can shift while the other stays stable)
- Independent cross-linguistic patterns (one sense translates, the other doesn't)

If they share mechanisms (e.g., both involve "edge/boundary" metaphor), they should show:
- Correlated syntactic behavior
- Correlated diachronic change
- Correlated cross-linguistic patterns

Test: Large-scale corpus study of polysemous words. Measure co-variation in syntactic contexts. If senses vary independently, they're separate HPCs. If they co-vary, shared maintenance.

**Falsification:**

If all polysemous words show shared maintenance, the distinction collapses. If none do, HPC is just relabelling homonymy.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Sense enumeration problem:** How many senses does *run* have? (Run a race, run for office, run water, run a program, etc.) HPC assumes you can count clusters. Polysemy resists enumeration.
- **Metaphorical extension:** Is metaphor a mechanism that maintains clustering, or is it analogical extension that creates *new* clusters? HPC doesn't clearly distinguish productive processes from static maintenance.

## 3. Pragmatics / Discourse

### 3.1 Speech Acts as HPCs

**What HPC lets you ask:**

Are speech acts (requests, commands, questions, assertions) maintained property clusters?

**The clustering properties:**
- **Illocutionary force** (what the utterance *does*)
- **Syntactic form** (imperatives for commands, interrogatives for questions)
- **Prosody** (rising intonation for questions)
- **Contextual felicity** (commands require authority)
- **Perlocutionary effect** (requests expect compliance)

**The maintenance mechanisms:**
- **Conventional association:** imperative mood → command force. Grammaticalized mechanism.
- **Contextual norms:** social hierarchy licenses commands. Pragmatic mechanism.
- **Interactive alignment:** speakers converge on shared interpretation. Interpersonal mechanism.

**What projection means:**

Knowing an utterance is a request predicts:
- Likely syntactic form (*Can you X?* not *You can X*)
- Likely prosodic contour (polite rising intonation)
- Likely response (compliance or refusal, not assertion)
- Contextual felicity (speaker lacks authority to command, so softens to request)

**Concrete prediction:**

If speech acts are HPCs, **cross-linguistic variation in form should correlate with mechanism strength**.

Example: In languages where imperative morphology is obligatory (e.g., Turkish), commands should show *tighter* clustering of formal and functional properties than in languages where imperative is optional (e.g., English *you will X* as command).

Test: Measure form-function correlation cross-linguistically. Predict: Languages with obligatory mood marking show less form-function mismatch (fewer indirect speech acts). If mismatch is constant across languages, obligatoriness doesn't strengthen maintenance.

**Falsification:**

If speech act categories are entirely context-dependent (same form = different force depending on context), there's no stable cluster. HPC fails.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Indirect speech acts:** *Can you pass the salt?* is syntactically a question but functionally a request. Is this one HPC (request) with variable realization, or two HPCs (question + request) in conflict? HPC doesn't handle form-function mismatches gracefully.
- **Gricean implicature:** Pragmatics often explains meaning via inference (what's *implicated*, not *said*). HPC assumes explicit properties maintained by mechanisms. Implicature is emergent from general reasoning, not mechanism-maintained. HPC might not extend to inferential pragmatics.

### 3.2 Discourse Relations as HPCs

**What HPC lets you ask:**

Are discourse relations (cause, contrast, elaboration) maintained property clusters?

**Traditional approaches:**
- Rhetorical Structure Theory: hierarchical relations between discourse units.
- Coherence relations: semantic/pragmatic links (cause, condition, concession).

**HPC reframes:** **What maintains the coherence relation?**

**Example: Causal relations**

**Clustering properties:**
- Semantic entailment (A causes B)
- Temporal ordering (A precedes B)
- Syntactic marking (*because*, *so*, *therefore*)
- Discourse structure (cause in subordinate clause, result in main clause)

**Maintenance mechanisms:**
- **Causal reasoning:** general cognitive mechanism (Talmy, Wolff).
- **Conventional marking:** *because* signals causation. Grammaticalized mechanism.
- **Discourse expectations:** result clauses presuppose causes. Pragmatic mechanism.

**Concrete prediction:**

If discourse relations are HPCs, **explicit marking should strengthen projection**.

Unmarked causal relations (*She was tired. She went to bed*) should show weaker projection than marked ones (*She went to bed because she was tired*). Measure: comprehension speed, inference strength, ambiguity resolution. Predict: Marked relations should show faster processing and stronger inferences.

Test: Reading time studies. If unmarked and marked relations process identically, explicit marking doesn't strengthen maintenance.

**Falsification:**

If discourse relations are entirely inferred from world knowledge (no linguistic maintenance), HPC adds nothing.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Underspecification:** Most discourse relations are implicit (*She was tired. She went to bed*). If maintenance requires explicit properties, underspecified relations aren't HPCs. But they're the majority of discourse.
- **Ambiguity:** Many relations are ambiguous (cause vs temporal sequence). HPC assumes stable clusters. Ambiguity resists stability.

### 3.3 Register and Genre as HPCs

**What HPC lets you ask:**

Are registers (casual, formal, academic, legal) maintained property clusters?

**The clustering properties:**
- Lexical choice (formal: *purchase*; casual: *buy*)
- Syntactic complexity (academic: more subordination)
- Discourse structure (legal: lengthy nominal phrases)
- Prosody (casual: contracted forms)

**The maintenance mechanisms:**
- **Social norms:** academic writing requires formal register. Social mechanism.
- **Audience design:** speakers adjust register for addressee. Interactive mechanism.
- **Entrenchment:** repeated exposure to register conventions. Cognitive mechanism.

**Concrete prediction:**

If registers are HPCs, **learning one property of a register should predict others**.

Example: If you learn that a text uses *purchase* instead of *buy*, you should infer:
- More complex syntax
- More nominal style
- Less contracted forms

Test: Present learners with partial texts in different registers. Measure their predictions for unseen properties. If predictions are accurate, register is a projectible cluster.

**Falsification:**

If register properties vary independently (lexical formality doesn't predict syntactic complexity), registers aren't HPCs.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Mixed registers:** Real language use mixes registers constantly (code-switching, stylistic blending). HPC assumes discrete clusters. Mixing resists discreteness.
- **Social construction:** Registers are socially constructed and vary across communities. HPC assumes stable mechanisms. Social change disrupts stability.

### 3.4 Information Structure: Topic, Focus, Given/New

**What HPC lets you ask:**

Are information structure categories (topic, focus, given, new) maintained property clusters?

**Example: Topic**

**Clustering properties:**
- **Referential:** topics are typically definite
- **Positional:** topics appear clause-initially (many languages)
- **Prosodic:** topics often lack prosodic prominence
- **Discourse:** topics are given or accessible

**Maintenance mechanisms:**
- **Discourse continuity:** topics track referents across clauses. Cognitive mechanism.
- **Syntactic marking:** topic position, topic particles (Japanese *wa*). Grammaticalized mechanism.
- **Prosodic marking:** deaccenting of given topics. Phonological mechanism.

**Concrete prediction:**

If topic is an HPC, **languages with explicit topic marking should show tighter clustering** of topic properties than languages without.

Test: Compare Japanese (topic particle *wa*) with English (positional topic). Measure:
- Definiteness of topics
- Prosodic reduction
- Discourse continuity

Predict: Japanese should show tighter correlation (explicit marking strengthens maintenance). If correlations are identical, explicit marking does no work.

**Falsification:**

If topic properties vary independently across languages, topic isn't an HPC.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Multidimensionality:** Information structure crosses levels (syntax, prosody, discourse). HPC assumes coherent clustering. Information structure is distributionally defined, not mechanistically unified.
- **Context-dependence:** What counts as topic depends on prior discourse. HPC assumes stable properties. Topics shift continuously.

## 4. Sign Language

### 4.1 Does the Coupling Spectrum Work Differently in Sign?

**What HPC lets you ask:**

The book claims a coupling spectrum: hard-coupled (phonemes) → loose-coupled (grammar) → composite (constructions). Does this spectrum hold in sign languages?

**Key difference:** Sign languages have **iconicity** (form resembles meaning) alongside arbitrariness. Spoken language is almost entirely arbitrary.

**Genuine new question:** **Does iconicity create a different coupling regime?**

**Hypothesis:** Iconicity might create *tighter* coupling between form and meaning for iconic signs (e.g., TREE in ASL shows tree-like upward branching), but *looser* coupling for arbitrary signs.

**Concrete prediction:**

If iconicity strengthens coupling, iconic signs should show:
- **Less form variation** across signers (everyone uses the same handshape)
- **Faster acquisition** (children learn iconic signs earlier)
- **More resistant to change** (iconicity stabilizes form)

Arbitrary signs should show:
- More form variation
- Slower acquisition
- More susceptible to change

Test: Measure form variation in ASL dictionary across signers. Compare iconic vs arbitrary signs. Predict: iconic signs show lower variance.

**Falsification:**

If iconic and arbitrary signs show identical variation, iconicity doesn't affect coupling.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Gradient iconicity:** Iconicity isn't binary. Some signs are highly iconic, others weakly iconic. HPC assumes discrete clusters. Gradient iconicity resists discreteness.
- **Conventionalization:** Iconic signs become conventionalized over time (form drifts from iconic motivation). Does this mean coupling *weakens* as the sign ages? HPC predicts stability from mechanisms. Conventionalization suggests drift.

---

### 4.2 Classifier Constructions as HPCs

**What HPC lets you ask:**

Sign languages have productive classifier constructions (handshapes represent object categories: flat, round, upright, etc.). Are these HPCs?

**The clustering properties:**
- **Handshape:** specific handshape for each classifier (flat hand for flat objects)
- **Semantic category:** classifiers group objects (vehicles, humans, thin objects)
- **Syntactic behavior:** classifiers can be verbal or nominal
- **Iconicity:** classifiers are often iconic (but not always)

**The maintenance mechanisms:**
- **Perceptual salience:** object shape is visually salient. Perceptual mechanism.
- **Functional grouping:** classifiers group manipulable objects. Functional mechanism.
- **Grammatical position:** classifiers occupy specific syntactic slots. Distributional mechanism.

**Concrete prediction:**

If classifiers are HPCs, **semantic transparency should correlate with coupling tightness**.

Example: In ASL, the "vehicle" classifier groups cars, bikes, trucks (semantically coherent). The "flat object" classifier groups papers, tables, roads (less coherent — shape-based, not functional). Predict:
- Vehicle classifier should show tighter form-function coupling (less variability)
- Flat-object classifier should show looser coupling (more variability)

Test: Measure handshape consistency across signers for different classifiers. Predict: semantically coherent classifiers show lower variance.

**Falsification:**

If all classifiers show identical variance, semantic coherence doesn't affect maintenance.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Productivity:** Classifier constructions are productive (you can create novel classifier uses on the fly). HPC assumes stable clusters. Productivity suggests open-ended extension, not bounded clustering.
- **Iconicity again:** Classifier systems are heavily iconic. HPC was built for arbitrary categories. Iconicity might be doing all the work, not mechanisms.

---

### 4.3 Does Visual-Gestural Modality Change Available Mechanisms?

**What HPC lets you ask:**

Spoken language maintenance mechanisms include:
- Acoustic perceptual magnets
- Articulatory alignment
- Phonotactic patterns

Do sign languages have **modality-specific mechanisms**?

**Candidate mechanisms unique to sign:**
- **Visual salience:** handshapes that are visually distinct are easier to maintain.
- **Spatial grammar:** sign languages use 3D space for syntax (verb agreement uses spatial loci). This is unavailable in spoken language.
- **Manual iconicity:** handshape can iconically represent object properties (impossible in vocal tract).

**Concrete prediction:**

If spatial grammar is a modality-specific mechanism, **agreement patterns in sign should be more stable than in spoken languages** (because spatial anchoring provides additional maintenance).

Test: Compare diachronic stability of verb agreement in sign languages vs spoken languages. Predict: sign languages should show less erosion of agreement over time.

**Falsification:**

If sign language agreement erodes at the same rate as spoken language, spatial anchoring doesn't add stability.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Modality effects:** HPC assumes mechanisms are domain-general (perceptual, cognitive, social). But modality might matter. If sign languages require a different HPC story, the framework isn't truly scale-invariant.
- **Small sample size:** There are fewer sign languages than spoken languages, and most are understudied. Typological predictions are harder to test.

## 5. First Language Acquisition

### 5.1 Beyond Individual Categories: Overall Trajectory

**What HPC lets you ask:**

The book's case studies focus on *individual* category acquisition (countability, definiteness, etc.). But HPC should also predict the *overall trajectory* of grammatical development.

**Traditional approaches:**
- Constructivist: item-based learning → abstraction.
- Generativist: innate principles → parameter setting.

**HPC reframes:** **Categories emerge when maintenance mechanisms kick in.** The question: **does the order of emergence reflect mechanism availability?**

**Concrete prediction:**

If categories require multiple mechanisms to stabilize, **early-emerging categories should require fewer mechanisms** (simpler maintenance regime).

Example: Nouns emerge before verbs. HPC predicts:
- Nouns have simpler maintenance (referential grounding, object permanence)
- Verbs require more mechanisms (event segmentation, argument structure, aspect)

Test: Count the number of mechanisms required for each category (operationalize from acquisition literature). Predict: negative correlation between mechanism count and age of emergence.

**Falsification:**

If categories requiring many mechanisms emerge just as early as simple ones, mechanism complexity doesn't predict trajectory.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Innateness:** Generativist accounts assume innate grammatical categories. HPC assumes construction from mechanisms. If UG is right, HPC is wrong.
- **Input frequency:** Usage-based accounts explain emergence via input frequency. HPC adds mechanisms. If frequency alone predicts emergence, mechanisms are decorative.

---

### 5.2 Critical Period as Mechanism Window

**What HPC lets you ask:**

The critical period for language acquisition (roughly age 0-7) is well-documented but poorly explained. HPC reframes: **is the critical period a window when maintenance mechanisms are most effective?**

**Hypothesis:** Neural plasticity (perceptual magnet formation, entrenchment) declines after age 7. If categories are maintained by neural mechanisms, the critical period is when those mechanisms can be installed.

**Concrete prediction:**

If the critical period is mechanism-specific, **different categories should have different critical periods** (depending on their maintenance mechanisms).

Example:
- Phonemes (perceptual mechanisms) should have the earliest critical period.
- Syntax (distributional mechanisms) should have a later critical period.
- Pragmatics (social mechanisms) might have no critical period (social learning continues into adulthood).

Test: Measure L2 acquisition success for different linguistic levels across ages. Predict: Phonology shows earliest dropoff, syntax intermediate, pragmatics latest.

**Falsification:**

If all linguistic levels show the same critical period, mechanism-specificity is wrong.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Maturational constraints:** The critical period might be a general neural maturation constraint (Lenneberg 1967), not mechanism-specific. If so, HPC adds nothing.
- **Social factors:** L2 acquisition is confounded by social factors (motivation, immersion, instruction). Hard to isolate mechanism effects.

---

### 5.3 Cluster Tightening: Developmental Prediction

**What HPC lets you ask (from the gauntlet reframing):**

If categories are maintained by mechanisms that create coupling, **children's diagnostics should become more correlated over time**, not just more accurate.

**Traditional view:** Children get better at using plural, better at using articles, independently.

**HPC view:** Children's diagnostics should *tighten* — the correlation between *uses plural* and *uses a(n)* should increase from age 2-4.

**Concrete prediction:**

Measure P(uses *a(n)* | uses plural) across ages. Predict: increases over time.

Compare:
- Age 2: Plural correct 40%, *a(n)* correct 30%, both correct 10%. P(a|pl) = 0.25.
- Age 4: Plural correct 80%, *a(n)* correct 70%, both correct 65%. P(a|pl) = 0.81.

Test: Longitudinal corpus study (CHILDES). Track individual children's diagnostic co-occurrence.

**Falsification:**

If diagnostics improve independently (P(a|pl) stays constant), coupling mechanisms aren't operating in acquisition. HPC is wrong about developmental dynamics.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Input patterns:** If diagnostics co-occur in the input (*a cat* is frequent), children might just be copying input patterns, not building maintenance mechanisms. Hard to disentangle.
- **Error patterns:** Children make errors (overgeneralization, undergeneralization). HPC predicts coupling should tighten, but errors might create *temporary* looser coupling (child uses plural without article). Does this count as mechanism failure, or just incomplete acquisition?

---

### 5.4 The p-Factor Question (Individual Differences)

**What HPC lets you ask (from psychiatry analogy in the gauntlet reframing):**

Is there a **general factor of grammatical tightness** across speakers? Some children might have generally sharper categories across the board (high entrenchment, strong alignment sensitivity). Others might have looser categories everywhere.

**Analogy:** In psychopathology, the p-factor captures individual differences in overall psychopathology (some people have tightly clustered symptoms across disorders). In grammar, the g-factor might capture individual differences in category sharpness.

**Concrete prediction:**

If a g-factor exists, **children who show tight coupling for countability should also show tight coupling for definiteness, verb argument structure, etc.**

Test: Measure diagnostic coupling across multiple categories for individual children. Run factor analysis. Predict: first factor explains substantial variance (general grammatical tightness).

**Falsification:**

If each category shows independent individual differences (no cross-category correlation), there's no g-factor. Mechanisms are category-specific, not speaker-general.

**Where HPC struggles:**

- **Domain-specificity:** Generativist linguistics assumes modules (phonology, syntax, semantics are separate). A general g-factor violates modularity. If generativists are right, HPC is wrong.
- **Task effects:** Different categories might be measured with different tasks (comprehension vs production, judgment vs usage). Individual differences might reflect task variance, not grammatical tightness.

---

## Summary: Where HPC Genuinely Opens New Questions

### Strongest applications (HPC adds real predictive power):

1. **Phoneme mergers and splits:** Hub-node collapse, budding, mechanism weakening *before* acoustic merger. Testable, novel.
2. **Sound change coordination:** Chain shifts as shared-mechanism phenomena. Predicts coordinated destabilization.
3. **Developmental cluster tightening:** Diagnostics should become more *correlated*, not just more accurate. Direct test of coupling mechanism in acquisition.
4. **The p-factor (individual differences):** General grammatical tightness across categories. Never been tested. Directly imported from psychiatry.
5. **Cross-linguistic comorbidity:** Categories that share mechanisms should co-occur typologically. Testable against WALS/Grambank.
6. **Register projection:** Learning one property of a register predicts others. Testable experimentally.

### Weakest applications (HPC is mostly relabelling):

1. **Allophones:** HPC doesn't distinguish categorical and gradient structure cleanly. Allophonic variation resists HPC treatment.
2. **Polysemy:** Sense enumeration problem. HPC assumes countable clusters. Polysemy resists enumeration.
3. **Indirect speech acts:** Form-function mismatches. HPC assumes stable clustering. Indirection resists stability.
4. **Discourse relations:** Mostly implicit, ambiguous, context-dependent. HPC assumes explicit properties. Bad fit.
5. **Critical period:** Might be general neural maturation, not mechanism-specific. HPC adds nothing if maturation is domain-general.

### Where HPC systematically struggles:

1. **Gradient phenomena:** Vagueness, continuous variation, underspecification. HPC assumes discrete clusters.
2. **Productivity:** Metaphor, novel classifier uses, creative speech acts. HPC assumes stable maintenance. Productivity is open-ended.
3. **Decomposition:** If categories are built from primitives (features, theta-roles, semantic atoms), they're not clusters. HPC and decomposition are incompatible.
4. **Context-dependence:** Information structure, speech acts, discourse relations are all context-dependent. HPC assumes stable properties.
5. **Mechanism heterogeneity:** Phonology crosses levels (neural, cognitive, social, distributional). HPC from biology assumes same-level mechanisms. Linguistics violates this constantly.

---

## Final Verdict

HPC is most powerful when:
- Categories have *multiple* properties that cluster non-accidentally
- Mechanisms can be independently identified and perturbed
- Projection can be operationalized (what knowing category membership predicts)
- Diachronic and developmental trajectories can be tracked

HPC is weakest when:
- Categories are gradient, vague, or context-dependent
- Properties are decomposable into primitives
- Mechanisms are heterogeneous (crossing ontological levels)
- Productivity and creativity are central (open-ended extension)

**The book's morphosyntactic case studies are the sweet spot.** Phonology, semantics, and pragmatics are harder fits. Sign language and acquisition offer some genuinely new questions (iconicity/coupling, p-factor, developmental tightening) but also expose HPC's limits (productivity, gradience, context-dependence).

**Recommendation for the book:** Stay focused on morphosyntax. Mention other branches as *extensions* (where HPC might apply), not as *demonstrations* (where HPC clearly succeeds). The coupling spectrum (Ch 13) is the right move: phonemes are at the hard-coupled end, constructions at the composite end. Don't overclaim.
