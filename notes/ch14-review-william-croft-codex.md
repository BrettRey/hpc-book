Strengths

The chapter’s central move is strong and publishable: recasting grammaticality as a maintained form-value coupling (“the zipper”), rather than as a primitive binary. The distinction in `The stabilizer: a feeling` between detector output and underlying category (`chapters/chapter14.tex:45`) is conceptually productive and aligns with known acceptability/processing dissociations. The chapter is also at its best when it explicitly limits itself. In `The phenomenology of coupling`, the sentence “readout of the mechanism, not evidence independent of it” (`chapters/chapter14.tex:76`) is exactly the right discipline signal for skeptics. Finally, the explicit scoped prediction/falsifier in the “Definiteness thread” (`chapters/chapter14.tex:299`) is a good sign: it shows willingness to risk empirical defeat.

Major Concerns

First, the argument is not yet mechanistic enough. You have excellent metaphors (“zipper,” “immune system,” “spinning top”), but the causal decomposition remains underspecified. What are the state variables, what updates them, and what generates threshold crossings in mismatch feeling? Without that, skeptics will call this redescriptive.

Second, acceptability vs. grammaticality risks circularity. You say the detector is tuned by entrenchment and grammaticality is the entrenched coupling (`Entrenchment in action`, especially `chapters/chapter14.tex:49`). But then what independently fixes “is grammatical” in illusion cases? If both ontology and measurement are entrenchment-sensitive, you need external diagnostics.

Third, the chapter overstates evidential status. `Empirical signatures` opens with “three bodies of evidence confirm this prediction” (`chapters/chapter14.tex:236`). That is too strong given the heterogeneity of evidence and the inferential leap from processing findings to ontology. The “schema drift” import from a relational-concept study (`chapters/chapter14.tex:244`) reads as analogical transfer, not yet direct support.

Fourth, comparative-concept discipline needs tightening. Cross-linguistic examples are mostly English-centered contrasts framed as if structural comparanda are already settled (age, progressive, countability, deitality). Claims like “the structural slot’s the same” (`chapters/chapter14.tex:53`) will trigger immediate pushback from typologists and construction grammarians: equivalence across languages must be argued, not assumed.

Fifth, the section `What doesn’t count` risks reification by disciplinary ownership (“That’s why syntax owns it,” `chapters/chapter14.tex:172`). Philosophers of language will object that this sounds stipulative, not discovered.

Priority Fixes

1. Replace metaphor-first exposition with an explicit process model: representation of constructional pairings, entrenchment update rule, processing-cost channel, and decision function producing acceptability judgments.
2. Downgrade “confirm” to “support” and distinguish direct evidence from analogical extension case by case.
3. Add a comparative-concept protocol: for each cross-linguistic example, state the mapping criteria and where comparability may fail.
4. Strengthen acceptability/grammaticality separation with independent diagnostics (corpus productivity, repair behavior, acquisition trajectories), not just intuitions.
5. Remove or qualify English-centric absolutes (“required,” “categorical,” “syntax owns it”) unless supported by distributional evidence across varieties.

Decisive Tests

1. Cross-linguistic matched-construction experiment: same communicative function, different morphosyntactic realizations, testing whether mismatch feelings track language-specific entrenchment rather than global inferential failure.
2. Entrenchment intervention study: repeated exposure plus production pressure, measuring when judgments shift and whether shifts predict transfer to novel tokens (true category movement vs mere task adaptation).
3. Triadic-register dissociation: independent measures for “jolt,” “block,” and “normative correction” to test whether the three-register architecture has behavioral reality.
4. Strong theory comparison with relevance-based account (`Relevance and the immune system`): construct cases where inferential recoverability is high but morphosyntactic mismatch remains, and vice versa. This is where a skeptical specialist will look first.
