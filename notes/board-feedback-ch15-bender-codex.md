**Strengths**

Chapter 15 has a strong central move: it reframes variation from “noise” to conditioned structure, then operationalizes that through explicit conditioning variables (`chapters/chapter15.tex:21`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:73`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:75`). The mixed-bin metaphor is clear and methodologically useful because it explains why pooled distributions look unstable while conditioned systems can remain tight (`chapters/chapter15.tex:64`). The chapter also does good multiscale mechanism work: dialect, register, and discourse community each get concrete feedback loops and timescales rather than purely taxonomic labels (`chapters/chapter15.tex:103`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:122`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:133`). Finally, the chapter aligns productively with the “detector is fallible and community-calibrated” stance from Chapter 14 (`chapters/chapter14.tex:269`) and with Chapter 16’s anti-level-privilege framing (`chapters/chapter16.tex:23`).

**Major Concerns**

The current rhetoric sometimes slides from mechanism talk into reification of social categories. Claims like “real boundary” for dialects (`chapters/chapter15.tex:99`), “ontologically distinct” register basins (`chapters/chapter15.tex:118`), “distinct clusters of speakers” as latent mixture components (`chapters/chapter15.tex:131`), and “realism proper” analogized to chemical bonds (`chapters/chapter15.tex:206`) risk naturalizing historically contingent, institutionally enforced distinctions. In a Bender-style framing, this is exactly where statistical-cluster language can be misread as discovering pre-given social kinds.

Field-relative caveats are present but under-signaled relative to stronger realist claims. You explicitly state field-relative projectibility (`chapters/chapter15.tex:75`), but nearby lines (“social variable…parameter of the grammar itself,” “no privileged scale”) can sound universal without reiterating project-purpose constraints (`chapters/chapter15.tex:43`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:166`). This creates a framing tension with the project’s own purpose-relative commitments (`notes/chapter15_planning.md:9`, `chapters/chapter16.tex:23`).

Corpus and institutional sampling bias need explicit treatment. The chapter invokes “corpora of spontaneous speech” and “millions of speakers” (`chapters/chapter15.tex:8`) plus YGDP documentation (`chapters/chapter15.tex:37`), but does not discuss corpus composition, register skew, or gatekeeping-heavy data pipelines. That omission is especially salient given your own planning note warning that standard-heavy data can reproduce “native speaker” ideology (`notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:108`, `notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:113`).
