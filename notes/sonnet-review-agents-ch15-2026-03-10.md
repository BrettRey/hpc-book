**Predictions section (lines 272-346):** Three forward-facing predictions (satiation, productivity asymmetries, individual differences) all operationalize projectibility -- what the coupling lets you **infer/extend/repair** under different conditions. ## Review from Ruth Millikan's Perspective

**1. One-sentence summary**

Grammaticality is a homeostatic property cluster constituted by maintained form–value–interpretant couplings (Peircean "zippers") whose stability arises from a noisy detector (the feeling of ungrammaticality) that corrects deviations and supports projection across novel instances.

---

**2. Strengths**

- **Lines 69–88: The five-part mechanism sketch grounds the account in causally specific timescales.** The decomposition (coupling state → processing → detector output → threshold → entrenchment update) avoids treating "grammaticality" as a mere label and instead identifies the recursive loop that keeps the form–value pairing stable. The reflexive equilibrium (detector tunes coupling; coupling tunes detector over historical time, line 86) is exactly what a teleosemantic account needs: selection history shapes the current state, which shapes future selection. This is proper-function thinking applied to linguistic norms.

- **Lines 98–115: The illusions argument is methodologically sound.** The garden-path and comparative illusion cases prove the detector dissociates from the structure, which blocks any naive identification of grammaticality with immediate phenomenology. The analogy to Müller-Lyer (line 114) is apt: perceptual illusions reveal mechanism architecture, and grammaticality illusions do the same. The selective fallibility literature (Phillips et al. 2011) is cited appropriately, and the claim that retrieval systems show structure-sensitivity when temporal priority aligns (line 115) connects to proper-function predictions: the system was selected *for* structure-tracking under normal input conditions, so it misfires predictably when those conditions are violated.

- **Lines 89–92: The age-expression case operationalizes the coupling/comprehension split.** The fact that we understand *I have twenty years* as an age claim *while also detecting a mismatch* (line 92) shows grammaticality isn't just failed comprehension. We derive the intended meaning via pragmatic inference, then notice the structural meaning the coupling delivers (possession/experience) doesn't match. This is a proper-function misfire: the form's *normal function* is to deliver possession, not age, so the detector flags it even though communication succeeds. The case refutes pure usage-based accounts (if usage were all that mattered, successful comprehension would silence the detector) and supports the maintenance-via-normativity story.

---

**3. Weaknesses**

- **Lines 19–21: The "zipper" terminology hides a deep ambiguity in what the "interpretant" is.** You say the interpretant is "the projective habit that makes the pairing count as a pairing," but then line 21 describes *licensing* (what the grammar permits) vs. *coupling* (what a community has entrenched) as if they're distinct. If the interpretant is the habit, then licensing can't be prior to it—licensing *is* the manifestation of the habit in novel cases. But if licensing is structural (the "zipper's teeth are cut by the grammar," line 21), then the interpretant seems to be doing less work than Peirce requires. The mediation relation (from your Ch 7, I infer) should constitute the sign–object link, not just "draw together" pre-existing slots. This matters because if the grammar pre-exists the interpretant, you've smuggled in a Platonist grammar after all. The age-expression case (line 21) helps: "the structural slot for *have* + NP exists in English, but the community hasn't filled it with age." But "exists" is doing heavy lifting. Does the slot exist independently of any interpretant, or only relative to some interpretant? If the former, you've got essentialist structure; if the latter, you need to say which interpretant grounds licensing vs. which grounds coupling. **Actionable fix:** Add a paragraph after line 21 distinguishing *licensing-as-structural-possibility* (which emerges from low-level compositional habits) from *coupling-as-entrenched-pairing* (which adds a second-order habit). Or collapse licensing into coupling and bite the bullet: there is no grammar independent of interpretants all the way down.

- **Lines 257–268: The dual-role claim (grammaticality as both category and mechanism) is asserted but not defended against the obvious circularity worry.** You say "the detector patrols the couplings that constitute other categories—but the detector is not the coupling itself" (line 266), which is fine, but then how does the detector *know* what to patrol? If it's tracking a would-be (line 264), the would-be must already be in place, which means the coupling pre-exists the detector's activity. But you also say the detector *maintains* the coupling (line 266), which implies the coupling depends on the detector. The reflexive loop (line 86) addressed this diachronically, but the synchronic story needs spelling out. The proper-function framework solves this: the detector's *proper function* is to track couplings that have been selected *because* they supported past projections. The detector doesn't create the coupling ex nihilo; it maintains a coupling that exists *because of its history of being maintained*. But you don't say this explicitly, so the reader is left wondering whether the account is circular. **Actionable fix:** Add 3–4 sentences after line 266 making explicit that the detector's proper function is historically grounded. The coupling exists *now* because past detectors corrected deviations that would have destroyed it. The circularity is a virtuous spiral, not a vicious circle, because the explanatory arrow goes through selection history.

- **Lines 352–358: The "contingency is not arbitrariness" paragraph gestures at convergence but doesn't engage with the hard question.** You say grammaticality recurs everywhere "because light is informative and photosensitive tissue is accessible" (analogy to eyes, line 356), but you don't specify *which* cognitive/communicative pressures make form–value coupling inevitable. Are you claiming that *structural meaning* (as defined line 17) is a universal functional niche? If so, say it. If not, the contingency claim is doing no work—every HPC is contingent in the trivial sense that its actual properties could have been otherwise. The eye analogy cuts both ways: eyes are convergent *because* the function (detect light gradients) is fixed by physics, and the mechanism space (proteins that change conformation in response to photons) is constrained by chemistry. What's the linguistic analogue? If it's "communicate compositionally structured content efficiently," then the argument needs fleshing out (and cites—Kirby, Tamariz, Cornish on iterated learning; Regier et al. on semantic typology; Christiansen & Chater on Now-or-Never). If it's something else, you need to say what. **Actionable fix:** Lines 352–358 should become a full subsection (§15.X: "Why grammaticality recurs") with 4–6 paragraphs laying out the convergence argument. Specify the function, specify the constraints, cite the experimental work on cultural transmission that shows form–meaning systematicity emerges under iterated learning even when participants start with arbitrary mappings.

---

**4. Key question at Q&A**

You say the feeling of ungrammaticality *maintains* the coupling (lines 61, 266), but feelings don't have causal powers on their own—only the behaviors they prompt do. So the real stabilizers are *hesitation, repair, and rephrasal* (line 61), not the feeling. If the feeling is epiphenomenal (a readout, as you say line 67), why call it a stabilizer at all? Wouldn't it be more parsimonious to say the *mechanisms triggered by the feeling* (correction routines, alignment pressures) are the stabilizers, and the feeling is just the proximate cue that recruits them? Or are you claiming the feeling has direct causal efficacy—that phenomenology itself does maintenance work? If the latter, you need an account of how qualia cause anything, which is a can of worms. If the former, the "feeling of ungrammaticality" framing is misleading—you should talk about the *correction reflex* or the *alignment drive*, and treat the feeling as an implementation detail.

---

**5. Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit.** The core machinery is sound—properly located in selection history, testable via dissociations, and empirically responsible—but the chapter needs to clarify (1) what the interpretant is doing if licensing pre-exists it, (2) how the detector's proper function grounds the dual-role claim without circularity, and (3) why convergence on grammaticality is predicted rather than just observed. These aren't fatal, but they're load-bearing, and a reader trained in teleosemantics will notice the gaps. The illusions argument and the age-expression operationalization are strong enough to carry the chapter once the conceptual architecture is tightened.

## Review: Chapter 15, "Grammaticality itself"

### 1. One-sentence summary
Grammaticality is itself a homeostatic property cluster—a maintained, projectible coupling between morphosyntactic form and structural meaning, stabilized by a noisy "feeling of ungrammaticality" detector that enables repair, projection, and looping effects characteristic of social kinds.

### 2. Strengths

- **The looping framework is correctly deployed** (line 88). You cite my 1999 work on looping effects and apply it precisely: "the category shapes the behaviour that sustains it." This is exactly what distinguishes interactive kinds from indifferent ones. The age-expression example (lines 90–92) demonstrates the looping cleanly—correction presupposes shared projection, which reinforces the coupling. The contrast with beaver classification (line 89) is apt: beavers don't change because we classify them; grammatical couplings do.

- **The detector/coupling distinction is architecturally sound** (lines 61–86). You resist collapsing the category into its readout—the three-timescale mechanism sketch (lines 72–79) and the separation of processing-as-filter from processing-as-modulator (lines 82–84) prevent the conflation. The whom/who cohort shift (line 84) is the right kind of evidence: durable repair-preference changes, not transient fatigue effects.

- **The three-register phenomenology is genuinely novel** (lines 117–154). Mapping qualitative jolt / reactive resistance / normative correction onto sign/object/interpretant gives you operational handles (Table 15.2) where most accounts offer only introspective mush. The dissociation predictions are falsifiable: if garden-path sentences don't trigger jolts without normative correction, or if comparative illusions don't pass fluency without stable interpretation, the triadic model is wrong. That's good philosophy of language.

### 3. Weaknesses

- **The HPC audit is too quick** (lines 336–346). You claim grammaticality "passes both" diagnostics of the Two-Diagnostic Test, but the argument compresses what should be the chapter's centerpiece into 11 lines. The whose/yesterday examples (lines 340–342) show partial independence of properties—fine—but you don't demonstrate *mechanisms holding the cluster together*, which is the homeostasis requirement. The loop in lines 69–79 sketches feedback (detector → repair → coupling), but you haven't shown that this loop *explains the co-occurrence* of the three properties better than essentialism or nominalism would. The cross-linguistic neural evidence (line 344) supports convergence but not maintenance. **Actionable fix:** Expand §15.6.3 with a mechanism-specific argument. Show how entrenchment, alignment, and transmission *predict* that form-value pairing, compatibility, and community convergence should cluster *because* the loop couples them, not just that they happen to co-occur.

- **The contingency/convergence dialectic is asserted, not resolved** (lines 352–357). You claim grammaticality's existence is convergent ("every known language maintains some form-value coupling") while its contents are contingent (embedded inversion in Irish English vs. standard English). But you haven't explained *why* the HPC framework makes this prediction while essentialism doesn't. An essentialist could say: "The *principle* of form-meaning mapping is universal (convergent); its *parameters* vary (contingent)." That's basically your position, reworded. The eye analogy (line 356) gestures at constraint-channeled convergence, but doesn't specify which constraints channel *what*. **Actionable fix:** Either (a) show that HPC predicts *degrees* of contingency (tight couplings resist drift, loose ones don't) where essentialism predicts none, or (b) acknowledge that this chapter doesn't yet settle whether HPC and parametric essentialism make distinct predictions about convergence patterns. The latter is honest; the former requires §15.7 to do more work.

- **Relevance theory is dismissed too quickly** (lines 326–330). You say the frameworks "differ in granularity"—relevance theory predicts broader failures, maintenance predicts construction-specific ones—but then claim *I have twenty years* "fails because the English age construction is be + years old, not because relevance-seeking fails in general" (line 330). That's not an argument; it's a restatement of your framework's vocabulary. Scott-Phillips could reply: "The construction *is* the entrenched relevance-optimization pattern; saying it fails 'because the coupling is be + years old' just relabels what I call a communicative presumption." You need to specify a case where the two frameworks make *different empirical predictions*, not just describe the same data in different terms. The three predictions (construction-specific satiation, coupling-indexed productivity, pre-emptive repair) are promising—but you haven't shown relevance theory *can't* accommodate them. **Actionable fix:** Add one paragraph testing the frameworks against each other. Does satiation work differently for relevance violations (e.g., redundancy) vs. morphosyntactic mismatches? If so, state the prediction and the evidence. If not, admit the frameworks may be notational variants at this grain.

### 4. Key question at Q&A

You write (line 88): "Biological HPCs can loop through other channels—niche construction is a loop—but not through classification, because a beaver doesn't change because you call it a beaver."

But *Castor canadensis* (North American beaver) and *Castor fiber* (Eurasian beaver) were classified as the same species for centuries, then split in 1820—and conservation policy, habitat management, and reintroduction programs diverged accordingly. The animals didn't change, but their *populations* changed because human interventions (niche construction shaped by classification) altered selection pressures. Isn't that a biological looping effect?

More generally: What's the principled boundary between "looping through classification" (your criterion for social kinds) and "looping through other channels" (which you allow for biological kinds)? If niche construction counts as non-classificatory looping for beavers, why doesn't *linguistic* niche construction (e.g., grammaticalization creating new structural slots) count the same way? Are you sure the interactive/indifferent distinction tracks the social/biological boundary, or does it track something else—like *rapidity of feedback* or *awareness of classification*?

### 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.**

The chapter correctly identifies grammaticality as a looping kind and deploys the detector/coupling architecture with care. The three-register phenomenology is original and the operational predictions (Table 15.2) are falsifiable. But the HPC audit (§15.6.3) compresses the argument where it should expand, the contingency/convergence dialectic (§15.7) asserts a contrast with essentialism without demonstrating it, and the relevance-theory discussion (§15.5.4) needs one concrete divergent prediction to justify the claim of framework difference. These are fixable. The chapter's core claim—that grammaticality is both maintained category and stabilizing mechanism—deserves the full argument it almost makes.

## Review of Chapter 15: "Grammaticality itself"
**Ewa Dąbrowska, Psycholinguist**

---

### 1. One-sentence summary

This chapter argues that grammaticality is itself a homeostatic property cluster—a maintained, projectible coupling between morphosyntactic form and structural meaning, detected by a noisy "feeling of ungrammaticality" shaped by entrenchment and processing, rather than an essentialist property of sentences.

---

### 2. Strengths

- **Individual differences receive principled treatment (lines 313-324).** The account correctly predicts that speakers with different exposure histories—including education levels, literacy, and L1/L2 background—will show systematic variation in grammaticality judgments. The citation of my 2012 and 2018 work is accurate and well-integrated. The French-dominant bilingual prediction for age expressions (Table 15.3, line 296-300) is precisely the kind of testable asymmetry that usage-based theory generates but essentialist accounts cannot.

- **The detector-vs.-coupling distinction is methodologically crucial (lines 59-93).** By separating the *category* (the maintained form–value coupling) from the *readout* (the feeling of ungrammaticality), the chapter explains why acceptability judgments are noisy, why illusions occur, and why frequency effects matter without collapsing grammaticality into pure statistics. The five-part mechanism sketch (lines 70-80) makes explicit how token-level processing, individual entrenchment, and community norms operate on different timescales—a welcome antidote to theories that confound these levels.

- **Satiation predictions are sharp and novel (lines 276-304).** The claim that satiation should be construction-specific, asymmetric across communities, and sensitive to competitor density (not just raw frequency) is exactly right. The conspicuous-absence account—"we could, but we don't" (line 283)—captures the subjective phenomenology better than zero-likelihood models. The bilingual satiation prediction (Table 15.2) is experimentally tractable.

---

### 3. Weaknesses

- **Individual differences are mentioned but underexploited (lines 313-324).** The section cites my work correctly but doesn't follow through on the implications. If grammaticality judgments vary systematically by education, literacy, and exposure—*within the same language community*—then the "community entrenchment" story needs much more specification. Whose entrenchment counts? Professional linguists'? University-educated speakers'? The median? The chapter gestures at grain-of-community questions (line 322: "at what scale is 'community' projectible?") but punts: "The answer is empirical, not stipulated." That's evasive. The empirical reality is that linguists' judgments are *not* representative of the population, yet we treat them as the gold standard. What does the HPC account predict when there's no convergent entrenchment—when the "community" is fragmented along class, education, and exposure lines? This needs confronting, not deferring.

- **The three-register phenomenology (lines 117-154) is speculative and underspecified.** Table 15.2 (lines 139-150) proposes that qualitative jolt, reactive resistance, and normative correction are dissociable registers with distinct timescales and observable markers. But the "falsifier" column is vague: "No early disruption above lexical baseline" (line 143) presupposes we know what the lexical baseline *is* for a given construction, which we often don't. More critically, the chapter provides no evidence that these three registers actually dissociate in the predicted ways. The examples (lines 130-132) are post-hoc: garden-path sentences may indeed produce jolt-without-correction, but that's consistent with many processing models, not distinctive to the triadic Peircean framework. Where's the evidence that *only* this three-way decomposition explains the data? And why should I believe Peirce's emotional/energetic/logical interpretants map onto these linguistic phenomena rather than being a forced fit?

- **The dual-role claim (§15.6, lines 256-268) is underdeveloped relative to its theoretical weight.** The chapter asserts that grammaticality is both a category (projectible) and a mechanism (stabilizes other categories like "noun"), and that this dual role is "characteristic of homeostatic systems rather than paradoxical" (line 352). But it's not self-evident *how* grammaticality-as-mechanism differs from grammaticality-as-category beyond a functional distinction. If the "detector patrols the couplings that constitute other categories" (line 266), what maintains the detector itself? The reflexive loop (line 86: "the coupling tunes the detector; the detector, over time, tunes the coupling") risks circularity unless there's an independent anchor. The chapter notes that "biological HPCs can loop through other channels" (line 88) but doesn't clarify whether the grammaticality loop is stabilized by anything *outside* the loop. This is where the HPC framework needs more analytic grip than metaphor.

---

### 4. Key question for Q&A

Your account predicts that speakers with less exposure to complex syntax—less formal education, less literacy—will have "reliably different acceptability judgments" and "different thresholds" (lines 318-321). But if there's no convergent entrenchment across education levels, what grounds the claim that there's a *single* grammaticality HPC for, say, English? Why isn't the right description that there are *multiple* overlapping grammaticality HPCs—one for university-educated speakers, one for working-class speakers, one for L2 learners—maintained by different mechanisms and projecting differently? And if that's true, whose grammaticality counts when we write the asterisk?

---

### 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The core argument is sound and the entrenchment-based account of individual differences is exactly right, but the chapter needs to confront—not defer—the grain-of-community problem. The three-register phenomenology needs either empirical support or explicit flagging as speculative. The dual-role claim needs sharper analytical development. Fix these and the chapter will be strong.

You claim grammaticality is maintained by a feeling that operates as a "noisy detector" (line 61), which admits illusions, individual differences, and satiation effects as instances of defeasibility (line 28). But when does defeasibility stop being a feature and start being a failure? If the detector is noisy enough that the category frays under contact, thins under generational drift, dissociates into triadic registers that fire independently, and shows individual differences linked to education and exposure (Dąbrowska 2012, line 319) – at what point is the category no longer projecting reliably enough to count as an HPC? You say "the boundaries of a grammatical community are wherever the maintenance mechanisms produce convergent entrenchment" (line 322), but you don't operationalize convergence. What's the threshold? How much divergence can the category tolerate before it stops being a category and becomes a loose family resemblance with no inferential licence?

---

### Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The detector/coupling distinction is genuinely clarifying, the age-expression thread is well-executed, and the satiation predictions are testable. But the triadic phenomenology is under-motivated, the community grain problem is deferred rather than solved, and the left-branch extraction argument rests on an unpublished source. The chapter earns its place in the book, but it needs tighter boundary conditions on what counts as "convergent entrenchment" and either empirical work or explicit flagging-as-speculative for the three-register proposal. The dual-role claim (grammaticality as both category and mechanism, §15.6) is important and defensible, but it depends on the projective stability that the community-grain problem currently threatens.

## Structured Review: Chapter 15 ("Grammaticality itself")

**Reviewer:** Ray Jackendoff

---

### 1. One-sentence summary

The chapter argues that grammaticality is a Homeostatic Property Cluster (HPC) kind—a maintained, projectible coupling between morphosyntactic form and structural meaning, mediated by a noisy "feeling of ungrammaticality" that stabilizes the system through repair and entrenchment.

---

### 2. Strengths

- **Theoretical precision on the form–value–interpretant triad (lines 19-22, 69-80).** The five-part mechanism sketch separating coupling state, processing channel, detector output, decision threshold, and entrenchment update is admirably concrete. This moves the discussion beyond hand-waving about "intuitions" to something empirically tractable. The distinction between coupling (what's entrenched) and licensing (what's permitted) is load-bearing and often conflated in the literature.

- **Sophisticated treatment of illusions as diagnostic evidence (lines 94-114, §15.3).** The garden-path and comparative illusion cases are used exactly right: they show the detector dissociates from the structure. The parallel to visual illusions (Müller-Lyer, line 114) is apt and underexploited elsewhere. The three-register phenomenology (qualitative jolt / reactive resistance / normative correction, lines 117-133) maps cleanly onto processing timescales and gives the account empirical teeth.

- **Falsifiability and empirical commitments throughout.** Table 15.2 (lines 136-150) specifies observable markers, confounds, and falsifiers for each register. The satiation predictions (Table 15.3, lines 287-301) are genuinely testable. The HPC audit (lines 333-346) explicitly states what would undermine the account. This is rare in theoretical linguistics.

---

### 3. Weaknesses

- **Structural meaning needs more defense (lines 15-17, 160-163).** The account hinges on "structural meaning" as the value side of the coupling, but the boundaries are asserted more than argued. Why is *Colorless green ideas sleep furiously* not ungrammatical despite semantic absurdity? The answer (line 162: "the morphosyntactic coupling is intact; the absurdity is lexical") presupposes we already know where morphosyntax ends and lexical semantics begins. But that boundary is precisely what's contested in interface debates. The *a*/*an* allomorphy case (lines 164-165) is presented as straddling the boundary, which weakens the distinction further. Either give independent criteria for what counts as "structural meaning" or acknowledge the boundary is itself an HPC (which would be fine, but commit).

- **The Irish English embedded inversion case undercuts the core claim (lines 352-355).** Reynolds writes: "In Irish English, *I don't know what is it* is grammatical and productive." If this is true—and the citations suggest it is—then what does it mean to say English "can't" have embedded inversion? The move to "hasn't been perturbed in most varieties" (line 352) treats grammaticality as dialect-relative, but then the projection claim (§15.1) becomes unstable: projection *to what population*? The chapter needs to either restrict the HPC claim to within-dialect coherence or explain how cross-dialectal divergence squares with "grammaticality as a category" rather than "grammaticalities as a family of overlapping categories."

- **Relevance theory dismissal is too quick (lines 326-331).** Scott-Phillips's account is acknowledged as complementary but then sidelined with a single counterexample (the age construction). But that example doesn't clearly favor the HPC account: *I have twenty years* fails relevance presumptions *because* English lacks the coupling, not independently of it. The two frameworks make overlapping predictions here. If Reynolds wants to claim the HPC account is more granular, he needs cases where relevance theory predicts failure but the HPC account predicts success (or vice versa), not cases where both predict failure for slightly different reasons.

---

### 4. Key question at Q&A

You treat grammaticality as both a mechanism (stabilizing other categories like noun, verb) and a category in its own right (lines 256-269). But this dual role creates a bootstrapping problem: if grammaticality-as-detector is what maintains noun-ness, and noun-ness is what feeds grammaticality judgments, how does the system get off the ground in acquisition? A child can't use grammaticality judgments to learn what nouns are if she needs to know what nouns are to generate grammaticality judgments. Does the HPC account require some non-grammatical anchor (frequency? phonology? semantics?) to bootstrap the coupling, or is there a circularity here that essentialist accounts avoid by positing innate categories?

---

### 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The chapter makes a genuinely novel claim—that grammaticality is an HPC kind rather than a Platonic object or a nominalist fiction—and it does so with unusual empirical discipline. The mechanism sketch, the three-register phenomenology, and the falsifiability commitments are all major strengths. But the structural meaning boundary needs tightening, the dialect-relativity problem needs resolution, and the dismissal of relevance theory needs more work. With those fixes, this would be a strong contribution.
