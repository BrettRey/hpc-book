**Strengths**
- The chapter states a nontrivial ontological claim early and clearly: grammaticality is a maintained coupling between morphosyntax and “structural meaning” (`§The HPC claim`). That is better than treating grammaticality as a primitive or mere notation convention.
- You explicitly separate structural meaning from truth-conditional content, pragmatic felicity, and lexical coherence (`§The HPC claim`, “wiring diagram, not the current”). That helps avoid category mistakes.
- The acceptability/grammaticality split is foregrounded in mechanistic terms (`§The stabilizer: a feeling`): acceptability is detector output; grammaticality is the maintained target. That is the right direction.
- `§The phenomenology of coupling` is commendably cautious in one key sentence: phenomenology is “a readout of the mechanism, not evidence independent of it.” That methodological discipline should be preserved.
- You include explicit falsifiability language in the “Definiteness thread” (“Scoped prediction,” “Falsifier”), which is rare and valuable in this literature.

**Major Concerns**
- The argument is not yet mechanistic enough for skeptical readers. “Zipper,” “immune system,” “standing wave,” and “spinning top” are vivid, but they still function mostly as redescriptions. A mechanism requires explicit variables, update dynamics, and error-correction pathways. “Bayesian” in `§The stabilizer` is asserted, not modeled.
- Normativity is under-distinguished. You move between (i) selected function (historically reproduced effects), (ii) current communal regularity (“what a community has entrenched”), and (iii) analyst-imposed utility (“syntax owns it”) without clear boundaries. In `§Entrenchment in action` (“the reality is constituted by what gets entrenched”), and `§Three registers` (“the habit demands its due”), normativity risks collapsing into current prevalence.
- There is a circularity risk: the “feeling” is both stabilizer of grammaticality and primary evidence for grammaticality (`§Grammaticality as mechanism and category`). Without independent criteria, the account can become detector-validates-target because target-is-what-detector-stabilizes.
- Some empirical passages read over-precise or programmatic rather than established: “~3-sigma absence,” “schema drift in real time,” and the LBE/Homer thought experiment (`§Empirical signatures`, `§Looking forward`). These may trigger immediate resistance from syntacticians as speculative rhetoric.
- `§What doesn’t count` and “syntax owns it” overstate disciplinary boundaries; many phenomena are interface-graded. A specialist will object that your own cross-channel tables already undermine strict ownership claims.

**Priority Fixes**
- Add a short subsection explicitly separating three normative levels: selected function, current-use calibration, and analyst task utility. State which claims live at which level.
- Replace at least one metaphor-heavy section with a concrete process model: input form, predicted structural value, mismatch metric, thresholding, repair action, learning update.
- Tighten acceptability-vs-grammaticality with independent evidence streams (production repair corpora, diachronic retention, acquisition trajectories), not only judgments.
- Downgrade or recast speculative claims unless directly supported; remove rhetorical flourishes that look like placeholders for evidence.

**Decisive Tests**
1. Longitudinal entrenchment test: track a novel construction across cohorts/registers; preregister predicted time-course for detector shift (acceptability), production uptake, and repair behavior.
2. Triadic dissociation test (`§Three registers`): experimentally separate jolt, blockage, and correction with time-resolved measures (RT/eye-tracking + repair choices). If triadic architecture is real, dissociations should be systematic, not anecdotal.
3. Anti-circularity test: predict grammatical status changes using non-judgment data first (usage + repair ecology), then test judgments out-of-sample.
4. Rival-theory adjudication (`§Relevance and the immune system`): construct cases with high inferential relevance but morphosyntactic mismatch, and vice versa; force differential predictions between relevance-tracking and form-value coupling accounts.
