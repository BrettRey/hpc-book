**Strengths**  
Chapter 15 has a strong coordination-mechanism spine. The opening puzzle (produced, understood, and punished) is concrete and analytically useful (`chapters/chapter15.tex:6`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:10`). The “mixed bin” move is excellent because it turns vague variation talk into a conditioning problem (`chapters/chapter15.tex:64`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:73`). The three-way decomposition into dialect, register, and discourse community is also mechanistic rather than taxonomic, especially where each section gives a feedback loop and timescale (`chapters/chapter15.tex:103`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:122`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:133`). This aligns well with Chapter 14’s layered-detector framing (`chapters/chapter14.tex:241`) and sets up Chapter 16’s demand for explicit empirical commitments (`chapters/chapter16.tex:37`).

**Major Concerns**  
The chapter sometimes moves too fast from “useful conditioning model” to “settled ontology.” Claims like “the social variable is a parameter of the grammar itself” (`chapters/chapter15.tex:43`) and “basins are ontologically distinct” (`chapters/chapter15.tex:118`) read stronger than the evidence presented in-chapter. Similar overreach appears in the realism crescendo (“as real as … chemical bond”) (`chapters/chapter15.tex:206`), which blurs descriptive mechanism claims with philosophical verdicts.  
There is also a mechanism-identification issue: “geography/network/identity are descriptions, not mechanisms” (`chapters/chapter15.tex:95`) is rhetorically sharp but too dismissive; those variables can be mechanism inputs. Better to say HPC integrates them into a causal loop rather than replacing them.  
Most importantly, inequity is under-integrated. The O’Connor point appears late and briefly (`chapters/chapter15.tex:147`), so the chapter still reads as if it mainly explains efficient coordination. The planning and board notes explicitly ask for inequity as a co-equal mechanism outcome, with descriptive-normative separation (`notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:20`, `notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:22`, `notes/chapter15_planning.md:9`). Right now, that requirement is only partially met.  
Finally, the “brain’s Bayesian machinery” phrasing (`chapters/chapter15.tex:179`) implies implementation-level commitment that the chapter has not earned.

**Priority Fixes**  
1. Add a short causal template after the mixed-bin formalization (`chapters/chapter15.tex:73`): repeated interaction -> conditional expectations -> sanction/reward -> distributional stabilization; then state this template yields both efficiency and inequity depending on payoff structure.  
2. Soften ontological assertions at `chapters/chapter15.tex:43`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:118`, and `chapters/chapter15.tex:206` into testable claims (“best current explanation,” “predicts X under Y”).  
3. Expand inequity with one concrete institutional case (e.g., hiring or classroom correction) near `chapters/chapter15.tex:147`, and explicitly separate descriptive claim (how norm stabilizes) from normative claim (whether it is justified).  
4. End the chapter with one diagnostic bridge sentence to Chapter 16 (`chapters/chapter16.tex:99`): what observable pattern would distinguish coordination-stabilized inequity from random bias noise.
