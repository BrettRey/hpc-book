**Strengths**
The revision is materially stronger on mechanism and testability. In `The stabilizer: a feeling`, the five-part “detector and maintenance loop” (coupling state, processing channel, detector output, threshold, entrenchment update) is the first place in this chapter where a skeptical reader can see a plausible causal architecture rather than a metaphor-only account. The explicit timescale split (“token-time parsing … individual entrenchment … community norm dynamics”) also improves inferential hygiene.  

`The phenomenology of coupling` is now closer to operationalized science than philosophical gloss: the three-register table with observable markers, confounds, and falsifiers is a real advance. `Entrenchment in action` also handles the acceptability/grammaticality circularity better than before by distinguishing synchronic dissociation from diachronic co-evolution (“reflexive equilibrium dynamic, not a vicious circle”).  

Boundary language is improved in `What doesn’t count` and `Grammaticality as a species of appropriateness`, especially “syntax gets first pass … not sole authority,” which aligns with field-relative framing.

**Remaining Concerns**
The biggest remaining issue is identification: the chapter still moves too quickly from acceptability phenomena to architectural claims. You often infer detector design from judgment behavior without enough independent anchors (production, corpus, learning trajectory, online processing). That is exactly where skeptics will press.  

Some bridge claims remain over-strong. `Syntactic satiation is schema drift in real time` reads as a theoretical identification, but the cited evidence does not yet force that equation. Likewise, “left-branch extraction remains categorically rejected regardless of exposure … across tasks, speakers, and presentation modes” is too absolute for current empirical heterogeneity. Similar overreach appears in “none of them trigger the specific feeling of ungrammaticality” and “the system won’t settle until the coupling closes.”  

Triadic phenomenology is staged, but not yet discriminative enough. The three registers currently rely on partly overlapping observables (RT disruption, hesitation/repair, correction norms), so a critic can say this is relabeling one latent factor. You partially anticipate this (“if measures collapse to one factor…”), but the chapter still sometimes writes as if triadic structure were already established.  

Finally, ownership claims are mostly calibrated, but “Grammaticality is appropriateness at the morphosyntactic grain” risks sounding annexationist unless you keep repeating “for this explanatory purpose” and “in this framework.”

**Priority Fixes (if any)**
1. Add one compact “Identification Strategy” paragraph: acceptability is one indicator, not the evidential base; state the independent data streams required.  
2. Downgrade categorical language to scoped probabilistic claims (“in current English datasets,” “in these tasks,” “to date”).  
3. Convert the five-part loop into a prediction matrix: each component, manipulation, expected signature, failure condition.  
4. In the triadic section, explicitly mark the proposal as a falsifiable staging hypothesis, not a settled decomposition.

**Decisive Tests (what now most matters)**
1. Orthogonal manipulation study: structural mismatch vs processing load crossed factorially, with preregistered predictions for dissociation between detector output and grammatical status.  
2. Longitudinal exposure perturbation: track whether repeated exposure shifts ratings, repair behavior, and production in lagged sequence (to test reflexive-equilibrium dynamics rather than mere task adaptation).  
3. Community-indexed calibration test: same speakers across register/community frames, measuring threshold shifts and repair convergence.  
4. Model comparison: pit MMMG predictions against relevance-based alternatives on out-of-sample data, not post hoc interpretation.
