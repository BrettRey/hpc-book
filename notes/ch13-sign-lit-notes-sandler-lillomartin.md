### Facts
- Sign and speech show comparable throughput: about one proposition every 1–2 seconds. (p. 3)  
- Intelligibility collapses at roughly the same acceleration point in both modalities (about 2.5–3x normal rate). (p. 3)  
- ASL topicalization is linked to a structured topic position plus non-manual marking (brow raise/head tilt/hold), not free ordering. (pp. 12–13)  
- ASL patterns with both “agreement-licensed nulls” and “discourse-licensed nulls” (plain-verb contexts), i.e., two null-argument routes. (pp. 15–16)  
- Classifier constructions can be morphologically very complex yet prosodically minimal (or, conversely, span larger prosodic domains). (pp. 16–17)  
- The chapter explicitly argues modality effects should be modeled bidirectionally (if sign simultaneity is modality-driven, spoken linearity also is). (p. 18)

### Quotations
- “modality independent upper limit” (p. 3)  
- “single cognitive system” (p. 4)

### Key arguments for HPC framing
- The program is explicitly substrate-neutral: same structural tools, different physical channel. (p. 4)  
- Anaphora-relevant behavior is already multi-property (syntax, discourse, agreement), not reducible to one defining cue. (pp. 12–16)  
- Sign-specific residue (especially classifier behavior) is treated as structured but partially modality-conditioned, consistent with cluster-style kinds. (pp. 17–18)

### Cross-references worth noting
- Bellugi & Fischer (1972); Fischer, Delhorne & Reed (1999); Emmorey (2002) on modality-independent processing limits. (p. 3)  
- Lillo-Martin (1986a), Quadros (1995), Bahan et al. (2000) on competing null-argument analyses. (p. 16)  
- Legate (2003) on Warlpiri as a spoken-language parallel to mixed null systems. (p. 16)

### Facts
- Referential loci are established for present and non-present referents; pronouns and agreement target those loci. (pp. 25–27)

- ASL agreement asymmetry: object agreement obligatory, subject agreement optional. (p. 30)

- Four-language comparison (ASL, DGS, Auslan, JSL) reports shared phonetic constraints on agreement realization. (p. 38)  
- Non-manual agreement proposals are criticized as partly untestable and plausibly semantic/pragmatic rather than pure agreement. (pp. 45–46)

### Quotations

- Systematic constraints + optionality/asymmetry argue for interacting properties, not a single essence condition. (pp. 30–38)  
- Non-manual evidence introduces controlled instability/competition (formal vs pragmatic cues), useful for HPC failure-mode analysis. (pp. 45–46)

### Cross-references worth noting

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/sandler-lillomartin2006-ch05-classifier-constructions.pdf

### Facts
- Classifier constructions are presented as productive, pervasive, and attested across many sign languages. (pp. 76–77)  
- They are built from bound morphemes (handshape/location/movement units with meaning), unlike ordinary lexical signs. (pp. 82–83, 90)  
- They violate standard lexical phonology constraints (symmetry/dominance), documented across multiple sign languages. (pp. 90–91)  
- Aspectual behavior differs from ordinary verbs; some classifier strings reject ordinary durational readings. (p. 90)  
- Classifier strings can be monosyllabic or extend across multiple intonational phrases with persistent handshape argument tracking. (pp. 91–92)

### Quotations
- “anomalous subsystem” (p. 76)  
- “not words … but bound morphemes” (p. 92)

### Key arguments for HPC framing
- Strong evidence for the formal-spatial cluster: highly structured, productive, but partially modality-shaped. (pp. 76, 82–83)  
- Persistent handshape across predicate chains provides a mechanism for continuity/entrenchment in referent tracking. (pp. 91–92)  
- Mixed regularity (systematic constraints + iconic load) supports cluster-membership modeling over single-feature definitions. (pp. 82, 90–93)

### Cross-references worth noting
- Supalla (1982/1986), Engberg-Pedersen (1993/1994), Schembri (2003), Aronoff et al. (2003). (pp. 77–83, 90–92)  
- Mithun (1984/1986a) verbal-classifier parallels from spoken languages. (pp. 84–89)  
- Explicit bridge to later morphosyntax in Chapter 20 and modality discussion in Unit 5. (pp. 77, 93)

### Facts
- Core mechanism: discourse referents are linked to loci; pronouns point to those loci, including non-present referents via established spatial anchors. (p. 371)  
- The chapter notes broad cross-sign-language convergence in this pronominal type. (p. 371 n.2)  
- Sign pronouns reduce classic spoken-language ambiguity by selecting unique loci in discourse. (p. 376)  
- Person-feature debate: strong argument for first vs non-first contrast, with second/third less grammatically distinct. (pp. 377–378)  
- Modality claim: referential indices are overtly realized in sign, while syntactic constraints remain shared with spoken languages. (p. 379)  
- Role shift/logophoricity is decomposed (shifted reference/attribution/locus) and linked to systematic structural contexts. (pp. 382–385)  
- ASL allows shifted first-person interpretation beyond direct quotation contexts (not just strict direct speech). (p. 383)  
- Null-argument debate: hybrid account (agreement-licensed + discourse/topic-licensed) vs uniform agreement account. (pp. 397–403)  
- Empirical challenge to non-manual licensing: eyetracking study reports no spontaneous plain-verb null objects and non-target gaze patterns. (p. 400)

### Quotations
- “associated with locations in space” (p. 371)  
- “important effect of modality” (p. 379)  
- eyegaze was “random” (p. 400)

### Key arguments for HPC framing
- Two-cluster architecture is explicit: semantic anaphora (indices/topics) + formal spatial realization (loci/agreement). (pp. 371–379, 397–403)  
- Role shift/logophoric phenomena show additional interacting mechanism layers, not a single anaphora trigger. (pp. 382–387)  
- Competing analyses and mixed evidence identify likely failure/competition zones (plain verbs, non-manual cues, neutral forms). (pp. 399–401)

### Cross-references worth noting
- Meier (1990); Lillo-Martin & Klima (1990); Neidle et al. (2000) on person/index analyses. (pp. 377–379)  
- Engberg-Pedersen (1993/1995) on shifted reference/locus distinctions. (pp. 382–383)  
- Thompson & Emmorey (2003) eyetracking evidence; Legate (2003) Warlpiri hybrid nulls. (pp. 400, 403)

### Facts
- Strong sentence-initial bias for topics is stated as a cross-linguistically familiar pattern in sign as well. (p. 405)  
- ASL topic system is split (tm1/tm2/tm3) with distinct structural/discourse functions (moved vs base-generated vs shifted-topic behavior). (pp. 407–411)  
- ASL topic stacking is constrained (max two; ordering restrictions by topic type). (pp. 410–411)  
- ISL comparison: less consistent dedicated non-manual topic marking; topic boundaries often cued intonationally (78% with phrase break). (p. 413)  
- Sentence-final prominence/focus uses structured operations (doubling, cleft-like constructions), not unconstrained free order. (pp. 414–430)  
- ASL doubling constraints: final position, head-only, one per sentence, island-sensitive. (pp. 418–422)  
- LSB differs: allows one double per clause and broader local doubling patterns, but still respects island boundaries. (pp. 423–424)

### Quotations
- “strong predominance of sentence-initial position” (p. 405)  
- “perhaps a pragmatic role” (p. 413)

### Key arguments for HPC framing
- Discourse alignment is multi-cue (syntax, prosody, non-manuals), matching mechanism-cluster expectations. (pp. 405–415)  
- Cross-language variation (ASL vs ISL vs LSB) is structured, not random, fitting HPC-style family resemblance. (pp. 413, 423–424)  
- Doubling/cleft constraints provide formal stability while allowing language-specific parameterization. (pp. 418–430)

### Cross-references worth noting
- Aarons (1994), Rosenstein (2001), Wilbur (1994a/1997), Petronio (1993), Quadros (1999). (pp. 407–430)

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/sandler-lillomartin2006-ch25-effects-of-modality.pdf

### Facts
- The chapter argues modality comparison strengthens claims of true universals when abstract properties converge across signed/spoken systems. (pp. 477–478)  
- Spatial reference points for pronouns/agreement are unbounded and not lexically listable, motivating analysis pressure. (pp. 481–485)  
- One response is index-sharing/copying analyses; another (Liddell) treats crucial location components as gestural rather than morphemic. (pp. 483–485)  
- Counterarguments for linguistic agreement status: verb-class restrictions, morphophonological constraints, syntactic effects, and acquisition patterns. (p. 486)  
- Proposed synthesis: agreement/pronouns combine linguistic structure with gestural-spatial specification. (pp. 487–488)  
- Working-memory asymmetry: signers show shorter memory span for signs; chapter links this to pressure for greater simultaneity per sign. (p. 493)  
- Language-age evidence: NSL and ABSL show early-stage kernels of agreement/classifier systems, with gradual conventionalization across cohorts/generations. (pp. 507–508)  
- ASL–ISL contrast suggests relative age/social ecology affects abstraction level in classifier systems. (p. 508)

### Quotations
- “unlimited number of possible locations” (p. 485)  
- “linguistic and extralinguistic aspects” (p. 486)  
- “shorter working memory span” (p. 493)

### Key arguments for HPC framing
- Best-fit account is explicitly hybrid (linguistic + gestural), i.e., cluster-based rather than essence-based. (pp. 487–488)  
- Mechanism layer is explicit: processing constraints, modality affordances, and diachronic entrenchment jointly maintain system properties. (pp. 493, 507–508)  
- Cross-linguistic developmental gradient (ASL/ISL/NSL/ABSL) supports homeostatic maintenance over time, with variable surface realization. (pp. 507–508)

### Cross-references worth noting
- Lillo-Martin & Klima (1990), Cormier et al. (1998), Aronoff et al. (2000/2005) for index-copying/agreement analyses. (pp. 482–484)  
- Liddell (1994/1995/2000) for gestural “indicating verb” challenge. (pp. 485–486)  
- Senghas (1995/2000), Kegl et al. (1999), Aronoff et al. (2004), Sandler et al. (2005) for emergence trajectories in new/village sign languages. (pp. 507–508)
