**3. Weaknesses**

- The chapter’s central term, `structural meaning`, is doing too much unexplicated work. It is introduced as what grammar contributes before lexical content fills the slots, but later mismatch detection depends on intended meaning and contextual comparison, and the HPC audit mixes pairing, compatibility, and entrenchment [chapter15.tex#L15](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/HPC%20book/chapters/chapter15.tex#L15) [chapter15.tex#L17](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/HPC%20book/chapters/chapter15.tex#L17) [chapter15.tex#L92](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/HPC%20book/chapters/chapter15.tex#L92) [chapter15.tex#L338](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/HPC%20book/chapters/chapter15.tex#L338). Actionable: define `structural meaning` operationally, with annotation criteria and worked borderline cases, so readers can tell whether a mismatch is in form, meaning, context, or analyst paraphrase.
- The chapter sometimes presents a research program as if it were already evidentially settled. The three-register table is explicitly predictive, the combined-violation example rests on “impressionistic reports,” and the chapter itself says some predictions await systematic testing [chapter15.tex#L152](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/HPC%20book/chapters/chapter15.tex#L152) [chapter15.tex#L262](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/HPC%20book/chapters/chapter15.tex#L262) [chapter15.tex#L274](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/HPC%20book/chapters/chapter15.tex#L274). The claims about individual differences and cross-linguistic neural signatures may be relevant, but because they invoke specific findings, they need verification [chapter15.tex#L319](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/HPC%20book/chapters/chapter15.tex#L319) [chapter15.tex#L344](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/HPC%20book/chapters/chapter15.tex#L344). Actionable: separate established evidence, illustrative conjecture, and proposed tests much more sharply.
- The chapter insists grammaticality is community-indexed, but it does not apply that discipline consistently. `She don't like it` is treated as a case that “demands `doesn't`,” which quietly reinstates a standard-language baseline at exactly the point where the chapter says norms are discourse-community specific [chapter15.tex#L206](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/HPC%20book/chapters/chapter15.tex#L206) [chapter15.tex#L230](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/HPC%20book/chapters/chapter15.tex#L230) [chapter15.tex#L323](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/HPC%20book/chapters/chapter15.tex#L323). Actionable: identify the relevant speech community for every starred example and stop using stigmatized vernacular forms as default evidence of ungrammaticality.

**4. Key question**

What empirical procedure would let two analysts decide, across dialects and contact settings, that a case involves failure of morphosyntactic coupling rather than mismatch in register, lexical choice, or contextual interpretation, without smuggling back in the very normative grammar this chapter wants to dislodge?

**5. Verdict**

Revise & Resubmit — the anti-essentialist core is strong, but the chapter needs a tighter account of `structural meaning`, stricter community-indexing of judgments, and a cleaner separation between evidence and programmatic prediction.
