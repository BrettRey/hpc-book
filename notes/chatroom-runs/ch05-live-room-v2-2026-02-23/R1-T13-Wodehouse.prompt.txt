Standard reference grammars classify them as pronouns, and their distribution supports that much: they head NP-like phrases in the same slots as core pronouns, as verb and preposition complements. But they are non-canonical pronouns. Unlike core personal pronouns, they lack a person/gender/case paradigm and are distributionally defective in the most salient way: they resist ordinary free subject use (cf. \mention{Somebody left} vs. \mention{*Each other left}).
