**Strengths**
- The chapter’s strongest phenomenological move is methodological restraint in **“The phenomenology of coupling: What it’s like”**: you explicitly say phenomenology is a “readout of the mechanism,” not independent proof. That is the right anti-introspection stance.
- The triadic proposal in **“Three registers”** is conceptually promising. The sequence from “qualitative jolt” to “reactive resistance” to “normative correction” can map onto distinct functional layers in cognition (pre-reflective perturbation, action-level inhibition/repair, socially sedimented normativity).
- You avoid treating first-person reports as sovereign evidence by immediately testing the model against **“Grammaticality illusions”** and **“Empirical signatures”** (garden paths, comparative illusions, satiation, individual differences). This keeps phenomenology tethered to psycholinguistic phenomena.
- **“What doesn’t count”** is useful negative delimitation. Separating grammatical mismatch from lexical, phonetic, and semantic anomaly helps prevent phenomenology from becoming a catch-all for “anything that feels off.”
- The key claim that the asterisk is a fallible instrument (“detector, not oracle”) is well aligned with current empirical linguistics and gives the phenomenology section real potential explanatory value.

**Major Concerns**
- The mechanism remains under-specified relative to the rhetoric. “Zipper,” “immune system,” and “spinning top” are productive heuristics, but they currently outrun a causal model. A reader can still ask: what exact computational/processing states correspond to each register?
- The triad is not yet operationalized. In **“Three registers,”** “all three registers fire” suggests simultaneity, but your own examples imply dissociation. Without timing criteria, the model risks post hoc labeling.
- The distinction among pre-reflective experience, reflective judgment, and norm-governed correction is present but not clean. “Feeling” is used for all three levels, which blurs phenomenological architecture:
  - pre-reflective perturbation during parsing,
  - reflective acceptability report in a task,
  - normative correction in interactional practice.
- The chapter overreaches ontologically in places. **“The HPC audit”** and **“Looking forward”** move from lived disturbance/correction to strong claims like “It earns its category status” and “there’s no ‘grammar’ there to resist.” Phenomenology plus behavioral data can support a mechanistic hypothesis; they do not by themselves settle metaphysics.
- **“Grammaticality as a species of appropriateness”** is insightful but risky. It may collapse grammatical normativity into broader social appropriateness unless you specify boundary conditions more sharply.

**Priority Fixes**
1. Add a compact operational table after **“Three registers”**: register, timescale, behavioral markers, experimental paradigms, predicted confounds, falsifier.
2. Rewrite “all three registers fire” to allow partial and dissociable activation profiles; this is essential for empirical tractability.
3. Insert a short clarifying subsection explicitly distinguishing:
   - pre-reflective disruption (online),
   - reflective metalinguistic judgment (offline),
   - norm-governed correction (interactional/social).
4. Temper ontology language in **“The HPC audit”** and **“Looking forward”**: frame HPC status as best current explanatory model, not settled ontic fact.
5. In **“Grammaticality as a species of appropriateness,”** define hard inclusion/exclusion tests so grammaticality does not dissolve into generic “offness.”

**Decisive Tests**
1. **Temporal dissociation test**: combine eye-tracking/EEG with immediate production repair and delayed acceptability ratings. Prediction: early “jolt” signals can occur without later rejection; falsifier: no temporal dissociation across conditions.
2. **Orthogonality manipulation**: factorially vary structural violation vs processing difficulty (e.g., garden-path load). Prediction: “block” tracks structural mismatch more than load; falsifier: block follows load equally in grammatical and ungrammatical items.
3. **Interactional norm test**: corpus + elicited dialogue across discourse communities tracking spontaneous correction types. Prediction: “normative correction” varies with community entrenchment while early disruption may not; falsifier: no community-structured correction differences.
4. **Entrenchment-shift longitudinal test** (your own “scoped prediction” logic): repeated exposure to novel constructions across cohorts/registers. Prediction: reflective judgments shift before pre-reflective fluency, then norm uptake; falsifier: no staged shift or no cohort/register structure.
