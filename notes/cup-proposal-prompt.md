## Source Material

### Synopsis (~880 words)

Linguistics has a metaphysics problem it doesn't know it has.

When linguists ask whether something is a noun, whether a construction is grammatical, whether two sounds are the same phoneme, they assume these questions have determinate answers. Categories exist; items either belong or don't; our job is to discover the boundaries. This assumption is almost never examined. It's also wrong, or at least, wrong in the way it is usually understood.

The default view is essentialist: categories are defined by necessary and sufficient conditions, and membership is binary. But essentialist definitions fail, reliably and predictably. *Furniture* denotes discrete objects yet rejects the plural (*three furnitures*). *The* marks definiteness except when it doesn't (*go to the hospital*, *the tiger is endangered*). Reciprocals (*each other*, *one another*) pass some pronoun diagnostics and fail others. The boundaries linguists need keep refusing to stay sharp.

The standard retreat is toward gradience: categories are prototypes or feature bundles, membership is graded, boundaries are fuzzy. This captures the phenomena but leaves stability unexplained. If categories are just statistical clusters in some similarity space, why do they persist? Why don't they drift into chaos? Why can children acquire them? Why do they support the generalizations that linguistics depends on? Prototype descriptions record gradience; they don't explain why the gradience holds still.

Both positions share an unexamined assumption: that the choice is between crisp essences and vague resemblance. There is a third option, developed in philosophy of biology and applicable here: categories can be real, stable, and projectible without being defined by necessary and sufficient conditions. They can have fuzzy boundaries without being arbitrary. What makes this possible is *mechanism*, the causal processes that hold category properties together.

This book argues that many core linguistic categories -- including grammatical categories -- are homeostatic property-cluster (HPC) kinds. As the book's central slogan puts it: **A category is a profile, stabilized by mechanisms, projectible relative to purposes.** An HPC kind is characterized not by an essence but by a family of properties that co-occur because underlying mechanisms (acquisition, entrenchment, alignment, transmission) keep them bundled. No single property is necessary; membership is graded; boundaries are genuinely fuzzy. But the category is real because the mechanisms are real, and it supports induction because the clustering is stable.

Crucially, projectibility is *field-relative*. A category like "Proper Noun" may project distributional facts for a syntactician while "Proper Name" projects referential facts for a semanticist. They are not one messy category, but two overlapping HPCs, each optimized for a specific domain of explanation. This principle offers a powerful diagnosis for linguistic controversy: subfields often clash when they fail to recognize they are tracking different, field-indexed profiles of the same causal system.

The framework originates in philosophy of biology, where it resolved the species problem, the long failure to define *species* by essential conditions. A species is not a type defined by morphology, genetics, or interbreeding capacity. It is a population whose properties cluster because gene flow, development, ecology, and selection maintain the clustering. The extension to linguistics is not a loose analogy. Linguistic categories are populations too, distributed across speakers, transmitted across generations, stabilized by processes that can be identified and tested. Grammatical categories are a central case: a grammatical category is a linguistic population whose properties cluster because acquisition, entrenchment, interactive alignment, and iterated transmission maintain the clustering over time.

Consider *the*: children acquire it early and overgeneralize it; high frequency entrenches its distributional frame; conversational feedback prunes anomalous uses; transmission across generations filters out unstable variants. These mechanisms explain why *the* remains a coherent category despite weak definites, generic definites, and other apparent exceptions: they're peripheral variation tolerated by a stable core, not counterexamples to a failed definition.

Getting the metaphysics right has consequences.

For methodology: boundary cases stop being embarrassing noise and become informative signal. A word that half-passes a diagnostic is not a failure of analysis; it is a datum about where the category's mechanisms lose grip. Methods for studying boundaries -- gradient judgments, corpus distributions, experimental probes -- become principled rather than ad hoc.

For theory: the gradience-versus-discreteness debate dissolves. Categories can be gradient at the edges and stable at the core because mechanisms do both -- they tolerate peripheral variation while maintaining central coherence. Generative and usage-based approaches have both identified real patterns: the structural regularities that generativists document and the frequency-sensitivity that usage-based work reveals. What neither tradition has provided is a satisfying account of why the patterns hold together. The HPC framework supplies that account. The stability is real; the explanation is mechanistic, not essentialist.

For typology: cross-linguistic categories can be assessed rather than assumed. When linguists posit RESULTATIVE or EVIDENTIAL as universal categories, they can now ask: Is this a single kind maintained by shared mechanisms, or a taxonomy grouping language-specific kinds that merely resemble one another? The answer is empirical, not stipulative. Failure modes -- where the proposed category fractures under examination -- become expected and informative.

The book develops this argument in four parts. Part I diagnoses the problem: what essentialism and nominalism each get wrong, and what the confusion has cost. Part II introduces the solution: projectibility (why categories support induction), homeostasis (why they hold together), and failure modes (how to recognize when you don't have a kind). Part III applies the framework to major grammatical categories (countability, definiteness, word classes, constructions) and extends it to non-grammar cases (phoneme contrasts, register) to show what transfers and what doesn't. Part IV draws out what changes: grammaticality itself is an HPC, social mechanisms keep categories aligned across speakers, cross-domain extension falls out of the framework as a predicted capacity, and a gauntlet of predictions and defeat conditions puts the theory at empirical risk.

The claim is not that HPC is a useful tool among others. The claim is that many linguistic categories are HPC kinds, that linguists have been implicitly theorizing about them with inadequate metaphysics, and that getting the metaphysics right changes what questions we ask and what answers we accept.

This is a foundations book. The foundations have been unclear. This book makes them explicit, defensible, and usable.

### Chapter Outline

**Part I: The Problem (3 chapters)**

- Ch 1: Words That Won't Hold Still -- The unexamined assumption that categories have determinate boundaries. The impasse: essentialism fails on data, prototype theory fails on explanation. The Huddleston email as through-line.
- Ch 2: Essentialism and its Discontents -- Why the default view fails. The CGEL verbless-clause microcosm. Systematic failures across traditions. Biology parallel: species concepts. Why essentialism persists despite its failures.
- Ch 3: What We Haven't Been Asking -- The nominalist temptation (Haspelmath, Croft). The cost: stability unexplained. Phase-transition analogy. Survey of traditions with "the right instincts" (usage-based, CxG, sociolinguistics, functionalism). Peirce-Boyd synthesis introduced.

**Part II: The Fix (5 chapters)**

- Ch 4: Categories Without Essences -- Boyd's HPC framework and Millikan's copied kinds. The species/grammar parallel. Five stabilizers sketched. Engagement with Ambridge, Khalidi, Slater, Magnus. Sign language convergence evidence.
- Ch 5: Discrete from Continuous -- How continuous substrates produce discrete categories. Phase transitions as model. Hyperreal formalization. Three worked examples (fun drift, English reciprocals, Shilluk number).
- Ch 6: The Stabilizers -- Mechanism inventory along timescale x locus dimensions. Case study 1: quotatives cross-linguistically. Case study 2: independent relative whose. Mechanisms as categories, categories as mechanisms.
- Ch 7: Projectibility and the Good Bet -- What mechanisms buy. The HPC slogan. Polish aspect case study. Peircean apparatus (interpretant = habit). Field-relative projectibility with worked examples. Degrees of projectibility.
- Ch 8: Failure Modes -- Thin, fat, and negative diagnosis. The inflation problem. Two-diagnostic test. Seven-step audit template. Coupling continuum (hard -> loose -> composite).

**Part III: Categories Reconsidered (6 chapters)**

- Ch 9: Countability -- Two-cluster architecture (individuation + count). Implicational hierarchy. Object-mass nouns. Diachronic cases (pea, data, cattle). ABM simulation. Cross-linguistic evidence.
- Ch 10: Definiteness and Deitality -- Two HPCs conflated: semantic definiteness and form cluster. Millikan's proper function. Weak definites, generic definites as derived functions. "Deitality" proposed. Cross-linguistic (Greek, Japanese).
- Ch 11: Lexical Categories -- Gradient of mechanism density. Noun/verb as skeleton, adjective as plumage, adverb as wastebasket, pronouns as braided clusters. Diachronic and acquisitional evidence.
- Ch 12: Pro-form Gender -- English gender as system-wide personhood partition. Maintenance spectrum: English (transparent) -> German (hybrid) -> French (entrenched). Chain-coherence phenomena.
- Ch 13: Sign Language Anaphor -- Modality independence test. Two-cluster architecture in sign. Spatial loci as formal cluster. Cross-linguistic sign variation (ASL, BSL, village sign languages).
- Ch 14: The Category Zipper -- Scale-invariance from phonemes to constructions. Four tiers of coupling. Designed-to-fail controls. Negative cases (Russian definiteness at zero).

**Part IV: What Changes (4 chapters)**

- Ch 15: Grammaticality Itself -- Grammaticality as maintained coupling. Structural meaning vs compositional semantics. Ungrammaticality as noisy detector. Illusions, satiation, individual differences.
- Ch 16: The Social Stabilization of Kinds -- Inter-speaker convergence. Categories as coordination equilibria. Dialect and register as two mechanistic dimensions. Belfast network study.
- Ch 17: How Categories Travel -- Cross-domain extension as predicted capacity. Boyd's theory-constitutive metaphor. Worked example: canine TRAIL. The Boyd circle (reflexivity as coherence). Engaging cognitive linguistics via Rapoport's Rules.
- Ch 18: The Gauntlet -- Predictions, stress tests, defeat conditions. Synthesis ledger. Asymmetric fraying and sticky-lock drift predictions. Explicit falsifiers. Research agenda.

Plus: Postscript, Glossary, Formalization Appendix, Predictions Appendix, Subject/Author/Language Indexes.
