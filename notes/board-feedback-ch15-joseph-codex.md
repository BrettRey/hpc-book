**Strengths**  
Chapter 15 is already strong on one Josephian priority: it treats variation as structured maintenance rather than failure. The opening red-pen vignette and double-copula case (`chapters/chapter15.tex:6`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:23`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:35`) effectively show how correction, uptake, and distribution can coexist. The mixed-bin argument is also clear and technically useful (`chapters/chapter15.tex:54`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:73`): you move from pooled noise to conditioned systems without romanticizing variation. The register section is especially good where it frames formality as monitored stance rather than “different language” (`chapters/chapter15.tex:112`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:114`), which aligns with the planning goal that varieties are conditioning environments (`notes/chapter15_planning.md:9`). Tone is mostly explanatory rather than moralizing, including the inequity passage (`chapters/chapter15.tex:147`).

**Major Concerns**  
The standards/standard-language distinction is not yet explicit enough. “Standard” shifts meaning across the chapter: named variety (`chapters/chapter15.tex:23`), formal condition (`chapters/chapter15.tex:65`), and community norm (`chapters/chapter15.tex:131`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:154`). That conflation is exactly what the Joseph notes warned against (`notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:8`). You need one early sentence that separates *standards* (monitoring regimes) from *a standard language* (historically elevated variety).  

Second, standardization is described as mechanism but under-historicized. The opening classroom correction is concrete (`chapters/chapter15.tex:6`), but it reads as transhistorical unless anchored to institutions and periodization. The board notes explicitly ask when this mechanism enters and why (`notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:10`). As written, “red ink,” “gatekeeping,” and “prestige norms” are persuasive but ahistorical (`chapters/chapter15.tex:14`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:112`).  

Third, native-speaker ideology is underdeveloped. Chapter 15 barely names the category, despite planning pressure to link prestige, gatekeeping, and inequity causally (`notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:20`, `notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:38`, `notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:98`, `notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:108`). Without that section, the inequity argument risks looking generic rather than specifically linguistic. Adjacent framing exists in Chapter 14 (`chapters/chapter14.tex:239`), but Chapter 15 needs its own mechanism account.

**Priority Fixes**  
1. Add a definitional hinge in §15.1: “standards are monitoring institutions; a standard language is one historically scaffolded variety.” Place it before the mixed-bin formalization (`chapters/chapter15.tex:21`).  
2. Historicize standardization with one dated institutional micro-case (exam policy, school correction regime, style manual rule), then tie it to your mechanism vocabulary (entrenchment, gatekeeping, looping). This directly answers `notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:10`.

Insert a native-speaker subsection after inequity (`chapters/chapter15.tex:147`): distinguish acquisition-history profile vs credential category, and model the causal chain (institutional screening -> corpus skew -> competence reification -> further screening). End with a diagnostic bridge to Chapter 16’s operational commitments (`chapters/chapter16.tex:101`).
