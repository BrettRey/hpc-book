**Strengths**
- The chapter has a clear target: grammaticality as a maintained form-meaning coupling, not an unanalyzed primitive. In `The HPC claim`, you correctly isolate “structural meaning” from truth-conditional semantics and pragmatic felicity, which is exactly the right move for a constructionist account.
- The best passages are construction-local: age (`I have twenty years`), progressive aspect (`I write` vs `I’m writing`), countability (`two furnitures`), and deitality (`go to the hospital` vs `go to hospital`). These show that what is “wrong” is a learned pairing, not metaphysical impossibility.
- `The phenomenology of coupling` is admirably self-disciplined at the outset (“readout… not evidence independent of it”). That methodological caveat helps prevent introspective inflation.
- `Grammaticality illusions` and `Empirical signatures` are directionally strong: you treat garden paths, comparative illusions, satiation, and individual differences as signatures of detector architecture, not annoyances.
- The explicit “Scoped prediction / Falsifier” near the end is publishable behavior. Keep that spirit and expand it.

**Major Concerns**
- The account is still too metaphor-forward to count as mechanistic in its current form. “Zipper,” “immune system,” “standing wave,” and “spinning top” are useful heuristics, but you need a process model with state variables: what is represented, how mismatch is computed, how thresholds update with exposure, and at what grain.
- You claim construction-specificity, but operationalization remains mostly global. A skeptical construction grammarian will ask: which constructions, with which inheritance relations, show which learning curves? Right now, many claims remain chapter-wide rather than construction-indexed.
- Acceptability vs grammaticality is asserted, not modeled. You say “acceptability’s what the detector reports; grammaticality’s the category,” but then often infer category status from the same reports without an explicit correction model for processing load, familiarity, and social-indexical pressure.
- `Three registers` (jolt/block/correction) risks post hoc Peircean mapping. A skeptic will say the triad is being fit to phenomenology after the fact. You need independent diagnostics for each register.
- The most vulnerable point for skeptical specialists is the strong LBE claim (especially “~3-sigma absence,” unsatiatability explanation via competitor density). That is the first place rejection will happen if methods and corpora are not nailed down.
- “Syntax owns it” in `What doesn’t count` is rhetorically punchy but conceptually risky; it invites immediate pushback from psycholinguists and philosophers who treat grammaticality judgments as multi-system outputs.

**Priority Fixes**
- Replace metaphorical machinery with an explicit detector model (inputs, priors, mismatch function, update rule, output).
- Commit to construction-grain hypotheses for 3-4 named constructions (age, progressive, determiner stacking, one extraction domain), each with predicted acquisition trajectory and error profile.
- Add a formal separation layer for acceptability and grammaticality (e.g., latent coupling strength vs observed rating as noisy measurement).
- Demote or remove over-precise placeholders unless methodologically defended (e.g., “3-sigma absence”).
- Tighten phenomenology: keep it minimal and test-linked; avoid letting it carry explanatory weight.

**Decisive Tests**
- Pre-registered 2x2 design crossing processing difficulty and entrenchment for the same construction family. If judgments track only processing, your coupling claim weakens; if they track entrenchment after processing control, it strengthens.
- Longitudinal exposure on marginal constructions with transfer tests. Crucial prediction: improvement should be construction-specific (and inheritance-structured), not globally “grammar-softening.”
- Community-indexed change test for deitality/determiner sequences across cohorts and registers. Your own “high-exposure informal first” prediction is excellent; implement it with panel data.
- Adversarial falsifier: if high-frequency, low-processing-cost forms remain stably rejected without social/register partitioning, detector-noise cannot explain the pattern; you would need a stronger grammatical constraint account.
