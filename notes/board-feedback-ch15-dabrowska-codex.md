**Strengths**  
From an individual-differences standpoint, Chapter 15 gets the central reframing right: variation is treated as mechanism output, not error noise (`chapters/chapter15.tex:21`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:41`). The latent-mixture framing is especially strong because it explicitly distinguishes unconditioned from conditioned distributions (`chapters/chapter15.tex:71`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:73`). You also place speaker-linked parameters inside the model rather than outside grammar (`chapters/chapter15.tex:73`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:154`). That is exactly the move needed to keep heterogeneity structurally meaningful. The acquisition section supports this by treating children as learning socially partitioned systems from the outset (`chapters/chapter15.tex:158`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:164`). Conceptually, this is well aligned with the structured-variance commitments made in Ch. 16 (`chapters/chapter16.tex:48`).

**Major Concerns**  
The chapter still under-specifies individual-level dispersion. Repeated claims that conditioned systems are “tight” (`chapters/chapter15.tex:69`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:156`) risk implying residual within-group variance is negligible, but no diagnostics tell us when speaker differences are theoretically central versus ignorable. Ch. 14 already provides the missing empirical anchor: systematic grammatical knowledge differences across adults (`chapters/chapter14.tex:295`, `chapters/chapter14.tex:297`). That evidence is not integrated here.  
Second, the planned “native speaker” treatment is absent from the draft (no native-speaker analysis in `chapters/chapter15.tex`), despite being marked as crucial in planning (`notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:6`, `notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:98`, `notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:103`). Without that section, the chapter risks replacing one abstraction (“English”) with another (“community norm”) while leaving native competence implicitly point-like.  
Third, evidential language occasionally outruns operationalization (e.g., “phenomenologically high” probabilities and realism claims: `chapters/chapter15.tex:177`, `chapters/chapter15.tex:206`).

**Priority Fixes**  
1. Add a short subsection explicitly defining `native speaker` as a distribution over constructions, not a single competence state. Separate acquisition-history nativeness from credential/gatekeeping nativeness (`notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:38`, `notes/ch15/board-ch15-plan-notes.md:98`).  
2. Extend the formalism from `P(Form|D,R,C)` to include speaker-level structure (e.g., `S_i`) and explicit variance predictions: when maintenance holds, variance should partition more between speakers/communities than within-speaker free fluctuation (`chapters/chapter15.tex:73`, `chapters/chapter16.tex:48`, `chapters/chapter16.tex:123`).  
3. Tighten evidence language to Ch. 16-style commitments: measurable signatures, held-out prediction criteria, and defeat thresholds (`chapters/chapter16.tex:99`, `chapters/chapter16.tex:124`). Recast broad assertions as “predicts / consistent with / falsified if,” and tie at least one claim directly to the individual-difference evidence already cited in Ch. 14 (`chapters/chapter14.tex:295`).
