**Strengths**

The revision is materially stronger and now looks like a serious mechanism paper rather than a slogan defense. In **“The stabilizer: a feeling”**, the five-part loop (“coupling state,” “processing channel,” “detector output,” “decision threshold,” “entrenchment update”) is a real advance: it gives reviewers something to operationalize. The explicit timescale split (token-time, individual, community) is especially valuable.

You also made the circularity issue explicit rather than evasive. The line in **“Entrenchment in action”** distinguishing synchronic misfire from diachronic reshaping (“reflexive equilibrium dynamic, not a vicious circle”) is exactly the right move for a looping-effects account. That is recognizably Hacking-compatible social reactivity, not static faculty metaphysics.

Triadic phenomenology is no longer just rhetoric. In **“Three registers”** plus Table 14.1 (markers, confounds, falsifiers), you now stage dissociable predictions. This is the first place where skeptical psycholinguists can say: fine, show me the factor structure.

Boundary language is also improved in **“Grammaticality as a species of appropriateness.”** The two operational tests for classifying a failure as grammatical (minimal morphosyntactic repair + convergent community repairs) are concrete and field-relative.

**Remaining Concerns**

Mechanism specification is still underidentified. “Coupling strength,” “threshold,” and “noise” are all latent and currently inferred from the same judgmental behavior. Without independent measures, different causal stories can fit the same data.

The triadic mapping risks partial relabeling of familiar processing stages. “Normative correction” is still vulnerable to contamination by schooling/prescriptivism, even though you acknowledge that confound. You need stronger discriminants between socially entrenched grammar and explicit norm policing.

Some precision claims remain too hard-edged for current evidence: e.g., left-branch extraction “remains categorically rejected regardless of exposure.” That is likely too absolute cross-speaker and cross-task. Likewise, “syntax gets first pass on it” will read as jurisdictional overreach unless further softened.

Reflexive equilibrium is now named, but the loop can still look circular unless you specify what anchors it from outside the detector’s own outputs (production distributions? interactional correction rates?).

**Priority Fixes (if any)**

1. Add a compact causal diagram (or mini-equations) explicitly separating state variables, observables, and update rules.  
2. Add one paragraph on identification: which measures track coupling vs threshold vs processing noise independently.  
3. Soften categorical language (“categorically,” “exactly what we’d expect”) unless tied to stated scope conditions.  
4. Tighten social-reactivity mechanism: prestige, network position, and register-gating should be part of the loop, not just the Homer vignette.

**Decisive Tests (what now most matters)**

1. Longitudinal interaction experiment: manipulate correction ecology and exposure separately; test whether thresholds shift faster than coupling, or vice versa.  
2. Orthogonal mismatch design: structural mismatch x processing load, with RT/eye-tracking/repair behavior, to test three-register dissociation.  
3. Cohort/register diffusion test for a novel construction (your scoped prediction near determiner sequences is good): informal/high-exposure groups should move first.  
4. Identification test: combine corpus production frequencies (coupling proxy) with online disruption metrics (detector proxy); if they collapse into one factor, your architecture weakens.
