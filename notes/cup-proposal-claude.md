# Book Proposal: Words That Won't Hold Still

**Cambridge Studies in Linguistics**
**Author:** Brett Reynolds (Humber Polytechnic, Toronto)
**Manuscript Status:** Complete (494 pages, 18 chapters)

---

## 1. Overview

Linguistics has a metaphysics problem it doesn't know it has. When linguists ask whether something is a noun, whether a construction is grammatical, or whether two sounds are the same phoneme, they assume these questions have determinate answers. Categories exist; items either belong or don't; our job is to discover the boundaries. This assumption, nearly universal across theoretical traditions, is rarely examined. It is also fundamentally mistaken.

The default view is essentialist: categories are defined by necessary and sufficient conditions, membership is binary, and exceptions signal analytic failure. But essentialist definitions fail, reliably and predictably, across every domain of linguistic analysis. Mass nouns denoting discrete objects reject the plural (*three furnitures*). The definite article *the* marks definiteness except in weak definites (*go to the hospital*) and generic definites (*the tiger is endangered*). Reciprocals (*each other*, *one another*) pass some pronoun diagnostics and fail others. Phonemes show gradient membership. Constructions resist clean boundaries. The patterns linguists need keep refusing to stay sharp.

The standard retreat is toward gradience: categories are prototypes, feature bundles, or statistical clusters; membership is graded; boundaries are fuzzy. This captures the phenomena but leaves stability unexplained. If categories are just similarity-based groupings, why do they persist across generations? Why don't they drift into chaos? Why can children acquire them from fragmentary input? Why do they support the inductive generalizations that linguistic theory depends on? Prototype descriptions record gradience without explaining why the gradience holds still.

Both positions share an unexamined assumption: that the choice is between crisp essences and vague resemblance. There is a third option, developed in philosophy of biology and directly applicable to linguistics. Categories can be real, stable, and projectible without being defined by necessary and sufficient conditions. They can have fuzzy boundaries without being arbitrary. What makes this possible is *mechanism* -- the causal processes that hold category properties together over time.

This book argues that many core linguistic categories, including major grammatical categories, are homeostatic property-cluster (HPC) kinds. An HPC kind is characterized not by an essence but by a family of properties that co-occur because underlying mechanisms keep them bundled. The mechanisms for grammatical categories are acquisition (children converge on shared patterns), entrenchment (high-frequency use stabilizes distributional frames), interactive alignment (conversational feedback prunes anomalies), and iterated transmission (generational filtering selects for learnable variants). No single property is necessary for category membership; boundaries are genuinely fuzzy; but the category is real because the mechanisms are real, and it supports induction because the clustering is stable.

The framework originates in philosophy of biology, where Richard Boyd's HPC theory resolved the species problem -- the long failure to define *species* by essential morphological, genetic, or reproductive criteria. A species is not a type defined by fixed properties. It is a population whose properties cluster because gene flow, developmental constraint, ecological pressures, and selection maintain the clustering. The extension to linguistics is not metaphorical. Linguistic categories are populations too: they are distributed across speakers, transmitted across generations, and stabilized by identifiable causal processes. The parallel is structural, not analogical.

Consider the English definite article *the*. Children acquire it early and overgeneralize it; high token frequency entrenches its canonical distributional frame (determiner slot in NPs); conversational feedback from interlocutors prunes anomalous uses; transmission across generations filters out unstable innovations. These mechanisms explain why *the* remains a coherent grammatical category despite weak definites, generic definites, and other apparent exceptions. Such cases are not counterexamples to a failed definition; they are peripheral variation tolerated by a stable mechanistic core. The category persists because the mechanisms persist.

Crucially, projectibility is *field-relative*. A category projects generalizations reliably within a particular domain of explanation, not universally. "Proper Noun" may project distributional facts for a syntactician (resists determiners, resists pluralization) while "Proper Name" projects referential facts for a semanticist (refers to individuals, tracks identity rigidly). These are not one messy category imperfectly analyzed. They are two overlapping HPCs, each optimized for a specific analytical purpose, maintained by partially distinct mechanisms. This principle offers a powerful diagnostic for persistent controversies: subfields often clash when they fail to recognize they are tracking different, field-indexed profiles of the same underlying causal system.

Getting the metaphysics right has consequences across linguistic methodology and theory.

For methodology, boundary cases stop being embarrassing noise and become informative signal. A word that half-passes a diagnostic is not a failure of classification; it is evidence about where the category's mechanisms weaken. Gradient acceptability judgments, corpus frequency distributions, and experimental probes of category membership become principled tools rather than ad hoc supplements. The book develops an explicit audit procedure for distinguishing genuine HPC categories from spurious groupings and provides a two-diagnostic stress test for proposed universals.

For theory, the gradience-discreteness debate dissolves. Categories can be gradient at the edges and stable at the core because mechanisms do both: they tolerate peripheral variation while maintaining central coherence. Generative linguistics has documented real structural regularities; usage-based linguistics has revealed real frequency effects. What neither tradition has provided is a satisfying account of *why the patterns hold together*. The HPC framework supplies that account. The stability is real; the explanation is mechanistic, not essentialist.

For typology, cross-linguistic categories can be empirically assessed rather than stipulated. When linguists posit RESULTATIVE or EVIDENTIAL as universal categories, they can now ask: Is this a single kind maintained by shared mechanisms across languages, or a typological label grouping language-specific kinds that merely resemble one another? The answer becomes an empirical question, not a terminological choice. Failure modes -- where a proposed category fractures under close examination -- become expected and informative rather than theory-threatening.

The book develops this argument systematically across four parts. Part I (three chapters) diagnoses the problem: what essentialism and nominalism each get wrong, what the confusion has cost, and why standard solutions fail. Part II (five chapters) introduces the HPC solution: what projectibility is and why it matters, how mechanisms stabilize clusters, how to identify failure modes, and how to audit proposed categories rigorously. Part III (six chapters) applies the framework to major grammatical categories -- countability, definiteness, lexical categories, pro-form gender -- and extends the analysis to sign language and multi-tier phenomena to test what transfers across modalities and scales. Part IV (four chapters) explores what changes when the metaphysics is corrected: grammaticality itself is an HPC, social mechanisms keep categories aligned across speakers, cross-domain extension falls out as a predicted capacity, and explicit predictions with defeat conditions put the theory at empirical risk.

The book engages deeply with philosophy of science (Boyd, Millikan, Khalidi, Slater, Magnus, Peirce), cognitive and usage-based linguistics (Bybee, Goldberg, Tomasello, Langacker, Croft), formal and descriptive traditions (Huddleston, Pullum, Baker), typology (Haspelmath, Dixon), and sociolinguistics (Labov, Milroy). It uses the Cambridge Grammar of the English Language (Huddleston & Pullum 2002) as its descriptive framework and draws on cross-linguistic evidence from typologically diverse languages, sign languages (including emerging systems like Nicaraguan Sign Language), and diachronic change. It includes agent-based simulations with stress tests (designed-to-fail controls) and a final chapter of explicit, falsifiable predictions.

The claim is not that HPC theory is a useful analytic tool among others. The claim is that many linguistic categories *are* HPC kinds, that linguists have been implicitly theorizing about them with inadequate metaphysics, and that getting the metaphysics right changes what questions we ask, what methods we trust, and what answers we accept.

This is a foundations book. It makes explicit what has been tacit, defensible what has been assumed, and usable what has been confused. It is the first monograph to apply HPC theory from philosophy of biology systematically to linguistic categories, and it does so not as loose analogy but as rigorous framework with testable consequences.

Cambridge Studies in Linguistics is the natural home for this work. The series has published landmark volumes on grammatical theory (Baker, Croft, Newmeyer), usage-based approaches (Bybee), typology (Dixon), and foundational debates (Haspelmath). This manuscript sits at the intersection of all four: it offers a new metaphysical foundation for grammatical categories, synthesizes insights from usage-based and formal traditions, engages typological questions about cross-linguistic validity, and tackles head-on the foundational question of what linguistic categories are. The series' broad mandate ("general, theoretical, and descriptive linguistics") and distinguished editorial board make it the ideal venue for work that cuts across subdisciplinary boundaries to address questions that matter to the field as a whole.

## 2. Chapter Summary

### Part I: The Problem

**Chapter 1: Words That Won't Hold Still** -- Linguists assume categories have determinate boundaries; this assumption is rarely examined and systematically misleading. The impasse between essentialism (fails on data) and prototype theory (fails on explanation) leaves the field without adequate foundations.

**Chapter 2: Essentialism and its Discontents** -- The default essentialist view fails reliably across traditions and domains. The CGEL verbless-clause debate as microcosm. Biology faced the same problem with species; HPC theory was the solution.

**Chapter 3: What We Haven't Been Asking** -- The nominalist temptation (Haspelmath, Croft) avoids essentialism by denying cross-linguistic categories but leaves stability unexplained. Usage-based, constructionist, and sociolinguistic traditions have "the right instincts"; the missing piece is mechanistic metaphysics.

### Part II: The Fix

**Chapter 4: Categories Without Essences** -- Boyd's HPC framework and Millikan's copied kinds applied to grammar. The species-grammar parallel is structural, not metaphorical. Five stabilizing mechanisms sketched. Engagement with Ambridge, Khalidi, Slater, Magnus.

**Chapter 5: Discrete from Continuous** -- How continuous substrates produce discrete categories. Phase transitions as model. Hyperreal formalization. Three worked examples: *fun* drift (English adjective), English reciprocals, Shilluk number marking.

**Chapter 6: The Stabilizers** -- Mechanism inventory along timescale and locus dimensions. Case study 1: quotatives cross-linguistically. Case study 2: independent relative *whose*. Mechanisms are categories; categories are maintained by mechanisms.

**Chapter 7: Projectibility and the Good Bet** -- What mechanisms buy: reliable inductive inference. The HPC slogan introduced. Polish aspect as case study. Peircean apparatus (interpretant, habit, would-be). Field-relative projectibility with worked examples. Degrees of projectibility.

**Chapter 8: Failure Modes** -- Thin clustering, fat clustering, and negative diagnosis. The inflation problem in typology. Two-diagnostic stress test. Seven-step audit template. Coupling continuum from hard (phonemes) to loose (grammatical categories) to composite (constructions).

### Part III: Categories Reconsidered

**Chapter 9: Countability** -- Two-cluster architecture (individuation + count morphosyntax). Implicational hierarchy. Object-mass nouns as boundary cases. Diachronic evidence (*pea*, *data*, *cattle*). Agent-based simulation with stress tests. Cross-linguistic variation.

**Chapter 10: Definiteness and Deitality** -- Two conflated HPCs: semantic definiteness and article-system form cluster. Millikan's proper vs derived function. Weak and generic definites as derived functions. "Deitality" proposed for form cluster. Greek and Japanese comparisons.

**Chapter 11: Lexical Categories** -- Gradient of mechanism density. Noun/verb as high-density skeletons, adjective as plumage (variable density), adverb as wastebasket (thin clustering), pronouns as braided sub-clusters. Diachronic and acquisitional evidence.

**Chapter 12: Pro-form Gender** -- English gender as system-wide personhood partition. Maintenance spectrum: English (transparent semantic basis) → German (hybrid) → French (morphologically entrenched). Chain-coherence phenomena (pronoun-antecedent agreement as maintenance check).

**Chapter 13: Sign Language Anaphor** -- Modality independence test. Two-cluster architecture survives in sign: functional cluster (reciprocal semantics, valency reduction) + formal cluster (spatial loci, reduplication). Cross-sign variation (ASL, BSL, village sign languages).

**Chapter 14: The Category Zipper** -- Scale-invariance from phonemes to constructions. Four coupling tiers. Designed-to-fail controls. Negative cases (Russian zero-marked definiteness fails HPC audit).

### Part IV: What Changes

**Chapter 15: Grammaticality Itself** -- Grammaticality as maintained structural coupling, not semantic compositionality. Ungrammaticality as noisy detector (illusions, satiation, individual differences all follow from mechanistic account).

**Chapter 16: The Social Stabilization of Kinds** -- Inter-speaker convergence mechanisms. Categories as coordination equilibria. Dialect and register as two mechanistic dimensions. Belfast social network study as empirical case.

**Chapter 17: How Categories Travel** -- Cross-domain extension as predicted capacity of HPCs. Boyd's theory-constitutive metaphor. Worked example: canine TRAIL (olfactory tracking → visual following → abstract pursuit). The Boyd circle (reflexivity as theoretical coherence). Engagement with cognitive linguistics via Rapoport's Rules.

**Chapter 18: The Gauntlet** -- Predictions, stress tests, and defeat conditions. Synthesis ledger compares HPC predictions against alternatives across five empirical domains. Asymmetric fraying and sticky-lock drift as novel predictions. Explicit falsifiers. Research agenda.

**Postscript, Glossary, Formalization Appendix, Predictions Appendix, Indexes** -- Technical resources and comprehensive indexing (subject, author, language).

## 3. Market Positioning

### Primary Audience

The primary readership is professional linguists and graduate students in theoretical, descriptive, and cognitive linguistics. The book addresses foundational questions about the nature of linguistic categories -- questions that cut across subdisciplines. Syntacticians asking what a grammatical category is, semanticists asking how definiteness works, typologists asking whether cross-linguistic categories are real, and acquisitionists asking how children learn from gradient input will all find direct engagement with their empirical domains. The argument is technical but accessible; it presupposes familiarity with grammatical analysis but not with philosophy of science (the relevant concepts are introduced clearly).

### Secondary Audiences

Philosophy of language and philosophy of science represent significant secondary audiences. The book offers the first systematic application of HPC theory to linguistic categories and develops original extensions (field-relative projectibility, multi-tier coupling, the projectibility-as-interpretant synthesis). Philosophers working on natural kinds, categorization, and scientific realism will find a substantive new case study with worked-out consequences.

Cognitive scientists, particularly those working on concepts, categorization, and learning, will recognize connections to prototype theory, conceptual combination, and inductive inference. The book provides a mechanistic account of how categories remain stable despite gradient structure -- a question central to cognitive science but rarely addressed with this level of linguistic detail.

Computational linguists and NLP researchers working on category induction, part-of-speech tagging, and cross-linguistic transfer will find the HPC framework relevant to questions of how categories generalize and when models trained on one language transfer to others.

### Neighbouring Works (Not Competitors)

There are no direct competitors. No existing monograph applies HPC theory to linguistic categories. The closest neighbours are:

- **Croft (2001), *Radical Construction Grammar*** -- shares skepticism about cross-linguistic categories but offers nominalism, not HPC realism.
- **Bybee (2010), *Language, Usage and Cognition*** -- documents usage-based mechanisms (entrenchment, frequency effects) but doesn't provide a metaphysical framework.
- **Haspelmath (2010), "Framework-free grammatical theory"** -- argues for comparative concepts over universals; HPC theory provides the missing ontology.
- **Khalidi (2013), *Natural Categories and Human Kinds*** -- philosophy of science foundation but no linguistic application.
- **Slater (2015), "Your Properties Are Showing"** -- develops HPC extensions but focuses on social kinds, not language.

The manuscript synthesizes these lines of work into a unified framework with systematic linguistic application.

### Course Adoption Potential

The book is not a textbook, but it will be assigned in graduate seminars on grammatical theory, philosophy of linguistics, usage-based approaches, and linguistic typology. It addresses questions that emerge naturally in advanced syntax and semantics courses (What is a grammatical category? Why do diagnostics conflict? Are categories discrete or gradient?) and provides a principled answer with worked examples. The final chapter's research agenda makes it especially valuable for seminars preparing students for dissertation research.

Upper-level undergraduate courses in linguistics and cognitive science may assign selected chapters (especially Ch 1-3, Ch 9-10, Ch 14-15) as accessible introductions to foundational debates.

### Why Now?

Three converging developments make this book timely:

1. **Empirical pressure**: Gradient acceptability, corpus frequency effects, cross-linguistic variation, and acquisitional data have accumulated to the point where essentialist foundations are untenable. The field needs a new framework.

2. **Theoretical readiness**: Usage-based, constructionist, and sociolinguistic traditions have identified the relevant mechanisms (frequency, entrenchment, alignment, transmission) but lack a unifying metaphysical framework. HPC theory provides it.

3. **Interdisciplinary momentum**: Philosophy of science has developed HPC theory substantially since Boyd's original work (Khalidi, Slater, Magnus, Craver); cognitive science has moved toward mechanistic explanation; linguistics is ready for the synthesis.

The manuscript arrives at the moment when the field has the empirical base and theoretical tools to take this step but hasn't yet done so. It will define the terms of debate for foundational questions about categories for the next decade.

## 4. Suggested Reviewers

**Martin Haspelmath** (Max Planck Institute for Evolutionary Anthropology / Leipzig University)
Leading typologist and critic of cross-linguistic categories; developed the comparative concepts framework. Would provide rigorous assessment of whether HPC theory answers typological objections to universals.

**William Croft** (University of New Mexico)
Author of *Radical Construction Grammar* and proponent of nominalism about linguistic categories. Ideal reviewer to assess whether the HPC alternative succeeds where essentialism fails and whether it avoids nominalism's explanatory gaps.

**Joan Bybee** (University of Arizona, Emerita)
Pioneering work on usage-based mechanisms (frequency, entrenchment) that stabilize grammatical patterns. Well-positioned to evaluate whether the mechanistic account is empirically adequate and theoretically productive.

**Muhammad Almoammer** (University of California, San Diego)
Works on lexical semantics, experimental syntax, and cross-linguistic categorization. Would assess the empirical case studies (countability, definiteness) and experimental predictions.

**Paul Pietroski** (Rutgers University)
Philosopher of language working on linguistic ontology and the nature of grammatical categories. Would evaluate the metaphysical argument and its consequences for linguistic theory.

**Adele Goldberg** (Princeton University)
Leading construction grammarian; work on argument structure and category learning. Would assess whether the framework successfully integrates constructionist insights and whether it has consequences for acquisition theory.
