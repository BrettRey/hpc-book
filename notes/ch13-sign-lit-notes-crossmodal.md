## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/zeshan2020-cross-modal-typology.pdf

**Facts**
- [p. 6] Relative vs absolute modality effects are explicitly distinguished; relative effects are attested in both modalities but with skewed frequency, absolute effects are attested in only one modality.
- [p. 6] Non-decimal numeral systems in sign are currently rare in the surveyed data, while spoken-language typology reports **21%** pure/hybrid vigesimal systems (Comrie 2013).
- [pp. 7–8] Spoken-language negation by affixation is frequent (**395/1,157** languages in Dryer 2013), while sign-language negative affixing is reported as rarer and more lexically constrained.
- [p. 8] Sign languages commonly use negative particles and non-manual negation (e.g., head movement), and suprasegmental negation is argued as an absolute or very strong relative modality effect.
- [p. 9] Distributive aspect in sign is described as using 3D spatial modulation (height, distance, movement extent, one- vs two-handed realization), with gradient/iconic mapping.
- [p. 13] Sensory-perception metaphor analysis is based on data from **24 sign languages**.
- [pp. 14–17] Two metaphor types are identified: **double-stage** (literal sensory meaning + metaphorical extension) and **single-stage** (metaphorical lexical meaning without an attested literal sensory lexical stage in that language).
- [p. 18] “All known sign languages” are reported as using spatial pointing for personal pronouns.

**Quotations**
- [p. 5] “The immediate aim of cross-modal typology must therefore be to chart the territory of a wide range of grammatical-functional domains.”
- [p. 13] “It is however vital to make these judgements within a principled framework.”
- [p. 18] “All known sign languages use pointing in space for personal pronouns.”

**Key arguments for HPC framing**
- [pp. 5, 10–11] Modality effects are treated as a **graded design space** with category movement as evidence grows; this supports non-essentialist, cluster-like theorizing rather than single-feature definitions.
- [pp. 11–13, 18] Classification depends on analytic choices (especially whether co-speech gesture is included), directly relevant to whether anaphora is modality-specific or modality-general.
- [pp. 14–17] Sublexical iconicity can contribute stable semantic structure; this supports an HPC view where multiple co-occurring properties (not one criterion) sustain the category.
- [p. 18] Pronoun pointing can shift from “absolute” to “relative” modality effect depending on multimodal comparison standards; this is an explicit modality-independence stress test.

**Cross-references worth noting**
- [pp. 1–2] Zeshan & de Vos (2012) on village/shared sign languages (variation backdrop).
- [pp. 6–8] Johnston (2018), Lutzenberger (2018) on negation/non-manuals.
- [p. 9] Liddell (2003) on linguistic+gestural mixture.
- [pp. 13–18] Zeshan & Palfreyman (2019) for sensory-perception metaphors.
- [p. 4] Hodge et al. (2019) on semiotic diversity/composite signaling.

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/garcia2020-deixis-anaphora-eyegaze.pdf

**Facts**
- [p. 1] The paper defines two sign-language meaning modes: “saying while showing” (transfer structures) vs “saying without showing” (lexical/pointing/fingerspelling).
- [p. 6] Transfer units are reported as highly frequent: up to **70%** in narratives and up to **30%** in prescriptive/argumentative genres.
- [p. 8] In cross-linguistic results cited from Pizzuto et al. (2008): lexical units favored for introducing referents (**50–83%**), transfer units favored for maintaining/reintroducing (**76–95%**), manual pointing for anaphoric reference only **3–7%**.
- [p. 8] Personal transfers are mostly for animate referents; size/shape and situational transfers are preferred for inanimates; double transfers reintroduce but do not introduce new referents.
- [p. 8] Comparative dataset covers 11 SLs: LSF, LIS, LSR, DGS, VGT, PJM, SASL, NS, Libras, LCSh, LSM.
- [pp. 11–12] Addressee gaze is described as fixed, signer gaze as highly mobile; gaze is argued to be central for creating/tracking loci.
- [p. 12] Gaze alone is described as sufficient to reactivate a locus/referent after establishment.
- [p. 15] Authors report multiple structural similarities across several SL corpora, especially in transfer structures.

**Quotations**
- [p. 1] “The concept of ‘enunciation’ is decisive for understanding the role of eye-gaze, as being at the foundation of deixis and the key vector of referential creation and tracking in SL discourse.”
- [p. 12] “The signer’s gaze alone is subsequently sufficient to reactivate the locus, thereby reactivating the associated referent.”
- [p. 13] “What distinguishes indeed the illustrative intent… is the prolonged break of eye contact between the signer and the addressee.”
- [p. 15] “In these visual languages… it is the shared eye-gaze that anchors deixis.”

**Key arguments for HPC framing**
- [pp. 1, 6, 12] Strong two-cluster architecture: referent tracking via lexical/pointing system plus transfer-based iconic system.
- [pp. 11–12] Causal mechanism claim: gaze coupling/decoupling organizes person distinctions and locus maintenance.
- [pp. 12–14] Role-shift and transfer are treated as controlled frame-switch mechanisms, not noise.
- [pp. 12, 14] Refresh strategy mechanism: loci can be activated/reactivated by gaze (with optional pointing), relevant to maintenance/failure dynamics.
- [p. 15] Cross-linguistic recurrence of transfer structures supports cluster stability across languages.

**Cross-references worth noting**
- [pp. 7–8, 15] Pizzuto et al. (2008) on cross-linguistic reference/anaphora patterns.
- [pp. 4–5, 8] Hodge et al. (2019) on semiotic diversity in reference.
- [p. 11] Meier (1990) on person distinction debates.
- [pp. 11, 15] Liddell (2003) as major competing account (gesture/proform directionality).
- [p. 15] Corpora links: LS-COLIN, Creagest, VGT.

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/schlenker2018-visible-meaning.pdf

**Facts**
- [p. 10] Loci can be created “on the fly” with no clear upper bound beyond performance/memory limits.
- [p. 10] Sign pronouns are reported to obey core binding constraints (Condition A/B, Strong Crossover) similarly to spoken-language systems.
- [pp. 11–14] ASL pointing/locus system is argued to support nominal, temporal, modal, and locative anaphora within one indexing system.
- [pp. 14–17] Dynamic binding claim: sign data are argued to show pronoun dependency on existential antecedents outside standard scope/c-command.
- [pp. 24–25] Role-shift typology: LSC/DGS allow mixed perspectives; ASL/LSF show shift-together effects in tested cases.
- [p. 30] Complement-set anaphora is degraded with default plural loci (ratings shown: few 3.6; most 2.8), but embedded loci permit complement reference (examples rated 7).
- [pp. 31–32] High/low loci encode systematic inferences (e.g., tall/powerful vs short) with quantified acceptability contrasts.
- [pp. 36–37] Height indexing shows quasi-gradient behavior and interacts with ellipsis patterns in controlled paradigms.
- [p. 40] Emmorey & Herzig (2003) experiment: gradient classifier positioning (30 stimuli) tracks gradient spatial interpretation by signers.
- [p. 48] Strickland et al. (2015): non-signers are reported as highly accurate at mapping endpoint-marked signs to telic interpretations.
- [pp. 46–49] Grammar and iconicity are argued to interact at the logical core; module-separation remains unresolved.

**Quotations**
- [p. 10] “there seems to be an unlimited number of potential distinctions in sign language”
- [p. 25] “the typology of context-shifting operations in sign language mirrors that found in spoken language… [but] in sign language Role Shift is overtly realized.”
- [p. 46] “sign language has, in some areas, strictly richer expressive resources than spoken language does”
- [p. 49] “one and the same expression… can display a logical behavior… while also having an iconic function.”

**Key arguments for HPC framing**
- [pp. 5–6] Explicit dual hypothesis: logical visibility + iconicity; anaphora is not one mechanism but coordinated properties.
- [pp. 10, 14–17, 21] Loci behave as both variable-like and feature-like (“featural variables”), supporting a cluster model over monocriteria.
- [pp. 24–26, 42–45] Role Shift combines formal context-shift behavior with iconic constraints (“maximal iconicity”), matching HPC-style co-maintained properties.
- [pp. 46–49] Modality challenge is explicit: either sign is richer, or speech+gesture comparison narrows the gap; this is directly relevant to substrate-neutral HPC evaluation.
- [p. 10] Memory/performance limits on locus differentiation provide direct evidence for processing-bound failure pressure.

**Cross-references worth noting**
- [pp. 9–10] Lillo-Martin & Klima (1990) on loci as indices.
- [pp. 18–21] Kuhn (2016) loci-as-features critique.
- [pp. 23–25] Quer (2005, 2013); Herrmann & Steinbach (2012) on role-shift/context-shift typology.
- [pp. 29–31] Nouwen (2003) on complement-set anaphora baseline.
- [p. 40] Emmorey & Herzig (2003) classifier gradience experiment.
- [p. 48] Strickland et al. (2015) telicity bias evidence.
- [pp. 47–48] Goldin-Meadow & Brentari (2017), Davidson (2015) for speech+gesture / demonstrational alternatives.

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/pizzuto2011-lexematic-units-poster.pdf

**Facts**
- [pp. 2–3] Two referential-expression classes are foregrounded: **Lexematic Units (LU)** and **Highly Iconic Structures (HIS)**.
- [p. 3] HIS are reported as frequent in discourse (“30% to 70% of recognizable RE,” citing prior literature).
- [p. 2] Poster reports prior findings that HIS can reach **70%** in narratives and **80–95%** in anaphoric/maintenance contexts (citing Sallandre 2003; Antinoro Pizzuto et al. 2008).
- [pp. 2–3] In the authors’ analyzed materials, HIS are used more frequently than LU for the same meaning units.
- [pp. 2–3] Authors report recurring/regular HIS composition patterns and stronger contextualization than LU.
- [p. 2] “to look” is analyzed as expressible through manual and non-manual components, including eye-gaze.
- [p. 3] Excluding HIS from lexical modeling is linked to perceived dictionary sparsity (reported averages ~2,500–5,000 terms).

**Quotations**
- [p. 3] “Referring to the same units of meaning, HIS were used more frequently than LU.”
- [p. 3] “we observed strong regularities among the HIS elements, and among the composition choices.”
- [p. 2] “The ‘to look’ action might be expressed by manual and non manual components…”

**Key arguments for HPC framing**
- [pp. 2–3] Supports two-cluster architecture: conventional lexematic units plus structured highly iconic units.
- [p. 3] HIS are argued to be patterned/regular rather than idiosyncratic, consistent with cluster-property stability.
- [p. 3] Calls for revising lexicon models to include iconic multilinear structures, consistent with non-essentialist category boundaries.

**Cross-references worth noting**
- [pp. 2, 4] Antinoro Pizzuto et al. (2008) on deixis/anaphora/HIS across ASL, LSF, LIS.
- [pp. 2–3, 4] Cuxac and Antinoro Pizzuto (2010) on emergence/norm/variation and LU/HIS distinction.
- [pp. 2, 4] Sallandre (2003) on discourse unit proportions.
- [pp. 3, 4] Johnston (2008) (“no lemmata, no corpus”) as opposing lexicalization pressure point.
