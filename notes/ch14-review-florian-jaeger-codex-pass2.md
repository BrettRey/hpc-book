**Strengths**

This pass is materially stronger. The mechanism is no longer just metaphorical: in `The stabilizer: a feeling`, the five-part loop (`Coupling state`, `Processing channel`, `Detector output`, `Decision threshold`, `Entrenchment update`) gives a usable scaffold for experimental design. The explicit timescale split (token-time vs individual entrenchment vs community dynamics) is especially important and publishable.

Triadic phenomenology is also much better staged as testable rather than introspective. `The phenomenology of coupling: What it’s like` plus Table “Operational readout for the three-register proposal” gives observables, confounds, and falsifiers. The sentence “If these measures collapse to one undifferentiated factor…” is exactly the right kind of self-risking move.

You also improved the circularity issue. The reflexive-equilibrium line in `Entrenchment in action` (time-slice separability, diachronic coupling of detector and category) reframes this as a dynamical feedback problem rather than a definitional loop.

Boundary language is substantially better calibrated in `Grammaticality as a species of appropriateness` and the “syntax gets first pass, not sole authority” formulation. That fits the book’s field-relative framing.

**Remaining Concerns**

The core gap is identifiability. The chapter still lacks a formal detector model that can be distinguished from strong processing-only alternatives. Right now, most evidence in `Grammaticality illusions` and `Processing difficulty and beyond` is also predicted by expectation/retrieval models without a distinct “grammaticality detector” construct.

Concretely, you need to show variance explained beyond surprisal, entropy reduction, retrieval interference, and reanalysis cost. Otherwise “noisy detector” risks being a relabeling of parser difficulty plus social priors.

Triadic phenomenology is operationalized, but still vulnerable to one-factor collapse: jolt, resistance, and correction may just be severity/introspection levels of the same latent process. You acknowledge this, but the chapter should state what statistical result would count as failure of triadic architecture (not just mnemonic downgrade).

Some claims remain too sharp for the evidential base:
- “Left-branch extraction remains categorically rejected regardless of exposure” is too absolute; keep “in current paradigms/samples.”
- “No coherent interpretation” for comparative illusions is too strong; many participants construct partial/coerced interpretations.
- `HPC audit` ends with “meets the two diagnostics strongly enough”; given current evidence, “provisionally” is safer.

**Priority Fixes (if any)**

Add a compact quantitative box (half page) defining latent variables and links:
- \(G\): coupling strength (construction × speaker × community)
- \(P\): processing load
- \(D\): detector output \(f(G, P) + \epsilon\)

Then state two competing model families and what empirical pattern would favor each. This one addition would significantly increase credibility with skeptical psycholinguists and philosophers of science.

**Decisive Tests (what now most matters)**

1. Fit hierarchical predictive models to the same datasets: processing-only vs processing+coupling vs processing+coupling+triadic channels; adjudicate with out-of-sample metrics (LOO/WAIC), not narrative fit.  
2. Run orthogonal manipulations of structural mismatch and processing load; test whether a coupling variable explains residual judgment/repair variance after processing controls.  
3. Do longitudinal satiation with near-categorical vs gradient constraints and explicit cohort/register structure; test predicted differential drift rates.  
4. Estimate a latent-factor model across RT/eye-tracking/EEG, repairs, and correction norms to test whether three partially dissociable registers are needed.  
5. Test community-indexed threshold adaptation (register-switch/code-switch contexts) with within-speaker random slopes; this is central to your ownership/boundary claims.
