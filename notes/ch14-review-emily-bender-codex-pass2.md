**Strengths**
- The revision substantially improves mechanism visibility. In **“The stabilizer: a feeling”**, the five-part loop (`coupling state`, `processing channel`, `detector output`, `decision threshold`, `entrenchment update`) plus explicit timescales makes the architecture testable rather than purely metaphorical.
- The chapter now does real operational work on phenomenology. **Table 14.1** (“Operational readout for the three-register proposal”) gives observables, confounds, and falsifiers for `qualitative jolt`, `reactive resistance`, and `normative correction`. That is a major step toward construct discipline.
- The acceptability/grammaticality problem is framed more rigorously as dynamics, not definitional recursion. The **“reflexive equilibrium dynamic, not a vicious circle”** line in **“Entrenchment in action”** is the right move.
- Boundary framing is improved relative to likely earlier drafts: **“syntax gets first pass on it, not sole authority”** and the appropriateness-channel table are consistent with field-relative projectibility and avoid most disciplinary imperialism.

**Remaining Concerns**
- Construct validity still wobbles around `structural meaning`. It is described as “wiring diagram,” but the chapter does not yet give criteria that let independent analysts decide whether a given violation is structural, lexical, or pragmatic without smuggling in prior grammaticality judgments.
- The detector model is verbally explicit but not analytically explicit. “Bayesian,” “threshold,” and “noise” are invoked, but no formal dependency is specified, so different causal stories remain compatible with the same data.
- Triadic phenomenology is better staged, but mapping `sign/object/interpretant` to the three registers still reads as interpretive overlay unless you specify dissociation predictions that would *fail* the mapping.
- Several claims still outrun evidence:
  - “the progressive … is required” in English (too categorical across dialects/registers/discourse types),
  - “Syntactic satiation is schema drift in real time” (identity claim stronger than evidence supports),
  - “meets the two diagnostics strongly enough” (audit sounds more conclusive than the chapter’s own evidence base).
- The chapter sometimes slides from “community-indexed detector” to broad ontological conclusions about grammaticality without an intermediate methodological bridge (how we infer category structure from noisy, socially stratified detectors).

**Priority Fixes (if any)**
- Add a compact formal scaffold (even toy-level): define variables over time (`G_t`, `D_t`, `P_t`, threshold `θ_t`) and state at least one directional equation-level prediction.
- Add a short “Construct Boundaries” subsection with decision rules for classifying mismatch as morphosyntactic vs other channels, using adversarial examples.
- Recalibrate absolute language to scoped language in the three spots above (progressive, schema drift identity, HPC audit strength).
- State a triangulation principle explicitly: no claim about grammaticality from ratings alone; require converging evidence (online processing + repair behavior + community distribution).

**Decisive Tests (what now most matters)**
- A factorial dissociation experiment manipulating structural mismatch and processing load independently. Prediction: early-jolt, repair behavior, and normative correction should partially dissociate, not collapse.
- Longitudinal exposure study on marginal constructions with hierarchical modeling of threshold shift vs pure familiarity effects (tests “detector tuning” vs “coupling change”).
- Cross-cohort/register change tracking for a genuinely emerging construction (your own falsifier logic in the definiteness thread is good; execute it).
- Latent-structure test: if a one-factor model fits all “three registers” as well as a three-factor model, triadic phenomenology should be downgraded to heuristic status.
