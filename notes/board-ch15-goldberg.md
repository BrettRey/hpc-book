1. **One-sentence summary**

The chapter argues that grammaticality is not a timeless rule-bound essence but a learned, community-maintained form-structural-meaning coupling, while acceptability judgments are only noisy detector readings of that coupling.

2. **Strengths**

- The distinction between entrenched coupling and mere formal possibility is the chapter’s best move: it lets you treat age expressions as failures of conventionalized constructional pairing rather than as mysterious rule gaps, and that is exactly the right usage-based direction (lines 21, 22, 90, 92).
- The detector section is unusually strong because it does not stop at sloganizing; it separates coupling state, processing load, detector output, threshold, and entrenchment update, and then ties them to different timescales and evidential consequences (lines 69, 73, 75, 79, 84).
- The “what doesn’t count” discussion gives the reader real diagnostics rather than rhetoric. The repair-based test for distinguishing grammaticality from register, accent, and lexical choice is concrete and potentially usable beyond this chapter (lines 204, 206, 207, 228).

3. **Weaknesses**

- I understand why you want a stable target for the detector, but the chapter keeps granting discreteness too early. You invoke “discrete grammaticality” outright (line 9), yet much of your own argument depends on graded entrenchment, defeasibility, and strain in the network (lines 28, 196, 198). As written, discreteness looks asserted rather than derived. Either show how categorical outcomes emerge from competition among learned constructions, or stop treating discreteness as the default ontology.
- I agree with the effort to distinguish coupling from judgment, but `licensing` is doing suspicious work. When you say “the grammar permits” slots that the community later fills (lines 21, 22), and contrast thinly coupled forms with forms “never licensed at all” (lines 63, 65), you risk smuggling back an autonomous grammar beneath the usage-based story. Actionably: redefine licensing as an emergent property of the learned network of constructions and inheritance links, not as a background permission system.
- The chapter’s boldest empirical claims outrun the evidence actually presented. The super-additive violation example is explicitly impressionistic (line 262), the predictions section admits that several claims are still prospective (line 274), and the cross-linguistic ERP convergence claim in the audit (line 344; finding needs verification) does not by itself establish a homeostatic maintenance account. Either give one worked empirical case in detail, or mark these passages more clearly as research commitments rather than support already in hand.

4. **Key question**

If grammaticality is fully grounded in a learned network of partially overlapping constructions, what explanatory work is left for a separate, system-level HPC of “grammaticality itself,” beyond the constructions and their competition?

5. **Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit** — the chapter is conceptually promising and often excellent, but it has not yet earned its discreteness claim, its notion of licensing, or its strongest mechanism-level empirical conclusions.
