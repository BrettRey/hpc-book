**Strengths**
- The chapter is materially stronger on mechanism than in pass 1. In **“The stabilizer: a feeling”**, the boxed **“Mechanism sketch (detector and maintenance loop)”** (coupling state, processing channel, detector output, threshold, entrenchment update) is now explicit enough to anchor experimental design.
- You now separate levels more cleanly: token-time parsing vs individual entrenchment vs community dynamics. That distinction is crucial for avoiding category mistakes in evidence.
- The triadic phenomenology is no longer purely rhetorical. In **“The phenomenology of coupling: What it’s like”**, the three-register table with **observable markers, confounds, and falsifiers** is a real operational move.
- The acceptability/grammaticality circularity is handled much better in the **“At a single time-slice… Across time…”** paragraph (reflexive equilibrium, not vicious circle). This is a publishable framing move.
- Boundary language is improved by **“Grammaticality as a species of appropriateness”** and the appropriateness-channels table; this is closer to the book’s field-relative architecture.

**Remaining Concerns**
- The chapter still oscillates between construction-level claims and chapter-global claims. You need more local mechanism instantiation at the level of specific constructions, not just examples.
- “Bayesian detector” remains under-specified. Skeptical readers will ask: what is the likelihood model, what is the prior, and what parameter changes under exposure?
- Triadic staging is improved but not yet fully dissociated. The three registers are still described as likely co-activating; you need stronger commitments about when they decouple.
- Several claims remain over-precise for current evidence, e.g. progressive **“required”** language, LBE described as categorically stable across tasks/populations, comparative illusion treated as having no coherent interpretation.
- The chapter occasionally reintroduces ownership rhetoric (“syntax gets first pass”) that can sound disciplinary rather than field-relative and purpose-indexed.
- In **“The HPC audit,”** projectibility/homeostasis still relies heavily on the same intuition stream the chapter is reinterpreting; add independent construction-level diagnostics.

**Priority Fixes (if any)**
1. Add one compact worked case (age construction or determiner stacking) where each mechanism variable is explicitly mapped to measurable outcomes and competing predictions.
2. Recalibrate strong modal verbs (“required,” “categorically,” “exactly analogous”) to scoped claims with population/register conditions.
3. Strengthen decoupling criteria for the three registers: state at least one concrete expected dissociation pattern per register pair.
4. Replace residual ownership phrasing with explicit purpose-indexed language (“for morphosyntactic diagnostics in this analytical task…”).

**Decisive Tests (what now most matters)**
1. Construction-level factorial experiment: manipulate structural mismatch and processing load independently; test whether detector outputs show the predicted interaction rather than collapsing to surprisal.
2. Register/cohort longitudinal test on one construction under change: do thresholds and repair norms shift in the predicted order (high-exposure speakers first, formal norms later)?
3. Triadic dissociation study: separate early jolt (online), reactive repair (interactional), and normative correction (metalinguistic choice); test whether they form partially independent factors.
4. Model comparison test: for the same construction set, pit morphosyntactic coupling predictions against relevance-only predictions; publishability rises sharply if you show non-overlapping empirical wins.

Overall: this is now a serious chapter draft. To convince skeptical linguists/philosophers, the next step is not more framing; it is sharper construction-grain commitments and harder dissociation evidence.
