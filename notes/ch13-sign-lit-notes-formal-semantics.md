## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/schlenker2017-sign-language-foundations-anaphora.pdf

### Facts
- Sign discourse can maintain many simultaneous loci; the paper gives an accepted ASL example with five third-person loci plus speaker/addressee (7-way tracking total) (p. 4).
- Temporal and modal anaphora are shown with the same pointing system used for nominal anaphora (including dual forms), with donkey-like dependencies (pp. 5–7).
- In “bishop”-type configurations, ASL requires different locus indexing to force different antecedents; same-locus versions are degraded/unacceptable for the intended asymmetric reading (pp. 10–11).
- Kuhn-style data are reported where same-locus possessives under `only` permit four readings (bound-bound, bound-free, free-bound, free-free), challenging strict loci=variables (p. 11).
- Complement-set anaphora with default plural loci is degraded (ratings around 3.6 / 2.8), but becomes fully acceptable with embedded loci (ratings 7) (pp. 13–14).
- Agreement-verb targeting is gradient and semantically structured: TELEPATHY prefers high targets, EXCHANGE prefers intermediate targets; body orientation can reverse preferences (pp. 15–16).
- High/low locus effects interact with posture (standing/sitting/lying), not only absolute height, suggesting structured/iconic locus representations (pp. 16–17).
- Height features can be ignored under ellipsis in some conditions (parallel to feature-ignoring behavior), unlike overt non-elliptical mismatches (pp. 18–19).

### Quotations
- “there is no clear upper bound on the number of loci that can simultaneously be used” (p. 4).
- “A sign language locus appears to play very much the role of a formal index” (p. 10).
- “loci may simultaneously play… the role of logical variables and of iconic depictions” (p. 19).

### Key arguments for HPC framing
- Strong evidence for a **cluster** account: variable-like tracking, feature-like behavior, and iconic/geometric constraints co-occur rather than reducing to one property (pp. 3, 11–13, 19).
- Mechanistic split is explicit: assignment/binding behavior plus iconic mapping constraints on loci and denotations (pp. 13–17).
- Failure/repair behavior is visible: ambiguity pressure, degraded same-locus parses, and disambiguation via finer spatial structure (pp. 10–12, 14).
- Supports modality-neutral semantics plus modality-specific implementation: same anaphoric logic, richer overt realization in sign (pp. 5–7, 19).

### Cross-references to track
- Kuhn (variables vs features) is central to the paper’s internal debate (pp. 10–12).
- Schlenker et al. 2013 for plural loci, agreement-targeting, and high/low locus experiments (pp. 13–17).
- Nouwen 2003 on complement-set constraints (pp. 13–14).
- Liddell 2003 on agreement-verb targeting zones (p. 15).
- Schlenker 2014a/2014b for richer iconic geometry and featural-variable reconciliation (pp. 17–19).

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/schlenker2018-sign-language-semantics.pdf

### Facts
- The paper explicitly restates “Logical Visibility” and “Iconicity” as two distinct claims for sign semantics (p. 3).
- It argues ASL can use one pronoun system across individual/temporal/modal reference, supporting a domain-neutral anaphoric module (p. 3).
- A pure demonstrative account is rejected because loci have bound uses under quantifiers (p. 4).
- Complement-set effects from embedded plural loci are treated as diagrammatic/iconic inference, not just ordinary grammar (pp. 5, 23–24).
- Role Shift typology: some languages allow mixed perspectives (e.g., LSC/DGS/NGT), others enforce Shift Together (ASL/LSF) (pp. 26–30).
- Quoted preliminary ASL data suggest cross-role-shift locus reuse is somewhat degraded versus non-role-shift controls (p. 31).

### Quotations
- “First, loci have bound uses under quantifiers” (p. 4).
- “some languages allow perspectives to be mixed, while others require that all indexicals ‘shift together’” (p. 26).
- “assignment of loci to discourse referents undergoes a Reset under role shift” (quoted hypothesis) (pp. 30–31).

### Key arguments for HPC framing
- Separability is rejected in strong cases: iconic and logical contributions can be tightly entangled in one derivation (pp. 5–6).
- Cross-linguistic Role Shift variation is treated as structured variation in constraints, not as random exceptions (pp. 26–30).
- Mechanism-level proposals include context-shift operators, maximal iconicity, and possible Reset-like constraints (pp. 26, 30–32).
- Supports two-cluster architecture: discourse-tracking logic + modality-linked geometric/iconic realization (pp. 3–6, 23–24).

### Cross-references to track
- Kuhn 2016 on loci-as-features challenge (pp. 3, 36 references).
- Schlenker et al. 2013 for complement loci and iconic anaphora (pp. 5, 23–24).
- Davidson 2015/2018, Maier 2016/2018, Quer 2005/2013/2018 for Role Shift analyses and typology (pp. 26–30).
- Deal 2017 on Shift Together as a broader context-shift pattern (pp. 26–30).
- Steinbach & Onea 2016 listed as relevant DRT-based spatial-reference work (p. 38 references).

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/kuhn2016-asl-loci-variables-features.pdf

### Facts
- The paper presents multiple counterexamples to strict loci=variables by showing same-locus pronouns can receive different interpretations in one structure (pp. 2, 10–12).
- Cross-linguistic locus reuse is documented (Japanese Sign Language possession, ASL locative-shift/coordination parallels) (pp. 11–12).
- Critical ASL example with same-locus pairs is acceptable (reported around 6/7), while a more ambiguity-prone parallel drops (around 4/7) (p. 12).
- Under `ONLY-ONE`, two same-locus possessives allow all four interpretations; judgments replicated with context and inference tasks, including ASL-only retest (pp. 14–15).
- Fine-grained ratings for those four readings are all high (7/7 or 6/7) (p. 15).
- Directional verb patterns behave like agreement mismatches vs matches (p. 16).
- Loci also show underspecification behavior parallel to spoken-language feature systems (pp. 17–18).
- The paper explicitly flags the typological consequence: effectively open-ended spatial feature inventories (p. 19).

### Quotations
- “the variable-based analysis under-generates” (pp. 1–2).
- “the strong loci-as-variables hypothesis has been falsified” (p. 18).
- “loci pattern with morphosyntactic features” (pp. 16–18).

### Key arguments for HPC framing
- Strong case for a **feature-mediated** cluster: locus behavior aligns with feature inheritance, agreement, and underspecification, not one defining variable property (pp. 15–18).
- Failure mode is explicit: accidental variable-capture predictions fail on observed readings (pp. 10–15).
- Mechanistic interpretation: ambiguity management and grammatical feature-checking jointly shape outcomes (pp. 12, 16–18).
- Keeps room for hybrid views (featural variables), but rejects pure one-to-one loci=variables (p. 18).

### Cross-references to track
- Jacobson 1999/2012 for variable-free architecture and uninterpreted features parallels (pp. 2, 16).
- Schlenker 2011/2014 for contrasting loci analyses and featural-variable reconciliation (pp. 7, 18).
- Lillo-Martin & Klima 1990 baseline loci-as-indices view (p. 1).
- Aronoff et al. 2005 on agreement/index-copy perspectives (pp. 17, 19).
- Morgan 2008, Perniss & Zeshan 2008 on locus reuse outside ASL (p. 10–11).

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/kuhn2022-disjunctive-discourse-referents-lsf.pdf

### Facts
- LSF disjunction data show dynamic-reference behavior where E-type uniqueness is unavailable, but interpretation remains well-defined (pp. 5–6).
- Split-antecedent disjunction can force one locus to index multiple discourse values (x, y, and x+y), incompatible with one-locus/one-variable assumptions (p. 6).
- `IX-alternating` yields disjunctive “whoever wins” readings, contrasted with `THE-TWO` dual forms that require two individuals (p. 8).
- Midpoint pointing can retrieve disjunctive referents; this requires a mechanism introducing midpoint loci in discourse representation (pp. 8–9).
- Verb agreement with disjunctive objects: plain verbs and midpoint agreement are acceptable; single-conjunct agreement patterns track structural constraints (pp. 11–12).
- Closest Conjunct Agreement pattern in LSF: choose-a accepted disjunctively; choose-b rejected (pp. 12, 17 table).
- Numerical ratings: key grammatical forms cluster high (~6.7–7), while critical ungrammatical contrasts can drop to 1 (p. 17).
- Final proposal: featural mediation + iconic mereology of sums resolves split-antecedent, midpoint, and agreement puzzles (pp. 14–16).

### Quotations
- “loci must be mediated by a featural layer” (p. 1).
- “a single locus indexes multiple variables” (p. 6).
- “pointing ‘between’ two loci is in fact pointing at the sum of the referents” (p. 15).

### Key arguments for HPC framing
- Supports a multi-property cluster: dynamic discourse tracking, feature mediation, and spatial-iconic composition all contribute (pp. 13–16).
- Failure points are diagnostic: pure loci-as-variables cannot model split antecedents, midpoint retrieval, and agreement patterns together (pp. 12–13).
- Mechanism proposal is explicit and compositional: sums of discourse referents mapped to spatial sums (pp. 14–15).
- Cross-modal alignment claim: parallels with spoken-language underspecification and agreement are systematic, not accidental (p. 15).

### Cross-references to track
- Schlenker 2011 as baseline dynamic+loci proposal under revision (pp. 5–6, 12, 16).
- Kuhn 2016 for feature-based loci (pp. 13, 16).
- Steinbach & Onea 2016 and Schlenker 2016 for implementation options (p. 13).
- Schlenker, Lamberton & Santoro 2013 for iconic complement-set mereology (p. 14).
- Bernardi & Szabolcsi 2008, Nevins & Weisser 2018 for feature subsumption/CCA parallels (pp. 12, 15).

### Facts
- The paper frames sign-language evidence as decisive for core semantic debates including donkey anaphora and indexical shift (p. 1).
- Overt locus marking in ASL/LSF is used to test variable-binding vs E-type analyses of donkey anaphora (pp. 2–3).
- In symmetric “someone meets someone” configurations, required different-locus pronouns support variable-style indexing constraints (p. 3).
- Role-shift/indexical-shift data in sign languages include non-quotational and apparently quotational uses with shared non-manual marking (pp. 4–5).
- Indexical shift in sign can occur in matrix-like contexts (LIS example), challenging analyses that tie shift only to complement-taking attitude operators (pp. 6–7).

### Quotations
- “the relation between pronominals and their antecedents is marked overtly” (p. 2).
- “crucial evidence favoring a variable binding approach over an e-type approach” (p. 3).
- “if indeed in sign languages indexical reference shift is not restricted to complement clauses…” (p. 7).

### Key arguments for HPC framing
- Sign data are used as adjudication points between competing anaphora mechanisms, not just descriptive add-ons (pp. 2–3).
- Suggests one broader system may underlie role-shift phenomena spanning quotation-like and non-quotation-like cases (pp. 5–7).
- Highlights modality effects that expose hidden structure while preserving many cross-modal semantic generalizations (p. 1).

### Cross-references to track
- Schlenker 2009/2010 on sign-language donkey anaphora (p. 2).
- Lillo-Martin & Klima 1991 and Heim 1990 on locus-linking and E-type formal-link issues (pp. 2–3).

## /Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/literature/steinbach-onea2016-drt-anaphora-sign-language.pdf

### Facts
- The paper proposes a DRT extension where discourse referent structure mirrors geometric opposition in signing space (pp. 1–2).
- It explicitly notes practical salience limits (roughly 2–3 recursive splits in real discourse), despite theoretical unboundedness (p. 2).
- Core empirical pattern: first/second referents are typically distributed ipsilateral vs contralateral; further discourse subdivides regions recursively (pp. 7–9).
- Anaphora resolution depends on region precision and prominence: high-prominence referents permit lax/neutral pointing or omission (topic drop) (p. 10).
- Empty-locus agreement/pronouns yield presupposition failure (oddness) when no discourse referent is linked there (p. 6).
- Overtly localizing an otherwise non-agreeing object increases its discourse prominence and forward accessibility (p. 11).
- Neutral INDEX can remain semantically ambiguous between candidates; prominence/pragmatics then bias resolution (p. 29).
- In 3-referent cases, explicit sub-locus anchoring (e.g., RR) predicts stronger future retrievability and can induce covert subdivision effects (pp. 30–33).
- Donkey-sentence derivation requires default grammatical feature assignment to derive intended anaphora (pp. 33–35).

### Quotations
- “R-loci… have no semantic interpretation of their own” (p. 1, Abstract).
- “The more prominent a discourse referent is, the less explicit the pointing to its R-locus can be” (p. 10).
- “Anaphoric disambiguation now boils down to a very simple rule” (p. 27).

### Key arguments for HPC framing
- Clear two-cluster architecture: formal geometric indexing + discourse-semantic resolution, with a pragmatic layer over both (pp. 11, 14).
- Mechanism-level account is explicit: recursive binary feature geometry (R/L) drives disambiguation and avoids listability issues (pp. 13–16).
- Failure/competition handling is central: unresolved presuppositions, neutral-pointing ambiguity, and prominence-driven repair are modeled (pp. 6, 29–31).
- Concludes modality-independent discourse engine with modality-specific feature realization (p. 36).

### Cross-references to track
- Barberà 2012 and Schlenker 2013 on space semantics/anaphora in sign (pp. 2, 7, 11–12).
- Kamp & Reyle 1993; van der Sandt 1992; Kamp et al. 2011 for DRT/presupposition machinery (pp. 2, 26).
- Ariel 2001; Grosz et al. 1995; Walker et al. 1998 for prominence/accessibility links (p. 11).
- Geraci 2013, Sutton-Spence & Woll 1999, Engberg-Pedersen 1993 for cross-linguistic spatial anchoring patterns (pp. 8–9).
- Kata Kolok (Marsaja 2008; Pfau 2011) as a contrasting village-sign reference system (absolute frame, not the same H-space strategy) (p. 8, fn.).
