**Strengths**

The revision is materially stronger on mechanism. In **“The stabilizer: a feeling”**, the five-part **“Mechanism sketch (detector and maintenance loop)”** finally gives a manipulable architecture (coupling state, processing channel, detector output, threshold, entrenchment update) and, crucially, separates timescales. That directly supports experimental design rather than metaphor-only discussion.

Triadic phenomenology is now close to operationalized. In **“Three registers”** plus Table `\ref{tab:14:three-register-operational}`, you provide observable markers, confounds, and falsifiers for each register. That is exactly the right move for skeptical readers: the claim can now fail in principled ways.

The acceptability/grammaticality circularity is handled much better in **“Entrenchment in action”** with the explicit timescale split and the line that this is **“a reflexive equilibrium dynamic, not a vicious circle.”** Philosophically, that is a substantial upgrade.

Boundary language is better calibrated than in pass 1. **“Grammaticality as a species of appropriateness”** and Table `\ref{tab:appropriateness-channels}` place grammar in a plural detector ecology rather than as sovereign judge. That aligns with field-relative framing.

**Remaining Concerns**

Mechanism is explicit but still under-specified for inference. “Detector output” is said to combine coupling and processing “plus noise,” but no functional constraints are given, so almost any pattern can be post-hoc accommodated. You need at least weak commitments (directionality, interaction form, expected nonlinearities).

Triadic staging remains partly programmatic. The three registers are conceptually neat, but current observables overlap heavily (hesitation/repair can reflect processing load, social monitoring, or task demand). Without sharper task logic, critics will read this as relabeling known effects.

Over-precision remains in pockets. Claims like left-branch extraction **“remains categorically rejected regardless of exposure”** are stronger than your own HPC contingency stance elsewhere. Similar issue with universal statements (“Every analysis of every construction…”) that invite easy counterexamples.

Heterogeneity is improved but still somewhat quarantined. **“Individual differences”** is good, but variation/education effects are still treated late, after the core mechanism is built around an implicit standardized speaker. For a usage-based account, this should be constitutive from the outset, not a validating add-on. Also, schooling is treated as a “confound” in Table `\ref{tab:14:three-register-operational}` when it is plausibly part of the maintenance mechanism itself.

**Priority Fixes (if any)**

1. Add minimal formal commitments for detector dynamics (even schematic equations/qualitative predictions).
2. Reframe education/register effects as mechanism-internal inputs, not external caveats/confounds.
3. Soften categorical language where evidence is mixed; scope claims by variety, cohort, and task.
4. Reduce metaphor stacking (“zipper,” “immune system,” “spinning top,” “standing wave”) in argument-critical paragraphs.

**Decisive Tests (what now most matters)**

1. Joint-model test: predict ratings from independently measured exposure, processing cost, and repair history; show incremental variance for coupling state beyond processing.
2. Register/cohort dissociation: same items across education strata and discourse communities, testing threshold shifts and repair convergence.
3. Three-register dissociation battery: early online disruption, immediate repair behavior, and delayed norm judgments on identical stimuli; test partial independence.
4. Change-in-progress validation: track a genuinely emerging construction longitudinally; show cohort-structured shift in mismatch judgments before formal standard uptake.
