**Strengths**

The revision is materially stronger and much more publishable. In `\section{The stabilizer: a feeling}`, the five-part “Mechanism sketch (detector and maintenance loop)” finally gives readers moving parts they can test, and the explicit timescale split (token-time, entrenchment, community dynamics) is excellent. `\section{The phenomenology of coupling: What it’s like}` is also a real advance: the “three registers” plus Table `\ref{tab:14:three-register-operational}` (markers, confounds, falsifiers) is the first place the triadic story looks experimentally tractable rather than merely interpretive.

On circularity, the paragraph in `\subsection{Entrenchment in action}` (“That’s a reflexive equilibrium dynamic, not a vicious circle.”) is exactly the right move. It distinguishes synchronic misfire from diachronic re-entrenchment and defuses the old charge of conceptual bootstrapping.

Boundary language is improved in `\subsection{Grammaticality as a species of appropriateness}`: positioning grammaticality as one channel among other appropriateness channels aligns with the book’s field-relative stance and reduces imperial syntax rhetoric.

**Remaining Concerns**

Mechanism specification is better but still not identification-ready. “Coupling state,” “decision threshold,” and “noise” are named, not yet operationally separable in data. A skeptical reader can still say the model redescribes outcomes post hoc.

Triadic phenomenology is staged, but the Peircean mapping (sign/object/interpretant) still risks reading as elegant overlay rather than necessary architecture. You partly acknowledge this (“mnemonic rather than mechanistic”), but uptake will require one concrete dissociation design, not only table-level placeholders.

Some claims remain over-precise or overconfident for current evidence:
- “Left-branch extraction … remains categorically rejected … across tasks, speakers, and presentation modes.”
- “The structure isn’t unusually complex.”
- “On current evidence, grammaticality appears to pass both [diagnostics]” then quickly “meets the two diagnostics strongly enough.”
These need tighter calibration language.

A few lines sound sectarian or rhetorically risky for philosophers/variationists:
- “That’s why syntax gets first pass on it.”
- “A constraint-based account says LBE can’t emerge in English.”
Both overgeneralize opponents and invite unnecessary disciplinary resistance.

**Priority Fixes (if any)**

1. Add one explicit experimental blueprint crossing structural mismatch, processing load, and exposure history, with predicted outcomes for each register.  
2. Replace categorical universals with calibrated quantifiers (“in many studies,” “in current English corpora/judgment tasks”).  
3. Recast ownership language: from “syntax gets first pass” to “morphosyntactic evidence is often most diagnostic for this channel.”  
4. Soften strawman contrasts with constraint-based work; distinguish strong impossibility claims from gradient constraint accounts.  
5. Keep “Homer Simpson” if you must, but trim or footnote: currently it risks sounding glib at the point you need maximum credibility.

**Decisive Tests (what now most matters)**

1. Factorial dissociation study: structural mismatch × processing difficulty, measuring early jolt, repair behavior, and delayed norm correction separately.  
2. Longitudinal entrainment/satiation design: same speakers over time to test threshold drift vs genuine coupling change.  
3. Cross-community calibration test (dialect/register): do predicted cohort-structured repair norms emerge for the same construction?  
4. Model comparison with relevance-based accounts (`\section{Relevance and the immune system}`): pre-register divergent predictions on repair choice, not only acceptability ratings.
