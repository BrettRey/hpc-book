**Strengths**
- The revision substantially improves mechanism explicitness. In §“The stabilizer: a feeling,” the five-part “Mechanism sketch (detector and maintenance loop)” plus timescale separation (token-time, entrenchment, community dynamics) gives a real basis for test design rather than slogan-level theorizing.
- Triadic phenomenology is now closer to operational science. In §“Three registers,” you move from Peircean mapping to measurable claims, and Table `\ref{tab:14:three-register-operational}` is especially useful because it includes confounds and falsifiers, not just markers.
- The acceptability/grammaticality circularity is handled much better than in many frameworks. The distinction between synchronic dissociation and diachronic coupling (“reflexive equilibrium dynamic, not a vicious circle”) is a major conceptual gain.
- Boundary language is improved by §“Grammaticality as a species of appropriateness,” especially the two operational tests for classifying failures by channel. This aligns with a field-relative framing better than disciplinary gatekeeping language.

**Remaining Concerns**
- Mechanism is still under-parameterized. “Detector output,” “threshold,” and “entrenchment update” are named, but causal direction and expected effect sizes are not specified. As written, many outcomes remain retrospectively explainable.
- Developmental plausibility remains the weakest link. From a usage-based acquisition view, you need explicit predictions about how children move from item-based routines to abstract coupling detection. Right now, adults are theorized; acquisition is largely asserted.
- The triadic registers are operationalized, but not developmentally staged. Do “jolt,” “resistance,” and “normative correction” emerge in that order? Are they dissociable by age, literacy, or interactional niche? This is where skeptical readers will press.
- Some claims still overreach relative to evidence: “exactly analogous to visual illusions,” “passes both strongly enough,” and near-categorical treatment of LBE resistance. These are plausible, but phrasing sometimes outruns demonstrated scope.
- The chapter sometimes blurs its own boundary by extending “form–value coupling” across phoneme, morpheme, lexicon, construction, and clause type. That is theoretically interesting, but it risks diluting the central claim about grammaticality at the morphosyntactic grain.

**Priority Fixes (if any)**
1. Add a compact formal appendix paragraph (even pseudo-equations) defining how coupling strength, processing load, and threshold combine into detector output, and how exposure updates each.
2. Add a short acquisition subsection with age-graded predictions: early item-based repair behavior, later abstraction, and still later sociolinguistic norm calibration.
3. Recalibrate strongest assertions with explicit scope conditions (“on current evidence,” “in these constructions,” “for these populations”).
4. Clarify whether cross-grain tables are analogical scaffolding or part of the core ontological claim.

**Decisive Tests (what now most matters)**
1. Longitudinal learning study (children and adults) on a controlled construction family to track whether detector shifts precede, follow, or co-evolve with coupling changes.
2. Register/community-switch experiment testing the claim that the detector is “calibrated to whoever you’re talking to, right now,” with within-session threshold shifts.
3. Developmental dissociation test for the three registers using RT/eye-tracking + repair behavior + normative correction choices across age cohorts.
4. Model comparison: one-factor acceptability model vs two-latent model (coupling state + detector noise). If the two-latent model does not outperform, the chapter’s central architectural distinction weakens substantially.
