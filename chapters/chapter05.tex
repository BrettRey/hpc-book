\section{The gradience problem}
\ixsq{gradience}
\label{sec:5:gradience-problem}

Linguistic categories look discrete: a word is a noun or it isn't, a sentence is grammatical or it's not, a sound is /\ipa{ɪ}/ or /\ipa{ɛ}/ in the \mention{pin}\ixlq{pin}/\mention{pen}\ixlq{pen} contrast for the dialects that maintain it. But the properties underlying those categories are continuous: frequency distributions shade smoothly from one pattern to another, acoustic cues vary continuously (vowel formants don't come in bins), semantic features don't come with sharp edges. How do discrete categories emerge from continuous substrates?

Both definition-based essentialism\ixsq{essentialism} and \enquote{unreal} exemplarism fail to solve this. As argued in Chapter~\ref{ch:kinds-without-essences}, we need a view where discrete structure emerges from continuous substrates and is stabilized by reliable mechanisms. Call that \term{dynamic discreteness\ixsq{dynamic discreteness}}~-- discreteness that's achieved, not given.

Scale is the key. A change that's negligible when you're far from a boundary becomes appreciable when you're close. That's why tolerance intuitions coexist with boundary intuitions~-- they track different regimes. And the mechanisms that maintain categories~-- acquisition, entrenchment, alignment\ixsq{alignment!convergence effects}, transmission~-- are what keep the boundary relatively stable.

What follows is predicate-by-predicate. This framework doesn't force exclusivity~-- an item can be in two basins, as with dual membership in the noun-adjective\ixsq{adjective} zone. It allows overlap in cases like \mention{fun}\ixlq{fun} or \mention{near}\ixlq{near} rather than forcing them into one category or the other. The question is what maintains that overlap.

\subsection{The phase-transition intuition}
\label{subsec:5:phase-intuition}

Chapter~\ref{ch:kinds-without-essences} introduced a physical analogy: phase transitions. Water has no essence of liquidity; the same molecules constitute ice, liquid, and steam depending on temperature and pressure. The boundaries are real and sharp enough for skating or swimming, but they're maintained by dynamics, not definitions.

Phase transitions produce categorical outcomes (solid, liquid, gas) from continuous substrates (temperature, pressure). That suggests a solution: if physical systems can exhibit discrete, stable structure without essences, perhaps linguistic categories can too. The next task is to say precisely what it is about the dynamics that produces discreteness.

The key insight from physics is that phase boundaries are \emph{scale-sensitive}. Near a phase boundary, small changes in temperature or pressure can flip the system from one phase to another. Far from the boundary, the same small changes leave the system solidly in one phase. Its sharpness is a feature of the system at a particular scale of observation. Zoom in far enough and you find molecules fluctuating; zoom out and you find stable ice or stable water. The discreteness emerges at the macroscopic level from continuous microscopic variation. It is measurement-indexed: phases are crisp relative to thermometers, not relative to individual molecules.

The same logic applies to vague predicates generally. Consider a heap of sand. Ten thousand grains is clearly a heap. One grain is clearly not. The boundary seems sharp~-- at some point, removing one more grain tips the collection from heap to non-heap~-- but we can't pinpoint it. This is the Sorites paradox. Standard responses include epistemicism (the boundary is sharp but unknowable), degree theory (heaphood comes in degrees), and supervaluationism (multiple precisifications are equally acceptable). What the phase-transition intuition adds is a picture where the boundary is sharp at the right scale, located in a determinate region, and maintained by dynamics rather than essences.

\subsection{Relative tolerance}
\ixsq{relative tolerance}
\label{subsec:5:relative-tolerance}

To make this precise, consider what \enquote{one grain doesn't matter} really means. It can't mean that removing one grain \emph{never} makes a difference~-- that would entail that even a single grain is a heap. What it means is that removing one grain doesn't matter \emph{when you have many grains}. Ten thousand minus one is still clearly a heap. Fifty minus one might not be.

The difference is relative, not absolute. Removing one grain from ten thousand is a 0.01\% change. Removing one grain from fifty is a 2\% change. The same absolute change has different significance depending on the scale.

That yields a principle:

\begin{quote}
Relative Tolerance\ixsq{relative tolerance}: Changes that are negligibly small \emph{relative to the current scale} preserve category\ixsq{category!membership graded} membership. Changes that are appreciable relative to the current scale may not.
\end{quote}

\enquote{Negligible} and \enquote{appreciable} are themselves vague. To make the principle precise, we need a framework that can handle the distinction rigorously. This is where nonstandard analysis helps.

The next section isn't a philosophical aside. The formalism is designed to derive a concrete empirical prediction: that distance-to-boundary should predict judgment variance, with a characteristic functional form. If that model is wrong, that variance signature shouldn't appear. If it is right, the variance pattern follows from the mathematics.

\section{A formal solution}
\label{sec:5:formal-solution}

\begin{framed}
\noindent Skip path. The next subsection develops a formal model using hyperreal numbers. Readers who prefer to skip the mathematics can take away three points and rejoin at §\ref{sec:5:geometry-to-mechanism}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Tolerance is scale-sensitive: small changes are tolerated, but they accumulate.
    \item Sharp boundaries exist at thresholds we can't precisely locate.
    \item Discrete categories and gradient intuitions are compatible~-- the discreteness is in the structure, the gradience\ixsq{gradience} is in our access to it.
\end{enumerate}
\end{framed}

Before turning to the equations, remember what the formal criteria below are for. They are in-principle constraints on what a successful measurement programme should recover, not push-button procedures. In practice the bottleneck is operationalization: choosing diagnostics, declaring scope, estimating boundaries, and separating mechanism signal from sampling artefact. The formalism disciplines research design and clarifies defeat conditions; it doesn't replace empirical labour.

\subsection{The hyperreal formalization}
\label{subsec:5:hyperreal}

The hyperreal numbers extend the reals with \term{infinitesimals}~-- quantities greater than $0$ but smaller than any positive real number \ixnq{Goldblatt, Robert}\citep{Goldblatt1998LecturesHyperreals}. Robinson's construction is rigorous, but we don't need its details. Here infinitesimals are a modelling device for making \emph{negligible relative to the current scale} precise without stipulating an arbitrary finite cutoff. I am not claiming speakers compute with hypernaturals; the hyperreals play the role calculus plays in physics. The application to vagueness is developed fully in \textcite{reynolds2025sorites}.

Here is how the framework applies to vague predicates. Let $P$ be a predicate like \mention{is a heap}, and let $\mu(x)$ be a measure of the relevant property~-- say, grain count. For successive cases $c_i$ and $c_{i+1}$ in a series, let $\Delta\mu_i = \mu(c_{i+1}) - \mu(c_i)$ be the change from one case to the next.

The Relative Tolerance\ixsq{relative tolerance} principle becomes:

\begin{quote}
If $|\Delta\mu_i|/\mu(c_i)$ is infinitesimal, then $P(c_i) \leftrightarrow P(c_{i+1})$.
\end{quote}

In plain language: if the fractional change is infinitesimally small, the predicate's truth value is preserved. This captures the intuition that proportionally tiny changes don't matter, while allowing that accumulated changes can.

Now model the entire Sorites series~-- from clear heap to clear non-heap~-- as a \emph{hyperfinite chain} of $H$ steps, where $H$ is an infinite hypernatural number (Figure~\ref{fig:hyperfinite-chain}). This is a standard construction in nonstandard analysis: a sequence indexed by hypernaturals, behaving internally like a finite sequence but containing infinitely many elements from the external perspective. Very roughly, a hypernatural is close to what a child has in mind when treating infinity as a number you could keep counting toward, except that nonstandard analysis makes that idea rigorous without introducing a greatest number.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/5.hyperfinite-chain.png}
\caption{The hyperfinite Sorites chain. The predicate $P$ (e.g., \emph{is a heap}) holds at every standard natural index: tolerance preserves truth across any finite portion of the series. The cutoff $K$ lies at a hypernatural index beyond all standard naturals~-- determinate within the model but not finitely specifiable.}
\label{fig:hyperfinite-chain}
\end{figure}

Within this hyperfinite chain, we stipulate clear endpoints: the series begins with $P$-true cases and ends with $P$-false ones. Given that setup, monotonicity yields an initial segment of $P$-true points followed by $P$-false points, and transfer guarantees a least index $K$ at which $P$ flips. This $K$ is a hypernatural~-- not a standard natural number, but a number in the extended system. And because we've stipulated that $P$ holds at all standard indices (encoding the intuition that tolerance holds throughout any finite portion of the series), $K$ cannot be standard: it is infinitely large compared to any standard natural.

The model yields three relevant zones:

\begin{itemize}
\item Far from $K$: In the clear part of the series, each step is infinitesimal relative to the current measure $\mu(c_i)$. Relative Tolerance\ixsq{relative tolerance} applies. The predicate is preserved.
\item Near $K$: As the series approaches the cutoff, the same absolute step is no longer infinitesimal relative to the current scale. Relative Tolerance\ixsq{relative tolerance} is silent. The predicate can flip.
\item At $K$: The boundary. Sharp, determinate, located at a specific hypernatural index~-- but epistemically inaccessible because we can't finitely specify which hypernatural. The specific value of $K$ is not a psychologically real parameter; it is the model's way of representing the fact that no finite characterization can pick out the boundary.
\end{itemize}

The Sorites induction fails because the inductive premise~-- \enquote{if $P(c_i)$ then $P(c_{i+1})$}~-- holds only where Relative Tolerance\ixsq{relative tolerance} applies, which is far from the boundary. Near the boundary, the premise is false. The chain breaks because tolerance doesn't apply uniformly across the entire series.

\subsection{Sharp boundaries, fuzzy appearances}
\label{subsec:5:sharp-fuzzy}

Within the hyperreal model, classical bivalent logic is maintained throughout. At every index~-- including hypernatural indices~-- the predicate is either true or false. There are no degrees of heaphood, no fuzzy membership values. The boundary is sharp.

But the boundary is also inaccessible. In the model, it lies at a hypernatural index that we can't specify using finite means. From our finite observational standpoint, we see clear heaps, clear non-heaps, and a region of uncertainty in between. The uncertainty is epistemic, not a matter of semantic indeterminacy: there's a fact of the matter about where the boundary falls; we just can't determine what it is.

The hyperreal view differs from epistemicism~-- Timothy Williamson's view that vague predicates have sharp boundaries, unknowable because of the limits of our discriminatory capacities \ixnq{Williamson, Timothy}\citep{williamson1994}. Both views yield sharp-but-inaccessible cutoffs.

But the sources differ. For the epistemicist, the boundary is a brute metaphysical fact. For the hyperreal view, the boundary's existence is a consequence of scale-sensitive tolerance plus monotonicity: the transfer principle entails a cutoff at some hypernatural index. The sharpness is a consequence of the formal setup, and the inaccessibility is built into what kind of thing a hypernatural index is.

The explanatory consequences diverge more sharply. Epistemicism holds that boundaries are static: fixed by semantics plus worldly facts, unchanging unless the predicate's meaning changes. The maintenance view\ixsq{maintenance view}, where categories are mechanism-maintained profiles projectible relative to purposes, ties the effective boundary to mechanisms of acquisition, entrenchment, alignment\ixsq{alignment!convergence effects}, and transmission, and so predicts structured boundary \emph{drift} under perturbations~-- something epistemicism can accommodate, but doesn't on its own explain or forecast. On this view, the hyperreal apparatus formally represents those dynamics and the variance signatures they predict.

This explains why tolerance intuitions and boundary intuitions can coexist. When you have a clear heap, you're right that one grain doesn't matter~-- the fractional change is negligible at that scale. When you're near the boundary, you're right that the situation is unclear~-- you're in the region where tolerance breaks down. Each intuition tracks a different regime.

The model also explains why boundaries are stable. The boundary follows from the structure of the hyperreal model, specifically from the tolerance principle together with the monotonicity of the series. Different ways of constructing the nonstandard model yield different specific values of the cutoff index $K$, but the structural features~-- sharp boundary, epistemic inaccessibility, scale-dependent tolerance~-- are invariant.

A different response to the discreteness problem is available. Khalidi, extending his critique of Boyd's homeostatic requirement, argues that categories can have genuinely \emph{fuzzy} boundaries~-- not sharp-but-inaccessible, just fuzzy \ixnq{Khalidi, Muhammad Ali}\citep[63--69]{khalidi2013}. Chemical isomers shade into each other as bond angles vary continuously. Biological species intergrade where populations overlap. If these paradigmatic special-science categories tolerate fuzziness, why should grammatical categories require sharp edges?

What matters here is the structure of tolerance intuitions themselves. The Sorites reasoning pattern~-- one grain doesn't make a difference, so no number of grains makes a difference~-- is compelling precisely because we feel the tolerance premise is true. Khalidi-style fuzzy realism needn't deny that categories have extensions; the stronger claim is that there's no uniquely privileged sharp cutoff. That is a serious alternative. But discrete verdicts alone don't settle it: speakers and institutions can impose categorical decisions on continua. So the explanatory target is narrower. The question is whether the data look like \emph{scale-dependent} tolerance around a sharp threshold, or like decision-level discretization over a fuzzy latent space.

This isn't to say Khalidi's \enquote{fuzzy kinds} are never apt. For weak linguistic classes~-- the thin ones from §\ref{sec:4:heterogeneity}~-- genuine fuzziness may be the right description. For robust categories, the choice is empirical, not metaphysical, and not binary. The competition is among three live models: fuzzy latent structure, fuzzy latent structure with categorical decision procedures, and sharp-but-inaccessible thresholds. The discriminators are measurable: boundary-local variance asymmetry, perturbation profiles across register and task, and latent-structure tests (Chapter~\ref{ch:what-changes}).

A third position deserves acknowledgement. \ixnq{Boyd, Richard N.}Boyd's own characterization of HPC kinds includes the claim that extensional vagueness is \enquote{not resolvable even given all the relevant facts and all the true theories} \citep[527]{boyd1979}. On Boyd's view, no refinement of the kind term yields a more extensionally precise term that preserves the naturalness of the kind. This sounds like ontological or semantic indeterminacy~-- boundaries that aren't just inaccessible but genuinely absent. This account refines Boyd rather than rejecting him. On the hyperreal picture, Boyd's irresolvable vagueness can be understood as a sharp boundary at a hypernatural threshold that can't be finitely specified. No standard refinement resolves it because no finite characterization can pick out the threshold~-- but the threshold is determinate within the model. On that reading, Boyd's point remains: no explicit definition will pin down the extension. The difference lies in how the indeterminacy is understood, not in the practical upshot. HPC kinds still resist explicit extensional characterization, and Boyd's framework, which this book otherwise adopts wholesale, is compatible with either reading. The argument for property clustering and causal mechanisms doesn't depend on whether boundaries are ontologically vague or merely epistemically inaccessible.

\section{From geometry to mechanism}
\label{sec:5:geometry-to-mechanism}

\subsection{From heaps to categories}
\label{subsec:5:heaps-to-categories}

Grammatical categories aren't heaps. But they face the same discreteness problem. If the underlying properties~-- frequency, phonetic realization, semantic features, distributional patterns~-- vary continuously, how do discrete categories emerge?

The hyperreal framework extends naturally to this multi-dimensional case. The single dimension of grain count becomes a multi-dimensional feature space; the single predicate $P$ becomes a family of predicates $P_1, \ldots, P_n$, each associated with a region $R_i$ corresponding to a category; and the linear Sorites series becomes a space of possible items, each located at some point in feature space.

The Relative Tolerance\ixsq{relative tolerance} principle generalizes:

\begin{quote}
If $d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x} + \Delta\mathbf{x}) / d(\mathbf{x}, \partial R_i)$ is infinitesimal, $P_i(\mathbf{x}) \leftrightarrow P_i(\mathbf{x} + \Delta\mathbf{x})$.
\end{quote}

Here $\mathbf{x}$ is a point in feature space, $\Delta\mathbf{x}$ is a perturbation, $d$ is a metric on the space, and $d(\mathbf{x}, \partial R_i)$ is the distance from $\mathbf{x}$ to the boundary of category $i$'s region. In plain language: perturbations that are infinitesimally small relative to the distance to the nearest boundary preserve categorization.

These boundaries don't come from an essence; that would be circular. They are estimated empirically~-- from distributional diagnostics, behavioural experiments, classifier decision surfaces~-- and the formalism characterizes what tolerance around that surface entails. Alternatively, boundaries can be treated as theoretical objects maintained by mechanisms, their locations inferred from invariances: if a diagnostic holds across contexts and tasks, it's tracking a stable basin, not an artefact of measurement. The reciprocals and specification-curve methods introduced later (§\ref{subsec:5:multi-category}) are exactly this kind of probe.

Now we need to say how distance should be measured in a space like this. The notation $d(\cdot, \cdot)$ is just a way of writing distance between two points. That raises an immediate question: what notion of distance should we use here? Grammatical features are heterogeneous. Some are ratio-scaled (frequency, duration). Some are ordinal (degree of acceptability). Some are binary (takes plural marking: yes/no). No single off-the-shelf metric applies to all.

We don't need a uniquely privileged metric; we need a notion of \emph{local small change} that's robust across reasonable ways of scaling and weighting features. If two distance measures agree locally about which perturbations count as tiny in the region of interest, they support the same Relative Tolerance\ixsq{relative tolerance} story.

There's a principled way to construct such metrics for heterogeneous feature spaces. The strategy, familiar from statistical ecology and clustering analysis, proceeds in two steps \ixnq{Gower, J. C.}\citep[cf.][]{gower1971}.

First, embed each feature into a dimensionless numerical scale:
\begin{itemize}
\item Ratio and interval features (frequency, duration, formant values) are standardized~-- divided by range or standard deviation~-- so that a unit change in one dimension is comparable to a unit change in another.
\item Ordinal features (ranked acceptability, degree of grammaticalization) are mapped to their rank position, rescaled to $[0,1]$.
\item Binary features (presence/absence of inflection, compatibility with a construction) are left as $\{0,1\}$.
\end{itemize}

After embedding, all coordinates are dimensionless and live in a common numerical space.

Second, place a weighted norm on the embedded vector:
\[
d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \left( \sum_k w_k \left| \phi_k(\mathbf{x}) - \phi_k(\mathbf{y}) \right|^p \right)^{1/p}
\]
where $\phi_k$ is the embedding function for feature $k$, $w_k > 0$ is a weight reflecting that feature's contribution to the homeostatic cluster, and $p = 1$ or $p = 2$. The weights can be estimated empirically (from regression coefficients, factor loadings, or feature-importance measures in classification tasks) or treated as parameters to vary in thought experiments.

The embedding is a heuristic, not a discovery procedure for Platonic dimensions. We don't expect factor analysis or multidimensional scaling to reveal the true axes of grammatical space~-- there may be no unique decomposition. What we expect is that any adequate embedding will recover dimensions that are interpretable: dimensions that correlate with independently motivated grammatical or semantic scales. If a derived dimension tracks degree-modification compatibility, and degree-modification compatibility tracks the conceptual scale of gradability, then the embedding is doing its job.

This construction is a normed cousin of Gower distance, widely used for mixed-type data \ixnq{Gower, J. C.}\citep{gower1971}, and it aligns with Gärdenfors's \emph{conceptual spaces}: multiple quality dimensions grouped into domains, each with its own metric, combined via weighted aggregation across domains \ixnq{Gardenfors, Peter@Gärdenfors, Peter}\citep{gardenfors2000}. The connection to HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!basin structure} is direct: the weights $w_k$ encode which properties play a stronger homeostatic role. Properties central to the cluster get large weights; free riders get small ones.

The claim is simply that a class of admissible metrics exists, all yielding the same small-change structure. The Relative Tolerance\ixsq{relative tolerance} condition holds for all of them, not just one distinguished choice. Figure~\ref{fig:basin-visualization} illustrates the resulting landscape.

Some dimensions are conceptual in Gärdenfors's sense~-- grounded in perception and cognition, interpretable as quality scales that structure thought independently of any particular language. Eventivity, animacy, degree-scale structure: these show up in acquisition patterns, constrain semantic extension, have correlates in non-linguistic cognition.

Others are grammatical diagnostics: compatibility with plural morphology, occurrence in comparative constructions, position relative to the head. These need not correspond to conceptual dimensions at all. They are distributional dimensions of how a language packages and stabilizes distinctions, sometimes tracking conceptual structure and sometimes reflecting language-specific morphosyntactic organization.

The feature space is heterogeneous, combining conceptual geometry with morphosyntactic, phonological, and other language-specific dimensions. The HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!basin structure} claim is that homeostatic mechanisms operate across these domains, sometimes aligning them and sometimes allowing them to pull apart. Where they pull apart, the basin may shift or split. So the geometry is a joint product of cognition and language-specific grammatical organization, not a reduction to either one.

Evidentiality provides a useful contrast. English has no grammaticalized evidential marking~-- no obligatory morphology indicating whether information is firsthand, reported, or inferred. But the conceptual dimension exists: speakers make evidential distinctions using lexical means (\mention{apparently}\ixlq{apparently}, \mention{reportedly}\ixlq{reportedly}, \mention{I saw that}).

In languages with grammaticalized evidentiality~-- Tibetan\ixgq{Tibetan}, Turkish\ixgq{Turkish}, many Amazonian languages\ixgq{Amazonian languages}~-- conceptual and grammatical dimensions are aligned. In English\ixgq{English}, the conceptual dimension floats free of any grammatical basin of its own. Speakers still make evidential distinctions, but those distinctions are expressed lexically and constructionally rather than organizing a stable grammatical category. The concept persists, but no grammatical basin forms around it.

Visualizing the landscape is the next step. The three-dimensional surface we have been imagining is a picture of the full feature space, with category cores corresponding to local minima and boundaries to the ridges and saddle regions between them. Any particular \enquote{valley and ridge} picture shows only a low-dimensional slice on which the relevant contrasts are visible. Choosing the metric~-- the weights $w_k$ and the embedding functions $\phi_k$~-- is, in effect, choosing a geometry for the landscape: which directions are steep, which are shallow, which features matter most for which basin. Relative Tolerance\ixsq{relative tolerance} then says: stay within one valley when perturbations are small in that geometry.\footnote{The physics metaphor is heuristic, not literal. Potential minima correspond to prototypes (geometrical centres of categories); basins of attraction correspond to convex regions where similarity to the prototype dominates; \enquote{forces} are learning and update processes~-- acquisition, entrenchment, alignment~-- that shift representations toward or away from category centres. The metaphor earns its keep by unifying these phenomena under a single image; it doesn't commit us to actual energy functions computed by the brain.}

Basin structure emerges once we have a metric. With a metric in hand, the multi-dimensional picture comes into focus. Each category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} occupies a region in feature space~-- a basin\ixsq{basin} of attraction. Items deep in a basin are stably categorized: small perturbations don't move them out. Items near the boundary are unstable: the same perturbation that would be negligible elsewhere can change their categorical profile by moving them, shifting the boundary around them, or bringing a neighbouring basin into overlap.

Where a category is organized around a single attractor, the basin will often look approximately convex in the relevant slice of the space. If two items are stably categorized as nouns, items intermediate between them in feature space should generally be too. This is Gärdenfors's criterion for natural categories, and it works here as a useful idealization because the mechanisms maintaining the basin\ixsq{basin}~-- entrenchment, analogy\ixsq{analogy}, transmission~-- operate by similarity. Items near the prototype\ixsq{prototype} pull their neighbours toward the same categorization. Apparent non-convexity, where it arises, may indicate that the relevant metric isn't the one assumed, that multiple overlapping basins are being conflated, or that the category has a multi-peaked structure~-- each of these is informative about the mechanisms at work.

The potential-well metaphor makes the point visually: a convex basin\ixsq{basin} corresponds to a single local minimum, and gradient descent from anywhere in the basin leads to the same attractor.

The boundary cases that trouble essentialism~-- \mention{fun}\ixlq{fun}, \mention{near}\ixlq{near}, \mention{otherwise}\ixlq{otherwise}~-- are items near basin boundaries. They exhibit properties of multiple categories because they're in the region where tolerance breaks down. Their instability isn't noise; it's evidence about where the boundaries lie.

The maintained categories behave as if they have sharp decision boundaries under ordinary measurement regimes; what is graded is stability and typicality, not membership. At any point in feature space, an item is either in category $i$ or not under the mechanisms currently in play. The appearance of gradience\ixsq{gradience} arises partly from epistemic limitations~-- we observe items at various distances from boundaries and can't determine exactly where the boundaries fall~-- but also from \emph{synchronic context effects}. For a given speaker in a given context, the same word may be pulled toward a more noun-like or adjective-like construal by the syntactic frame. The basin\ixsq{basin} metaphor accommodates this: mechanisms set the default landscape, but context induces local deformations that pull items toward or away from boundaries. This is \term{real gradience\ixsq{gradience}\ixsq{real gradience}}~-- gradient structure that provides evidence about category organization rather than mere measurement noise.

\subsection{Mechanisms and basins}
\label{subsec:5:mechanisms-basins}

The hyperreal model sharpens the geometry of category boundaries. This subsection turns to the processes that stabilize that geometry in use and transmission. Those processes are one part of the broader HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!discreteness} framework, not the whole of it.

For present purposes, we can distinguish a geometrical side from a stabilizing side. On the geometrical side, grammatical categories are regions in a feature space, with prototypes at centres and boundaries where similarity to multiple prototypes is balanced. On the stabilizing side, that geometry persists because mechanisms~-- acquisition, entrenchment, alignment, transmission, functional pressure~-- keep items clustered and boundaries relatively stable. Conceptual-space approaches develop the first side in rich detail \ixnq{Gardenfors, Peter@Gärdenfors, Peter}\citep{gardenfors2000,gardenfors2014}; HPC frameworks develop the second \ixnq{Boyd, Richard N.}\citep{boyd1991,boyd1999}.

Rosch's insight that categories have graded internal structure organized around prototypes is preserved here in a narrower role. Typicality is graded; membership is not. Items inside a category vary in how typical they are, how central to the basin, and how far from competing boundaries. HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!membership} retains the gradience\ixsq{gradience} while relocating it from membership to stability. A typical noun sits deep in the noun basin, far from competition; an atypical noun sits nearer a boundary and is more vulnerable to drift, eruptions, and erosion. The mechanisms~-- entrenchment, alignment, transmission~-- maintain that structure.

The hyperreal formalization adds precision about boundaries: sharp but located at unreachable distances, exactly as tolerance intuitions suggest. Prototype\ixsq{prototype} theory describes the internal shape; the stabilizing mechanisms discussed here explain why that organization persists; hyperreals explain the sharpness.

Stability here is dynamic, maintained by active forces rather than static rigidity. The homeostatic mechanisms are those stabilizing forces. They maintain the landscape geometry without defining it.

Acquisition\ixsq{acquisition} shapes the initial configuration: learners sensitive to input distributions reproduce the basin structure. Entrenchment\ixsq{entrenchment!frequency effects} deepens the basins: high-frequency items anchor the category, steepening the walls around the prototype.

Interactive alignment\ixsq{interactive alignment} maintains consistency: speakers coordinate usage, effectively pushing deviants back toward the norm. Consider alignment\ixsq{alignment!interactive} in action. Suppose you say \mention{that was very fun}\ixlq{fun}~-- using the degree modifier that signals adjectival status. Your interlocutor might accommodate, adopting \mention{very fun}\ixlq{fun} in their next turn. Or they might resist, persisting with \mention{a lot of fun}\ixlq{fun}. Either way, the micro-choice shifts local expectations: accommodation reinforces the adjectival pattern; resistance maintains the nominal one. Scale this up, and you get a distributed mechanism for maintaining shared norms.

Iterated transmission\ixsq{iterated transmission} acts as a filter across generations. Not all variants survive; the ones that do tend to be clear exemplars well inside basins. As \ixnq{Kirby, Simon}\ixnq{Cornish, Hannah}\ixnq{Smith, Kenny}\textcite{kirby2008} demonstrated, transmission bottlenecks can select for learnable structures. In their experimental evolution of artificial languages, random mappings became compositional over ten generations because the bottleneck of imperfect learning filtered out the arbitrary variants. This pattern isn't inevitable~-- it depends on particular task pressures and on the right balance of expressivity and compressibility~-- but it illustrates how structure can emerge without intentional design.

Finally, functional pressure ensures the basins serve communicative needs. Categories exist because they package referents or encode events. Where functional pressure is strong, basins are deep and boundaries are sharp.

All these forces act together to maintain the basin\ixsq{basin} geometry. That doesn't exhaust the broader HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!discreteness} picture, but it is one indispensable part of it. The hyperreal model describes the shape: sharp thresholds with scale-dependent tolerance, and instability concentrated in a determinate seam-region that ordinary finite measurement can't pinpoint. The stabilizing story told in this subsection explains why that structure persists: mechanisms operating across multiple timescales, maintaining the clustering without defining it.

\subsection{Multi-category spaces}
\label{subsec:5:multi-category}

Linguistic categories don't exist in isolation. Nouns compete with verbs; adjectives shade into adverbs; prepositions overlap with both. Instead of a set of independent regions, the basin\ixsq{basin} structure is a tessellation of feature space, with categories abutting each other along shared boundaries. Khalidi calls such overlapping classification schemes \term{crosscutting kinds\ixsq{crosscutting kind}\ixsq{kind!crosscutting}} and argues they pose no threat to realism: the same entity can be a node in multiple causal networks simultaneously \ixnq{Khalidi, Muhammad Ali}\citep[69--72, 92--97]{khalidi2013}.
This creates additional structure. An item near the noun-verb\ixsq{verb!boundary cases} boundary might be stably a noun (because it's deep in the noun basin) or unstably categorized (because small changes could push it into the verb basin). The same item might be far from the noun\ixsq{noun!boundary cases}-adjective\ixsq{adjective} boundary, so its nominal vs.\ adjectival status is never in doubt. Category\ixsq{category!boundary cases} membership is determinate in multiple dimensions simultaneously, with different degrees of stability in different directions.

Consider the data for \mention{fun}\ixlq{fun}~-- for fun, as it were. In the frequency-of-degree-modification dimension, it has drifted toward adjectives: \mention{very fun}\ixlq{fun} is increasingly acceptable. In the takes-a-determiner dimension, it remains nominal: \mention{the fun we had}\ixlq{fun} is unremarkable. The item sits near the noun-adjective\ixsq{adjective} boundary in one dimension, deep in noun territory in another. Its apparent mixed status reflects its location in a multi-dimensional space, not indeterminacy about what categories are.

American historical corpus evidence makes the trajectory visible \ixnq{Davies, Mark}\citep{davies2010coha}. Degree-modified \mention{fun}\ixlq{fun}~-- \mention{really fun}\ixlq{fun}, \mention{so fun}\ixlq{fun}, \mention{rather fun}\ixlq{fun}, \mention{very fun}\ixlq{fun}~-- is rare through most of the 19th and early 20th centuries but rises sharply from the late 20th century onward (Figure~\ref{fig:fun-coha}). The pattern is consistent with a shift from marginal adjectival-like uses to robust productivity. \mention{Rather fun} shows early isolated footholds (British epistolary usage appears as early as 1827); the American surge comes later. The diagnostics co-move across several degree environments rather than hinging on a single fragile string.

Boundary phenomena and operationalizing distance come next. The \mention{fun}\ixlq{fun} example shows one kind of change: diachronic drift of an item toward a boundary. Other cases involve the basin structure shifting around a relatively stable item, producing an overlap region rather than a simple crossing. The next issue is how to study items that sit \emph{at} a boundary synchronically~-- stably intermediate, not in transition but genuinely in between.

English reciprocals~-- \mention{each other}, \mention{one another}~-- offer a detailed case study \ixnq{Reynolds, Brett}\citep{reynolds2025reciprocals}. Standard reference grammars classify them as pronouns, and their distribution supports that much: they head NP-like phrases in the same slots as core pronouns, as verb and preposition complements. But they are non-canonical pronouns. Unlike core personal pronouns, they lack a person/gender/case paradigm and are distributionally defective in the most salient way: they resist ordinary free subject\ixsq{subject} use (cf. \mention{Somebody left} vs. \mention{*Each other left}). 

On the other side, reciprocals are built from determinative material (\mention{each}\ixlq{each}/\mention{one}\ixlq{one}) and impose a determinative-like semantic constraint: they are obligatorily anaphoric and require plural antecedents. But they aren't determiners: they can't occur in determiner function (\ungram\mention{each other friends} in the intended sense). My aim isn't to force a verdict from a handful of tests, but to motivate the boundary question: reciprocals sit where the pronoun and determinative profiles exert opposed pulls, making them an ideal probe for how to operationalize stable in-betweenness.

The methodology in \ixnq{Reynolds, Brett}\textcite{reynolds2025reciprocals} addresses the stability of this in-betweenness by applying three diagnostics:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Invariance under analytic perturbation: Does the boundary position shift when you change the distance metric, the comparison set, or the feature weights? Across several ways of mapping and measuring the space~-- correspondence-analysis plots, set-overlap distances, and robustness checks across many analytic choices~-- reciprocals sit midway between pronoun and determinative anchors~-- a stable intermediate, not an artefact of a particular operationalization.

\item Cross-dimensional tension: Do different feature families pull in different directions? Morphology pulls reciprocals toward determinatives (they lack the case paradigm of core pronouns). Semantics pulls them toward pronouns (they denote referents, not quantities). Syntax and phonology contribute little. The signature of a boundary item is exactly this: opposed pulls from mechanisms that maintain different basins.
\item Clear anchors behave cleanly: Do items unambiguously inside each basin\ixsq{basin} show the expected clustering? Core pronouns like \mention{he}\ixlq{he} and \mention{herself}\ixlq{herself} sit deep in the pronoun basin; determinatives like \mention{every}\ixlq{every} and \mention{either}\ixlq{either} sit deep in the determinative basin. The methodology confirms what intuition expects: the anchors cluster where they should. The interesting finding is that reciprocals don't.
\end{enumerate}

Whether the boundary position looks \enquote{statistically significant} depends on which comparison items you choose. Pick one reasonable set and the result is extreme; vary the comparison set and the result wobbles. But the \emph{qualitative} finding~-- reciprocals sit between pronoun and determinative anchors~-- is stable across all choices. Under an HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!membership} reading, this is exactly what genuine boundaries should look like: the diagnosis persists, but decisive p-values elude us because the item really is intermediate.

This operationalizes the chapter's theoretical picture. \enquote{Distance to boundary} is something we can measure. \enquote{Stability of ambiguity} is invariance under analytic perturbation~-- the in-betweenness persists when you change your measurement instrument. Reciprocals sit in the overlap region of Figure~\ref{fig:disjoint-overlap} because the basin\ixsq{basin} structure itself has overlap.

The same analysis applies to the existential debates about feature systems raised in §\ref{sec:3:lexeme-obsession}. Is \textsc{gender\ixsq{gender}} a natural category\ixsq{kind!natural}, or is it reducible to \textsc{number}? The question becomes: do gender\ixsq{gender} and number occupy distinct basins maintained by distinct mechanisms, or is gender a sub-region of a larger number basin, maintained by the same mechanisms at a finer grain?

If the mechanisms maintaining gender systems~-- agreement patterns, acquisition pathways, functional pressures toward nominal classification~-- are distinct from those maintaining number systems, then gender is a genuine natural category for linguistics: a separate basin in the space of grammatical features. If the mechanisms overlap substantially~-- if gender turns out to be number's machinery for individuation, as some theorists have argued~-- then the basins merge, and \textsc{gender\ixsq{gender}} as a cross-linguistic category\ixsq{category!cross-linguistic} dissolves into \textsc{number}.

The question is empirical, not definitional. The hyperreal model tells us what to look for: distinct basins with sharp thresholds and scale-dependent tolerance. The HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!two-diagnostic test} framework tells us how to investigate: identify the mechanisms, trace their operation, determine whether they cluster distinct properties or the same properties at different scales.

Consider the Shilluk\ixgq{Shilluk} number system introduced in §\ref{sec:3:lexeme-obsession}. English\ixgq{English} marks number with a suffix: \mention{cat}\ixlq{cat}, \mention{cats}\ixlq{cat}. Shilluk\ixgq{Shilluk} marks it through stem-internal changes in tone, vowel length, and voice quality~-- a singular/plural pair might differ only in whether the vowel is short, long, or overlong, or in whether the tone is Low or Fall. The exponence is lexicalized: each pair requires lexical learning; no productive rule derives one from the other.

On the HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!basin structure} + hyperreal view, both English\ixgq{English} and Shilluk\ixgq{Shilluk} number systems occupy basins in a feature space whose dimensions include degree of obligatoriness (how often number is obligatorily specified), agreement scope (specifier, verb, other constituents), and semantic function (individuation of entities). The exponence dimension~-- where English\ixgq{English} is affixal and Shilluk\ixgq{Shilluk} is fusional/suprasegmental~-- places them in different regions of morphophonological space but the same region of morphosyntactic and semantic space. They're in overlapping basins, not identical ones~-- independent systems shaped by similar pressures, not one system shared across languages.

The mechanisms maintaining the two systems are partially shared (acquisition of a grammatical contrast where specified, iterated transmission of the singular/non-singular distinction within the contrastive subsystem) and partially distinct (rule-based productivity in English\ixgq{English}, lexical storage in Shilluk\ixgq{Shilluk}). The prediction is that where the mechanisms align~-- the functional pressure to distinguish singular from plural~-- the categories should pattern similarly across languages. Where they diverge~-- the phonological substance of the exponence~-- we should expect variation. That is broadly the pattern that emerges. Within the contrastive subsystem, the basin\ixsq{basin} boundary for \textsc{number} is sharp in both languages (a noun is singular or plural, not in between), but the basin's location in the full feature space differs, because different mechanisms weight different dimensions. The same analysis extends to noun-class systems like Swahili's\ixgq{Swahili}, to Mandarin\ixgq{Chinese!Mandarin} classifiers, to German\ixgq{German} gender. Wherever there's grammatical categorization, there should be basin structure maintained by mechanisms.

Even if the HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!cross-linguistic} analysis confirms that English\ixgq{English} number and Shilluk\ixgq{Shilluk} number are both \textsc{number}, it still tells us \emph{why} they deserve the same label: overlapping mechanisms maintaining overlapping basins, not definitional fiat. It also makes predictions. If we found a putative \enquote{number} category maintained by entirely different mechanisms with no overlap in the functional dimension, the framework would treat it as a different category under the same label.

\section{Empirical consequences}
\label{sec:5:empirical-consequences}

\subsection{Gradient judgments, discrete categories}
\label{subsec:5:gradient-discrete}

The discreteness problem has a close cousin: the gradient-judgment problem. Speakers' acceptability\ixsq{acceptability} judgments are notoriously gradient. Asked to rate sentences on a 1--7 scale, they distribute across the range, with clear acceptability at the extremes and uncertainty in the middle. If categories are discrete, why are judgments gradient?

The hyperreal framework suggests an answer. Distinguish the category\ixsq{category!reality of} from its measurement, the ontology from its epistemology.

Grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality}~-- the property of conforming to a speaker's maintained grammar~-- is discrete. Relative to that system, a sentence either satisfies the constraints or it doesn't. The boundary between grammatical and ungrammatical\ixsq{ungrammatical} is sharp, located at a hyperreal threshold in some underlying dimension (perhaps accumulated constraint violation, or distance from prototype, or processing cost).

Some frameworks treat the competence object itself as inherently graded or stochastic, not just the measurement channel. The present framework makes a different bet: gradience should concentrate near maintained boundaries, not distribute uniformly across the space. If the probabilistic alternative is right, there's no reason to expect variance to peak specifically in seam regions; Chapter~\ref{ch:grammaticality-itself} takes up this contrast directly.

Acceptability\ixsq{acceptability}~-- the measured response in judgment tasks~-- reflects grammaticality filtered through noise. Processing difficulty, frequency effects, task demands, individual variation, and measurement error all intervene between the discrete category and the gradient response. Grammaticality is the maintained category; acceptability is a noisy instrument whose bias terms are themselves mechanism-sensitive (frequency shapes entrenchment, task framing activates different basin configurations, exposure history shapes where the boundary lies for each speaker).

One reason this distinction is so easy to lose sight of is that the phenomenology\ixsq{phenomenology} is asymmetric. When a sentence is well-formed, nothing in particular announces itself: processing just runs. The felt data is mostly negative~-- the \enquote{catch} of a violation, the extra effort, the moment of repair. In that sense there is usually no salient positive feeling of grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality}, just as there is no feeling of having sufficient oxygen; the experiences that demand attention are the absences. This is why boundary detection is cognitively natural: violations have a signature, conformity is typically silent.

A second reason is methodological. Temperature isn't something you see; it's a theoretical magnitude inferred from what thermometers do. Likewise grammaticality is a latent property that we access only through instruments~-- judgments, reaction times, eye movements, priming, production choices~-- each of which can misread the target in both directions, yielding false comfort (illusory well-formedness) or false alarm (grammatical but degraded). The point is clearest in \emph{satiation} effects: repeated exposure can raise acceptability\ixsq{acceptability} for initially degraded strings, even though it's implausible that the grammar itself has been rewritten over the course of a short experimental session. Satiation is informative here: it shows that the mapping from grammaticality to felt acceptability is plastic, and that we shouldn't identify the construct with any one measurement channel.

Not all of these factors work the same way. Random fluctuations~-- trial-to-trial variation in attention, incidental processing load, measurement noise~-- blur the signal without shifting it. But frequency effects and task demands can systematically shift the mapping from grammaticality to acceptability\ixsq{acceptability}. A borderline construction may be judged more acceptable if it's high-frequency than if it's low-frequency, even when both are equally grammatical. Instructions emphasizing naturalness may locate the boundary differently than instructions emphasizing correctness. These are biases, not noise. The two-layer model accommodates them: different contexts may activate different effective basin\ixsq{basin} configurations, shifting where tolerance breaks down.

This predicts:
\begin{itemize}
\item Clear cases cluster at scale extremes (1s and 7s), because items deep in the grammatical or ungrammatical\ixsq{ungrammatical} basin\ixsq{basin} are stably categorized and noise doesn't flip them.
\item Borderline cases distribute across the middle, because items near the boundary are sensitive to noise~-- small processing fluctuations can shift judgments in either direction.
\item The gradient spread is widest near the boundary, because that's where the signal-to-noise ratio is lowest (see Figures~\ref{fig:two-layer-model} and~\ref{fig:judgment-variance}).
\end{itemize}

The prediction is testable. If the two-layer model is right, items independently rated as near category\ixsq{category!boundary cases} boundaries should show higher variance in acceptability\ixsq{acceptability} judgments than items rated as central. The prediction isn't that boundary items get middling ratings~-- that's compatible with gradient grammaticality~-- but that their ratings should be more variable across trials and subjects. In the simplest latent-threshold models, variance is maximized in the boundary region and falls off as items move deeper into either basin\ixsq{basin}. The exact functional form depends on the measurement channel (binary choice vs.\ rating scale), the noise model, and the way context deforms the local landscape; what is robust is the qualitative signature: variance tracks proximity to the seam. To be precise: in simple latent-threshold models with additive noise, one expects a single variance peak near the threshold; the hyperreal story doesn't add to that statistical prediction~-- it explains why the threshold exists and why it resists finite specification. Existing work on gradient acceptability\ixsq{acceptability} already points in this direction: \ixnq{Sprouse, Jon}\ixnq{Schutze, Carson T.@Schütze, Carson T.}\ixnq{Almeida, Diogo}\textcite{sprouse2013} found that judgment variability correlates with distance from prototypical (un)grammaticality, and \ixnq{Dabrowska, Ewa}\textcite{dabrowska2010} showed systematic individual differences in judgments of complex constructions~-- exactly what you'd expect if different speakers have slightly different basin\ixsq{basin} structures.

This last point deserves emphasis. The basin\ixsq{basin} structure isn't uniform across speakers. Speakers with different input histories~-- more or less exposure to written registers, different dialectal backgrounds, varying levels of literacy~-- will have basins with different shapes and different boundary locations. The hyperreal model accommodates this naturally: each speaker's grammar\ixsq{grammar} is a different model, with a different boundary index $K$. What varies across speakers isn't just noise; it's the underlying geometry. The framework predicts that variability in judgments should be highest for constructions that fall near the boundary \emph{for most speakers}~-- and that speakers who agree on central cases may nonetheless diverge on peripheral ones, because their boundaries are located at different hyperreal indices.

This distinction isn't new; it's been with us since Chomsky. The hyperreal framework adds precision by locating the boundary at a specific (if unspecifiable) hyperreal threshold and by treating gradience\ixsq{gradience} as the predictable consequence of epistemic limitations near a sharp boundary.

Chapter~\ref{ch:grammaticality-itself} develops this picture fully, arguing that grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} itself is a homeostatic property cluster\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!membership}~-- a category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} maintained by mechanisms, with a sharp boundary at hyperreal distance. For now, the point is that gradient judgments and discrete categories are compatible. The discreteness is in the structure; the gradience\ixsq{gradience} is in our access to it.

\subsection{Dual membership}
\label{subsec:5:dual-membership}

A final issue is dual membership. If categories are discrete~-- if at every point in feature space an item is either in or out~-- how can there be genuine dual membership? How can \mention{near}\ixlq{near} be both a preposition\ixsq{preposition} and an adjective\ixsq{adjective}, not contextually selected, not in transition, but stably both? English is comfortable with a kind of dual citizenship here: items like \mention{near}\ixlq{near} can hold both passports without being in transit.

Discreteness holds predicate-by-predicate, not across predicates. The predicates \mention{is a preposition\ixsq{preposition}} and \mention{is an adjective\ixsq{adjective}} are each bivalent: at any point, each is either true or false. But they aren't mutually exclusive, so both labels can apply to the same item.

This follows naturally from the HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!membership} framework. Categories are maintained by mechanisms, not defined by partition. The mechanisms maintaining English prepositionhood~-- patterns of complementation, head-of-PP status, lack of degree modification~-- are partially independent of those maintaining English adjectivehood~-- gradability, predicative use, comparative morphology. An item can fall within the tolerance threshold for both, satisfying the clustering criteria for each.

Where the mechanisms align, the basins are disjoint: English nouns and verbs occupy separate regions because the mechanisms that maintain them pull in different directions (Figure~\ref{fig:disjoint-overlap}, left). Where the mechanisms cross, the basins overlap: English adjectives and prepositions share enough properties~-- predicative function, modification of nominals~-- that some items cluster with both (Figure~\ref{fig:disjoint-overlap}, right).

The regions of overlap aren't arbitrary. They're located where the property clusters themselves overlap~-- where an item can satisfy the tolerance criteria for both categories simultaneously. These are exactly the cases that trouble essentialism\ixsq{essentialism}: items that have \enquote{mixed} status because they satisfy criteria for multiple categories. On the HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!membership} + hyperreal view, that mixed status is better analyzed as dual location: the item is in two basins at once, stably, because the mechanisms that maintain each basin\ixsq{basin} both apply.

The reciprocals case from §\ref{subsec:5:multi-category} exemplifies this cleanly. Morphology maintains the determinative basin\ixsq{basin}; semantics maintains the pronoun basin\ixsq{basin}. Reciprocals satisfy both. The mixture\ixsq{mixture} weights from the blend model~-- 0.534 pronoun-like for \mention{each other}, 0.487 for \mention{one another}~-- aren't noise or measurement error. They are the empirical signature of genuine dual location: stable membership in overlapping basins, maintained by partially independent mechanisms.

\subsection{Summary: discreteness without essence}
\label{subsec:5:discreteness-summary}

The discreteness problem asked: if underlying properties are continuous, how do discrete categories emerge?

The chapter's answer has three connected parts.

First, scale-sensitive tolerance explains how sharp boundaries can arise from continuous substrates without becoming finitely locatable. In the hyperreal formalization, the boundary is sharp, located at a hyperreal threshold, epistemically inaccessible but structurally determinate.

Second, in multi-dimensional feature spaces, categories form basin structures rather than simple one-dimensional cutoffs. That makes room for overlap, dual membership, and different kinds of change.

Third, those structures persist because mechanisms~-- acquisition, entrenchment, alignment\ixsq{alignment!convergence effects}, transmission, functional pressure~-- stabilize them across time.

Together, these explain how categories can be:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Real: structured and stabilized by causal mechanisms, not merely stipulated.
\item Discrete: with sharp boundaries, not gradient membership.
\item Fuzzy at the edges: because tolerance fails near boundaries, producing instability and apparent gradience\ixsq{gradience}.
\item Stable: because mechanisms maintain the basin\ixsq{basin} structure across time.
\item Capable of change: because mechanisms can shift, basins can migrate, boundaries can move.
\end{itemize}

The result preserves what made essentialism attractive~-- real structure, sharp boundaries, determinacy~-- without requiring definitions, necessary and sufficient conditions, or essences. Categories count as real because they're maintained in ways that support reliable projection, not because they're defined. Their boundaries are sharp because tolerance is scale-dependent, not because some single property is binary. The determinacy is causal-structural rather than definitional.

Nominalism is also right about something important: categories don't need essences in order to be real. The HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!vs.\ essentialism} framework, formalized via hyperreal tolerance, shows how natural categories can lack essences while remaining discrete, structured, and empirically tractable. Dynamic discreteness\ixsq{dynamic discreteness} and real gradience\ixsq{gradience} are two aspects of that same picture.

\begin{definitenessthread}
This chapter predicts a signature phenomenon for definiteness\ixsq{definiteness}: judgments about borderline cases (\eg weak definites like \mention{go to the hospital}\ixlq{hospital}) should show higher variance than judgments about clear cases, because they sit near the boundary where tolerance fails. Register shift (task context, formality) should modulate variance without eliminating the underlying discreteness. The prediction would be falsified if borderline definiteness showed no variance asymmetry, suggesting the category lacks the basin\ixsq{basin} structure this chapter describes.
\end{definitenessthread}
