\chapter{Projectibility and the good bet}\ixsq{projectibility!defined}
\label{ch:projectibility}

\epigraph{If there aren't [pedicabs], Mr Tagomi thought, I would be well advised to retire to secluded place and kill myself.}{~-- Philip K. Dick, \textit{The Man in the High Castle} (1962)}

Mr.~Tagomi has slipped between worlds, and he needs to know which one he's in. Pedicabs exist in his home world but not in the other. He's betting his life on the projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!induction from} of a single feature.

This is the core of what a category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} is: not a container or a definition, but a licence to infer.

You encounter \mention{car}\ixlq{car}. It takes determinatives, pluralizes, functions as an argument. You've never seen \mention{pedicab}\ixlq{pedicab}~-- but you bet it behaves likewise. You're usually right. The category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} \term{noun} lets you project from instances you've seen to instances you haven't. That's the bet, and it usually pays off.

The previous chapter traced how categories are maintained: acquisition, entrenchment, alignment, functional pressure, transmission. Those mechanisms do real causal work. But the question remains: what do they buy you? Why does maintenance matter?

Here's the answer. Maintenance matters because it underwrites projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!defined}, and projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!vs.\ convenience} is what makes a category\ixsq{category!reality of} real for the purposes at hand.

This isn't a further property that HPCs happen to have. A category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} just is whatever supports reliable inference\ixsq{inference} across novel instances. For the categories at issue in this book, the mechanisms don't maintain a cluster that then, as a bonus, turns out to be projectible. They underwrite the projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!defined} itself. The cluster is the visible trace; the inferential role is the substance.

Peirce saw this a century ago: the meaning of a sign is the habit\ixsq{habit} it produces~-- the inferences it licenses. A category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} that doesn't support inference\ixsq{inference} isn't a category\ixsq{category!vs.\ class} at all; it's a filing label. What I've been calling \enquote{homeostasis\ixsq{homeostasis!defined}} is what keeps the inference\ixsq{inference} reliable. What I've been calling \enquote{the cluster} is the profile that the inference\ixsq{inference} tracks.

The slogan, then:

\begin{quote}
\emph{A linguistic category is a profile of co-occurring properties, stabilized by mechanisms, projectible relative to purposes.}
\end{quote}

The rest of this chapter unpacks that claim. Polish\ixgq{Polish} aspect\ixsq{aspect} provides a worked example~-- a case where the textbook definition fails to project but the mechanisms succeed. Field-relative projectibility\ixsq{field-relative projectibility} explains why different disciplines can carve the same extensions differently without either being wrong. And the epistemic payoff connects this framework to the deepest question in philosophy of science: what makes induction possible?

Models succeed at NPI\ixsq{NPI} prediction only to the extent they learn each item's distribution, not the category\ixsq{category!vs.\ class}. The label names a distributional class\ixsq{class}, not a single mechanism-maintained category.


\subsection{Unpacking the framework}
\label{sec:7:slogan}

Chapter~\ref{ch:kinds-without-essences} compressed the maintenance view\ixsq{maintenance view} into a slogan: \emph{a linguistic category is a profile of co-occurring properties, stabilized by mechanisms, projectible relative to purposes}. The three parts aren't on a par; the first two matter because they make the third possible.

The first term, \emph{profile}, names what you can observe: properties cluster, members resemble each other, regularities recur. But surface clustering isn't yet an explanation. Any two objects share indefinitely many properties, and some clusters are only descriptive conveniences. The profile is the visible pattern, not yet an account of why that pattern holds.

What \emph{stabilized by mechanisms} adds is the missing explanatory layer. Acquisition, entrenchment, functional pressure, and transmission keep some properties travelling together and make the clustering robust under perturbation. \emph{Projectible relative to purposes} then names the payoff. A category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} supports induction\ixsq{induction}, but only for questions those mechanisms underwrite. That is why a syntactician and a semanticist can arrive at different right-sized categories over overlapping extensions without either carving arbitrarily: different mechanisms stabilize different clusters for different inferential purposes.

The architecture, then, runs from mechanism to projection, with the visible profile as the trace left behind. Projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!vs.\ convenience} is what maintenance buys you.

This framing guards against three confusions. First, it resists nominalism\ixsq{nominalism} by refusing to treat categories as mere conventions and by keeping the non-arbitrariness of mechanism in view. Second, it resists absolutism by refusing to treat projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!field-relative} as all-or-nothing and by keeping purpose-relativity in view. Third, it resists conflation. Mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!vs.\ essences} is metaphysics~-- what keeps the cluster clustered. Projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!vs.\ convenience} is epistemology~-- what inferences the cluster licenses. A category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} can be maintained without being projectible, if the mechanisms don't align with your question, and a pattern can seem projectible without being maintained, if you've mistaken correlation for causation.

The remainder of this chapter turns first to Polish\ixgq{Polish} aspect\ixsq{aspect}, a case that looks as if crisp definitions ought to work. In practice, the textbook characterization is maintained by tradition but doesn't project to usage, while the distributional structure projects reliably because it tracks the actual mechanisms.


\section{The definitional bet}
\label{sec:7:definitional-bet}

Polish\ixgq{Polish} verbal aspect\ixsq{aspect} is a good place to begin because it looks like a paradigm case of a grammaticalized binary. Every Polish\ixgq{Polish} verb bears aspectual marking: imperfective or perfective\ixsq{perfective}. The opposition is obligatory, pervasive, and ancient~-- codified in grammars for centuries, taught in every language classroom.

The textbooks offer crisp definitions. Imperfective presents an event as unbounded~-- ongoing, habitual, incomplete. Perfective\ixsq{perfective} presents it as bounded~-- a completed whole with temporal limits (Figure~\ref{fig:aspect-viewpoint}). The terminology varies (totality, resultativeness, telicity), but the core claim is the same: there's an invariant semantic content distinguishing the two aspects, and knowing that content should let you predict which aspect a speaker will choose.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/7.aspect-viewpoint.png}
\caption{The textbook account of Slavic\ixgq{Slavic languages} aspect. Imperfective presents the event from inside, as ongoing; perfective presents it from outside, as a completed whole. This is the \enquote{viewpoint} metaphor that has dominated aspectual theory for a century.}
\label{fig:aspect-viewpoint}
\end{figure}

This is essentialism\ixsq{essentialism} applied to morphology: the definitions are the essence, the essence determines behaviour, and learning the essence should let you project. If aspect really has invariant semantic content of that sort, then a model that knows that content should predict Polish aspectual choices in usage. \ixnq{Divjak, Dagmar}\ixnq{Milin, Petar}\ixnq{Borowski, Maciej}\textcite{divjak2025learnability} tested exactly that by building three computational models of Polish\ixgq{Polish} verb usage:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A \term{lemma-concrete} model that learns cue--outcome associations without using an abstract aspect label as a predictor at all.
    \item An \term{aspect-concrete} model that learns aspect from usage cues~-- distributional patterns in the input.
    \item An \term{aspect-abstract} model that learns aspect from the semantic labels proposed in the theoretical literature~-- boundedness\ixsq{boundedness}, totality, resultativeness.
\end{itemize}

The aspect-abstract model formalizes the textbook view, so if the invariant meanings are doing the real explanatory work for usage prediction, this model should win. Instead, on corpus data the aspect-abstract model performs reasonably~-- 87\% accuracy \ixnq{Divjak, Dagmar}\ixnq{Milin, Petar}\ixnq{Borowski, Maciej}\citep{divjak2025learnability}~-- but that aggregate score hides a striking asymmetry. The model predicts imperfective well, at 98\% correct, but predicts perfective\ixsq{perfective} much more poorly, at only 77\%~-- you lose the bet nearly one time in four. The semantic invariants that aspectologists have proposed for a century~-- the definitions on which projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!vs.\ convenience} supposedly depends~-- capture only half the system for this purpose. Likely this is because perfective\ixsq{perfective} is more lexically heterogeneous: perfective\ixsq{perfective} verbs are marked by a variety of prefixes, each with its own distributional profile, and they appear in a narrower range of tense frames. Imperfective is closer to a default, less marked, more uniform. The asymmetry isn't random; it tracks where the semantic definition aligns with distributional reality for predicting usage (imperfective) and where it doesn't (perfective\ixsq{perfective}).

The problem becomes clearer when the models are validated against native-speaker judgments in a gap-filling task. There the aspect-abstract model performs \emph{worse} than the simpler alternatives: native speakers' preferences align better with a model that doesn't use an abstract aspect label as a predictor than with one that does, and the lemma-concrete model~-- the one that treats each verb individually, without abstracting to aspect~-- best predicts which forms humans prefer. This is a failure of definitional projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!Goodman's problem} for the purpose at hand. The pattern is real and the usage is systematic, but the definitions don't project to speaker choices: knowing what \enquote{perfective\ixsq{perfective}} supposedly \emph{means} doesn't reliably tell you when Polish\ixgq{Polish} speakers will \emph{use} it. (A moderate reader might object that the textbook definitions are simplistic models, not metaphysical essences~-- that their failure is a failure of \emph{that} model, not of semantic-level explanation generally. Fair enough. But even treating definitions as models rather than essences, the projective power here lies in distributional cue structure and lexeme-level entrenchment. Semantic paraphrase plays at best a partial, uneven role.)


\section{The mechanistic alternative}
\label{sec:7:mechanistic-alternative}

Why the textbook view fails becomes clearer in \ixnq{Divjak, Dagmar}\ixnq{Wi\'sniewski, Jakub}\ixnq{Milin, Petar}\textcite{divjak2024aspect}'s corpus study of Polish\ixgq{Polish} aspect\ixsq{aspect}, which reveals a usage landscape strikingly different from the textbook picture.

First, lexical bias is the norm. About 90\% of Polish\ixgq{Polish} verbs strongly prefer one aspect~-- greater than 90\% of their tokens appear in the preferred form. Only 11\% of aspectual pairs are genuinely equiprobable. The textbook suggestion that speakers \enquote{choose} aspect based on how they view the event applies to a small minority of cases.

Second, tense carries most of the signal. A simple model using just the superlemma (verb identity) and three-way tense distinction (past, present, future) achieves predictive-performance (F1) scores of 0.95 for imperfective and 0.90 for perfective\ixsq{perfective}~-- the perfective\ixsq{perfective} asymmetry persists, just smaller. For the purpose of predicting usage, aspect\ixsq{aspect} is largely predictable from tense, not from semantic viewpoint.

Third, context cues appear where needed. Temporal adverbs and other contextual markers show up reliably only with the 11\% of verbs that lack lexical bias. The system is informationally efficient: redundant cues don't clutter unambiguous cases.

Finally, the landscape is already shaped~-- like a riverbed cut by the water that flows through it. For most verbs, learning the verb means entering a channel that use keeps cutting deeper. The category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} isn't freshly applied at each utterance; the same flow that carved it keeps maintaining it.

Taken together, these findings reframe the question worth asking if the aim is to predict Polish aspectual usage. Instead of \enquote{what does aspect mean?}~-- a question that invites ever more refined invariant definitions~-- the question becomes \enquote{what maintains the aspectual patterns speakers produce?} That question is open, investigable, and already yielding answers: distributional signatures, acquisition dynamics, lexical entrenchment. The textbook view isn't simply wrong; it's pursuing a question whose answer can't be recovered by the methods it uses on their own. The mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!vs.\ essences} view doesn't replace it so much as explain why it worked as well as it did, and what it was missing.


Seen that way, the maintenance view\ixsq{maintenance view} comes into focus. A Polish\ixgq{Polish} speaker can project aspectual behaviour to unfamiliar verbs not by applying a definition, but by relying on the cue--outcome structure extracted from the input. The definitions~-- boundedness\ixsq{boundedness}, totality~-- predict only imperfective, and even then imperfectly. For that predictive purpose, what actually projects is something different: the web of regularities that learners have already encountered.

Consider: you've learned \mention{\ipa{robić}} (impf) / \mention{\ipa{zrobić}} (pf)~-- \enquote{do}, \enquote{accomplish}. You encounter an unfamiliar verb \mention{\ipa{pływać}}~-- \enquote{swim}. How do you know that \mention{\ipa{przepływać}} will be perfective\ixsq{perfective}?

The textbook answer would be that you apply the semantic definition of perfectivity~-- the event is bounded, complete, telic~-- and infer that \mention{\ipa{przepływać}} (\enquote{swim across}, \enquote{complete the crossing}) satisfies those criteria. But as the preceding results show, that semantic route predicts usage poorly. Worse, it predicts perfective\ixsq{perfective} worse than imperfective~-- exactly the wrong way round for the case at hand.

On the mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!entrenchment}-based story, by contrast, you've learned that certain prefixes, in certain tense contexts, pattern with perfective\ixsq{perfective}. You've learned that verbs of motion typically have strong aspectual biases carried by morphological material. You project not from a definition but from a web of cue--outcome associations maintained across your linguistic experience.

For predicting Polish usage, this is projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!induction from} grounded in mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!defined} rather than definition (Figure~\ref{fig:aspect-mechanism}). The traditional label \term{perfective\ixsq{perfective}} remains useful~-- you can introspect about it, metalinguistic discourse depends on it~-- but its psycholinguistic reality needn't rest on invariant semantic content. The good bet is on the consistency of the distributional patterns that maintain it.

\section{Labels aren't mechanisms}
\label{sec:7:labels-mechanisms}

\ixnq{Divjak, Dagmar}\ixnq{Milin, Petar}\ixnq{Borowski, Maciej}\textcite{divjak2025learnability} push the story in a surprising direction: the best model~-- the one that most closely matches native-speaker preferences~-- is the one that doesn't use the abstract \term{aspect\ixsq{aspect}} label as a predictor at all.

The lemma-concrete model~-- the one that treats each verb individually, without abstracting to aspect~-- predicts human behaviour better than the aspect-aware models. Under this AIC-based comparison, relative support for the aspect\ixsq{aspect}-aware models is extremely low.\footnote{AIC 3,891 for lemma-concrete versus 3,954 for aspect-concrete and 4,037 for aspect-abstract; evidence ratios in the $10^{13}$ to $10^{17}$ range~-- numbers so large that the model-comparison conclusion is statistically compelling.}

What these results mean for the ontological status of aspect\ixsq{aspect} becomes clearer once three questions are separated. First, is there a stable Polish\ixgq{Polish} pattern that linguists can usefully label \enquote{aspect\ixsq{aspect}} for descriptive and comparative purposes? Yes. The corpus regularities, speaker agreement, and reliable acquisition all point to a real, socially shared, historically stable clustering. Second, do the traditional semantic definitions help predict Polish\ixgq{Polish} aspectual choices? Only unevenly, and especially poorly for perfective\ixsq{perfective}. That's a failure of \emph{definitional} projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!vs.\ convenience} for that purpose, not automatically a failure of the inherited label as such. Third, do speakers actually rely on an abstract aspect representation when making aspectual choices? The lemma-concrete result suggests that they may not. The relevant causal structure may instead lie in more local cue patterns.

Once those questions are distinguished, the apparent contradiction mostly disappears. What fails is the textbook essence as a guide to predicting Polish\ixgq{Polish} aspectual choices, not the existence of a stable, learnable, intersubjectively shared aspectual pattern~-- and not necessarily the inherited label itself, which may be gathering together more than one coupled profile.

In that sense, Polish\ixgq{Polish} aspect\ixsq{aspect} is close to an ideal maintenance-view demonstration. The label tracks a real pattern, but for predicting usage the projective work is done by a bundle of more local mechanisms: lexeme-specific entrenchment, tense-conditioned expectations, and prefixal cueing. What projects for that purpose is that cue structure, not the invariant semantic paraphrases, so the projective unit is smaller than the inherited label. Speakers can project aspectual behaviour \emph{without} representing \enquote{aspect} as such, because they are relying on a web of cue--outcome associations with an aspectual signature. Those lemma-specific patterns don't displace aspect\ixsq{aspect} as a generalization; they help ground it. What speakers exploit for this task is a multi-level network in which verb-level cues and broader aspectual regularities coexist~-- the general emerging from the particular. That answers the objection that a lemma-level model leaves no room for the larger generalization: the generalization can be real as an emergent alignment\ixsq{alignment!convergence effects}, even if online prediction runs at a more local level.

That, in turn, clarifies the puzzle of naming. I keep the label \term{aspect\ixsq{aspect}} because Polish\ixgq{Polish} preserves a historically continuous opposition that still supports real generalizations. That doesn't require an earlier stage at which one semantic essence and one morphosyntactic pattern lined up perfectly. Mechanistic drift\ixsq{mechanistic drift}\ixsq{language change} can instead change how tightly the properties gathered under a label are coupled and which mechanisms do most of the stabilizing work. Think of a volcanic island (Figure~\ref{fig:island-volcano}). Over time, coral colonizes the flanks, soil accumulates, and vegetation takes root. The point isn't that the island has become less truly an island, but that what keeps it in place changes. Polish\ixgq{Polish} aspect\ixsq{aspect} is like this. The inherited label names a durable opposition, but for predicting present-day usage the relevant stabilizers are lexeme-specific cue structures rather than textbook semantic paraphrases. The label names the island, not any single mechanism that has helped maintain it. (For a child acquiring Polish\ixgq{Polish}, even that history is indirect: learners encounter the current cue ecology, not a succession of earlier maintenance regimes. The \enquote{mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!perturbation of} drift} is a diachronic observation about the collective system, not a stage in individual acquisition.)

The cross-linguistic term \term{aspect\ixsq{aspect}} functions as a \term{comparative concept\ixsq{comparative concept}}; within Polish\ixgq{Polish}, it names a historically inherited pattern whose contemporary projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!induction from} for usage prediction is anchored in lexeme-specific cue structures rather than in the textbook semantic paraphrases on their own. Used carefully, the label still earns its keep for comparative and historical purposes, but it's best treated as the name of an emergent clustering rather than the explanatory unit that makes the clustering project. Later chapters return to a broader possibility already visible here: an inherited label may cover more than one stabilized profile, coupled tightly enough to travel under one name. Genealogical continuity on its own isn't enough: the label remains appropriate only if it still picks out a stable pattern at some explanatory level. An island that has fully eroded, with no coral keeping it together, is no longer an island~-- and deserves no name.

\section{Generalizing the lesson}
\label{sec:7:generalizing-lesson}

Polish\ixgq{Polish} aspect\ixsq{aspect} illustrates a general lesson of the maintenance view\ixsq{maintenance view}. What these results challenge isn't the existence of the opposition traditionally called aspect, but the assumption that its textbook characterization is the mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!vs.\ essences} that makes it projectible. Aspect\ixsq{aspect} has been defined for centuries, but those definitions fail to predict usage. Mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!entrenchment}-based accounts succeed where definitions fail, because the same system becomes predictable once you attend to the distributional structure~-- lexical bias, tense context, morphological cues~-- rather than the semantic essence.

That separation matters because it shows what learning one case buys for the next. The category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} and the mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!defined} don't need to coincide. \term{Aspect\ixsq{aspect}} is a useful label for a real pattern, but speakers may implement that pattern as a network of cue--outcome associations rather than as an explicitly represented binary opposition. The mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!vs.\ essences} lives below the category\ixsq{category!reality of}; the projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!induction from} comes from the mechanism. When a proposed characterization fails to project, that failure is diagnostic: it tells you the characterization does not track the mechanisms. Polish\ixgq{Polish} aspect\ixsq{aspect} is a worked example, not an isolated curiosity. The same pattern~-- definitional characterizations that fail to project, mechanism-based descriptions that succeed~-- appears across grammatical domains.

\ixnq{Ambridge, Ben}\ixnq{Barak, Libby}\ixnq{Wonnacott, Elizabeth}\ixnq{Goldberg, Adele E.}\ixnq{Hesketh, Anna}\ixnq{Leshem, Raya}\ixnq{Naigles, Letitia R.}\textcite{ambridge2020} found that the causative alternation (\mention{break the vase} vs \ungram\mention{laugh the man}) is predicted by a continuous semantic dimension (directness of causation) across five typologically unrelated languages. The semantic definitions of verb\ixsq{verb!as category} classes don't work; the mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!functional pressure} (sensitivity to causal directness) does. Strikingly, a model trained on five languages predicts native-speaker judgments in a sixth (Balinese) without exposure. The mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!functional pressure} projects cross-linguistically; the definitional verb classes don't.

\ixnq{Saldana, Carmen@Saldaña, Carmen}\ixnq{Herce, Borja}\ixnq{Bickel, Balthasar}\textcite{saldana2022} showed that morphological syncretism patterns are learned better when syncretic cells share semantic similarity~-- not when they share features in a definitional sense. The learnability gradient tracks similarity, not category membership. What projects is the similarity structure, not the feature system\ixsq{feature system}.

The point isn't that BERT is a psychological model. It's that even here, grammatical structure becomes projectible through distributed learning rather than through explicit definitions. \ixnq{Chi, Ethan A.}\ixnq{Hewitt, John}\ixnq{Manning, Christopher D.}\textcite{chi2020} demonstrated that grammatical relations (subject, object) emerge spontaneously in multilingual BERT, a neural language model trained on many languages~-- and trained without any symbolic grammar~-- and transfer across languages. A probe trained only on English\ixgq{English} successfully identifies grammatical relations in French\ixgq{French}. The categories exist, but defining grammatical roles isn't what produces them; distributed learning over functional pressures is. The mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!acquisition} projects cross-linguistically; the formal definitions are epiphenomenal.

These aren't the only examples. Chapter~\ref{ch:lexical-categories} develops the case for word classes in detail. The upshot is that projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!induction from} is one empirical test for mechanistic grounding. When a category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} supports reliable induction, that's evidence that real stabilizing mechanisms are doing explanatory work. When it doesn't, something about the proposed characterization may be off, incomplete, or mismatched to the purpose at hand.


\section{What projection is}
\label{sec:7:what-projection-is}

The cases above show that projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!defined} tracks mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!defined}, not definition. But I haven't yet said what projection actually \emph{is}: what happens when you encounter \mention{pedicab}\ixlq{pedicab} and bet it behaves like \mention{car}\ixlq{car}.

Chapter~\ref{ch:what-we-havent-been-asking} already introduced Peirce's answer. A sign matters because of the habit\ixsq{habit} of inference\ixsq{inference} it generates~-- what Peirce called its logical/final interpretant\ixsq{interpretant} \ixnq{Peirce, Charles Sanders}\citep[CP~5.491]{peirce1931cp}. The interpretant\ixsq{interpretant} is a readiness to act rather than a gloss or a mental image. Encounter a noun and you project: heads a phrase, takes determinatives, fills argument slots. That projection is the interpretant\ixsq{interpretant} in play. It isn't something added after the category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} is identified; it is what the category does.

Peirce's term for the content of such a disposition is a \term{would-be\ixsq{would-be}} \ixnq{Peirce, Charles Sanders}\citep[CP~5.467]{peirce1931cp}. The meaning of \mention{hard}\ixlq{hard} takes the form of a conditional: this object \emph{would} resist a knife-edge. Projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!defined} is the same kind of thing. A category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} is projectible not because it has projected successfully in the past but because it sustains conditionals that reach to instances not yet encountered. The Polish\ixgq{Polish} case makes this concrete. One maintained would-be\ixsq{would-be} is production-facing: when a speaker wants to express this kind of event with this verb in this tense frame, the system pulls toward the perfective form. The textbook definition proposes a different would-be. The difference is that the first is maintained by mechanisms, while the second misfires as a guide to usage.

Why not just say \enquote{mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!vs.\ essences}} and leave Peirce aside? Because mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!defined} without mediation is only covariation, and bare covariation can mislead. Properties can covary by accident (NPIs share sensitivity to negative contexts, but no mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!vs.\ essences} maintains their co-occurrence as a category\ixsq{category!vs.\ class}). Mere covariation is non-semiotic. Projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!defined} requires a mediating habit\ixsq{habit} that makes the relation general, conditional, and predictive. The mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!defined} keeps that habit reliable; the habit is what carries the inference.

This also gives the book's non-reductive position. Mechanisms sustain habits, but habits aren't reducible to mechanisms. Remove the mechanism and the habit\ixsq{habit} degrades. Still, the inferential disposition isn't identical with the causal machinery that sustains it. The Polish\ixgq{Polish} volcano case showed the same thing: one mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!perturbation of} went dormant, but the broader aspectual pattern persisted because other mechanisms took over.

Habits also feed back on the signs that sustain them. Every use of \mention{be like} helps maintain the processing and social conditions that make later uses likely. That is the reciprocal maintenance Chapter~\ref{ch:stabilizers} documented~-- the point Peirce compressed in the remark that \enquote{men and words reciprocally educate each other} \ixnq{Peirce, Charles Sanders}\citep[CP~5.313]{peirce1931cp}.

Finally, habits are indexed to circumstances. A syntactician's training generates one interpretant\ixsq{interpretant} from \mention{Brett}\ixlqs{brett}{Brett} (distributional expectations, structural prediction); a semanticist's generates another (referential tracking, possible-world reasoning). The same sign yields different projections under different conditions. This is field-relative projectibility\ixsq{field-relative projectibility} in Peircean terms. For the purposes of this book, the triad is simple: the form cluster\ixsq{form cluster} (distributional, morphosyntactic), the object\ixsq{object} (semantic construal), and the interpretant\ixsq{interpretant} (the inferential habit\ixsq{habit} that couples them). The case studies in Part~III apply this decomposition systematically.


\section{Field-relative projectibility}
\ixsq{field-relative projectibility}
\label{sec:7:field-relative}

The same extension can support different projectible categories for different analytical purposes. This isn't perspectivalism~-- different views of the same thing. It's a claim about ontology: there are genuinely different HPCs that happen to overlap in extension, each projectible in its own domain.

\subsection{The tomato problem}
\label{sec:7:tomato}

Is a tomato a fruit or a vegetable? The question is undecidable only if you think there's one right answer.

A botanist classifies tomatoes as fruits~-- they develop from the ovary of a flowering plant and contain seeds. This classification projects: knowing that tomatoes are fruits lets the botanist predict ripening patterns, seed dispersal mechanisms, and responses to plant hormones. The category\ixsq{category!examples} \mention{fruit}\ixlq{fruit} supports reliable induction for botanical purposes.

A chef classifies tomatoes as vegetables~-- savoury, used in main courses, paired with salt rather than sugar. This classification also projects: knowing that tomatoes are culinary vegetables lets the chef predict flavour pairings, cooking methods, and menu placement. The category\ixsq{category!examples} \mention{vegetable}\ixlq{vegetable} supports reliable induction for culinary purposes.

Neither classification is wrong, and they aren't even in conflict: they are \emph{different HPCs} that happen to have overlapping extensions. The botanist's \mention{fruit}\ixlq{fruit} and the chef's \mention{vegetable}\ixlq{vegetable} are each maintained by mechanisms appropriate to their domain~-- reproductive biology for one, flavour chemistry and culinary tradition for the other. Each projects reliably within its field\ixsq{field}.

The tomato problem is a problem only if you assume projectibility is absolute~-- that a category either projects or doesn't, full stop. Once you recognize that projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!field-relative} is field\ixsq{field}-relative, the problem dissolves. What remains is the work of specifying which category projects for which purposes.

\subsection{Proper nouns and proper names}
\label{sec:7:proper-nouns-names}

Linguistics has its own tomatoes. Words like \mention{Brett}\ixlqs{brett}{Brett}, \mention{Canada}\ixlqs{canada}{Canada}, and \mention{The Dominican Republic}\ixlq{The Dominican Republic} sit at the overlap between what semanticists call \term{proper names}\ixsq{proper name} and what syntacticians call \term{proper nouns}\ixsq{noun!as category}\ixsq{proper noun}.

A semanticist works with \term{proper names}\ixsq{proper name}: expressions that refer directly to individuals without descriptive content, that are rigid designators (picking out the same individual across possible worlds), and that create referential opacity (\enquote{Lois believes Superman can fly} doesn't entail \enquote{Lois believes Clark can fly}). These properties cluster because of the cognitive and communicative functions names serve: tracking individuals across contexts requires stable reference without shifting descriptive content.

A syntactician works with \term{proper nouns}\ixsq{proper noun}: words that head nominal projections, resist articles in certain languages, trigger particular agreement patterns, and fill argument slots. These properties cluster because of distributional pressures: words that behave similarly in one syntactic environment tend to behave similarly in others.

The extensions overlap substantially~-- most expressions that are proper names\ixsq{proper name} are also proper nouns\ixsq{proper noun}. But the overlapping extension doesn't mean they're the same category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind}. They're different HPCs, maintained by different mechanisms, projectible for different purposes (Table~\ref{tab:proper-noun-name}).

A syntactician \emph{knows} that \mention{Brett}\ixlqs{brett}{Brett} is a proper name\ixsq{proper name}. But that semantic fact doesn't project for syntactic purposes. What projects syntactically is the distributional chain: \mention{Brett}\ixlqs{brett}{Brett} heads a nominal phrase $\rightarrow$ fills a subject slot $\rightarrow$ triggers third-person singular agreement. The syntactician can reliably predict that novel proper nouns will behave distributionally like familiar ones. The proper-name status is real but orthogonal to that prediction.

This isn't esoteric. Every introductory syntax course teaches students to distinguish \enquote{what a word means} from \enquote{how it behaves}. What hasn't been articulated is why this distinction exists: because meaning and behaviour are tracked by different mechanisms, and different mechanisms produce different HPCs. The field\ixsq{field}-relativity of projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!field-relative} explains why syntacticians can't just read off predictions from semantics, and vice versa.

\subsection{Constituency and dependency}
\label{sec:7:constituency-dependency}

The same logic applies to rival syntactic formalisms. Phrase-structure grammars, like \textit{CGEL}'s, represent sentences as hierarchies of \term{constituent}s~-- noun phrases, verb phrases, clauses nested inside each other. Dependency grammars, like \ixnq{Gibson, Edward}\citeauthor{gibson2025}'s (\citeyear{gibson2025}), represent sentences as networks of \term{dependency relation}s~-- subject-of, object-of, modifier-of~-- linking words directly without intermediate phrasal nodes.

The rivalry is old, but the maintenance view\ixsq{maintenance view} dissolves the \enquote{which is \emph{real}?} framing. Both formalisms track genuine structure, maintained by mechanisms, projectible for different purposes.

Constituency is maintained by substitution, movement, and coordination: expressions that can be pronominalized together, fronted together, or conjoined are grouped as constituents. Knowing that \mention{the cat}\ixlq{cat} is a noun\ixsq{noun!distributional profile} phrase lets you predict it can be replaced by \mention{it}, moved to topic position, or coordinated with \mention{the dog}\ixlq{dog}. The category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} projects for questions about \emph{what groups with what}.

Dependency is especially useful for questions shaped by processing constraints: working memory tracks which words await completion, and dependency length predicts reading time and comprehension difficulty \ixnq{Gibson, Edward}\ixnq{Futrell, Richard}\ixnq{Levy, Roger P.}\citep{gibson2000,futrell2020}. Knowing that a verb\ixsq{verb!distributional profile} governs a distant subject\ixsq{subject} lets you predict processing slowdown. The category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} projects for questions about \emph{what costs what}.

The extensions overlap~-- every sentence can be described both ways~-- but the HPCs are distinct. A syntactician asking \enquote{what can move together?} needs constituency. A psycholinguist asking \enquote{why is this sentence hard?} needs dependency. Neither formalism is epiphenomenal; each tracks mechanisms relevant to its questions. The debate is dissolved, not decided.

This fits naturally with what \ixnq{Carroll, Sean M.}\textcite{carroll2016} calls \term{poetic naturalism}: a sparse naturalistic metaphysics paired with a permission structure for higher-level vocabularies. Poetic naturalism tells you when it's licit to treat a vocabulary as tracking something real~-- when the description captures a pattern that admits information-compressing, predictively useful summary, and when it coheres with descriptions at other grains \ixnq{Dennett, Daniel C.}\citep{dennett1991}. The HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!mechanisms} framework tells you one way those patterns stay put: by specifying the mechanisms that maintain the clustering. Constituency is real because substitution, movement, and coordination actively cluster the relevant properties; dependency is real because processing constraints make dependency structure especially projectible for the questions psycholinguists ask. The poetry has plumbing.

\subsection{Why colour doesn't grammaticalize}
\label{sec:7:colour}

The constituency/dependency case and the proper-name case both stay within linguistics~-- different subfields or formalisms carving the same extensions differently. A starker example crosses domains entirely: colour.

Colour is maximally salient. Trichromatic vision evolved because colour predicts things that matter for survival: fruit ripeness, toxicity signals, mate health, camouflage. The warm/cool boundary shows categorical perception~-- adults distinguish warm from cool colours faster than they distinguish within either category \ixnq{Holmes, Kevin J.}\ixnq{Regier, Terry}\citep{holmes2017}. Colour terms partition efficiently across languages, with the warm/cool split emerging early in colour-vocabulary evolution \ixnq{Kay, Paul}\ixnq{McDaniel, Chad K.}\citep{kay1978}. If any perceptual domain should grammaticalize, colour should. But noun\ixsq{noun!cross-linguistic}-classification systems regularly encode animacy\ixsq{animacy}, shape, and size~-- never colour. No documented language has verb\ixsq{verb!cross-linguistic} agreement triggered by whether the subject is warm- or cool-coloured. The warm/cool distinction is robust, salient, and cross-linguistically stable in the lexicon. But it's entirely absent from closed-class morphology \ixnq{Seifart, Frank}\citep{seifart2010}.

\ixnq{Prasertsom, Ponrawee}\ixnq{Smith, Kenny}\ixnq{Culbertson, Jennifer}\textcite{prasertsom2026} tested whether this gap reflects domain-specific constraints on grammar\ixsq{grammar}~-- a hard-wired filter excluding colour from the set of possible grammatical features~-- or something more general. Their experiments compared animacy\ixsq{animacy}-based and colour-based noun\ixsq{noun!cross-linguistic}-class learning in an artificial language. Participants learned both, but animacy\ixsq{animacy}-based classes were learned better. More strikingly, when the input was ambiguous between animacy\ixsq{animacy} and colour as the classification basis, participants overwhelmingly inferred animacy\ixsq{animacy}~-- 77.5\% generalized to animacy\ixsq{animacy}, nearly half of them categorically.

This bias isn't grammar-specific. In a non-linguistic sorting task, the same preference appeared: participants sorted images by animacy\ixsq{animacy} rather than colour, even when the experimenters manipulated within-category similarity to favour colour. The bias persisted across three stimulus sets designed to progressively disadvantage animacy\ixsq{animacy}.

What drives the asymmetry? Not salience~-- colour is as perceptually salient as animacy\ixsq{animacy}. The answer lies in predictive structure. Knowing something is animate predicts a cluster of other properties: self-initiated motion, goal-directedness, organic composition, susceptibility to certain event roles. These predictions hold regardless of what specific entity you're considering. Knowing something is red predicts little beyond its surface appearance~-- and what it does predict (ripeness, toxicity) is object-relative. Red means different things for apples, frogs, and traffic lights.

\ixnq{Prasertsom, Ponrawee}\ixnq{Smith, Kenny}\ixnq{Culbertson, Jennifer}\textcite{prasertsom2026} confirmed this computationally. They extracted word embeddings for 472 frequent physical nouns from child-directed speech and clustered them by animacy\ixsq{animacy} versus colour. Animacy\ixsq{animacy}-based clusters were more compact and more distinct; logistic classifiers learned animacy\ixsq{animacy} categories faster and generalized better to unseen nouns~-- without any built-in knowledge of animacy\ixsq{animacy}. The distributional structure of language itself encodes the asymmetry.

The point is purpose-relative projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!field-relative} across domains. Colour is projectible \emph{for ecological purposes}: predicting edibility, danger, health. The mechanisms stabilizing colour categories are perceptual and action-guiding~-- they evolved to support decisions about what to eat, avoid, or approach. But grammar\ixsq{grammar} serves different purposes: argument realization, reference tracking, event-structure encoding. Animacy\ixsq{animacy} predicts properties relevant to those purposes; colour doesn't.

The upshot isn't that colour categories are fake or that grammatical categories are special. Both are HPCs, maintained by mechanisms appropriate to their domains. What differs is the domain of projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!field-relative}. Evolution gave us colour vision because colour projects for survival-relevant inferences. It didn't give us colour-based agreement because colour doesn't project for grammatical inferences. The same extension~-- red things, warm-coloured things~-- supports robust categorization in one domain and fails to support it in another.

This extends the proper-name pattern. \term{Proper name\ixsq{proper name}} projects for semantic purposes; \term{proper noun}\ixsq{proper noun} projects for syntactic purposes. \term{Warm-coloured} projects for ecological purposes; nothing projects it for grammatical purposes~-- so nothing grammaticalizes. The framework doesn't multiply categories recklessly; it asks what cluster of properties is maintained by what mechanisms, and whether that cluster projects for the questions you're asking. Chapter~\ref{ch:lexical-categories} returns to animacy\ixsq{animacy} in its analysis of noun\ixsq{noun!vs.\ verb}/verb\ixsq{verb!boundary cases} stability.

\subsection{The discipline: three checks}
\label{sec:7:three-checks}

The slogan~-- \emph{a linguistic category is a profile of co-occurring properties, stabilized by mechanisms, projectible relative to purposes}~-- maps onto three diagnostic checks:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Cluster check.} Does property covariance hold across samples and contexts? If the properties don't cluster, there's no profile to explain.
    \item \textbf{Homeostasis\ixsq{homeostasis!perturbation test} check.} Are there identifiable perturbations under which the cluster reconstitutes toward its attractor basin (or fails)? If not, the mechanism hasn't been identified.
    \item \textbf{Projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!induction from} check.} Are there counterfactual-supporting inferences in new contexts? If learning about one member doesn't tell you about others, the category isn't earning its keep.
\end{enumerate}

These checks can yield different answers for different fields. A category might pass all three for syntax and fail the projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!field-relative} check for semantics. That's not a defect of the framework~-- it's the framework working. The checks aren't absolute; they're indexed to purpose.

This discipline guards against two temptations. First, the essentialist temptation: treating one field's category as the \enquote{real} one and others as derivative. If proper names are semantically fundamental, proper nouns must be \enquote{just} the syntactic reflection of semantic reality. But the mechanisms are different; neither is derivative. Second, the nominalist temptation: treating categories as arbitrary conventions because different fields carve differently. But the carving isn't arbitrary; each field's categories are maintained by mechanisms appropriate to that field's explanatory goals.


\section{The epistemic payoff}
\label{sec:7:epistemic-payoff}

The aspect case shows that even a descriptively real category can fail to project if its definition misaligns with its maintaining mechanisms. The same problem underlies a familiar question in the philosophy of science.

Liu Cixin's turkey parable states it narratively:

\begin{quote}
Every morning on a turkey farm, the farmer comes to feed the turkeys. A scientist turkey, having observed this pattern to hold without change for almost a year, makes the following discovery: \enquote{Every morning at eleven, food arrives.} On the morning of Thanksgiving, the scientist announces this law to the other turkeys. \ixnq{Liu, Cixin}\citep[ch.~6]{liu2008}
\end{quote}

The turkey's law is perfectly confirmed by all available evidence. The problem lies in its grounding: the law isn't tied to the mechanism that produces the feeding pattern. The farmer's purpose~-- invisible to the turkey~-- determines when the correlation breaks. Had the turkey understood \emph{why} food arrives (fattening for slaughter), it would've predicted its own demise rather than its next meal.

Goodman's \mention{grue}\ixlq{grue} problem\ixsq{grue problem} states the same structure more abstractly. If every emerald observed before time $t$ is both green and grue, why project \mention{green}\ixlq{green} rather than \mention{grue}\ixlq{grue}? Goodman's own answer invoked entrenchment\ixsq{entrenchment!defined}: \mention{green}\ixlq{green} is projectible because it's been projected successfully in the past, while \mention{grue}\ixlq{grue} isn't. The circularity is deliberate~-- projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!Goodman's problem} is bootstrapped from track record.

That problem shaped much of the later discussion. \ixnq{Quine, Willard Van Orman}\textcite{quine1969} argued that the success of induction presupposes categories: we project properties because we assume the instances we've observed are \enquote{of a kind\ixsq{kind!natural}} with those we haven't. \ixnq{Kornblith, Hilary}\textcite{kornblith1993} went further, arguing that induction works because categories exist in the world~-- the clustering of properties in those categories underwrites our ability to learn from experience.

The maintenance view\ixsq{maintenance view} breaks the circle. A habit\ixsq{habit} isn't self-grounding; it is sustained by something. The stream's habit\ixsq{habit} of flowing in a particular channel persists not because it has flowed there before but because water, gravity, and geology maintain it. \term{Green} is projectible not because speakers have projected it before but because mechanisms sustain the would-be\ixsq{would-be}: chromium traces interact with light in stable ways, and a visual system tuned by selection detects the reflectance. Nothing comparable sustains grueness. The term changes its prediction at time $t$, but no mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!defined} in emeralds changes with it. Projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!Goodman's problem} tracks mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!vs.\ essences}, not predicational habit\ixsq{habit}.

For linguistic categories, the same logic applies. \term{Noun\ixsq{noun!as category}} is projectible because mechanisms like acquisition\ixsq{acquisition}, entrenchment\ixsq{entrenchment!frequency effects}, and functional pressure keep nominal properties clustering together. A pseudo-category like \mention{nerboun}\ixlq{nerboun} (noun if acquired before age 5, verb otherwise) isn't projectible because no mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!defined} produces that pattern. A category can correlate with usage and still fail as a guide to induction if its characterization misses the mechanisms that maintain it.

\subsection{Is projectibility interest-relative?}
\label{sec:7:interest-relativity}

\ixnq{Craver, Carl F.}\textcite{craver2009} and \ixnq{Onishi, Yukinori}\ixnq{Serpico, Davide}\textcite{onishi2022} argue that any mechanism-based account inherits context-dependence. Which mechanism you attend to, at what level of abstraction, with what boundaries~-- all depend on your explanatory goals. If so, the maintenance view\ixsq{maintenance view} answer to Goodman looks circular: a category is projectible relative to the mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!defined} you're interested in, and the mechanism you're interested in is whichever one makes the category projectible.

The key distinction is between choosing a level because it answers your question, and choosing a level because it privileges your category. A pedicab can be explained by engineering (frame geometry, gear ratios) or by physics (forces, friction, momentum). Both levels are real; both describe genuine causal structure. Your question selects the level. But the causal structure at that level determines whether your answer is correct. Interest picks the question; it doesn't fabricate the mechanism that answers it.

This is precisely what went wrong with the turkey. Its interest was survival, but it coarse-grained at the wrong level: time of day rather than time of year. The correlation was real at one level (11 AM $\rightarrow$ food) but collapsed at another (Thanksgiving $\rightarrow$ slaughter). The turkey's interest didn't invent the mechanisms; it just selected the wrong one to track.

For linguistic categories, the same logic applies. If your question is \enquote{what triggers perfective here?} the answer lives at the cue-structure level, not the semantic-definition level. Both levels exist; the semantic level just doesn't answer the production question. The aspect case shows the stakes: if your goal is to predict corpus distributions, or even to teach people to speak Polish\ixgq{Polish}, the textbook mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!vs.\ essences} (semantic boundedness\ixsq{boundedness}) is the wrong one~-- it predicts imperfective well and perfective\ixsq{perfective} poorly. If your goal is to predict native-speaker gap-filling, the lemma-concrete mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!entrenchment} is the right one~-- it outperforms aspect-aware models. The prediction either succeeds or fails. That's the empirical constraint on which mechanism matters.

This is where the maintenance view\ixsq{maintenance view} improves on conventionalism. Interest selects among real causal structures; it doesn't invent them. Some coarse-grainings preserve prediction because they track the mechanisms that maintain the pattern, and others don't. That is the kind of emergence \ixnq{Carroll, Sean M.}\ixnq{Parola, Achyuth}\textcite{carrollparola2024} describe: descriptions that remain predictive because they discard the right information rather than arbitrary information.

\ixnq{Lemeire, Olivier}\textcite{lemeire2018} argues that no purely epistemic theory~-- one that defines categories solely by their inductive usefulness~-- can account for naturalness. The objection is serious because projectibility alone is too permissive. What's needed is the right \emph{sort} of projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!vs.\ convenience}~-- the sort that comes from genuine homeostatic mechanisms rather than accidental correlations. The turkey's law projected until Thanksgiving; a mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!defined}-grounded law would've predicted the farmer's purpose. On this view, projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!induction from} is evidence of mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!defined}, not a replacement for it.\footnote{\ixnq{Khalidi, Muhammad Ali}\citeauthor{khalidi2013}'s (\citeyear{khalidi2013}) \enquote{nodes in causal networks} account illustrates the difference. NPIs, for instance, are causally grounded in one sense~-- they share semantic sensitivity to negative contexts. What they lack is the homeostatic mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!defined} that would make them an HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!vs.\ simple causal theory} category\ixsq{kind!vs.\ class}. They're stable at equilibrium rather than dynamically maintained: balls sitting at the bottom of a trough, not tops that must be spun to stay upright.}

This also suggests a robustness criterion for mechanistic categories. A genuine mechanistic category\ixsq{kind!defined}~-- as opposed to a convenient label~-- should exhibit \emph{learning transfer}, \emph{intervention stability}, and \emph{cross-context generalization}. Train on one subset and test on another: does it transfer? Intervene on the mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!perturbation of}: does the pattern shift as predicted? Apply the generalization in a new context: does it hold? Labels without mechanisms should fragment under these pressures; mechanism-maintained categories should persist. The computational evidence in this chapter~-- lemma-concrete models transferring to unseen verbs, cue--outcome associations generalizing across tense frames~-- is one version of that robustness test.

Once interests are fixed by a task, the next question is how strongly different categories support that task. This is the next implication of mechanistic grounding: projectibility comes in degrees. (Recall that field-relative projectibility\ixsq{field-relative projectibility}~-- §\ref{sec:7:field-relative}~-- is a distinct point: the same extension can be carved by different HPCs for different fields. What follows concerns degrees of projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!induction from} \emph{within} a single field.)

\subsection{Degrees of projectibility}
\ixsq{projectibility!induction from}
\label{sec:7:degrees-projectibility}

Categories aren't equally projectible. The framework expects a gradient, and the evidence supports it.

At one end of that gradient are high-frequency, highly entrenched categories whose mechanisms are strong and consistent. English determinatives are the obvious case: a closed class, stable across speakers, predictable in distribution. Learn \mention{each}\ixlq{each} and you can project to \mention{every}\ixlq{every} with high confidence. The mechanisms (entrenchment\ixsq{entrenchment!frequency effects}, functional specificity) are tight enough that the category approaches definitional coherence.

At the other end are low-frequency, loosely maintained classes whose mechanisms are variable or in flux. Nonce formations, idiolectal forms, and constructions undergoing change are less projectible because the mechanisms haven't stabilized. Knowing about one instance tells you less about others.

The middle ground is where the action is. Within a language, synchronically, major categories~-- such as nouns, verbs, and adjectives in English\ixgq{English}~-- are projectible on average but variable at the margins. You can project from typical English\ixgq{English} nouns to novel English\ixgq{English} nouns with high confidence; you can project from \mention{fun}\ixlq{fun} to other English\ixgq{English} adjective-noun boundary cases with less. The mechanisms are strong enough to produce robust clustering at the core but not strong enough to determine the periphery. The gradient extends cross-linguistically and diachronically. Because the stabilizing mechanisms partially overlap, you can project from English\ixgq{English} adjectives to German\ixgq{German} adjectives with moderate confidence; you can even project, if you're cautious, to the Japanese\ixgq{Japanese} words like 大きい (\mention{ookii}\ixlgqs{ookii}{ookii}{Japanese}{big} `big') and 寒い (\mention{samui}\ixlgqs{samui}{samui}{Japanese}{cold} `cold'). Similarly, you can project from Modern English\ixgq{English!Modern} nouns backward to Old English\ixgq{English!Old} nouns, with confidence decaying as the time depth increases. The predictive power doesn't vanish at the border of a language or an era; it diminishes precisely as the stabilizing mechanisms diverge.

The prediction is testable: core category\ixsq{category!membership graded} members~-- high-frequency, prototypical, functionally central~-- should elicit consistent judgments; peripheral members~-- low-frequency, boundary-straddling, functionally ambiguous~-- should elicit variable ones. That variance reflects weak entrenchment\ixsq{entrenchment!frequency effects}.

Recent experimental work confirms the pattern. In grammaticality judgments for Dutch\ixgq{Dutch} syntax, peripheral structures are correctly identified only 57\% of the time (SD $\approx$ 0.27), while core structures are correctly identified 90\% of the time (SD $\approx$ 0.09)~-- the variance triples at the periphery \ixnq{Favier, Sybren}\ixnq{Huettig, Falk}\citep[Table~2]{favier2021}. Similarly for syntactic variants indexed to verb frequency: low-frequency verbs elicit higher acceptance of violations (a fourfold increase in error rates: 6.58\% vs.\ 1.58\%), because weak entrenchment\ixsq{entrenchment!frequency effects} permits wider variation in what speakers treat as grammatical \ixnq{Sassenhagen, Jona}\ixnq{Blything, Rob}\ixnq{Lieven, Elena V. M.}\ixnq{Ambridge, Ben}\citep{sassenhagen2018}. In morphology, the pattern is starker still: high-frequency English\ixgq{English} adjectives elicit near-unanimous agreement on comparative form, while low-frequency and nonce English\ixgq{English} adjectives split speakers \ixnq{Graziano-King, Janet}\ixnq{Cairns, Helen Smith}\citep{grazianoking2005}.

This is what the maintenance view\ixsq{maintenance view} predicts. Where mechanisms are strong and consistent, judgments converge; where mechanisms are weak or variable, judgments diverge. The variance tracks the strength of the causal structure. Projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!induction from} comes in degrees, graded by entrenchment\ixsq{entrenchment!frequency effects}. Speaker-by-speaker experience also matters. Low-frequency forms are \enquote{weakly entrenched} only in aggregate~-- for any given form, some speakers have encountered it often, others rarely, others never. What looks like category\ixsq{category!boundary cases} fuzziness at the population level is actually speaker-by-speaker variation in experience \ixnq{Dabrowska, Ewa}\citep{dabrowska2012}. This reinforces the maintenance account: categories fail to project uniformly because the maintaining mechanisms haven't operated uniformly across the population.


\subsection{What the framework offers}
\label{sec:7:what-framework-offers}

The argument so far has been negative: traditional definitions don't ground projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!vs.\ convenience}; mechanisms do. But that raises a fair question for researchers who already study mechanisms. If entrenchment, distributional learning, alignment, and functional pressure are already on the table, what does the maintenance view\ixsq{maintenance view} add?

The main addition is to connect mechanism to projectibility and projectibility to ontology. Mechanistic work already shows how patterns emerge, stabilize, and change. What it often leaves implicit is what those patterns let us infer, and for which purposes. The maintenance view\ixsq{maintenance view} makes that explicit. Where consistent mechanisms sustain a pattern across speakers and contexts, and where that pattern supports the inferences a task requires, the category has a claim to reality for those purposes. Operationally, the evidence lies in population-level coordination over time: whether the pattern stabilizes or recovers under perturbation, and whether the resulting cluster supports successful projection.

It also clarifies how different research programs fit together. Work on entrenchment, alignment, distributional learning, and iterated transmission can be read as different approaches to the same empirical question: what stabilizes a pattern strongly enough that it projects? Framed that way, prediction comes to the foreground, and individual variation becomes evidence about where projection is strong, weak, or purpose-limited rather than noise to be averaged away. Section~\ref{sec:7:simulation-interlude} showed one computational route for testing such predictions, and Chapter~\ref{ch:what-changes} turns that logic into explicit defeat conditions.

The same framing also gives realism without essentialism\ixsq{essentialism} and separates historical origin from current maintenance. What built a category may differ from what now lets it project, just as the volcanic-island metaphor suggests. That matters for grammaticalization, semantic drift, and language change more generally: genealogy matters, but current projectibility for current tasks depends on current mechanisms. None of this requires abandoning existing research programs. It changes their framing by treating mechanistic work as part of explaining what a category is good for, not just how it happened to arise.

This returns to the question that opened the chapter. Mr.~Tagomi needed to know which world he was in, and learners of Polish\ixgq{Polish} need to know which verb form to use. Both rely on projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!induction from}. Learning about one instance licenses projection to others because the same mechanisms that shaped the instance you learned are also shaping the instances you haven't yet encountered. Categories maintained by consistent mechanisms produce consistent members, and that consistency underwrites induction.

That's the epistemic payoff. HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!mechanisms} categories are categories you can learn from because causal structure keeps their members similar in the respects that matter for the task. The mechanism that makes a word a noun also helps make other nouns; the mechanism that biases a verb toward perfective also biases similar verbs, and the mechanism that entrains a speaker to one usage pattern entrains related ones. The guarantee isn't automatic, though. A grouping can fail because its characterization misses the maintaining mechanisms~-- as aspect-abstract fails~-- or because the mechanisms are too weak or too variable~-- as nonce formations fail. When that happens, what remains is a \term{class\ixsq{class}} rather than a category\ixsq{category!vs.\ class}: a label without enough causal structure behind it.


The volcanic-island lesson cuts both ways. A label can outlast the mechanism that first made it explanatory, and categories can also fail to be maintained~-- or be maintained too loosely, or in ways that don't project.

The next chapter asks how to recognize those failures: when what is being tracked is a label or a short-lived fashion rather than a mechanism-maintained cluster, what failure looks like, and what it teaches us about the categories that succeed.


Part~III puts the same diagnostics to work across four core case studies plus a modality stress test. Countability\ixsq{countability}, definiteness, word classes, and pro-form gender each reveal the same pressure point: inherited labels that decompose into semantic and morphosyntactic HPCs, maintained by distinct mechanisms and projectible for different analytical purposes. Chapter~\ref{ch:sign-anaphor} then presses the same diagnostics in sign-language discourse reference. What follows, then, isn't philosophy hovering above the cases but a working set of tools for explaining why familiar categories hold together, come apart, and matter.
