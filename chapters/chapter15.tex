\chapter{Grammaticality itself}
\label{ch:grammaticality-itself}
\epigraph{it's impossible to get rid of the illusion in spite of our better knowledge.}{~-- Hermann von Helmholtz, \textit{Treatise on Physiological Optics} (trans. J. P. C. Southall).}

The asterisk is the most common symbol in syntax, and one of the least likely to come with operating instructions. But what does the asterisk diagnose?

Chapter~\ref{ch:what-we-havent-been-asking} asked a question almost no one asks: is grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} itself a category\ixsq{kind!linguistic kinds as}? Not whether this sentence is grammatical, but what kind of thing we're probing when we ask. If linguistic categories are homeostatic property clusters, then grammaticality itself is an HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!grammaticality}~-- a projectible, maintained coupling between form and value\ixsq{value}. And the feeling of ungrammaticality\ixsq{feeling of ungrammaticality} that guides our judgments is not an oracle; it's a noisy detector\ixsq{detector}, shaped by entrenchment and processing ease, capable of illusions.

Chapter~\ref{ch:dynamic-discreteness} introduced a two-layer picture: discrete grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} filtered through processing noise to yield gradient acceptability\ixsq{acceptability}. What this chapter adds is the claim that grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} is a full HPC in the book's sense, and that the feeling of ungrammaticality is the stabilizer that maintains it. The sections that follow develop the coupling, identify the detector's characteristic failure modes, and test the account against empirical evidence.


\section{The HPC claim}
\label{sec:14:hpc-claim}

Grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} is one form--value\ixsq{value} pairing among many. Most often, the \enquote{value\ixsq{value}} side is a kind of meaning~-- but not compositional semantics, not truth conditions, not the full propositional content. At the grain that matters here, it is usually \term{structural meaning\ixsq{structural meaning}}; later sections will note cases where the value side is not meaning at all.

\term{Structural meaning\ixsq{structural meaning}} is what grammar\ixsq{grammar} contributes before lexical content fills the slots. It includes dependency relations, argument structure, tense--aspect anchoring, clause type, and information structure\ixsq{information structure}. It is \term{subcompositional\ixsq{subcompositional meaning}}: \mention{Colorless green ideas sleep furiously} has impeccable structural meaning\ixsq{structural meaning} and absurd lexical content, which is why it feels strange but not \term{ungrammatical\ixsq{ungrammatical}}. Structural meaning\ixsq{structural meaning} is therefore not semantic coherence, contextual felicity, or whatever the hearer infers overall. It is specifically the morphosyntactic contribution to interpretation.

The HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!grammaticality} claim, then, is that grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} is, prototypically, the maintained, projectible coupling between form and grammatical value\ixsq{value} at the relevant grain. One such pairing, once mastered, extends to close analogues and novel instances. This is the \term{zipper\ixsq{zipper}}: form provides one set of teeth, grammatical value\ixsq{value} the other. Ungrammaticality\ixsq{ungrammatical} is what it feels like when the zipper\ixsq{zipper} won't close; a grammatical\ixsq{grammaticality} sentence is one that goes \mention{zip}. The rest of this chapter asks what the zipper metaphor actually commits us to~-- what it predicts, how the detector works, and where it breaks down.


\section{What grammaticality lets you do}
\label{sec:14:projectibility}

None of what follows will surprise a working linguist. But spelling it out matters, because the obvious cases are exactly the evidence that grammaticality projects. If you know a form--value\ixsq{value} pairing is grammatical\ixsq{grammaticality}, you can do something with it. This is the inductive licence that makes grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} a category in the book's sense, not just a property of individual sentences. To use the vocabulary of Chapter~\ref{ch:stabilizers}, grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} sustains a good bet\ixsq{good bet}: encounter this pattern and you can trust it in new territory.

The bets are concrete. A speaker who has mastered the double-object construction\ixsq{construction} (\mention{gave her the book}\ixlq{give}) deploys it with verbs never heard in that frame: \mention{texted her the address}, \mention{Slacked him the link}. The would-be\ixsq{would-be} is: encounter this form and you \emph{would} get transfer plus affected recipient. The same projection drives analogical extension\ixsq{analogy}~-- the progressive-aspect coupling (\mention{I'm writing}) extends to \mention{I'm Googling}, \mention{I'm Zooming} as new verbs enter the language. In both cases, new lexical content slots into an existing form--value\ixsq{value} pairing. The speaker hasn't memorized these sentences; she's projected from the coupling.

Repair provides a third line of evidence. You can't fix the wiring unless you know what the wiring should deliver~-- and every correction presupposes projection\ixsq{projectibility!induction from} from the known coupling. A speaker reaching for \mention{adaptiveability} catches the error mid-word and self-corrects to \mention{adaptability}\ixlq{adaptability}, because the would-be\ixsq{would-be} fires before production completes. Post-hoc correction works the same way: both parties share the would-be\ixsq{would-be}. But these projections aren't all equally strong. Tight couplings project further: subject--verb agreement extends across novel combinations. Loose couplings project less: island constraints\ixsq{island constraint} show construction\ixsq{construction}-specific limits, and speakers disagree about the edges.

Compare this to mere acceptability\ixsq{acceptability}. Acceptability tells you how the detector\ixsq{detector} responded to one token on one occasion. Grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} tells you what to expect across tokens~-- across speakers, across novel instances, across contexts. The category projects; the detector\ixsq{detector} reading doesn't. §\ref{sec:14:value-every-grain} will show this coupling operating at every level of the grammar\ixsq{grammar}, from phonemic contrast to clause type.


\section{The detector}
\ixsq{stabilizer!defined}
\label{sec:14:detector}

The proximate \term{stabilizer\ixsq{stabilizer!as mechanism role}}~-- the thing that corrects deviations in real time~-- is a \term{feeling of ungrammaticality\ixsq{feeling of ungrammaticality}}. When something's wrong with the form--value\ixsq{value} coupling, speakers notice. They hesitate, repair, rephrase. But the feeling is a \term{detector\ixsq{detector}}, not an oracle~-- and like all detectors, it's noisy. It's not a direct readout of the underlying structure. Instead, it's informed by probability: a novel sentence is weighted by its likelihood under the distribution of attested forms~-- not just distance from the nearest exemplar, but probability given the entire pattern. Low-probability forms feel suspect even when grammatically licensed. The distribution itself is built by entrenchment\ixsq{entrenchment!frequency effects}: exposure tunes the prior. This is why \term{acceptability\ixsq{acceptability}} and \term{grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality}} come apart. Acceptability\ixsq{acceptability} is what the detector\ixsq{detector} reports. Grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} is the category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind}~-- the HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!grammaticality} whose core is the form--value\ixsq{value} coupling. The two usually align, but they don't always.

Coupling shapes processing; processing shapes detector\ixsq{detector} output; detector\ixsq{detector} output crosses threshold; threshold triggers repair; repair reshapes coupling. To keep this concrete, we can write the loop with five moving parts:
\begin{quote}
\textbf{Mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!defined} sketch (detector\ixsq{detector} and maintenance loop).}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Coupling state}: how strongly a construction\ixsq{construction} currently links morphosyntactic form to structural meaning\ixsq{structural meaning} for a speaker in a community.
    \item \textbf{Processing channel}: token-time load~-- surprisal, memory pressure, reanalysis cost.
    \item \textbf{Detector\ixsq{detector} output}: a graded mismatch signal combining coupling state and processing load, plus noise.
    \item \textbf{Decision threshold}: above some level, speakers report \enquote{ungrammatical\ixsq{ungrammatical}} and/or initiate repair.
    \item \textbf{Entrenchment\ixsq{entrenchment!defined} update}: exposure and correction histories move both coupling strength and threshold over time.
\end{enumerate}
This keeps the levels separate: token-time parsing (\textasciitilde100 ms--seconds), individual entrenchment (days--years), and community norm dynamics (years--generations). The loop's function is to keep the coupling in a state that supports projection: perturb it, and the system restores not just the co-occurrence but the inferential licence it underwrites.
\end{quote}

This sketch commits us to an identification strategy. If processing load rises while coupling is stable, we should see transient detector\ixsq{detector} spikes without durable shifts in repair norms. If coupling weakens at matched load, we should see durable shifts in repair preferences and cohort trajectories. If thresholds move while coupling remains stable, ratings should move before production and repair distributions do. So ratings alone can't settle the ontology: the evidential standard is convergence across online processing, repair behavior, and community-distributional data (Chapters~\ref{ch:failure-modes} and~\ref{ch:what-changes}).

But here's the puzzle: if grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} \emph{is} the maintained coupling, and entrenchment\ixsq{entrenchment!resistance to change} is what maintains it, then \enquote{tuning the detector\ixsq{detector}} and \enquote{shaping what counts as grammatical} can't be fully separated over historical time. At a single time-slice, they \emph{are} separable~-- detector\ixsq{detector} output can misfire against a currently entrenched state, as illusions show. Across time, though, detector\ixsq{detector}-guided repair and uptake reshape the coupling itself. The coupling tunes the detector\ixsq{detector}; the detector\ixsq{detector}, over time, tunes the coupling. That's not a vicious circle; it's a reflexive equilibrium, and the age-expression data show what it looks like in practice.

Consider age expressions: in French\ixgq{French} and Spanish\ixgq{Spanish}, age is expressed with \mention{avoir}\ixlgqs{avoir}{avoir}{French}{have}/\mention{tener}\ixlgqs{tener}{tener}{Spanish}{have}: \mention{J'ai vingt ans} (\enquote{I have twenty years}). In English\ixgq{English}, \ungram\mention{I have twenty years} is ungrammatical\ixsq{ungrammatical} as a statement of age~-- though perfectly fine as a statement of time (until retirement, on the job, etc.). The structural slot is the same; the construction\ixsq{construction}-specific entrenchment\ixsq{entrenchment!frequency effects} differs. The coupling is real; its contents are conventional.

We couldn't correct grammatical errors if we couldn't derive the intended meaning without the grammar. When someone says \ungram\mention{I have twenty years} for their age, we understand what they mean~-- and then we notice the mismatch. The feeling of ungrammaticality\ixsq{feeling of ungrammaticality} arises from comparison: the grammar's value\ixsq{value} versus the intended value\ixsq{value}. We need both to detect the error. Comprehension is partly independent of grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality}~-- and grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} is partly a matter of checking the grammar's signal against other inference channels.

\subsection{Illusions}
\ixsq{grammaticality illusion}
\label{sec:14:illusions}

The strongest evidence that the feeling is dissociable from the structure comes from \term{grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} illusions}~-- cases where the detector\ixsq{detector} misfires. The classic case is the garden-path sentence\ixsq{garden-path sentence}:

\ea[]{\label{ex:14:garden-path}
\mention{The horse raced past the barn fell.}\ixlq{horse}}
\z

This is grammatical~-- a reduced relative clause (\mention{the horse [that was] raced past the barn fell}\ixlq{horse}). But the processing system commits to the main-clause parse (\mention{the horse raced past the barn}\ixlq{horse}) and crashes when \mention{fell}\ixlq{fell} appears. The detector\ixsq{detector} reports ungrammaticality; the structure is fine.

The converse is the \term{comparative illusion\ixsq{comparative illusion}}~-- sometimes called the \term{Escher sentence\ixsq{Escher sentence}}:

\ea[]{\label{ex:14:comparative-illusion}
\mention{More people have been to Russia than I have.}\ixlq{people}}
\z

Each local chunk parses. \mention{More people have been to Russia}\ixlq{people}~-- fine. \mention{Than I have}~-- fine as a comparative clause. But the whole doesn't compose cleanly: what's being compared? For many speakers, the sentence lacks a stable global interpretation, but it feels acceptable because each piece satisfies local constraints.

These illusions are exactly analogous to visual illusions. The Müller-Lyer lines are the same length, but the perceptual system reports otherwise. The illusion doesn't show perception is broken; it shows perception is a mechanism with characteristic failure modes~-- detector, not oracle. \ixnq{Phillips, Colin}\ixnq{Wagers, Matthew W.}\ixnq{Lau, Ellen F.}\textcite{phillips2011grammatical} develop the point systematically, showing comprehension exhibits \term{selective fallibility\ixsq{selective fallibility}}~-- impressive accuracy for some constraints, systematic vulnerability for others. The pattern reveals the architecture: a retrieval system that's structure-sensitive when structural cues have temporal priority, and error-prone when they don't.


\section{The phenomenology of coupling}
\ixsq{phenomenology}
\label{sec:14:phenomenology}

If the detector has characteristic failure modes, it should also have characteristic registers~-- layers that dissociate in predictable ways. Three are visible in ordinary grammatical experience.

\subsection{Three registers}
\label{sec:14:three-registers}

The core phenomenological datum is pre-reflective disruption. Around that core, two downstream layers matter for behavior and stabilization. Together, these three registers map onto the triadic architecture of §\ref{sec:13:mediation}. In Peircean terms, they align with emotional, energetic, and logical interpretants, respectively.

First, a qualitative jolt: a pre-reflective sense that something's off, the way a sour note feels sour before you know which note it is. This is the sign-vehicle doing its work~-- the perceivable form registers as wrong before you can say why.

Second, reactive resistance: the hesitation, the repair, the sense that you \emph{can't} say it that way. This is the object\ixsq{object} pushing back~-- the brute constraint on what the form can mean. The felt result is blockage, not mere surprise.

Third, normative correction: the stabilized habit\ixsq{habit} that makes the correction count as a correction. The fact that \mention{I'm twenty years old} is the right repair for \ungram\mention{I have twenty years} is not arbitrary; it's the interpretant\ixsq{interpretant}~-- the would-be\ixsq{would-be}~-- reasserting itself. Encounter this construction and you \emph{would} get this structural meaning\ixsq{structural meaning}. When the would-be\ixsq{would-be} misfires, the habit\ixsq{habit} demands its due. The correction counts as a correction because both speaker and hearer are projecting from a shared form--value\ixsq{value} coupling (§\ref{sec:14:projectibility}).

When the coupling fails, these registers often co-activate, but they needn't. The readout can be partial and dissociable: jolt without stable correction, correction without strong jolt, or persistent blockage with weak explicit norm talk.

This triadic mapping predicts a specific dissociation already visible in the illusion literature (Table~\ref{tab:14:three-register-operational}): a sentence can trigger a jolt without a structural violation (garden paths), or pass local fluency checks without global well-formedness (comparative illusions). If the three-register readout is right, we should expect patterned misfires rather than random noise.

Peirce called the felt disequilibrium that forces inquiry \enquote{doubt}~-- not sceptical doubt, but an irritation that demands repair \ixnq{Peirce, Charles Sanders}\citep[CP~5.374]{peirce1931cp}. Grammatical doubt works the same way. The would-be\ixsq{would-be} has misfired; the habit\ixsq{habit} is unsatisfied; the feeling is what unsatisfied habits feel like from the inside. It forces repair not because speakers follow rules consciously, but because the system won't settle until the coupling closes. That specificity~-- morphosyntactic coupling, not general appropriateness~-- is what the next section develops.


\section{What doesn't count}
\label{sec:14:negative-space}

The form--value\ixsq{value} coupling is specifically morphosyntactic, and this predicts what won't trigger ungrammaticality. A phonetic error, like \mention{[\ipa{fɪlɔzəfi}]} for \mention{philosophy}\ixlq{philosophy}, can misfire without being ungrammatical\ixsq{ungrammatical}. A lexical error, like saying \mention{airport}\ixlq{airport} when you meant \mention{train station}, can misfire without being ungrammatical\ixsq{ungrammatical}. A semantic anomaly, like \mention{Colorless green ideas sleep furiously}, can misfire without being ungrammatical\ixsq{ungrammatical}.

These violations trigger different responses. Phonetic errors trigger \mention{what?} because the phoneme\ixsq{phoneme}'s value\ixsq{value} is the contrast\ixsq{contrast}~-- that's the entire form--value\ixsq{value} coupling at that level, and when it breaks, comprehension stalls. Lexical errors trigger correction or confusion. Semantic anomaly triggers puzzlement. But none of them trigger the specific feeling of ungrammaticality\ixsq{feeling of ungrammaticality}~-- the sense the wiring is broken.

The allomorphy of the indefinite article makes the point cleanly. English speakers reject \ungram{*a apple} and \ungram{*an banana} with the same jolt they bring to syntactic violations. This looks phonological, but the selection rule is entrenched as a grammatical requirement~-- part of the morphosyntactic zipper\ixsq{zipper}. A mispronunciation of \mention{banana}\ixlq{banana} would trigger \mention{what?}; distributing the wrong allomorph triggers the mismatch signal. The edge case reveals the boundary of the morphosyntactic coupling\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!membership}, and the feeling of ungrammaticality\ixsq{feeling of ungrammaticality}~-- if somewhat muted~-- is the detector\ixsq{detector} at work.

At the sub-word levels, value\ixsq{value} reduces to its simplest forms. For phonemes, it is nothing \emph{but} contrast\ixsq{contrast}: the /p/ in \mention{pat}\ixlq{pat} contributes no semantic content but distinguishes the word from \mention{bat}\ixlq{bat}, \mention{cat}\ixlq{cat}, \mention{sat}\ixlq{sat}. When contrast\ixsq{contrast} fails~-- when you can't tell which phoneme\ixsq{phoneme} was intended~-- you say \mention{what?} For morphemes, value\ixsq{value} is the contribution to word meaning or grammatical function: the \mention{-ed}\ixlq{-ed} in \mention{walked}\ixlq{walked} signals past tense; the \mention{un-}\ixlq{un-} in \mention{unhappy}\ixlq{unhappy} reverses polarity. Break the coupling~-- use \mention{-ed}\ixlq{-ed} on a noun, or \mention{un-}\ixlq{un-} on a verb\ixsq{verb!distributional profile} that doesn't license it~-- and ungrammaticality results.

At the word level, value\ixsq{value} is lexical meaning plus category\ixsq{category!membership graded} membership: \mention{dog}\ixlq{dog} contributes dog-ness \emph{and} nounhood\ixsq{nounhood}, so using a word in the wrong category slot produces ungrammaticality rather than mere confusion. At the constructional level, value\ixsq{value} is the structural meaning\ixsq{structural meaning} the pattern contributes~-- the double-object construction\ixsq{construction} (\mention{gave her the book}) signals transfer plus affected recipient; the \term{passive} (\mention{was eaten}) demotes the agent~-- and breaking the construction\ixsq{construction}'s slot-filling requirements is what stops the zipper\ixsq{zipper} from closing. At the clause-type level, value\ixsq{value} is illocutionary force: \term{declarative}\ixsq{declarative} structure signals assertion; \term{interrogative} structure signals question; \term{imperative}\ixsq{imperative} structure signals command. Mismatch the structure and the force~-- use rising intonation on a declarative to fake a question~-- and you're exploiting the zipper\ixsq{zipper}, not breaking it.

This table helps explain why grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} is primarily diagnosed at the morphosyntactic grain~-- the level where form--value\ixsq{value} couplings are tight, obligatory, and enforced.

The tightness reflects what morphosyntactic contrasts are like. They're predominantly discrete~-- maintained that way by paradigmatic acquisition, agreement systems, and deep entrenchment~-- so violations register as kind-mismatches, sharp and salient. Where morphosyntactic contrasts are less entrenched, the signal is correspondingly gradient: island violations and argument-structure alternations produce graded acceptability, not the crisp rejection that subject--verb agreement violations do. And phonological violations can be just as sharp when comparably entrenched: try starting an English word with /\ipa{ŋ}/, a gap maintained across every token in the language. The sharpness tracks the maintenance profile, not the domain. Gradient judgments aren't noise to be smoothed away; they're signal~-- evidence of the coupling under strain.

Phoneticians have their own coupling (\term{contrast\ixsq{contrast}}), and when it breaks, the response is \mention{what?}, not ungrammaticality. Semanticists have theirs (\term{compositionality}, \term{inference}), and when it breaks, the response is puzzlement or absurdity. Grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} names the failure mode where the morphosyntactic zipper\ixsq{zipper} won't close. That's why syntax gets first pass on it in this explanatory frame, not sole authority over all appropriateness\ixsq{appropriateness} judgments.

\subsection{Grammaticality as a species of appropriateness}
\ixsq{grammaticality}\ixsq{appropriateness}
\label{sec:14:appropriateness}

This grain-specific view suggests a larger picture. Grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} is not sui generis; it's a \emph{species} of appropriateness\ixsq{appropriateness}, narrowed to the morphosyntactic channel.

\term{Appropriateness} in general is the coupling between a form and \emph{all} the values it signals: structural meaning, social indexicality, register stance, identity marking. Grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} is what you get when you restrict attention to one channel~-- morphosyntactic form and structural meaning\ixsq{structural meaning}. But morphosyntax is not the only grain at which form--value couplings are maintained and enforced (Table~\ref{tab:appropriateness-channels}).

Operationally, a failure counts as grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} only if two tests pass. First, the minimal repair that preserves intended message requires a morphosyntactic change, not just lexical substitution, accent shift, or register adjustment. Second, speakers in the relevant discourse community\ixsq{discourse community} show convergent repair preferences across contexts. If either test fails, classify the case under another appropriateness\ixsq{appropriateness} channel.

Each channel has its own detector\ixsq{detector}, its own failure mode, and its own phenomenology. Grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} failure triggers hesitation, repair, the jolt of \enquote{wrongness}. Register\ixsq{register} mismatch triggers a different response: the raised eyebrow, the red ink on contractions, the sense that something's \enquote{off} without being \emph{ungrammatical\ixsq{ungrammatical}}. Accent mismatch triggers identity attribution: \enquote{that's not how we say it here.} Lexical inappropriateness triggers precision failure: \enquote{that's not quite the right word.}

The channels aren't orthogonal. A single utterance is evaluated simultaneously on all of them. And they interact: a register\ixsq{register}-inappropriate form can \emph{feel} ungrammatical\ixsq{ungrammatical} (through prescriptive training or hypercorrection) even when the morphosyntax is impeccable. But they're semi-independent: you can have perfect grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} with terrible register\ixsq{register} fit (\mention{I would like to utilize the restroom facilities} is grammatical but register\ixsq{register}-weird in casual speech), and native speakers can have strong grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} intuitions with weak register\ixsq{register} intuitions in unfamiliar domains.

This is where the entrenchment story connects to the social: entrenchment is always entrenchment \enquote{within a discourse community\ixsq{discourse community}}. The grammar\ixsq{grammar} isn't floating free; it's tethered to the people who use it. When the community shifts~-- through contact, migration, register change~-- the entrenchment shifts with it. What feels ungrammatical\ixsq{ungrammatical} in one community may be unremarkable in another. And speakers flow between communities~-- sometimes instantaneously. \mention{Do you lift?} licenses intransitive \mention{lift}\ixlq{lift} for weightlifters but not for warehouse workers; the grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} of the construction depends on which community you're invoking, and you can switch in mid-conversation.

Code-switching within a clause~-- \mention{Vamos a hacer shopping}~-- is governed grammar, not error, but only within the discourse community of the bilinguals who maintain it. The detector\ixsq{detector} is not calibrated once; it's calibrated to whoever you're talking to, right now.

Chapter~\ref{ch:social-stabilization} develops this picture further, showing how dialect\ixsq{dialect}, register\ixsq{register}, and discourse community\ixsq{discourse community} act as conditioning variables that select which appropriateness\ixsq{appropriateness} couplings apply in a given context.

The upshot is that grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} is appropriateness\ixsq{appropriateness} at the morphosyntactic grain. The asterisk probes one detector\ixsq{detector}; the raised eyebrow probes another. Both are form--value couplings maintained by social mechanisms, and both can trigger repair~-- but only the morphosyntactic coupling plays the dual role that the next section describes.


\section{Grammaticality as mechanism and category}
\ixsq{grammaticality}\ixsq{mechanism!vs.\ essences}\ixsq{category!as HPC kind}
\label{sec:14:dual-role}

Grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} plays a dual role in the framework. As a \term{category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind}}, it's an HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!grammaticality}~-- the maintained coupling between morphosyntactic form and structural meaning\ixsq{structural meaning}. It sustains a would-be\ixsq{would-be}~-- not in a parenthetical sense, but as its constitutive function. §\ref{sec:14:projectibility} showed the evidence: speakers deploy form--value\ixsq{value} pairings in novel sentences, extend them by analogy\ixsq{analogy} to new verbs, and repair deviations by projecting from the known coupling to the correct form. Grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} is a category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} \emph{because} it projects, not merely because it's maintained.

As a \term{mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!defined}}, it's a stabilizer\ixsq{stabilizer!as mechanism role} for other HPCs. The category \term{noun} is maintained partly because nounhood\ixsq{nounhood} is grammatical~-- because using a word in a noun slot, with noun morphology, in noun constructions, triggers no mismatch signal. Grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} is the enforcement mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!functional pressure} for the form--value couplings that constitute all other categories.

This dual role~-- maintainer and maintained~-- is characteristic of homeostatic systems. A \term{spinning top\ixsq{spinning top}} is both an object and the spin that keeps it upright; remove the spin and the top falls. The coupling\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!grammaticality} is constitutive~-- it defines what counts as grammatical production~-- while the detector\ixsq{detector} is regulative~-- it monitors deviations and triggers repair. That distinction doesn't require Platonism; it requires only a self-maintaining target state, which is what \term{homeostatic} means. And it changes the evidential status of the asterisk: if grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} is a maintained coupling and acceptability\ixsq{acceptability} is a noisy detector\ixsq{detector}, the asterisk is a signal from a measuring instrument~-- informative, but fallible, and calibrated to the community in which it was trained.


\section{Empirical signatures}
\label{sec:14:empirical}

If the feeling of ungrammaticality\ixsq{feeling of ungrammaticality} is a noisy detector\ixsq{detector}~-- detector, not oracle~-- then laboratory studies should reveal its characteristic failure modes. Two bodies of evidence and one competing account bear on this prediction.

\subsection{Satiation}
\label{sec:14:satiation}

\term{Syntactic satiation\ixsq{syntactic satiation}} is the finding that repeated exposure to a marginally acceptable sentence can improve its perceived acceptability\ixsq{acceptability} \ixnq{Snyder, William}\parencite{snyder2000grammaticality}. But the effect is selective. Some constraints are susceptible to frequency-based updating: repeated exposure makes them feel better. Others resist satiation entirely. In current English judgment datasets, left-branch extraction (\ungram\mention{Which did you buy car?}\ixlq{car}) remains near-categorically rejected even under exposure manipulations \ixnq{Snyder, William}\parencite{Snyder2022}. The intended meaning is easily grasped~-- \mention{Which car did you buy?}\ixlq{car}~-- and the structure is not unusually complex. The resistance has appeared across multiple tasks and speaker samples, though not uniformly in every paradigm.

Why the split? If the feeling of ungrammaticality\ixsq{feeling of ungrammaticality} arises from comparing input against entrenched form--value pairings, then repeated exposure should matter~-- but only for constraints that are frequency-sensitive in the first place. When you hear a marginal sentence ten times, you're adding exemplars to the distribution; the pairing becomes more familiar, the mismatch signal weakens, and the sentence feels better.

But why does the mismatch signal weaken? It's not just that the existing category gets wider; the category itself moves. \ixnq{Vagnino, Richard}\ixnq{Walker, Caren}\textcite{vagninoWalker2026} term this \term{schema drift\ixsq{schema drift}}. In their study of relational concepts, they found that repeated exposure to diverse exemplars caused the abstract schema itself to drift, accommodating new forms. They explicitly contrast this with the standard view of \term{rerepresentation\ixsq{rerepresentation}} (changing the input to fit the category\ixsq{category!boundary cases}). As they put it: \enquote{instead of changing the square peg to better fit the round hole... the hole might also be altered to better fit the peg.}

Syntactic satiation\ixsq{syntactic satiation} may be one route by which schema drift\ixsq{schema drift} appears in real time. The \enquote{hole} (the grammatical category\ixsq{category!boundary cases}) deforms to accommodate the repeated \enquote{peg} (the marginal sentence).

But when a constraint behaves as near-categorical in current English evidence~-- as left-branch extraction appears to~-- repeated brief exposure has shown little or no shift. The resistance doesn't imply the structure is unimaginable; it implies the learner has detected a \term{conspicuous absence\ixsq{conspicuous absence}}. Fronting is productive in English; the \emph{potential} for extraction is visible. But in this specific configuration, the community systematically avoids it (a conspicuous low-base-rate gap), using entrenched \term{workarounds\ixsq{workaround}} instead: pied-piping (\mention{whose book}), fused-head constructions (\mention{whose is this}), and the \mention{big mess} construction (\mention{how good a teacher}). \ixnq{Reynolds, Brett}\textcite{reynolds2026lbe} argues that this density of alternatives can make the gap resistant to short-run satiation because competitor pathways are strongly activated.

The gap persists because the potential is there but the practice is absent. Learners infer that the community has tacitly agreed \emph{not} to use the available mechanism in this way. The detector\ixsq{detector} is not just saying \enquote{this doesn't happen}; it's saying \enquote{we could do this, but we don't.} The feeling of ungrammaticality\ixsq{feeling of ungrammaticality} tracks this specific, entrenched abstinence.

\subsection{Individual differences}
\label{sec:14:individual-differences}

Speakers differ systematically in their grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} thresholds. The limiting case is trivial: we have no feeling of grammaticality whatsoever for languages we don't speak. A sentence in Zulu\ixgq{Zulu} or Finnish produces no mismatch signal for me because there's nothing to compare it against~-- no entrenched pairings, no expectations. Foreign language learners occupy the middle ground: their feelings are degraded, unreliable, slow to fire, and easily fooled by surface plausibility. Only with sufficient exposure does the feeling sharpen.

Within a single language community, the variation persists. \ixnq{Dabrowska, Ewa}\textcite{dabrowska2012} shows that grammatical knowledge is not uniform across the adult population: speakers with less formal education, less exposure to complex syntactic constructions, and less metalinguistic training produce reliably different acceptability\ixsq{acceptability} judgments~-- not just noise, but systematic divergence.

That's the prediction from community-indexed entrenchment\ixsq{entrenchment!frequency effects}. If grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} is what a community has entrenched, then members with different exposure histories will have different thresholds. The variation isn't random; it tracks social and educational ecologies. Chapter~\ref{ch:words-wont-hold-still}'s \mention{think myself through that argument}~-- interpretable by analogy to \mention{talk oneself through} and \mention{work oneself through}, but rejected by some speakers~-- is exactly this: the detector\ixsq{detector} fires or doesn't depending on how richly the analogical base has been entrenched.

\subsection{Relevance and maintenance}
\label{sec:14:relevance}

\ixnq{Scott-Phillips, Thom}\textcite{scottphillips2024communication} offers a complementary account. On his view, grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} intuitions are byproducts of relevance-tracking: we detect when utterances violate basic presumptions about communicative efficiency. Unacceptability arises not from mere inefficiency, but from an inherent impossibility of interpreting an utterance consistently with those presumptions.

The maintenance framework shares several premises: both reject an innate grammar faculty; both locate intuitions within general cognitive systems; both recognize that communicative pressures shape conventions. The frameworks differ in mechanism. Relevance theory locates failure in inferential impossibility; the maintenance account locates failure in morphosyntactic form--value\ixsq{value} mismatch~-- the zipper\ixsq{zipper} that won't close.

The difference has empirical consequences. Relevance theory predicts broader failures when relevance presumptions collapse; the maintenance account predicts construction\ixsq{construction}-specific failures where entrenched pairings misalign. On the maintenance view, \ungram\mention{I have twenty years} fails as an age statement because the English age construction is \mention{be} + \mention{years old}, not because relevance-seeking fails in general. The maintenance account also generates predictions that relevance theory alone doesn't: that satiation should be construction\ixsq{construction}-specific (tracking entrenchment depth, not general relevance), that productivity asymmetries should track coupling strength, and that repair should fire pre-emptively when the would-be\ixsq{would-be} conflicts with the form under production (§\ref{sec:14:projectibility}). These are projectibility predictions, not just mechanism descriptions.


\section{Passing the tests}
\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!two-diagnostic test}
\label{sec:14:hpc-audit}

§\ref{sec:8:diagnostics} introduced the Two-Diagnostic Test\ixsq{two-diagnostic test}. Grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} passes both. It sustains a would-be\ixsq{would-be}: a mastered form--value\ixsq{value} pairing projects to novel instances (§\ref{sec:14:projectibility}). And it's homeostatic: the feeling of ungrammaticality\ixsq{feeling of ungrammaticality} stabilizes the coupling, correcting deviations through hesitation, repair, and community-level norm enforcement (§\ref{sec:14:detector}). On current evidence, then, grammaticality is an HPC kind~-- maintained by the same mechanisms that maintain countability\ixsq{countability}, definiteness, gender, and lexical categories, and serving as stabilizer for all of them.


\section{Looking forward}
\label{sec:14:looking-forward}

We began with an asterisk, and we end with the same question: what does the asterisk diagnose?

Grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} is not a primitive. It's not a direct readout of structure. It's not possibility versus impossibility, but coupling versus mismatch: form and value\ixsq{value}, zipper\ixsq{zipper} teeth that mesh or don't. The feeling that signals mismatch is a noisy instrument~-- detector\ixsq{detector}, not oracle~-- shaped by processing constraints and what a community has entrenched. And the dual role~-- maintainer and maintained~-- is not paradoxical; it's characteristic of homeostatic systems, as the spinning-top\ixsq{spinning top} analogy of §\ref{sec:14:dual-role} illustrates.

The equilibrium is also \term{contingent\ixsq{contingency}}. On strong constraint-based readings, left-branch extraction \emph{can't} emerge in English~-- a grammatical principle blocks it. The HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!vs.\ essentialism} account says only that it \emph{hasn't} under current social and statistical conditions. But imagine a high-status speaker~-- or a low-status one with memetic cachet like Homer Simpson~-- introducing \mention{Whose did you eat sandwich?} to the language. Use the form, get the laugh, signal the group membership. Homer doesn't just provide new evidence; he spawns a new discourse community\ixsq{discourse community}. If social uptake shifts, priors shift. On this view, what resists at a given moment is entrenched convention, not a timeless prohibition. The mechanism is maintenance, not law~-- but that raises the question of whether the maintenance itself is contingent.

But contingency is not arbitrariness. The claim is about the \emph{contents} of the coupling, not its \emph{existence}. That every known language maintains some form--value coupling~-- that grammaticality exists everywhere~-- is itself a convergence fact, explained by shared cognitive and communicative pressures that make projection useful. Eyes evolved independently dozens of times, not because optics is a law of biology, but because light is informative and photosensitive tissue is accessible. Grammaticality recurs for the same reason: shared constraints channel the maintenance into convergent outcomes. The HPC framework predicts this: contingent contents, convergent architecture.

The contingency is the point, not the problem. What a community has entrenched is what it can project from. Grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} is the maintained coupling that makes the good bet\ixsq{good bet} possible~-- encounter this form--value\ixsq{value} pairing and you \emph{would} get this structural meaning\ixsq{structural meaning}, within a community and across novel instances. The asterisk probes that coupling~-- which is what makes grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} more than a feeling, and why Chapter~\ref{ch:social-stabilization} asks what keeps the coupling aligned well enough for shared projection.
