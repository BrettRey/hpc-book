\chapter{The Social Stabilization of Kinds}
\label{ch:social-stabilization}
\epigraph{Children have never been very good at listening to their elders, but they have never failed to imitate them. They must, they have no other models.}{~-- James Baldwin, \textit{Nobody Knows My Name} (1961)}

Chapter~\ref{ch:grammaticality-itself} concluded with the observation that grammaticality is a noisy detector\ixsq{detector}~-- a stabilizer\ixsq{stabilizer!as mechanism role} that monitors the form--value\ixsq{value} coupling and corrects deviations in real time. But a detector isn't calibrated in a vacuum; it's calibrated to a community.

I'm driving along Mississauga Road toward Humber, just before the QEW underpass, listening to \textit{EconTalk} as I do most Mondays. Russ Roberts says, \mention{The claim is, is that because of the tunnel infrastructure, it was necessary.}\ixlq{claim}\footnote{\textit{EconTalk}, \enquote{A Military Analysis of Israel's War in Gaza (with Andrew Fox),} 9 February 2026.} The double \mention{is} tugs at me. I don't stop the episode. I don't think less of Roberts. But I notice~-- and the noticing is already a mechanism at work.

In corpora of spontaneous speech, this \term{double copula\ixsq{double copula}} construction\ixsq{construction} is robust. It isn't a stutter or a planning failure; it's predictable, frequent, and stable across speakers and contexts. It clusters with focus-marking contexts and certain subject nouns (\mention{thing}\ixlq{thing}, \mention{problem}\ixlq{problem}, \mention{reason}\ixlq{reason}, \mention{claim}\ixlq{claim}). Speakers produce it; hearers understand it without difficulty. Nobody teaches it. The construction\ixsq{construction} appears to be young~-- first identified by \ixnq{Janda, Richard}\textcite{janda1980} and with no clear attestation before the late twentieth century~-- but it's already robust across dialects, registers, and age cohorts~-- Roberts is in his seventies. Children acquire it without instruction, from the same ambient speech that transmits everything else.

Here's the puzzle. Millions of speakers maintain this pattern without ever coordinating. How?

Chapter~\ref{ch:stabilizers} introduced the mechanisms that keep categories tight: acquisition, entrenchment, interactive alignment\ixsq{interactive alignment}, iterated transmission\ixsq{iterated transmission}. Those mechanisms live in the speaker's head. They explain why \emph{you} maintain the cluster. But they don't explain why the cluster stays aligned \emph{across} heads~-- why my \mention{noun}\ixsq{noun!as category} and your \mention{noun} pick out roughly the same thing, why we both produce the double copula\ixsq{double copula} in the same contexts without comparing notes.

The answer is that stabilizers aren't only internal. The most powerful ones are barely visible: speakers converge toward their interlocutors in real time, adopt forms associated with groups they want to belong to, and abandon variants that fail to communicate. These constant, low-level adjustments do most of the alignment\ixsq{alignment!convergence effects} work. Explicit correction~-- red pens, editorial markup, classroom grammar~-- is a special case: institutional, intermittent, and restricted to a few registers. It matters where it operates, but it isn't the engine.

Parts II and III showed how countability\ixsq{countability}, definiteness, lexical categories, and pro-form gender cohere despite variation~-- and how Chapter~\ref{ch:sign-anaphor} extends the same architecture to sign-language discourse reference. What we didn't yet explain is how these systems stay coherent, and therefore projectible, \emph{across} speakers. The answer is social stabilization: homeostatic machinery that operates between minds, not just within them.

\section{The signal in the noise}
\label{sec:16:signal-in-noise}

In much formal work, variation is treated as residual variance around an idealized competence system. In the HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!variation} framework, variation is the signature of mechanisms operating at a different scale.

Call it noise often enough and every mechanism starts to look like an exception.

Return to the double copula\ixsq{double copula}. If you measure it against the \mention{Standard English\ixgq{English!Standard}} cluster, it looks like an error:

\ea\label{ex:double-copula-narrative}
\ea \mention{The thing is is that we need to leave.}\ixlq{thing}
\ex \mention{The problem is is the money.}\ixlq{problem}
\z
\z

It appears to be a stutter, a planning failure, or a chaotic blending of two syntactic frames. Under this lens, it degrades the category\ixsq{category!boundary cases} of the copula~-- pure noise.

But if you measure it in a corpus of spontaneous speech, especially in planning-heavy contexts, it looks like a recurrent attractor with non-trivial frequency. It has stable properties: it occurs predominantly with \mention{reason}\ixlq{reason}, \mention{problem}\ixlq{problem}, and \mention{thing}\ixlq{thing}; it projects (forms like \mention{The catch is is...} are predictable); and it's maintained by communicative efficiency, signaling a focus structure or a restart.

The construction\ixsq{construction} isn't a slip. \ixnq{Coppock, Elizabeth}\textcite{coppock2018double} argues that it's a focus-marking device, with the second \mention{is} introducing a specificational clause. The Yale Grammatical Diversity Project documents its distribution across North American varieties: stable, predictable, and grammaticalized in specific discourse contexts.\footnote{See \url{https://ygdp.yale.edu/phenomena/double-is} for an overview.}

So is it \term{grammatical}?

The question is ill-posed because it treats \mention{English}\ixlqs{english}{English} as a single, flat population. If you pool all speakers and all contexts into one bucket, the double copula\ixsq{double copula} looks like a blurred edge attached to the copula category. But the event isn't random. It is \term{conditioned\ixsq{conditioned analysis}}. In spontaneous planning contexts, its probability is much higher than in edited prose contexts.

This suggests that the projectible\ixsq{projectible} regularities don't live at the level of \mention{English sentences} simpliciter. They live at the level of \mention{English sentences in context X}: conditioned\ixsq{conditioned system} systems whose distributions are stable because the same social mechanisms keep regenerating them. The social variable isn't an external \mention{influence}\ixlq{influence} on the grammar\ixsq{grammar}; it is a parameter of the grammar\ixsq{grammar} itself.

\ixnq{Sperber, Dan}\textcite{sperber1996} calls such stable equilibria \term{cultural attractors}\ixsq{cultural attractor}. If each transmission\ixsq{transmission} of a cultural item~-- a story, a pronunciation, a grammatical pattern~-- involves imperfect imitation or deliberate modification, why doesn't the item drift beyond recognition? Because not all variations are equally viable. \emph{Little Red Riding Hood} has been retold with countless small deviations, but it remains recognizable: the communicative function (a cautionary tale for children) keeps pulling variants toward a stable form.

Linguistic conventions work the same way. Pronunciation varies, but communicative success~-- being understood, sounding like someone your interlocutor wants to talk to~-- pulls variants toward a common attractor. Extreme deviations don't spread for many reasons: they may fail to communicate, but they may also fill no functional niche, index nothing anyone wants to index, or simply lack a transmission chain dense enough to sustain them. Institutional correction is one more filter, but a narrow one. The double copula\ixsq{double copula} persists in spontaneous speech not because speakers copy it perfectly, but because it fills a niche~-- focus-marking~-- and leans on a structural analogue that's already in the grammar (\mention{What the problem is is the money}\ixlq{problem}, where the double \mention{is} is required). It has something to do and somewhere to attach. That's why it spreads; that's why it sticks.

\section{The mixed bin problem}
\ixsq{mixed bin}
\label{sec:16:mixed-bin}

To see why the double copula\ixsq{double copula} looks puzzling, take a simple mixture\ixsq{mixture} case. Two factories produce bolts of different sizes, and each factory is internally precise. If you measure each factory separately, you see tight clusters. If you dump both bins into one pile and measure \mention{Bolt}\ixlq{bolt} as one category, you get a bimodal distribution and conclude the process is noisy. The process isn't noisy; the analysis failed to condition on source.

Linguistic variation is a latent mixture\ixsq{mixture} problem. When we say that subject--verb agreement is variable in \mention{English}\ixlqs{english}{English} because we see \mention{There's two men} alongside \mention{There are two men}, we're looking at the mixed bin\ixsq{mixed bin}. Under formal conditions, agreement is categorical. Under casual spoken conditions, \mention{there's}\ixlq{there's} acts as an invariant presentative particle\ixsq{particle}. \ixnq{Krejci, Bonnie}\ixnq{Hilton, Katherine}\textcite{krejciHilton2022} identify three variants in existential constructions: agreeing \mention{there are}, invariant \mention{there's}\ixlq{there's}, and levelled \mention{there is}. The invariant \mention{there's}\ixlq{there's} isn't agreement failure; it's a presentational particle\ixsq{particle} with its own distributional profile~-- stable in casual speech, near-absent in edited prose.

Both systems are tight. Both systems are homeostatic. The apparent looseness of the category\ixsq{category!membership graded} comes from summing them together.

One pooled curve isn't one grammar\ixsq{grammar}.

The \term{unconditioned probability\ixsq{unconditioned probability}} is the messy sum. The \term{conditioned probability\ixsq{conditioned probability}} is the tight coupling. Mechanisms of homeostasis\ixsq{homeostasis!in grammar} are most clearly visible at the conditioned\ixsq{conditioned analysis} level. We align to \emph{the variety}, not the language. \ixnq{Haspelmath, Martin}\textcite{haspelmath2018} makes a parallel point in typology\ixsq{typology}: treating comparative labels as if they were language-particular categories commits the \term{general category\ixsq{category!cross-linguistic}\ixsq{general category fallacy} fallacy} and erases the very structure we are trying to explain; cross-linguistic claims should target phenomena, not whole incommensurable systems.

The contrast is the same as weather reporting: the city-wide average is the pooled curve; your street-level forecast is the conditioned one.

Varieties, then, aren't labels but conditioning structure. What speakers acquire is something like the probability of a form given a dialect\ixsq{dialect} (a durable speaker-linked parameter), a register\ixsq{register} (a situational reweighting), and a discourse community\ixsq{discourse community} (a latent mixture\ixsq{mixture} component). The apparent looseness lives in the unconditioned distribution~-- the one you get only after averaging over all those variables.

The mechanisms in previous chapters~-- entrenchment, frequency, interactive alignment\ixsq{interactive alignment}~-- explain why an individual speaker maintains categories. They don't explain why a community does. Why do different speakers, with different input histories, converge on the same grammatical categories rather than drifting into private idiolects? That's the question this chapter answers. Variationist sociolinguistics\ixsq{sociolinguistics} has been studying it for decades: \ixnq{Labov, William}\textcite{labov1972} on stable variables vs.\ changes in progress, Milroy and Milroy on network structure, Eckert on the indexical field\ixsq{indexical field} (§\ref{sec:16:indexicality}), \ixnq{Tagliamonte, Sali A.}\textcite{tagliamontedarcy2007} on tracking change across generations. HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!methodology} gives that tradition a philosophical framework~-- it makes the implicit ontology of variationist work explicit~-- but it doesn't hand variationists new empirical questions they can't already formulate in their own terms. What it does do is vindicate a methodological stance that some variationists already hold: correlation isn't enough; you need the mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!vs.\ essences}. Many regression studies stop at showing that age or class predicts a variant. \ixnq{Milroy, Lesley}\textcite{milroy1987} and \ixnq{Eckert, Penelope}\textcite{eckert2000} insist on more~-- on identifying the social structures that maintain the pattern. HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!vs.\ essentialism} makes that insistence principled, and it makes the prediction explicit: weaken a stabilizer, and the category\ixsq{category!reality of} should degrade in identifiable ways. Milroy's Belfast work, where network dissolution led to dialect\ixsq{dialect} levelling \ixnq{Milroy, Lesley}\citep{milroy1987}, is exactly that prediction confirmed. The social mechanisms variationists have been studying are the same kind of stabilizer that operates at every other level of language. A label like \mention{internet English} that covers Reddit, formal blogs, and arxiv preprints isn't a register\ixsq{register}~-- it's a fat class\ixsq{fat class} (§\ref{sec:8:fat}), too much internal diversity, too little shared maintenance.

Part of the answer is general. Once speakers converge on a way of carving the conditioning space, deviation is individually costly~-- you mark yourself as odd, as foreign, as not-one-of-us~-- so the carving persists. Any coordination equilibrium resists perturbation, because unilateral departure doesn't pay \ixnq{O'Connor, Cailin}\citep{oconnor2019unfairness}. This is a maintenance mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!functional pressure} operating on the conditioning structure itself, not just on the categories within it. Network density, gatekeeping, and accommodation are the specific channels; the costliness of uncoordinated behaviour is the general principle that makes all of them stick.

Cost has more than one dimension here: per-token effort, monitoring burden, and face/prestige management risk. A regime can be cheap on one dimension and costly on another~-- especially when recurrent mismatch threatens solidarity, deference, credibility, or prestige affiliation. Interactive alignment\ixsq{interactive alignment} is cheap per exchange and face-sensitive by design: speakers adjust in real time to avoid sounding alien, rude, or out-of-register. But this local repair depends on dense repeated contact and shared attention, and it tolerates local drift.

Institutional enforcement~-- copy-editing, grading, moderation, peer review~-- solves a different problem. It is expensive per intervention, but it creates auditable, transportable trust: a signal produced in one interaction can be interpreted similarly by strangers elsewhere. It also allocates prestige by marking some forms as high-status and others as provincial, amateur, or outgroup. That is why institutional stabilization concentrates at certification and selection bottlenecks~-- publication venues, exams, hiring pipelines~-- rather than in ordinary conversation.

The result is layered maintenance. Ambient alignment maintains the center of the distribution; institutions police tails and high-consequence choke points; transmission distributes both across cohorts. Which layer dominates is an incidence question: who bears correction, compliance, and face costs, and who captures coordination, screening, and prestige returns. Where benefits are concentrated and costs are diffuse, high-friction norms can persist even with weak communicative payoff.

This is also field-relative projectibility\ixsq{field-relative projectibility} (§\ref{sec:7:field-relative}) made concrete. The conditioning variables~-- dialect\ixsq{dialect}, register, discourse community\ixsq{discourse community}~-- are what make projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!field-relative} field\ixsq{field}-relative in practice. A form that projects reliably in casual speech may project unreliably if you pool formal and informal contexts. The field\ixsq{field} isn't an assumption you bring to the data; it's the structure that makes the data intelligible. Table~\ref{tab:16:conditioning} sets out the specific claims.

\section{How the bin unmixes}
\label{sec:16:three-mechanisms}

If the \term{mixed bin\ixsq{mixed bin}} is the wrong place to look for homeostasis\ixsq{homeostasis!vs.\ mere stability}, how do speakers unmix the bin? They don't have labels on the bolts. They have to infer the source.

The inference runs along at least two dimensions: \term{community}~-- who you interact with~-- and \term{activity}~-- what you're doing. Sociolinguists know these as \term{dialect\ixsq{dialect}} (variety according to user) and \term{register\ixsq{register}} (variety according to use). Each is maintained by a distinct cluster of mechanisms operating at a distinct timescale. The conditioning space is certainly richer than two axes, but these are the cuts that yield the most predictive lift for the questions this chapter asks. They aren't metadata around grammar\ixsq{grammar}. They're part of the conditioning structure that makes a public grammar\ixsq{grammar} stable.

A book that argues against essentialising linguistic categories shouldn't essentialise its own analytical tools. Dialect\ixsq{dialect} and register\ixsq{register} aren't discrete types with necessary-and-sufficient definitions; they're attractor basins in a continuous conditioning space, maintained by partially distinct but overlapping mechanisms. When the two dimensions converge~-- when a dense social network is organised around recurrent communicative tasks~-- you get what \ixnq{Swales, John M.}\textcite{swales1990} called a \term{discourse community\ixsq{discourse community}}: a zone of high mechanistic density where community and activity mechanisms reinforce each other. The conditioning space is continuous; the useful cuts through it are purpose-relative.

You can see the dimensions at work in one case. The same student says \mention{The thing is is...}\ixlq{thing} in hallway speech, deletes one \mention{is} in a graded essay, and leaves the form intact when transcribing informal interview speech in a methods assignment. The token didn't change; the conditioning did.

And it's not just classrooms. A waiter asks \mention{And yourself?}~-- a reflexive pronoun where everyday English uses plain \mention{you}\ixlq{you}. No style guide teaches this; most would correct it. But the form thrives because it indexes deference and attentive service in a way that \mention{And you?} doesn't. New servers pick it up from experienced ones~-- convergence, not instruction~-- and prescriptive pressure can't kill it because the social reward outweighs the grammatical cost. The reflexive/plain boundary persists partly because speakers on both sides of it need the distinction as a resource.

\ixnq{Mallon, Ron}\textcite{mallon2003} and \ixnq{Khalidi, Muhammad Ali}\textcite{khalidi2015three} frame the general point philosophically: social kinds become projectible\ixsq{projectible} when stable social mechanisms keep regenerating a cluster. This chapter makes that claim operational for linguistic kinds.

\subsection{Dialect: The factory setting}
\ixsq{dialect}
\label{sec:16:dialect}

In sociolinguistics\ixsq{sociolinguistics}, a \term{dialect\ixsq{dialect}} is traditionally defined as \mention{variety according to the user}~-- the way a person speaks because of who they are, not what they're doing \ixnq{Halliday, M. A. K.}\citep{halliday1978}. Dialects are tied to region, ethnicity, class, or network history. They travel with the speaker across situations.

Labov's foundational insight was that dialect\ixsq{dialect} differences aren't random noise but \term{orderly heterogeneity\ixsq{orderly heterogeneity}}: systematic, structured variation that can be described with variable rules \ixnq{Labov, William}\citep{labov1972}. African American English\ixgq{English!African American (AAVE)}, for instance, isn't \mention{broken Standard English\ixgq{English!Standard}} but a rule-governed system with its own phonological and morphosyntactic constraints. This was a major advance. But it left a puzzle: if dialects are systems, what makes them cohere? What keeps AAVE\ixgq{English!African American (AAVE)} from drifting into something unrecognizable across generations?

The standard sociolinguistic answer invokes geography, social networks, and identity. The maintenance view\ixsq{maintenance view}, with its emphasis on mechanism-maintained profiles and projectibility relative to purposes, treats those as the channels through which mechanisms operate. Dialects are \term{maintained kinds}: transmission within communities, alignment\ixsq{alignment!interactive} in interaction, and identity-marking pressures keep regenerating the profile. The dialect\ixsq{dialect} boundary isn't only where rules differ. It is where the \emph{maintenance regime} differs.

Consider the dialect\ixsq{dialect} continuum problem. Dutch\ixgq{Dutch} shades into German\ixgq{German} across the Rhine; Scandinavian\ixgq{Scandinavian languages} varieties form a chain of mutual intelligibility. Where does one dialect\ixsq{dialect} end and another begin? \enquote{A language is a dialect with an army and a navy,} but HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!membership} adds a narrower claim: maintenance regimes can be distinct even when diagnostics are gradient. The mechanisms that maintain Dutch\ixgq{Dutch} and German\ixgq{German} differ~-- media systems, school systems, prestige ecologies~-- even if no single feature draws a sharp line. The continuum is partly epistemic: boundaries are hard to locate because maintenance is distributed.

Think of dialect\ixsq{dialect} as a factory setting~-- a set of priors you load when you meet a speaker from a specific region. If a speaker consistently uses \mention{needs washed} (the Midland US pattern), I don't conclude that English syntax is collapsing; I conclude that this speaker has a different parameter setting. The construction remains stable because I have conditioned\ixsq{conditioned analysis} on the dialect\ixsq{dialect}.

The feedback loop is mostly invisible. Speakers converge toward interlocutors in real time~-- automatically, through interactive priming \ixnq{Pickering, Martin J.}\ixnq{Garrod, Simon}\citep{pickering2004}, and socially, through accommodation to valued interlocutors \ixnq{Giles, Howard}\ixnq{Coupland, Nikolas}\ixnq{Coupland, Justine}\citep{giles1991}. In Pickering and Garrod's model, producing and comprehending the same structures aligns speakers' representations at every level~-- phonological, syntactic, semantic~-- so that repeated interaction produces convergence even when neither party is trying to accommodate. This is the cognitive engine beneath the social one. They adopt forms associated with groups they want to belong to, and tight networks maintain variants through sheer frequency of mutual exposure. But convergence alone would dissolve boundaries. What keeps them sharp is \term{divergence}~-- the active accentuation of one's own variety when group identity is salient \ixnq{Gallois, Cindy}\ixnq{Ogay, Tania}\ixnq{Giles, Howard}\citep{gallois2005}. A Welsh speaker who leans into her local features in the presence of RP speakers isn't failing to converge; she's doing identity work, pushing tokens back toward her own cluster centre. Convergence tightens clusters from within; divergence sharpens boundaries from without; and non-accommodation~-- simply holding steady~-- stabilizes through inertia. Together these mirror the basin\ixsq{basin} dynamics of §\ref{sec:5:geometry-to-mechanism}: attraction toward cluster centres, repulsion at boundaries. Overt correction operates too, but it is institutional, intermittent, and restricted to a few registers.

Convergence predicts that when network density shifts, features should cross community boundaries~-- and they do. My own children (early twenties, mixed Japanese\ixgq{Japanese}-Anglo, suburban Ontario) use habitual \mention{be} unselfconsciously: \mention{She be working late}. They presumably acquired it through peer networks dense enough to transmit the pattern, and the form fills a genuine aspectual niche~-- English\ixgq{English} otherwise marks habituality clumsily. The boundary between AAE\ixgq{English!African American (AAVE)} and other varieties is real, maintained by distinct prestige ecologies, but permeable where convergence mechanisms are strong and the functional niche is open.

The boundary is real, even when the border marker is fuzzy.

\subsection{Register: The mode switch}
\ixsq{register}\ixsq{mode}
\label{sec:16:register}

Where dialect\ixsq{dialect} is \mention{variety according to user}, \term{register\ixsq{register}} is \mention{variety according to use} \ixnq{Halliday, M. A. K.}\citep{halliday1978}. The same speaker shifts across situations: casual at breakfast, formal in a job interview, technical in a lab report. Halliday's field\ixsq{field}/tenor\ixsq{tenor}/mode\ixsq{mode} model names the main situational dimensions that reweight linguistic choices.

Subsequent work confirmed that these situational shifts aren't random. \ixnq{Biber, Douglas}\textcite{biber1988} applied multidimensional analysis to large corpora and showed that texts cluster into recognizable register types along interpretable dimensions (\eg\ involved vs.\ informational, narrative vs.\ non-narrative). The clusters are real: academic prose genuinely differs from casual conversation in systematic, measurable ways. But what \emph{maintains} those clusters? Biber's methodology describes the patterns; it doesn't explain their stability.

The HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!variation} framework offers an answer: registers are attractor basins maintained by social mechanisms. Register\ixsq{register} is a mode\ixsq{mode} switch~-- we don't swap grammars when we walk into a lecture hall; we reweight options. In formal writing, speakers suppress some high-utility spoken forms (double copula, \mention{get}\ixlq{get}-passive) and raise others (nominalizations). Most of this reweighting is self-monitored: speakers shift because they've internalised the norms. But when self-monitoring fails, institutional gatekeeping supplies the backstop.

\mention{Gonna}\ixlq{gonna}\ixsq{grammaticalization!gonna} illustrates the point. The form is phonologically reduced, syntactically integrated, and semantically bleached~-- a grammaticalization\ixsq{grammaticalization} in progress. In casual speech it's unremarkable; in formal writing it's red-penned. The difference isn't grammaticality; it's register maintenance. The same mechanisms that entrench the form in speech~-- frequency, reduction, functional utility~-- are counteracted in formal contexts by institutional gatekeeping. One form, two conditioning regimes.

The \term{Academic Register}\ixsq{academic register} discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:the-category-zipper} is the prime example~-- and the one case where explicit correction really is a primary mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!functional pressure}. Academic prose isn't a natural dialect\ixsq{dialect} acquired in childhood; it's a learned, inhibited stance maintained by institutional filtering. When a student submits a paper with too many contractions, the red ink flows. When a job candidate gives a colloquial research talk, eyebrows rise. \ixnq{Pullum, Geoffrey K.}\textcite{pullum2019-normativity} argues that grammar\ixsq{grammar} is a normative domain~-- grammars make claims about correctness~-- and that such normativity can be made precise without implying obligations or duties. The academic register\ixsq{register} is where that normativity is most explicitly enforced: \enquote{In this room, we only use the nominalized subset.}

The obvious objection is the same as for dialect\ixsq{dialect}: \enquote{Registers blend into each other.} And the HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!vs.\ essentialism} reply is the same: maintenance infrastructures differ enough to support distinct attractors, even when no sharp line separates them. Academic gatekeeping doesn't operate on Twitter; Twitter's norms don't govern journal submissions. The boundary is where one maintenance regime ends and another begins.

Same speaker, same grammar\ixsq{grammar}, different weighting.

\subsection{The convergence zone: discourse community}
\ixsq{discourse community}
\label{sec:16:community}

Where community and activity mechanisms are both strong, you get what applied linguistics calls a \term{discourse community\ixsq{discourse community}}: a group whose members share communicative conventions~-- specialised vocabulary, preferred genres, implicit standards of argument. Academic disciplines are paradigm cases: physicists, linguists, and literary critics each write differently, and those differences aren't random~-- they're maintained by disciplinary training, peer review, and citation practices.

Discourse community\ixsq{discourse community} isn't a third kind of conditioning. It's the overlap region where identity-indexed mechanisms (acquisition, alignment\ixsq{alignment!convergence effects}, identity marking) and activity-indexed mechanisms (functional pressure, genre convention, self-monitoring) converge and are reinforced by an additional stabilizer: institutional gatekeeping. Newcomers read what works, reproduce it, and get rewarded with citations, invitations, and degrees. Gatekeeping (journal acceptance, degree conferral, peer review) acts as the filter, but imitation of prestige models is the engine. The convergence zone\ixsq{convergence} is a coordination equilibrium: once established, deviation is costly and conformity is self-reinforcing.

This acts as a category\ixsq{category!reality of}'s immune system\ixsq{immune system}: when a weird form appears, we partition rather than widen, saying \enquote{That's tech-speak} or \enquote{That's a Reddit thing.}

By assigning the deviant form to a specific sub-community, we protect the general category from drift. The odd bolt doesn't contaminate the bin because we mentally tag it as \mention{Factory B}. This source attribution allows English to host massive internal diversity without losing its structural coherence. The clusters stay tight because we keep the bins separate effectively.

Source attribution is how systems stay stable without pretending to be uniform.

This is closely related to what \ixnq{Hacking, Ian}\textcite{hacking1999} calls \term{looping kinds}\ixsq{looping kind}\ixsq{kind!looping}: classifications that loop back to shape the behaviour they describe. When speakers learn that a form is \mention{incorrect}\ixlq{incorrect} or \mention{formal}\ixlq{formal}, they adjust~-- and the adjustment reinforces the category\ixsq{category!reality of}. The category shapes what it classifies. In HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!vs.\ essentialism} terms, the loop produces an extrinsically homeostatic cluster\ixsq{interactive kind}\ixsq{kind!interactive}.

The same machinery can stabilize inequity. \ixnq{O'Connor, Cailin}\textcite{oconnor2022contracts} shows that once agents recognize social categories and condition behaviour on them, discriminatory norms can emerge as stable outcomes of individually rational choices~-- coordination equilibria whose dynamics actively entrench initial asymmetries rather than correcting them.
Incidence matters here: if incumbents capture most of the screening benefit while newcomers bear most of the compliance cost, the equilibrium can be both stable and systematically unfair.

\section{Acquisition as source inference}
\ixsq{acquisition}\ixsq{inference}
\label{sec:16:acquisition}

The two dimensions~-- community and activity~-- aren't independent. They form a unified conditioning structure that unmixes the bin. Dialect\ixsq{dialect} is durable and speaker-linked; register is situational and interaction-linked; and where both converge, institutional norms add a further stabilizer. A single utterance is conditioned\ixsq{conditioned analysis} by all of these at once.

This isn't only an analyst's convenience. The developmental evidence suggests that both components of the framework~-- cluster detection and conditioning~-- are operative from the start. Infants discriminate their native language's rhythmic class within days of birth \ixnq{Mehler, Jacques}\ixnq{Jusczyk, Peter}\ixnq{Lambertz, Ghislaine}\ixnq{Halsted, Nilofar}\ixnq{Bertoncini, Josiane}\ixnq{Amiel-Tison, Claudine}\citep{mehler1988}, distinguishing between rhythmic classes but not within them \ixnq{Nazzi, Thierry}\ixnq{Bertoncini, Josiane}\ixnq{Mehler, Jacques}\citep{nazzi1998}; by five months they prefer to look at speakers of their native language, and by ten months they preferentially accept toys from native-accent speakers over foreign-accent ones \ixnq{Kinzler, Katherine D.}\ixnq{Dupoux, Emmanuel}\ixnq{Spelke, Elizabeth S.}\citep{kinzler2007}. That's source inference\ixsq{inference} before metalinguistic awareness, before production, before named-language concepts. At the same time, \ixnq{Wiese, Heike}\textcite{wiese2023} argues that children learn \term{Communicative Situations\ixsq{communicative situation}} (\term{com-sits}), not named languages: in one com-sit\ixsq{communicative situation} a child learns \mention{doggie}\ixlq{doggie}; in another, \mention{Wauwau}\ixlq{wauwau}. The variants are initially situation-bound, then bundled as recurring interactional profiles. At a different analytic grain, single-neuron evidence shows that content and interactive task context can be represented in largely separate populations and recombined via context reinstatement \ixnq{Bausch, Marcel}\ixnq{Niediek, Johannes}\ixnq{Reber, Thomas P.}\ixnq{Mackay, Sina}\ixnq{Bostr\"om, Jan}\ixnq{Elger, Christian E.}\ixnq{Mormann, Florian}\parencite{bausch2026}; this doesn't identify com-sits directly, but it makes context-conditioned\ixsq{conditioned system} retrieval mechanistically plausible.

Children don't start with one mixed bin\ixsq{mixed bin} and later discover variation. They build both broad distributional clusters and fine-grained conditioning from the start, and only later project labels like \mention{English}\ixlqs{english}{English} over those bins. The loop runs through linguistic form itself: categories make social inference\ixsq{inference} possible; social inference\ixsq{inference} maintains categories. Kinzler's finding that accent trumps race in preschoolers' friendship choices suggests that linguistic features are among the primary cues the social machinery uses to operate~-- which would mean the categories are maintained by the very inferences they make possible.

Methodologically, the implication is scale flexibility. If cognition tracks conditioned\ixsq{conditioned system} systems, it can track them at dyadic, community, and institutional scales when those scales are stably maintained. The defeat test is straightforward: if conditioning repeatedly fails to recover projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!induction from}, the mixed-bin claim weakens. If it repeatedly succeeds, social stabilization has earned explanatory weight.

\section{Indexicality as the inverse function}
\ixsq{indexicality}
\label{sec:16:indexicality}

Hear \mention{The thing is is...}\ixlq{thing} in conversation and you infer a source quickly: informal planning, not polished prose. That inference\ixsq{inference} is \term{indexicality\ixsq{indexicality}}.

If grammar predicts form from social condition, social meaning\ixsq{social meaning} runs the inference\ixsq{inference} in reverse: hearing a form and attributing a source. The same machinery that supports production supports listener-side source attribution.

This is why the tug matters. When I notice the double copula\ixsq{double copula} on a podcast, I'm not correcting anyone~-- I'm performing source attribution. The form indexed a register\ixsq{register} (spontaneous planning) that didn't match my expectation for the context. The \emph{feeling} of oddness isn't evidence that the form is ungrammatical\ixsq{ungrammatical}~-- the form--value\ixsq{value} coupling is intact (Chapter~\ref{ch:grammaticality-itself}). It's evidence that the listener conditioned\ixsq{conditioned analysis} on a different register\ixsq{register} and detected a mismatch. The inference\ixsq{inference} is automatic, and it does more stabilizing work than any red pen.

Source attribution captures one direction~-- hearing a form and inferring a source~-- but the full picture is richer. \ixnq{Eckert, Penelope}\textcite{eckert2008indexical}'s \term{indexical field\ixsq{indexical field}} isn't a lookup table from forms to fixed social categories; it's an ideologically structured space of potential social meanings that a form carries. \mention{It is posited that...} can index authority, distance, or pedantry, depending on community history and local stance norms~-- and speakers don't just passively trigger these values; they deploy them as resources for constructing personae. In the terms of §\ref{sec:7:what-projection-is}, the indexical field\ixsq{indexical field} is the would-be\ixsq{would-be} space: the set of interpretive habits a sign could activate, depending on the context that completes the triad. The conditioning structure in §\ref{sec:16:mixed-bin} captures which forms appear under which conditions; the indexical field\ixsq{indexical field} captures what those appearances mean. Same maintenance machinery, different grain.

Indexicality\ixsq{indexicality} closes the stabilization loop, but not as a simple binary. Convergence tightens clusters from within~-- speakers move toward forms that work. Divergence sharpens boundaries from without~-- speakers accentuate their own variety when group identity is salient, pushing tokens back toward the cluster centre. Indexical inference\ixsq{inference} provides the feedback signal for both: listeners draw conclusions from what you say, and speakers know it. You don't need a red pen if using the wrong form makes people think you're from a different group, or a different register, or a different century. Explicit correction is a rare institutional special case; the constant, invisible pressure is social inference\ixsq{inference}. And some of that inference\ixsq{inference} gets mislabelled. The \emph{feeling} of ungrammaticality that Chapter~\ref{ch:grammaticality-itself} analysed as a noisy detector\ixsq{detector} is partly an indexical signal: the form triggered a source attribution that clashed with the expected register. The listener reports \enquote{that sounds wrong} when what happened was \enquote{that sounds like the wrong context.} Grammaticality\ixsq{grammaticality} judgments, in other words, aren't immune to the social conditioning this chapter describes. They're one of its products.

\section{The nominalist challenge, socially grounded}
\label{sec:16:nominalism-social}

This returns us to the challenge from Chapter~\ref{ch:what-we-havent-been-asking}. Haspelmath and \ixnq{Croft, William}Croft argued that because categories lack definitions, they can't be cross-linguistically real (Haspelmath) or even language-internally global (Croft). We are now in a position to see what they got right, and where the HPC\ixsq{homeostatic property cluster!vs.\ essentialism} framework allows us to go further.

They were right about the failure of definitions. If \textsc{noun\ixsq{noun!cross-linguistic}} requires a set of necessary and sufficient conditions that holds across all languages, then \textsc{noun\ixsq{noun!cross-linguistic}} doesn't exist. If \textsc{subject\ixsq{subject}} requires a definition that covers every construction in English without exception, then \textsc{subject\ixsq{subject}} doesn't exist.

The false choice is essence or stipulation. The maintenance view\ixsq{maintenance view} adds a third option: categories as profiles of co-occurring properties, stabilized by mechanisms, projectible relative to purposes. That framing preserves what comparative concepts and constructional analyses get right while adding explicit maintenance claims.

For Haspelmath, comparative concepts remain analyst tools, but they can also track recurring attractors when functional and social pressures repeatedly rebuild similar basins (Chapter~\ref{ch:how-categories-travel} explains why such cross-domain recurrence is predictable rather than coincidental). For \ixnq{Croft, William}Croft, constructions remain primary, but construction\ixsq{construction}-level generalizations can still be robust when alignment, acquisition, and gatekeeping keep reuse trajectories stable across speakers.

The central claim is mixture\ixsq{mixture}, not uniformity. Sociolinguistic complexity isn't evidence against categories; it's evidence that categories are parameterized by conditioning structure. The form \mention{I done it}\ixlq{done} in one community and \mention{I did it} in another isn't just variant selection; it's different coupling structures under different social parameters. The \mention{done} community maintains a partial syncretism\ixsq{syncretism}~-- past tense and past participle share a form in declaratives but not in inversion (\mention{did you?}, not \mention{*done you?})~-- while the \mention{did} community maintains full differentiation. Each coupling is stable, acquired reliably, and projectible within its community. That's not one category\ixsq{category!reality of} with two labels; it's two HPC profiles with different cluster structures, each maintained by its own social parameters.

\ixnq{O'Connor, Cailin}\textcite{oconnor2019games} sharpens the point: categories can look like they reflect natural structure even when they're coordination solutions. What counts as a \enquote{natural} grouping depends on what the community needs to do together~-- the properties that matter are the ones relevant to shared action. Maintained kinds live in exactly that space~-- conventional enough to be coordination equilibria, structured enough to be projectible.

But the game has two payoff dimensions, not one. Communicative clarity rewards convergence: using forms your interlocutor will understand. Identity management rewards divergence: using forms that mark you as a member of the group you want to belong to. When the two align (converging with your in-group), clusters tighten. When they conflict~-- wanting to be understood by an outgroup while maintaining distinctiveness~-- the result is code-switching, style-shifting, register mixing. These boundary phenomena are the predicted stress points of the homeostasis\ixsq{homeostasis!in grammar}, the places where the two objectives pull apart. The framework doesn't just predict stable categories; it predicts where stability should fray, and what form the fraying should take.

That predictive specificity is what earns the closing commitment. The chapter closes with a method claim, not a victory claim. If social stabilization is real, analyses should improve when conditioning is modelled explicitly. If they don't, the mechanism story should be revised or rejected.

No conditioning gain, no maintenance claim.

Chapter~\ref{ch:how-categories-travel} takes the argument in an unexpected direction: if categories are maintained clusters, then cross-domain extension on partial property overlap is a predicted capacity, not an add-on. That chapter addresses the tradition closest to HPC in spirit~-- cognitive linguistics~-- and shows that its central phenomenon falls out of the framework for free. Then Chapter~\ref{ch:what-changes} turns the accumulated claims into a gauntlet with observables, baselines, and defeat conditions. The immediate workflow change is straightforward: report pooled and conditioned\ixsq{conditioned analysis} models side by side, test whether projectibility returns under conditioning, and state what result would count against your analysis.
