\section{Introduction: the gauntlet}
\label{sec:16:intro-gauntlet}

A theory that survives every possible result explains nothing; if it can't lose, it can't win.

The constructive argument is behind us: definitions fail, mechanisms do the work, projectibility\ixsq{projectibility} is indexed to purpose, and the case studies have put the framework under load. Now comes the expensive part. A framework earns its keep only when it names the outcomes that would kill it~-- and shows its receipts.

This chapter is short on purpose. No new slogan, no new case study. Just a gauntlet: what we predict, what would falsify those predictions, and what work follows if we take the risk seriously. Build studies that can make the framework wrong. That's the instruction. Figure~\ref{fig:16:gauntlet} maps the evaluation pathway.

One running case will keep this honest: English\ixgq{English} \mention{data}\ixlq{data}. Picture a conference Q\&A. A corpus linguist puts up a slide: \enquote{The data \emph{is} clear.} Someone in the third row winces. Someone else nods. In the hallway afterwards, the argument isn't about the findings; it's about the verb\ixsq{verb}. That argument is the test bench~-- not because \mention{data}\ixlq{data} is exotic, but because it sits exactly where the framework has to perform: at a boundary that's shifting, conditioned, and measurable.

Three diagnostics make the case concrete. Agreement (\mention{the data are}\ixlq{data} vs \mention{the data is}\ixlq{data}) tracks community norms as much as grammar. Tight count packaging (\mention{a datum}\ixlq{datum}, \mention{three data}\ixlq{data}) tests singulative\ixsq{singulative} anchoring. Loose packaging (\mention{many data}\ixlq{data} vs \mention{much data}\ixlq{data}) sits between the two. Together they give us a noun whose maintenance regime is visibly in motion.

\section{No level privilege, no level immunity}
\label{sec:16:no-level-privilege}

At the quantum scale there are no cats. At the scale of the isolated token, there are no categories. The epigraph from Ladyman and Ross isn't decoration; it's the framework's ontological commitment. A category\ixsq{category} can be real at phonological, morphosyntactic, semantic, discourse, or social grain~-- provided it's maintained and projectible at that grain. No level gets a crown. No level gets an exemption.

Every proposed category\ixsq{category} owes three debts: show clustering, identify the stabilisers that maintain it, and demonstrate forward projection~-- what learning one case buys you for the next. Clustering invites stabilisers; stabilisers underwrite projection; projection is where clustering earns its keep. That chain is the discipline behind the flexibility. Traditional labels don't get grandfathered in. New decompositions don't get rewarded just for being novel. The \term{meta-Occam\ixsq{meta-Occam}} principle from Chapter~\ref{ch:kinds-without-essences} still applies: be parsimonious about mechanisms, not about labels.

Comparative concepts can stay useful without guaranteeing a language-particular category\ixsq{category} in every grammar. We can compare \term{adjective\ixsq{adjective}}-like behaviour across languages even where no dedicated adjective\ixsq{adjective} category\ixsq{category} is maintained inside a given language. That's a null case, not a framework failure.

\section{The empirical commitments}
\label{sec:16:empirical-commitments}

The maintenance view\ixsq{maintenance view} is only worth anything if it yields risky expectations. Here are two.

\subsection{Prediction 1: Fraying is asymmetric, not random}
\label{sec:16:prediction-fraying}

Start with \mention{data}\ixlq{data}. As singulative\ixsq{singulative} anchoring weakens (\mention{datum}\ixlq{datum} recedes from ordinary use), tight frames should erode first: \mention{a datum}\ixlq{datum} and \mention{three data}\ixlq{data} become rare or odd. Looser frames hold longer: \mention{many data}\ixlq{data} persists. Agreement lags behind both, because agreement tracks community convention, not just semantic structure. And the variance should partition between speakers more than within them~-- individual commitments stay mostly sharp even while the population distribution looks graded.

That's the prediction in one case. The principle is general. Margins fray, but they don't fray at random: ignore conditioning and structure masquerades as noise; ignore structure and noise masquerades as structure. When stabilisers uncouple, we should see patterned decay keyed to which mechanism\ixsq{mechanism} weakened first~-- not all diagnostics collapsing at once.

Chapter~\ref{ch:countability} gave the template: when a noun's anchor weakens, some cues destabilise first; others hold. Chapter~\ref{ch:definiteness-and-deitality} showed the same architecture with a different split: semantic and morphosyntactic behaviour drifting out of step in constrained ways. Chapter~\ref{ch:lexical-categories} showed the typological analogue: edges fray where support is thin.

If fraying turns out to be uniform free noise across diagnostics and speakers, the maintenance account has overfit. The key point isn't \enquote{some variation}. It's structured non-synchrony.

\subsection{Prediction 2: Drift has an order (the sticky-lock effect)}
\label{sec:16:prediction-sticky-lock}

Drift should have an order. Usage and semantic pressure move first; morphosyntactic locks lag; lagging locks fail in bursts rather than smoothing out forever. The lock is \enquote{sticky}: it persists after the pressure profile that built it has shifted.

Not instant category\ixsq{category} death. Not indefinite linear blur. Staged decoupling~-- and, across unrelated languages under similar pressures, convergence on similar local fixes even when the material details differ.

The lag is architectural, not hierarchical. Semantic, morphosyntactic, and discourse representations can update at different rates without any one level being ontologically primary. Scope matters: language-particular predictions target concrete diagnostics inside one grammar; comparative predictions target recurring \emph{shapes} of drift across grammars; constructional predictions target specific form--meaning pairings regardless of lexical label (the competition between \mention{these data are}\ixlq{data} and \mention{this data is}\ixlq{data} as partially distinct constructions, for instance).

Back to \mention{data}\ixlq{data}. If \mention{datum}\ixlq{datum} recession yields staged erosion~-- tight before loose~-- we get sticky lag. If everything moves in lockstep, the prediction fails. And in cohorts where \mention{datum}\ixlq{datum} is absent from child-directed input, loose packaging (\mention{many data}) should stabilise before tight singulative\ixsq{singulative} packaging (\mention{a datum}\ixlq{datum}). That ordering is testable now, with age-graded corpora and perturbation designs. Trajectories, not snapshots.

\section{Model-criticism conditions}
\label{sec:16:falsification}

These aren't thought experiments. They're the two ways we could be wrong.

\subsection{Stress test A: Mechanism-light projection}
\label{sec:16:stress-essentialist}

Find a robust, cross-linguistically stable grammatical category\ixsq{category} that projects strongly while showing no identifiable maintenance structure~-- no acquisition bias, no alignment\ixsq{alignment} pressure, no functional anchoring\ixsq{functional anchoring}, no institutional reinforcement, no attractor-like distributional profile. If that pattern replicates across datasets and preregistered proxy controls, the maintenance account needs substantive revision. Categories that project without maintenance are the essentialist's best case and our worst.

\subsection{Stress test B: Baseline parity after noise controls}
\label{sec:16:stress-nominalist}

Show that usage variation is effectively a random walk once sampling noise is controlled: no reliable conditioning by frequency, no alignment\ixsq{alignment} effects, no stable register or community attractors, no basin\ixsq{basin}-like recovery after perturbation. If categories don't predict beyond convenience labels on out-of-sample data, the account needs scope reduction or re-specification. Categories that predict nothing beyond their labels are the nominalist's best case and our worst.

To limit post hoc rescue, preregister the proxy family for \enquote{maintenance structure} and report how conclusions change under alternative specifications. Evaluate cumulatively: when major domains repeatedly show no mechanism\ixsq{mechanism}-linked lift, downgrade strongly; when results are mixed, narrow scope; when lift is robust and replicated, raise confidence.

\subsection{Serious downgrading and revision}
\label{sec:16:serious-downgrade}

One mechanism\ixsq{mechanism} claim can fail in one domain while others carry weight elsewhere. That's correction, not collapse. But if repeated studies show weak support for patterned fraying, no reliable lag structure, and no stable lift over convenience labels across major domains, the framework should be treated as provisional heuristic vocabulary rather than settled explanatory ontology. That isn't business as usual. It's a substantive loss of status.

\section{Operationalizing the gauntlet}

\section{Conclusion: the zipper\ixsq{zipper} under load}
\label{sec:16:zipper-at-scale}

If you've ever overpacked a suitcase until the zipper\ixsq{zipper} protested, you already understand boundary behaviour. Put the zipper\ixsq{zipper} under load. Ask what keeps the teeth meshed. Ask where slip begins. Ask which perturbation makes the slider skip. Chapter~\ref{ch:the-category-zipper} gave the image; this chapter treats it as an instrument.

The shift is this. The question is no longer \enquote{what are categories, in themselves?} It's: what keeps this category\ixsq{category} together, for whom, under which pressures, and for how long? That isn't a retreat from realism. It's the only version of realism that can survive the evidence.

At the quantum scale there are no cats. At the scale of the isolated token there are no categories. Categories live at the scale where maintenance operates~-- where acquisition, entrenchment, alignment\ixsq{alignment}, and gatekeeping converge to keep a profile projectable\ixsq{projectable}. That's the book's ontological commitment, and it's worth stating plainly in the final pages: we aren't arguing that categories are fictions sustained by habit. We're arguing that the sustaining \emph{is} the reality. Remove the maintenance and you don't reveal the category\ixsq{category} underneath. You dissolve it.

What changes in practice? Fieldwork stops asking \enquote{is X a Y?} and starts asking \enquote{what maintains X as a Y, and where does that maintenance thin out?} Argumentation stops treating boundary cases as embarrassments and starts treating them as the most informative data in the sample. Study design stops aiming for the clean centre and starts building gauntlets~-- predictions that can fail, stress tests that can bite, baselines that can win.

The framework is young and the evidence is partial. The predictions in this chapter could fail. Some probably will. That's not a caveat bolted on for modesty; it's the whole point. A theory that can't lose can't win, and a category\ixsq{category} that can't dissolve was never real.

If the maintenance story keeps showing predictive lift under this gauntlet, confidence in its realism rises. Where it doesn't, narrow the scope, revise the mechanisms, replace the components that repeatedly underperform. At the scales where categories project, they are as real as anything else we measure~-- and just as mortal.

Words that won't hold still can still hold together~-- until they don't.
