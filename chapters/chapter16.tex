\section{Introduction: the gauntlet}
\label{sec:16:intro-gauntlet}

A theory that survives every possible result explains nothing; if it can't lose, it can't win.

The constructive argument is behind us: definitions fail, mechanisms do the work, projectibility\ixsq{projectibility!field-relative} is indexed to purpose, and the case studies have put the framework under load. Now comes the expensive part. A framework earns its keep only when it names the outcomes that would kill it~-- and shows its receipts.

This chapter is short on purpose. No new slogan, no new case study. Just a gauntlet: what we predict, what would falsify those predictions, and what work follows if we take the risk seriously. Chapter~\ref{ch:failure-modes}'s seven-step audit asks what a single category owes; the gauntlet asks what the framework owes across domains. Build studies that can make the framework wrong. That's the instruction. Figure~\ref{fig:16:gauntlet} maps the evaluation pathway.

One running case will keep this honest: English \mention{data}\ixlq{data}. Picture a conference Q\&A. A corpus linguist puts up a slide: \enquote{The data \emph{is} clear.} Someone in the third row winces. Someone else nods. In the hallway afterwards, the argument isn't about the findings; it's about the verb\ixsq{verb!boundary cases}. That argument is the test bench~-- not because \mention{data}\ixlq{data} is exotic, but because it sits exactly where the framework has to perform: at a boundary that's shifting, conditioned, and measurable.

Three diagnostics make the case concrete. Agreement (\mention{the data are}\ixlq{data} vs \mention{the data is}\ixlq{data}) tracks community norms as much as grammar. Tight count packaging (\mention{a datum}\ixlq{datum}, \mention{three data}\ixlq{data}) tests singulative\ixsq{singulative} anchoring. Loose packaging (\mention{many data}\ixlq{data} vs \mention{much data}\ixlq{data}) sits between the two. Together they give us a noun whose maintenance regime is visibly in motion.

\section{No level privilege, no level immunity}
\label{sec:16:no-level-privilege}

At the quantum scale there are no cats. At the scale of the isolated token, there are no categories. The epigraph from Ladyman and Ross isn't decoration; it's the framework's ontological commitment. A category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} can be real at phonological, morphosyntactic, semantic, discourse, or social grain~-- provided it's maintained and projectible at that grain. No level gets a crown. No level gets an exemption.

Every proposed category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} owes three debts: show clustering, identify the stabilisers that maintain it, and demonstrate forward projection~-- what learning one case buys you for the next. Clustering invites stabilisers; stabilisers underwrite projection; projection is where clustering earns its keep. That chain is the discipline behind the flexibility.

\section{The empirical commitments}
\label{sec:16:empirical-commitments}

The maintenance view\ixsq{maintenance view} is only worth anything if it yields risky expectations. Here are two.

\subsection{Prediction 1: Fraying is asymmetric, not random}
\label{sec:16:prediction-fraying}

Start with \mention{data}\ixlq{data}. As singulative\ixsq{singulative} anchoring weakens (\mention{datum}\ixlq{datum} recedes from ordinary use), tight frames should erode first: \mention{a datum}\ixlq{datum} and \mention{three data}\ixlq{data} become rare or odd. Looser frames hold longer: \mention{many data}\ixlq{data} persists. Agreement lags behind both, because agreement tracks community convention, not just semantic structure. And the variance should partition between speakers more than within them~-- individual commitments stay mostly sharp even while the population distribution looks graded.

That's the prediction in one case. The principle is general. Margins fray, but they don't fray at random: ignore conditioning and structure masquerades as noise; ignore structure and noise masquerades as structure. When stabilisers uncouple, we should see patterned decay keyed to which mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!perturbation of} weakened first~-- not all diagnostics collapsing at once.

Chapter~\ref{ch:countability} gave the template: when a noun's anchor weakens, some cues destabilise first; others hold. Chapter~\ref{ch:definiteness-and-deitality} showed the same architecture with a different split: semantic and morphosyntactic behaviour drifting out of step in constrained ways. Chapter~\ref{ch:lexical-categories} showed the typological analogue: edges fray where support is thin.

If fraying turns out to be uniform free noise across diagnostics and speakers, the maintenance account has overfit. The key point isn't \enquote{some variation}. It's structured non-synchrony.

\subsection{Prediction 2: Drift has an order (the sticky-lock effect)}
\label{sec:16:prediction-sticky-lock}

Drift should have an order. Usage and semantic pressure move first; morphosyntactic locks lag; lagging locks fail in bursts rather than smoothing out forever. The lock is \enquote{sticky}: it persists after the pressure profile that built it has shifted.

Not instant category\ixsq{category!boundary cases} death. Not indefinite linear blur. Staged decoupling~-- and, across unrelated languages under similar pressures, convergence on similar local fixes even when the material details differ.

The lag is architectural, not hierarchical. Semantic, morphosyntactic, and discourse representations can update at different rates without any one level being ontologically primary. Scope matters: language-particular predictions target concrete diagnostics inside one grammar; comparative predictions target recurring \emph{shapes} of drift across grammars; constructional predictions target specific form--meaning pairings regardless of lexical label (the competition between \mention{these data are}\ixlq{data} and \mention{this data is}\ixlq{data} as partially distinct constructions, for instance).

Back to \mention{data}\ixlq{data}. If \mention{datum}\ixlq{datum} recession yields staged erosion~-- tight before loose~-- we get sticky lag. If everything moves in lockstep, the prediction fails. And in cohorts where \mention{datum}\ixlq{datum} is absent from child-directed input, loose packaging (\mention{many data}) should stabilise before tight singulative\ixsq{singulative} packaging (\mention{a datum}\ixlq{datum}). That ordering is testable now, with age-graded corpora and perturbation designs. Trajectories, not snapshots.

\section{Model-criticism conditions}
\label{sec:16:falsification}

These aren't thought experiments. They're the two ways we could be wrong.

\subsection{Stress test A: Mechanism-light projection}
\label{sec:16:stress-essentialist}

Find a robust, cross-linguistically stable grammatical category\ixsq{category!cross-linguistic} that projects strongly while showing no identifiable maintenance structure~-- no acquisition bias, no alignment\ixsq{alignment!convergence effects} pressure, no functional anchoring\ixsq{functional anchoring}, no institutional reinforcement, no attractor-like distributional profile. If that pattern replicates across datasets and preregistered proxy controls, the maintenance account needs substantive revision. Categories that project without maintenance are the essentialist's best case and our worst.

\subsection{Stress test B: Baseline parity after noise controls}
\label{sec:16:stress-nominalist}

Show that usage variation is effectively a random walk once sampling noise is controlled: no reliable conditioning by frequency, no alignment\ixsq{alignment!convergence effects} effects, no stable register or community attractors, no basin\ixsq{basin}-like recovery after perturbation. If categories don't predict beyond convenience labels on out-of-sample data, the account needs scope reduction or re-specification. Categories that predict nothing beyond their labels are the nominalist's best case and our worst.

To limit post hoc rescue, preregister the proxy family for \enquote{maintenance structure} and report how conclusions change under alternative specifications. Evaluate cumulatively: when major domains repeatedly show no mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!perturbation of}-linked lift, downgrade strongly; when results are mixed, narrow scope; when lift is robust and replicated, raise confidence.

\subsection{Serious downgrading and revision}
\label{sec:16:serious-downgrade}

One mechanism\ixsq{mechanism!perturbation of} claim can fail in one domain while others carry weight elsewhere. That's correction, not collapse. But if repeated studies show weak support for patterned fraying, no reliable lag structure, and no stable lift over convenience labels across major domains, the framework should be treated as provisional heuristic vocabulary rather than settled explanatory ontology. That isn't business as usual. It's a substantive loss of status.

\subsection{A second case: pro-form gender\ixsq{gender!pro-form}}
\label{sec:16:gender-gauntlet}

The \mention{data}\ixlq{data} case tests erosion~-- what happens when a stabiliser weakens. Pro-form gender\ixsq{gender!pro-form} tests maintenance~-- what keeps a tight system tight. Together, the two cases put the gauntlet under load in both directions.

A family's dog has died. \enquote{She was a good dog,} says the owner. \enquote{It was old,} says the vet. Same referent; different construals; different pro-forms. The choice isn't random and it isn't a mistake. It tracks the speaker's construal of the designatum along the personhood\ixsq{personhood hierarchy} hierarchy that Chapter~\ref{ch:proform-gender} set out: personal or non-personal, and if personal, epicene or sex-specific. The vet reports a biological fact; the owner mourns a companion.

That hierarchy is maintained by three converging stabilisers. First, \term{cognitive grounding}\ixsq{cognitive grounding}: the person/non-person distinction maps onto a pre-linguistic conceptual split tied to Theory-of-Mind\ixsq{Theory of Mind} development. Children don't learn to distinguish persons from objects through grammar; grammar rides a distinction already in place. Second, \term{semantic transparency}\ixsq{semantic transparency}: the mapping from personhood to pro-form is largely predictable~-- unlike French or German noun-class gender, which must be memorised item by item. High learnability means low acquisition error, which means the system reconstructs itself cheaply in every generation. Third, \term{alignment and repair}\ixsq{alignment!in gender}: misgendering triggers real-time correction. Call a person \mention{it}\ixlq{it} and the violation is immediate, social, and repaired on the spot. This triad~-- cognitive scaffold, transparent mapping, conversational enforcement~-- is what keeps English pro-form gender\ixsq{gender!pro-form} coupled tightly despite having no morphological agreement to lean on.

Each stabiliser generates a testable prediction. Cognitive grounding predicts that children's pro-form assignment for novel entities (robots, AI assistants, animated characters) should track Theory-of-Mind\ixsq{Theory of Mind} attribution, not animacy per se: a child who attributes intentions to a robot should produce \mention{he}\ixlq{he} or \mention{she}\ixlq{she}, while a child who treats the same robot as a tool should produce \mention{it}\ixlq{it}. Semantic transparency predicts rapid stabilisation: when a novel entity enters a speech community (a new pet species, a named AI), pro-form assignment should converge quickly once the community's personhood construal settles, with no extended period of gender-agreement errors comparable to those seen in opaque systems. Alignment and repair predict that gender corrections should be faster and more socially clustered than other grammatical repairs~-- more like correcting a factual error than correcting a morphological slip.

The chain-coherence constraint adds a system-level prediction. Chapter~\ref{ch:proform-gender} showed that once a referent is construed as personal within a reference chain, switching to a non-personal pro-form is disfavoured (\mention{the dog who attacked his owner} is coherent; \mention{the dog which attacked his owner} is degraded). If this constraint is real, mixed-gender chains should incur measurable processing costs~-- longer reading times, higher error rates in recall, and more frequent spontaneous repairs. If it isn't, if mixed chains process as smoothly as consistent ones under controlled conditions, the coupling claim needs narrowing.

What would falsify the maintenance account? Three findings, any one of which would force serious revision. First, no designatum conditioning: if pro-form selection shows no reliable sensitivity to personhood construal once syntactic context is controlled, the mechanism story is wrong. Second, no repair clustering: if gender mismatches are repaired no more readily or forcefully than other agreement errors, the social-enforcement stabiliser isn't doing distinctive work. Third, random walk under controls: if novel-entity assignment shows no convergence pattern~-- no predictable trajectory from uncertainty to settled pro-form~-- and instead looks like noise, the transparency mechanism is weaker than claimed. The \mention{data}\ixlq{data} case asks whether erosion is patterned. The gender case asks whether maintenance is earned.

\section{The research agenda}
\label{sec:16:research-agenda}

Throughout this book I've made predictions I haven't tested. Individually, each promissory note is defensible~-- a theory book earns its keep by generating testable claims, not by running every experiment. But the notes accumulate, and the reader deserves an honest ledger. Here are the outstanding debts, collected in one place so they can't hide.

\textsc{Countability} (Ch.~\ref{ch:countability}). The two-cluster architecture predicts that classifier languages should show the same tight/loose asymmetry as English: diagnostics closest to individuation should resist change while packaging diagnostics should drift. The prediction is stated but the cross-linguistic work hasn't been done. A systematic comparison of Mandarin, Thai, and Japanese classifier systems against the implicational hierarchy would be a direct test.

\textsc{Definiteness} (Ch.~\ref{ch:definiteness-and-deitality}). Two predictions need experimental confirmation. First, prosodic rescue should selectively affect \mention{there}-resistance (a form-cluster property) but not partitive licensing (a deeper distributional fact)~-- a dissociation that would confirm the two-cluster architecture independently of the acquisition data. Second, children should master the distributional restrictions (the form cluster) before the semantic ones (identifiability, uniqueness)~-- and critically, this acquisition ordering needs to be shown as a prediction of the architecture rather than merely consistent with it.

\textsc{Lexical categories} (Ch.~\ref{ch:lexical-categories}). The adjective-as-plumage diagnosis has an acknowledged falsifier: a language with a small, closed adjective class that nevertheless has its own non-borrowed morphological paradigm would be skeletal, not plumage. Whether such a case exists is an empirical question that the typological literature should be able to answer. Separately, the claim that lexical category is itself an HPC~-- a category of categories~-- is sketched but not tested.

\textsc{Pro-form gender} (Ch.~\ref{ch:proform-gender}). The three maintenance mechanisms (cognitive grounding, semantic transparency, alignment/repair) generate predictions that remain promissory: children should assign pro-forms to robots based on Theory-of-Mind tests, not animacy alone; novel entities should be rapidly assigned to the personhood hierarchy; gender repairs should be faster than number repairs. The chain-coherence effects, which carry substantial theoretical weight, rest on constructed examples and introspective judgments rather than corpus or experimental evidence.

\textsc{The category zipper} (Ch.~\ref{ch:the-category-zipper}). The discourse level appears in the stabiliser-weighting table but received no evidence. If the framework claims scale-invariance, skipping discourse is conspicuous. Even a negative case~-- showing why discourse categories resist the two-diagnostic test~-- would strengthen the argument.

\textsc{Grammaticality} (Ch.~\ref{ch:grammaticality-itself}). The three-register phenomenology is testable: if the qualitative, reactive, and normative readouts collapse to a single factor under controlled measurement, the tripartite structure is mnemonic rather than mechanistic. Schema drift (Vagnino and Walker) predicts specific satiation asymmetries that can be tested with current methods.

\textsc{Social stabilisation} (Ch.~\ref{ch:social-stabilization}). The mixed-bin diagnostic predicts that conditioning should recover tight structure from apparently loose distributions. The methodological commitment~-- no conditioning gain, no maintenance claim~-- needs worked demonstrations beyond the double copula and existential \mention{there's}. Register-based predictions (academic writing, clinical discourse) are the most accessible testing grounds.

That's seven open fronts. Some will confirm the framework; some will force scope narrowing; some may require substantive revision. The point of collecting them here isn't to immunise the theory but to ensure that the debts have addresses. A framework that can't point to its own outstanding IOUs has stopped being empirical.

To see what the gauntlet changes in practice, consider a concrete case. A syntactician studies \mention{otherwise}\ixlq{otherwise}~-- the word that opened Chapter~\ref{ch:words-wont-hold-still}. The old question: is it an adverb or a conjunction? The HPC question: what maintains its distributional profile, and where does maintenance thin out? The study design shifts accordingly. Instead of eliciting categorical judgements (acceptable/unacceptable) on constructed sentences, the researcher tracks which syntactic frames resist perturbation across registers, which vary between speakers, and which have changed diachronically. The gauntlet adds a further demand: preregister the variance structure you expect, name the outcome that would force you to treat \mention{otherwise}\ixlq{otherwise} as a convenience label rather than a maintained category, and report what you find either way. The question hasn't just been reframed. The standards for answering it have been raised.

\section{Conclusion: the zipper\ixsq{zipper} under load}
\label{sec:16:zipper-at-scale}

If you've ever overpacked a suitcase until the zipper\ixsq{zipper} protested, you already understand boundary behaviour. Put the zipper\ixsq{zipper} under load. Ask what keeps the teeth meshed. Ask where slip begins. Ask which perturbation makes the slider skip. Chapter~\ref{ch:the-category-zipper} gave the image; this chapter treats it as an instrument.

The shift is this. The question is no longer \enquote{what are categories, in themselves?} It's: what keeps this category\ixsq{category!as HPC kind} together, for whom, under which pressures, and for how long? That isn't a retreat from realism. It's the only version of realism that can survive the evidence.

At the quantum scale there are no cats. At the scale of the isolated token there are no categories. Categories live at the scale where maintenance operates~-- where acquisition, entrenchment, alignment\ixsq{alignment!convergence effects}, and gatekeeping converge to keep a profile projectable\ixsq{projectable}. That's the book's ontological commitment, and it's worth stating plainly in the final pages: we aren't arguing that categories are fictions sustained by habit. We're arguing that the sustaining \emph{is} the reality. Remove the maintenance and you don't reveal the category\ixsq{category!reality of} underneath. You dissolve it.

\textcite{ladyman2007}\ixnq{Ladyman, James}\ixnq{Ross, Don} call this \term{scale-relative ontology}\ixsq{scale-relative ontology}: what counts as a real pattern depends on the grain at which you measure. Their target is physics, but the principle transfers. A phoneme\ixsq{phoneme!as HPC kind} is real at the grain of the syllable; a noun\ixsq{noun!as HPC kind} is real at the grain where distributional, morphosyntactic, and semantic properties cluster and project; a register is real at the grain where conditioning structure recovers predictability from apparent noise. No level of linguistic organisation gets a crown, and no level gets an exemption~-- which is just the three-debts discipline restated as ontology. The \term{meta-Occam\ixsq{meta-Occam}} principle from Chapter~\ref{ch:kinds-without-essences} follows: be parsimonious about mechanisms, not about labels. If a traditional label names a maintained profile, keep it; if it doesn't, replace it without guilt.

The comparative consequence is equally direct. Comparative concepts can stay useful without guaranteeing a language-particular category\ixsq{category!cross-linguistic} in every grammar. Linguists can compare adjective\ixsq{adjective}-like behaviour across languages even where no dedicated adjective\ixsq{adjective} category\ixsq{category!cross-linguistic} is maintained inside a given language. That's a null case, not a framework failure. The absence of a category at one grain, in one language, doesn't threaten the reality of that category at the grain where it is maintained elsewhere. Scale-relative ontology gives the resources to say this without equivocation.

What changes in practice? Fieldwork stops asking \enquote{is X a Y?} and starts asking \enquote{what maintains X as a Y, and where does that maintenance thin out?} Huddleston's\ixnq{Huddleston, Rodney} puzzle about \mention{otherwise}\ixlq{otherwise} now has a shape: not \enquote{which box?} but \enquote{what maintains this boundary, and for whom?} Argumentation stops treating boundary cases as embarrassments and starts treating them as the most informative data in the sample. Study design stops aiming for the clean centre and starts building gauntlets~-- predictions that can fail, stress tests that can bite, baselines that can win.

The framework is young and the evidence is partial. The predictions in this chapter could fail. Some probably will. That's not a caveat bolted on for modesty; it's the whole point. Chapter~\ref{ch:words-wont-hold-still} noted that foundational questions in physics~-- dismissed for decades as philosophy~-- produced Bell's theorem and the tools of quantum computing. The gauntlet is our Bell test: foundational clarity turned into empirical risk. A theory that can't lose can't win, and a category\ixsq{category!reality of} that can't dissolve was never real.

If the maintenance story keeps showing predictive lift under this gauntlet, confidence in its realism rises. Where it doesn't, narrow the scope, revise the mechanisms, replace the components that repeatedly underperform. At the scales where categories project, they're as real as anything else we measure~-- and just as mortal.

Words that won't hold still can still hold together~-- until they don't.
